# Arizona Military Installations and Compatibility

## Summary

Arizona is one of the most militarily active states in the country, with major installations supporting year-round flight operations, training ranges, and logistics functions. The presence of these installations directly constrains adjacent land use through federal airspace requirements, Arizona's military airport compatibility statutes, and local general plans that incorporate military compatibility elements. CRE developers operating near any Arizona installation must identify applicable noise contours, accident-potential zones (APZs), and height restrictions before underwriting or entitling a project.

## Major Arizona Military Installations

### Luke Air Force Base
Located in the West Valley (Glendale/Litchfield Park area), Luke AFB is the primary F-35 training base in the United States. Flight operations are intensive and generate significant noise footprints extending into rapidly developing residential and commercial corridors in Goodyear, Peoria, Surprise, and Glendale. Luke's Military Influence Planning Area (MIPA) and associated noise/APZ contours have been incorporated into the general plans of multiple surrounding municipalities.

### Davis-Monthan Air Force Base
Located in southeast Tucson, Davis-Monthan hosts the 355th Wing (A-10 and other aircraft) and the Air Force Materiel Command's aircraft storage and reclamation mission (known as the "Boneyard"). Its noise contours and APZs extend into Tucson's urban fabric and affect infill development in the southeast quadrant of the metro.

### Marine Corps Air Station (MCAS) Yuma
Located adjacent to the City of Yuma in southwest Arizona, MCAS Yuma is a major tactical air training installation. Its flight corridors over the desert and proximity to the Yuma Proving Ground create a large controlled area that limits commercial and residential development on its fringes.

### Fort Huachuca
Located in Sierra Vista (Cochise County), Fort Huachuca is the Army's primary intelligence center and home of the Army's electronic warfare and unmanned aerial systems testing. Its presence shapes the entire Sierra Vista economy and constrains development around the installation's perimeter.

### Yuma Proving Ground

Its boundaries and associated restricted airspace effectively cap development in large areas of the county.

## How It's Regulated — Arizona Military Compatibility Framework

### State Enabling Authority

The Arizona Department of Emergency and Military Affairs (DEMA) plays a coordination role, and the Arizona Military Airport Preservation Working Group (or its successor entity — verify current name) has produced compatibility guidance.

### Joint Land Use Studies (JLUS)

Most major Arizona installations have been the subject of a federally funded Joint Land Use Study (JLUS), a collaborative planning process between the installation, surrounding local governments, and the Department of Defense's Office of Local Defense Community Cooperation (OLDCC, formerly OECDA). JLUS documents identify noise contours, APZs, and compatible/incompatible land uses, and they produce recommendations for local ordinance adoption. Local governments are not legally required to implement JLUS recommendations, but most Arizona municipalities with installations nearby have incorporated them into their general plans and zoning codes.

### Noise Zones and Accident-Potential Zones

The standard compatibility framework uses:

- **Noise contours** expressed in Day-Night Average Sound Level (DNL or Ldn), measured in decibels. Common thresholds: DNL 65 dB (residential generally incompatible above this level), DNL 70 dB, DNL 75 dB.
- **Accident-Potential Zones (APZs):** defined areas at the end of runways with elevated risk from aircraft accidents. APZ-I (highest risk, immediately off the runway end) and APZ-II (lower risk, further out) have explicit use-incompatibility tables — certain uses (high-density residential, schools, hospitals, assembly) are prohibited; others (industrial, warehousing, open space) are compatible.
- **Clear Zones:** The area immediately off the runway end that must remain clear of all structures.

### Height Restrictions

Military airports generate Federal Aviation Regulations Part 77 imaginary surfaces (horizontal, conical, transitional, approach surfaces) that cap structure heights in the vicinity. These surfaces are filed with the FAA and must be checked via FAA Form 7460-1 (Notice of Proposed Construction or Alteration) before building anything tall near an active military airport. Arizona counties and municipalities often codify these restrictions in their zoning codes as airport overlay zones.

## CRE Implications

**Height is constrained near all active military airports.** Even commercial and industrial projects well outside the APZs may be subject to FAA/military height limits via the Part 77 surfaces. A warehouse, data center, or cell tower that exceeds the imaginary surfaces requires FAA coordination and potentially Air Force coordination.

**Use restrictions in APZs are real.** Multifamily residential, assisted living, schools, and daycares are frequently incompatible uses in APZ-II and definitely incompatible in APZ-I. Retail and office intensity may also be capped. This can eliminate or substantially reduce the value of entitled pads near Luke AFB or Davis-Monthan.

**Disclosure at sale/lease.** Failure to disclose military noise/APZ status can create legal exposure. Any title report or Phase I-equivalent due diligence for a property near a major installation should include a military compatibility check.

**Encroachment policy.** The Department of Defense actively monitors encroachment that degrades training or testing missions. Lobbying from installations can influence local general plan decisions in ways that limit upzoning near the base perimeter.

**Economic anchor.** Conversely, installations are major employment anchors: Fort Huachuca is the economic engine of Sierra Vista; Luke drives much of the West Valley industrial and retail economy. Properties that do not sit within the noise/APZ zones but are proximate to the installation can benefit from defense contractor tenancy, hospitality demand, and retail traffic.
