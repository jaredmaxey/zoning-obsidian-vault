# Arizona Tribal Lands Overview

## Summary

Arizona is home to 22 federally recognized tribal nations and one of the largest concentrations of tribal land in the United States. Tribal lands held in federal trust — often called "Indian trust land" or simply "reservation land" — are subject to a fundamentally different legal regime than fee-simple private land. They are NOT subject to state or local (county/city) zoning authority, building codes, or land-use regulations, and they are not subject to state court jurisdiction in the ordinary sense. The primary regulatory authorities are the applicable tribal nation (via its own tribal government, tribal codes, and tribal courts) and the federal government through the Bureau of Indian Affairs (BIA) and other agencies. This dual sovereignty framework has profound implications for any CRE operator considering a project on or adjacent to tribal land.

## How It's Regulated

### Federal Trust Status

Land held in federal trust is legally titled in the United States government for the benefit of a tribe or individual tribal member. It cannot be sold, mortgaged, or transferred without federal approval, and it is generally exempt from state and local property taxes. The BIA administers trust land under federal statutes including the Indian Reorganization Act and subsequent legislation (exact citations — TBD — verify against current BIA statutory authority). The result is a regulatory island: the usual Arizona state entitlement process (rezonings, conditional use permits, general plan amendments) does not apply.

### Tribal Governance and Codes

Each tribal nation exercises its own governmental authority over its land. Larger nations such as the Navajo Nation and Tohono O'odham Nation have comprehensive tribal codes covering land use, business licensing, and development standards. Development on tribal land requires compliance with the applicable tribe's permit and approval process, which varies significantly from tribe to tribe. Some tribes have well-staffed planning departments with formal zoning ordinances; others have less formalized processes. There is no single "tribal zoning" framework — practitioners must research the specific tribe's governance structure.

### Leasing Mechanisms

Because trust land cannot be transferred in fee simple, CRE development on tribal land is typically structured as a long-term ground lease:

- **BIA-approved leases:** Historically, leases on trust land required BIA approval, creating delays and administrative burdens for commercial development.
- **HEARTH Act (2012):** The Helping Expedite and Advance Responsible Tribal Homeownership Act authorized tribes to approve and execute their own leases on tribal land without BIA review, provided the tribe has adopted a federally approved leasing regulation. This substantially streamlined the commercial leasing process for participating tribes. (Verify which Arizona tribes have adopted HEARTH Act regulations.)
- **Business Site Leases:** The standard instrument for commercial and industrial development on tribal land. Lease terms can be long (50–99 years in some cases — TBD — verify typical term ranges), which allows commercial financing, though lenders remain cautious given the unique collateral regime.
- **Tribal Economic Development Bonds:** Certain tribal entities can issue tax-exempt bonds for economic development projects on or near tribal land, offering a financing tool not available in the standard private market.

### Federal Involvement

Even with HEARTH Act delegation, federal law still governs many aspects of tribal land development: environmental review under NEPA may be required, Section 106 (National Historic Preservation Act) consultation may apply, and BIA retains supervisory roles. Projects with federal nexus (federal permits, federal funding) trigger full federal environmental review.

## Major Arizona Tribal Nations and Reservations

The following tribes are among the largest and most commercially active in Arizona. This list reflects nations generally known to have significant land holdings and/or existing commercial development; practitioners should verify enrollment, jurisdiction boundaries, and governance structures directly with each nation.

| Tribal Nation | Region / Location | Commercial Activity Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Navajo Nation | Northeast AZ (and portions of NM, UT) | Largest reservation in the U.S. by area; extensive coal, gas, and retail leasing history |
| Tohono O'odham Nation | Southern AZ (Sonoran Desert, near Tucson) | Large land base; casino gaming, energy development |
| Gila River Indian Community | South-central AZ (between Phoenix and Tucson) | Active commercial/industrial leasing; major retail and gaming presence |
| Salt River Pima-Maricopa Indian Community | Northeast Metro Phoenix (Scottsdale/Tempe area) | High commercial activity; extensive retail, office, and entertainment development |
| Fort McDowell Yavapai Nation | Northeast Metro Phoenix (near Fountain Hills) | Casino gaming and resort development |
| Ak-Chin Indian Community | South of Phoenix (near Maricopa) | Agriculture, gaming, commercial development |
| White Mountain Apache Tribe | East-central AZ (White Mountains) | Timber, gaming, tourism |
| San Carlos Apache Tribe | East-central AZ (Globe area) | Mining history, gaming |
| Hopi Tribe | Northeast AZ (surrounded by Navajo Nation) | Distinct sovereign; limited commercial development; unique land tenure issues |

## CRE Implications

**Standard entitlement does not apply.** A developer cannot file a rezoning application with the county or city when working on trust land. The entitlement process is the tribe's own approval process, which must be understood on its own terms.

**Financing is structurally different.** Fee-simple collateral underwriting does not work for trust land. Lenders must understand leasehold financing and, often, must obtain tribal waiver of sovereign immunity to perfect a security interest. This limits the lender pool to experienced tribal-land lenders and increases cost of capital. Some commercial banks and the BIA itself have tribal lending programs.

**Tax exposure changes.** Trust land is generally exempt from state and local property tax, which can reduce operating costs but also eliminates a key underwriting input. Sales taxes and other tribal taxes may apply in their place.

**Adjacency matters.** Even properties on fee-simple land adjacent to a reservation may face unique considerations: road access across trust land, utility easements, water rights (Arizona's water law interacts with tribal water rights in complex ways — see [[Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)]] for the state-side framework), and viewshed/cultural resource constraints.

**Economic opportunity.** Tribal land in metro Phoenix (Salt River Pima-Maricopa, Gila River) is in a uniquely valuable position: land in the path of growth that is not subject to municipal zoning. Tribes have leveraged this to develop major retail, entertainment, and hospitality projects on their own terms. For the right operator with the right tribal relationship, the absence of standard zoning creates flexibility that fee-simple land in the same geography does not have.
