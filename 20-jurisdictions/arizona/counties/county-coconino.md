# Coconino County, Arizona

## Overview

Coconino County is Arizona's second-largest county by land area and one of the largest counties in the contiguous United States. It is anchored by the City of Flagstaff (the county seat) and encompasses a vast, ecologically diverse territory including the Colorado Plateau, the Grand Canyon's south rim, the Navajo Nation, and the scenic red-rock landscape around Sedona. Despite its enormous geographic footprint, the county's unincorporated developable area is significantly constrained by the large share of land held by the federal government (National Forests, National Parks, and Monument designations), tribal trust lands, and state trust land.

For CRE purposes, unincorporated Coconino County's most relevant development contexts are: (1) rural residential and resort/tourism-oriented parcels in and around Sedona (Sedona straddles the Coconino/Yavapai county line — parcels in the unincorporated Coconino portion are regulated by Coconino County); (2) rural residential land on the fringes of Flagstaff; and (3) agricultural land in the Verde Valley. [[Land]] evaluation in this county requires careful identification of land ownership status (federal, tribal, state trust, or private fee) before assuming developability.

Coconino County's Comprehensive Plan guides long-range growth for unincorporated areas, with a strong emphasis on environmental stewardship, rural character preservation, and coordination with federal land management agencies. Tourism and hospitality-related development near scenic corridors is a recurring CRE use type in this county.

## Governing Body

The Coconino County Board of Supervisors (five members, elected by district) is the governing authority for land-use in unincorporated Coconino County. The Board approves major rezonings, General Plan Amendments, and subdivision plats following public hearings and recommendations from the Coconino County Planning and Zoning Commission. The Commission is the primary hearing body for most land-use applications.

## Unincorporated Area Zoning

Coconino County regulates unincorporated land through the **Coconino County Zoning Ordinance** (exact title and current edition — TBD — verify against the official county code). The ordinance covers the range of uses found in a large, predominantly rural county with significant scenic and environmental values.

Zone district families typically include (TBD — verify exact district names and codes against the current ordinance):

- **Agricultural/Rural** — large-lot districts covering the vast unincorporated rural areas; many parcels carry legacy agricultural designations even where development interest exists
- **Residential** — graduated density from rural residential to suburban residential
- **Commercial/Resort** — commercial districts applicable in settlement areas and resort/tourism corridors, including areas near Sedona and scenic highways
- **Industrial** — limited industrial uses appropriate to rural county contexts
- **Special/Overlay Districts** — scenic corridor overlays, floodplain, and environmental resource overlays in sensitive areas near Grand Canyon, Sedona, and National Forest boundaries

Sedona note: Sedona is a City that straddles the Coconino/Yavapai county line. Unincorporated land surrounding the City of Sedona in Coconino County is regulated by Coconino County; unincorporated land in the Yavapai County portion is regulated by Yavapai County. The City of Sedona has its own zoning for its incorporated territory.

[[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] applications are heard by the Planning and Zoning Commission with a recommendation to the Board. [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] (or Special Use Permit — verify terminology) is required for conditional uses. [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] proceedings are required when a proposed use conflicts with the Comprehensive Plan's land-use designation.

## Subdivision Regulations

Coconino County administers subdivision of unincorporated land under its subdivision regulations, consistent with Arizona Revised Statutes Title 11. Process elements include:

- **Preliminary Plat** review addressing layout, access, utilities, and environmental constraints
- **Final Plat** recordation after conditions and improvement agreements are in place
- **[[Subdivision Approval]]** by the Board of Supervisors for major subdivisions; administrative tracks for minor subdivisions (thresholds TBD — verify)

## Permit Process

Development in unincorporated Coconino County generally follows this sequence (high-level; verify current process with Planning and Zoning):

1. **Pre-Application Meeting** — strongly recommended given the complexity of federal land adjacencies, tribal boundaries, and environmental constraints
2. **Land Ownership / Status Review** — confirm parcel is private fee land and not subject to federal, tribal, or state trust land restrictions
3. **Entitlement (if required)** — [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], or [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] through Planning and Zoning Commission and Board of Supervisors
4. **Subdivision Plat (if applicable)** — [[Subdivision Approval]] via subdivision review track
5. **Site Plan / Development Plan Review** — for commercial, resort, and multifamily projects
6. **Building Permit** — submitted after entitlement; includes plan review
7. **Inspections and Certificate of Occupancy**
