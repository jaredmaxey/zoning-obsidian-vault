# Pinal County, Arizona

## Overview

Pinal County occupies a strategic position in Arizona's I-10 corridor between the Phoenix and Tucson metropolitan areas, making it one of the state's fastest-growing counties. The county seat is Florence. Unlike Maricopa and Pima counties, Pinal County's unincorporated areas represent a very large share of the county's total land area — much of it agricultural, desert, and tribal land that is experiencing intense development pressure from Phoenix metro expansion and from the growth of newer cities like Maricopa (the city) and Casa Grande.

From a CRE perspective, Pinal County is a primary [[Land]] play and [[Entitlement Play]] market: large parcels of agricultural and rural land are being converted to residential, industrial, and logistics uses as the county absorbs Phoenix metro overflow and attracts semiconductor and manufacturing investment (notably in the Queen Creek/San Tan Valley corridor and Casa Grande area). The county's Planning and Development Services Department manages entitlements for a large unincorporated territory with significant infrastructure buildout needs.

The county's General Plan addresses land-use patterns, transportation infrastructure, and growth management for unincorporated areas. Water availability — governed in part by the Arizona Department of Water Resources — is a critical constraint for large-scale development in Pinal County and must be addressed in the entitlement process.

## Governing Body

The Pinal County Board of Supervisors (five members, elected by district) is the governing authority for land-use decisions in unincorporated Pinal County. The Board approves rezonings, General Plan Amendments, and major subdivision plats following recommendations from the Pinal County Planning and Zoning Commission. The Commission holds public hearings on most land-use applications.

## Planning Department

**Pinal County Planning and Development Services** administers zoning, subdivision, building permits, and inspections for unincorporated Pinal County. The department does not exercise authority within incorporated city or town limits. Applicants should consult the department early in the process given the complexity of water availability, infrastructure financing, and entitlement sequencing common in Pinal County development projects.

## Subdivision Regulations

Pinal County administers subdivision of unincorporated land under its **Subdivision Regulations** (exact title — TBD — verify), consistent with Arizona Revised Statutes Title 11. Large master-planned projects are common, requiring:

- **Preliminary Plat** approval addressing layout, phasing, infrastructure, drainage, and water availability documentation (a key step given Arizona's Active Management Area requirements)
- **Final Plat** recordation after conditions and improvement agreements or bonds are in place
- **Infrastructure financing plans** often via Community Facilities Districts (CFDs) or improvement districts
- **[[Subdivision Approval]]** by the Board of Supervisors; administrative tracks for minor subdivisions (thresholds TBD — verify)

Water availability certificates from the Arizona Department of Water Resources (ADWR) are required for subdivisions of six or more lots in Pinal County's Active Management Area.

## Permit Process

Development in unincorporated Pinal County generally follows this sequence (high-level; verify current process with Planning and Development Services):

1. **Pre-Application Conference** — recommended; staff identifies water availability issues, infrastructure requirements, and entitlement path
2. **Water Availability / ADWR Assessment** — critical early step for any subdivision or major development
3. **Entitlement** — [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], or [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] as needed; heard by Planning and Zoning Commission; Board decision
4. **Subdivision Plat** — [[Subdivision Approval]] process including infrastructure improvement plans
5. **Site Plan / Development Plan Review** — for commercial, industrial, and multifamily projects
6. **Building Permit** — submitted after entitlement clearance; includes plan review by county building division
7. **Inspections and Certificate of Occupancy**

Infrastructure financing — often through Community Facilities Districts — is a common component of large projects and is arranged in parallel with the entitlement process.
