# Arizona State Overview

## Overview

Arizona is one of the fastest-growing states in the U.S., with the Phoenix (Maricopa County) and Tucson (Pima County) metropolitan areas driving outsized demand across multifamily, industrial, retail, and mixed-use asset classes. Population growth from domestic migration, a business-friendly regulatory posture, and a below-national-average cost of doing business make Arizona an attractive entry market for CRE developers. The two constraints that distinguish Arizona from most Sun Belt peers are water availability and the scale of state trust land — both require early diligence on any greenfield or land play.

Land-use authority in Arizona flows from the state legislature to municipalities and counties through two distinct enabling-statute titles. Municipal zoning is authorized under [[A.R.S. Title 9 — Cities and Towns (Municipal Zoning)]], while county zoning authority over unincorporated areas derives from [[A.R.S. Title 11 — Counties (County Zoning)]]. Virtually all urban development occurs inside municipal boundaries or in areas subject to pending annexation, so Title 9 is the operative frame for most commercial projects.

## Statutory Framework

Arizona's primary land-use enabling statutes are:

- **[[A.R.S. Title 9 — Cities and Towns (Municipal Zoning)]]** — Governs municipal incorporation, general plans, zoning districts, rezoning and variance procedures, development agreements, and municipal impact fees. This is the foundational authority for every incorporated city and town's zoning ordinance.
- **[[A.R.S. Title 11 — Counties (County Zoning)]]** — Governs county government structure, comprehensive plans, and zoning of unincorporated areas. County zoning applies only outside municipal boundaries and outside any city's annexation planning area.
- **[[A.R.S. Title 33 — Property]]** — Governs conveyances, liens, condominiums, planned communities, HOAs, and landlord-tenant law; directly relevant to structuring CRE acquisitions, ground leases, and for-sale product.
- **[[A.R.S. Title 32 — Professions and Occupations (Real Estate Licensing)]]** — Governs brokerage licensing and the subdivision public report (Arizona Department of Real Estate jurisdiction), a prerequisite for marketing lots or units in a new subdivision.
- **[[A.R.S. Title 45 — Waters]]** — Governs the groundwater code, Active Management Areas (AMAs), and ADWR authority; directly gates development feasibility in most of the Phoenix, Tucson, and Prescott metro areas. See also [[Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)]].

## Home Rule vs. Dillon's Rule

Arizona is generally a **Dillon's-Rule state**: local governments possess only the powers expressly granted by the state legislature, necessarily implied by those grants, or indispensable to declared purposes. Without a legislative grant, a municipality cannot act.

The significant exception is the **charter city**. Arizona's constitution allows cities above a population threshold to adopt a charter, which confers broad home-rule authority over "purely municipal affairs." Charter cities — including Phoenix, Tucson, Mesa, Chandler, Scottsdale, Tempe, Glendale, Peoria, and Gilbert, among others — may legislate on land use, zoning, and development standards independently, without needing specific state authorization, as long as the subject matter is a local (not statewide) concern. When state law and a charter city ordinance conflict, the charter city's rule governs on municipal affairs; state law governs on matters of statewide concern.

**Practical effect for CRE:**
- In charter cities, developers interact with locally enacted zoning codes that may diverge substantially from neighboring jurisdictions. Phoenix's zoning ordinance, for example, is fully self-contained and does not require state approval to amend.
- In non-charter towns and in unincorporated county areas, state enabling statutes define the procedural floor (notice requirements, hearing procedures, appeal rights) that the local code must meet or exceed.
- Development agreements — long-term vesting of entitlements in exchange for developer obligations — are authorized by statute for charter and non-charter municipalities alike; they are a standard tool in large Arizona master-planned projects.

## State-Level Agencies

| Agency | Role |
|--------|------|
| **Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE)** | Licenses brokers and salespersons; issues subdivision public reports (required before marketing unsubdivided land or new subdivision lots/units); regulates timeshare and membership camping. |
| **Arizona State Land Department (ASLD)** | Manages roughly 9.2 million acres of state trust land on behalf of beneficiaries (primarily K–12 education). Trust land must be auctioned at appraised value before disposition; master-planned communities and new towns commonly involve trust land lease or auction. ASLD is a key stakeholder for any large greenfield development in the Phoenix fringe, East Valley, or West Valley. |
| **Arizona Department of Water Resources (ADWR)** | Administers the groundwater code, designates Active Management Areas, issues Certificates of Assured Water Supply (CAWS) and Designations of Assured Water Supply (DAWS), and manages the 100-year water-adequacy review that gates subdivision approvals in AMAs. See [[Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)]]. |
| **Arizona Department of Housing (ADOH)** | Administers the federal Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program as the state's qualified allocation plan (QAP) administrator; also manages HOME funds and state housing trust fund. |
| **Arizona Commerce Authority (ACA)** | Business recruitment and incentive programs; administers certain Opportunity Zone facilitation and the Arizona Competes Fund. |
| **Arizona Department of Environmental Quality (ADEQ)** | Environmental permitting including underground storage tanks, air quality, and water quality; relevant to brownfield redevelopment. |

## Tax Environment

Arizona imposes **no state transfer tax** on real property — one of the cleanest closing environments in the country. Property taxes are levied by counties based on assessed value; residential assessment ratios differ from commercial. Commercial property is assessed at a higher ratio than owner-occupied residential (TBD — verify current assessment ratios with county assessors; `needs-verification`).

**Opportunity Zones:** Arizona contains designated Qualified Opportunity Zones across multiple counties, including tracts in central Phoenix, south Tucson, and portions of Pinal County. Federal OZ tax-deferral and exclusion benefits apply; no separate state OZ incentive layer as of this writing — TBD verify current state legislative status (`needs-verification`).

**LIHTC:** ADOH allocates federal 9% and 4% LIHTC credits under the state Qualified Allocation Plan (QAP). Developers seeking LIHTC financing in Arizona must apply through ADOH's competitive cycle for 9% credits or via bond-financed 4% credits with ADOH approval.

## Disclosure & Closing Norms

- **Public Report (ADRE):** Any subdivision of six or more lots, parcels, or interests requires an ADRE-issued Public Report before marketing. The report discloses infrastructure commitments, water supply status, and material conditions. Delivery of the Public Report to each buyer is a legal prerequisite to contract execution in most for-sale residential and commercial lot contexts.
- **Title and Escrow:** Arizona is a title-insurance state. Escrow is the standard closing mechanism, typically handled by a title company acting as escrow agent. There is no state-mandated attorney-at-closing requirement. The buyer typically selects the title company in commercial transactions, though negotiated.
- **No Transfer Tax:** Arizona does not impose a real estate transfer tax or documentary stamp tax — a meaningful cost advantage relative to many states.
- **Earnest Money / Deed of Trust:** Arizona is a deed-of-trust state for mortgage security. Non-judicial trustee's-sale foreclosure is the standard remedy; the statutory timeline is relatively short compared to judicial-foreclosure states.

## Key Counties

The seven counties most relevant to CRE development in Arizona:

- [[Maricopa County, Arizona]] — Phoenix MSA; largest county by population and economic activity; home to Phoenix, Scottsdale, Tempe, Mesa, Chandler, Gilbert, Glendale, Peoria, and dozens of other municipalities.
- [[Pima County, Arizona]] — Tucson MSA; second-largest metro; University of Arizona anchors knowledge-economy demand; significant defense and aerospace presence.
- [[Pinal County, Arizona]] — Phoenix–Tucson corridor; fastest-growing county; major greenfield industrial and residential development activity; large areas of state trust land and tribal land.
- [[Yavapai County, Arizona]] — Prescott metro; retirement and second-home market; AMA-governed groundwater; significant rural and exurban land play activity.
- [[Coconino County, Arizona]] — Flagstaff; Northern Arizona University; tourism and outdoor recreation driver; high-altitude constraints on development.
- [[Mohave County, Arizona]] — Lake Havasu City, Kingman, Bullhead City; Colorado River frontage; growth from California migration; less complex regulatory environment.
- [[Yuma County, Arizona]] — Agriculture and logistics; border economy; military (MCAS Yuma); growing industrial and cold-storage demand.

## Notable Statewide Programs

- **Assured Water Supply Program:** ADWR's 100-year water adequacy requirement is the single most important statewide constraint on greenfield development in Active Management Areas. Any subdivision in an AMA must demonstrate a 100-year assured water supply via a Certificate or Designation before ADRE can issue a Public Report. See [[Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)]].
- **LIHTC (ADOH):** Competitive 9% LIHTC allocation and 4% bond-financed LIHTC; QAP scoring rewards proximity to transit, services, and income targeting.
- **Opportunity Zones:** Federal program; Arizona tracts identified. No separate state-level incentive layer confirmed as of drafting — verify (`needs-verification`).
- **State Trust Land Auctions (ASLD):** Large-scale development often requires bidding on or leasing state trust land; ASLD auctions are the primary mechanism for converting trust land to private development in the Phoenix and Tucson fringes.
- **Development Agreements:** Arizona statutes authorize municipalities and counties to enter long-term development agreements that vest entitlements. Widely used in master-planned community contexts to lock in approved uses, densities, and infrastructure obligations for 10–20 year horizons. Relevant to [[Entitlement Play]] and [[Ground-Up Development]].
