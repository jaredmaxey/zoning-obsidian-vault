# A.R.S. Title 11 — Counties (County Zoning)

## Scope

A.R.S. Title 11 governs Arizona's fifteen counties — their organization, elected officials, powers, finances, and regulatory authority. For land-use and CRE purposes, the critical grant is the authority for counties to adopt zoning ordinances and comprehensive plans that regulate land within **unincorporated** portions of the county — that is, areas outside any incorporated municipality's boundaries. Once a parcel is annexed into a city or town, Title 11 county zoning no longer applies; the municipality's Title 9 authority takes over.

Title 11 county zoning is directly relevant to rural land plays, agricultural-to-industrial conversion projects, large-lot residential subdivisions on the urban fringe, and any [[Ground-Up Development]] project on land that has not yet been incorporated or is not subject to annexation.

## Key Chapters and Articles Relevant to Development

- **County Government Structure (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes the Board of Supervisors as the governing body for all county functions, including land-use decisions. The Board of Supervisors is the ultimate decision-maker for county rezoning and comprehensive plan amendments.

- **County Planning and Zoning Authority (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Grants counties the power to adopt zoning ordinances for unincorporated areas, create use districts, regulate bulk standards, and adopt comprehensive plans. The county planning and zoning commission (or equivalent body) serves an advisory role; the Board of Supervisors makes final decisions.

- **Comprehensive Plans (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Arizona counties are required to adopt a comprehensive plan covering land use, transportation, open space, and housing elements. County zoning must generally be consistent with the comprehensive plan. Rezonings that conflict with the adopted plan require a comprehensive plan amendment, adding a legislative step to the entitlement process.

- **Rezoning and Variance Procedures (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes notice, public hearing, and decision procedures for [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] applications in unincorporated areas. The county planning and zoning commission hears applications and makes recommendations; the Board of Supervisors acts. Standards for [[Variance]] grants parallel the municipal standard: hardship, not self-created, consistent with the spirit of the ordinance.

- **Subdivision Regulations (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Counties regulate subdivision of unincorporated land. Plat approval, dedication requirements, and coordination with ADRE public reports apply in the county context as in municipalities. Large rural subdivisions (often called "land-splits" below a certain parcel count) may have lighter procedural requirements but still require state public report compliance.

- **County Island and Annexation Context:** Title 11 and Title 9 interact at the urban fringe. "County islands" — unincorporated parcels surrounded by a municipality — are common in Arizona and represent both an entitlement opportunity (county regulations may differ from the surrounding city's) and an annexation risk. Developers underwriting county-island parcels should assess annexation likelihood and the applicable post-annexation zoning.

## Practical Implications for CRE

1. **Rural and Exurban Land Plays:** County zoning governs agricultural land, rural commercial sites, mining-adjacent industrial parcels, and large lots on the urban fringe. For a [[Land]] acquisition or [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] of an unincorporated parcel, Title 11 is the operative statutory frame.

2. **Slower Entitlement Timelines:** County planning departments typically have fewer staff than major city departments. Public hearing scheduling, staff report preparation, and decision timelines may be longer than in well-resourced municipal departments. Underwrite entitlement timelines conservatively.

3. **Comprehensive Plan Amendment Risk:** County comprehensive plans are updated infrequently. A rezoning that requires a comprehensive plan amendment is a legislative act requiring Board of Supervisors approval — adding political and timing risk on top of the standard zoning decision.

4. **Water and Infrastructure Context:** Unincorporated county areas frequently lack access to municipal water and sewer service. Development feasibility depends on ADWR-assured water supply compliance (for AMAs) or on private well and septic permitting. Infrastructure cost burden on the developer is typically higher in county-area projects than in served municipal areas.

5. **Large-Lot / Agricultural Zoning:** Counties maintain agricultural and rural residential zones that permit a wide range of uses by right or with a use permit. Understanding the county's use matrix is essential before drawing conclusions about a parcel's development potential from aerial imagery or tax records alone.
