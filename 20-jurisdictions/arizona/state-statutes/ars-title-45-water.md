# A.R.S. Title 45 — Waters

## Scope

A.R.S. Title 45 is Arizona's comprehensive water law, encompassing surface water rights, groundwater rights, and the regulatory framework for managing water in areas of critical groundwater overdraft. For CRE and land development purposes, the most consequential provisions are the **1980 Groundwater Management Act** (codified within Title 45), the designation of **Active Management Areas (AMAs)**, and the **Assured Water Supply (AWS) program** — all administered by the Arizona Department of Water Resources (ADWR). Title 45 establishes the statutory authority that makes water availability a hard prerequisite for subdivision approvals in the state's major metropolitan markets.

## Key Chapters and Articles Relevant to Development

- **Surface Water Rights (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Arizona follows the prior appropriation doctrine for surface water — "first in time, first in right." Surface water rights are administered through a permit system; holders must demonstrate beneficial use. Colorado River allocations under the Law of the River are administered by the federal Bureau of Reclamation in conjunction with state law, and Arizona's Central Arizona Project (CAP) deliveries flow from this framework.

- **Groundwater Code and Active Management Areas (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** The 1980 Groundwater Management Act created the AMA framework in response to unsustainable groundwater overdraft in the Phoenix, Tucson, Prescott, and Pinal basins. AMAs are:
  - **Phoenix AMA** — Maricopa County and portions of adjacent counties; the state's largest.
  - **Tucson AMA** — Pima County.
  - **Prescott AMA** — Yavapai County (Prescott area).
  - **Pinal AMA** — Pinal County; agricultural transition focus.
  - **Santa Cruz AMA** — Santa Cruz County (Nogales area).

  Within AMAs, groundwater pumping is regulated, and users must hold groundwater rights or access delivered water (CAP, reclaimed water, effluent). The goal in most AMAs is "safe yield" — a balance between annual groundwater depletion and recharge by a target year (TBD — verify current safe-yield target year for each AMA).

- **Assured Water Supply (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Within AMAs, Title 45 requires that developers subdividing land demonstrate a **100-year assured water supply** before ADRE can issue a Public Report. The statute defines two mechanisms:
  - **Certificate of Assured Water Supply (CAWS):** Issued to a water provider (municipal utility or private water company). Once a water provider holds a CAWS, it may serve new development within its service area without individual project-level AWS review.
  - **Designation of Assured Water Supply (DAWS):** Issued directly to a developer or landowner who is not served by a CAWS-holding provider. The developer must demonstrate 100 years of water supply from a physical, legal, and financial standpoint.
  See [[Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)]] for full program detail.

- **Groundwater Rights — Irrigation Non-Expansion Areas (INAs):** Certain areas outside AMAs are designated as Irrigation Non-Expansion Areas where new agricultural irrigation wells are restricted. Less directly relevant to urban CRE but material for agricultural-land conversion plays.

- **Groundwater Replenishment Program (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** The Arizona Water Banking Authority and the Central Arizona Groundwater Replenishment District (CAGRD) allow developers and water providers to demonstrate 100-year supply by committing to replenish future groundwater pumping through underground storage. Many subdivisions in the Phoenix and Pinal AMAs satisfy their AWS requirement through CAGRD membership rather than direct water delivery contracts.

- **Water Provider Certification (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes ADWR's authority to certify municipal and private water providers serving AMAs; certification conditions include infrastructure adequacy, financial capability, and source-water reliability.

## Practical Implications for CRE

1. **Water as the Binding Constraint on Greenfield Development:** In Phoenix, Tucson, and Prescott AMAs, a developer cannot obtain a subdivision Public Report without AWS compliance. For large greenfield projects, securing water supply — whether through a CAWS-holding utility or through a project-specific DAWS — is often the longest-lead and most uncertain step in the entitlement process. Underwriting must account for this.

2. **CAGRD as a Cost Item:** Developers whose water providers use CAGRD membership to satisfy 100-year supply obligations must understand that CAGRD membership carries an ongoing assessment obligation on each lot or acre — a development cost and long-term carrying obligation for future owners. This cost is disclosed in the ADRE Public Report.

3. **Colorado River Supply Risk:** CAP water (Arizona's primary supplemental surface water source) is subject to federal shortage declarations triggered by Lake Mead elevations. Arizona, as a Lower Basin junior priority holder, is among the first to face CAP reductions under shortage. This adds uncertainty to water supply portfolios that rely heavily on CAP, affecting ADWR's evaluation of AWS applications.

4. **Pinal AMA — Industrial and Logistics Development:** Pinal County's rapid industrial and data-center growth intersects with the Pinal AMA's agricultural-transition mandate. New non-agricultural uses in the Pinal AMA require water rights or alternative supply; the mechanics differ from the Phoenix AMA. Water diligence is critical on any Pinal County industrial acquisition.

5. **Rural Areas Outside AMAs:** Outside AMAs, Arizona law does not require a 100-year assured water supply for subdivision approvals. However, developers relying on private wells must comply with well permitting under Title 45 and may face practical supply constraints. ADRE still requires water disclosure in Public Reports for non-AMA subdivisions.
