# A.R.S. Title 32 — Professions and Occupations (Real Estate Licensing)

## Scope

A.R.S. Title 32 covers the licensing and regulation of dozens of professions and occupations in Arizona. For CRE purposes, the two most material chapters are the **real estate broker and salesperson licensing chapter** (administered by the Arizona Department of Real Estate, ADRE) and the **subdivision public report chapter**, which requires developers to obtain and deliver a public report before marketing or selling any subdivision of six or more lots, parcels, or interests. Title 32 does not itself authorize zoning or land-use regulation, but it imposes disclosure and licensing obligations that directly affect the CRE transaction and development process.

## Key Chapters and Articles Relevant to Development

- **Real Estate Broker and Salesperson Licensing (Chapter — TBD, verify exact citation):** Requires that any person acting as a real estate broker or salesperson in Arizona hold a license issued by ADRE. The license categories distinguish between salesperson and broker; a broker may operate independently, while a salesperson must work under a licensed broker. CRE brokerage — including the sale, lease, and property management of commercial assets — requires a broker or salesperson license. Unlicensed activity is a criminal offense.

- **Subdivision Public Report (Chapter — TBD, verify exact citation):** This is the most deal-critical provision in Title 32 for developers. Any subdivision of **six or more lots, parcels, or interests** in land — including residential subdivisions, commercial pad-site subdivisions, condominium conversions, and timeshare projects — requires ADRE to issue a Public Report before the developer may market or sell. Key elements:
  - The Public Report discloses infrastructure status (streets, utilities, water, sewer), any conditions or restrictions, and material facts about the subdivision.
  - For subdivisions within Active Management Areas (AMAs), the Public Report cannot be issued until ADRE confirms that the subdivision has a Designation of Assured Water Supply (DAWS) from ADWR or that the water utility providing service holds a Certificate of Assured Water Supply (CAWS). This is the direct statutory link between the water-adequacy regime and subdivision marketing.
  - Delivery of the Public Report to each prospective buyer is a legal requirement before executing a purchase contract. Buyers have a right of rescission during a statutory review period after delivery (TBD — verify current rescission period).
  - Public Reports are required for Arizona subdivisions marketed out of state as well as in-state.

- **Cemetery and Timeshare Regulation (TBD — verify chapter):** ADRE also regulates timeshare projects and membership camping under Title 32. Disclosure and public report requirements apply analogously to time-share intervals.

- **Property Management (TBD — verify chapter):** Property management for compensation requires a broker's license or employment under a licensed broker. This applies to commercial property management arrangements.

## Practical Implications for CRE

1. **Subdivision Public Report as a Development Gate:** A developer of a new subdivision — including a commercial industrial park or pad-site subdivision — cannot begin selling or marketing individual parcels until the ADRE Public Report is issued. This adds a regulatory processing step that should be included in the project schedule. ADRE processing times vary; complex projects or those requiring water-adequacy documentation take longer.

2. **Water Adequacy as a Public Report Prerequisite:** For projects in Phoenix, Tucson, Prescott, and other AMAs, the Public Report pathway is blocked until ADWR confirms 100-year assured water supply. Developers should pursue ADWR designation concurrently with entitlement, not sequentially, to avoid downstream schedule compression.

3. **Broker Licensing for Investment Sales:** CRE investment sales, lease-up brokerage, and tenant representation in Arizona require ADRE licensure. Out-of-state brokers facilitating Arizona transactions must ensure they comply with Arizona's licensing requirements or engage a licensed Arizona broker under a co-brokerage arrangement.

4. **Condominium Conversions:** A multifamily apartment converted to for-sale condominiums is a subdivision under Arizona law and requires a Public Report. The conversion process involves ADRE filing, physical inspection compliance, and HOA formation under A.R.S. Title 33.

5. **Disclosure Liability:** Failure to deliver a Public Report before contract execution exposes the developer to contract rescission and regulatory action by ADRE. This is a hard compliance requirement with no discretionary waiver for CRE developers.
