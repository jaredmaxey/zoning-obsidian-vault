# A.R.S. Title 9 — Cities and Towns (Municipal Zoning)

## Scope

A.R.S. Title 9 governs the organization, powers, and procedures of Arizona's incorporated cities and towns. For commercial real estate and land-use purposes, it is the foundational enabling statute: it grants municipalities the authority to adopt zoning ordinances, require general plans, establish subdivision regulations, levy impact fees, and enter binding development agreements. All Arizona cities and towns derive their land-use regulatory power from this title, whether or not they have adopted a home-rule charter. Charter cities may go beyond the floor Title 9 sets; non-charter municipalities must stay within its express grants.

## Key Chapters and Articles Relevant to Development

- **Municipal Incorporation and Powers (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes the legal existence of Arizona cities and towns, the types of government they may adopt, and the scope of their general police powers, including land-use regulation.

- **General Plans (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Requires municipalities above a minimum population threshold to adopt and periodically update a comprehensive general plan covering land use, circulation, housing, and other elements. A municipality's zoning ordinance must be consistent with its general plan; a [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] is therefore the prerequisite step when a proposed rezoning is inconsistent with the adopted plan.

- **Zoning Authority (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Grants municipalities the power to divide their territory into zones, establish permitted and conditional uses, and regulate bulk standards (height, setbacks, FAR, density, lot coverage). The zoning ordinance must be adopted after public notice and hearing.

- **Rezoning and Variance Procedures (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes the procedural requirements for [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] applications — notice, public hearing, planning commission recommendation, and governing-body decision. Also establishes the authority to create boards of adjustment and the standards for granting a [[Variance]] (hardship, not self-created, consistent with spirit of the code) and a [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]].

- **Development Agreements (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Authorizes municipalities to enter legally binding development agreements with property owners or developers. A development agreement may vest zoning entitlements for up to a specified term, lock in development standards applicable at the time of execution, and define infrastructure obligations. Widely used in large master-planned community and mixed-use projects.

- **Subdivision Regulations (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Grants municipalities the authority to regulate the subdivision of land, including plat approval, infrastructure dedication requirements, and the coordination of lot sales with ADRE's public report process. See [[Subdivision Approval]].

- **Impact Fees (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Authorizes municipalities to levy development impact fees (DIFs) on new development to fund necessary public services infrastructure (roads, water, sewer, parks, public safety). Arizona's impact-fee statute contains detailed requirements for Infrastructure Improvement Plans (IIPs), fee-nexus studies, credit and reimbursement provisions, and fee schedules that must be updated on a defined cycle. Impact fees are a significant development cost item in fast-growing Arizona municipalities.

- **Planned Area Development / Specific Plans (TBD — verify whether authorized under Title 9 or under municipal ordinance authority):** Many Arizona cities authorize planned-area development (PAD) or specific-plan zoning, which applies custom development standards to a defined project area. These typically require a concurrent rezoning to the PAD designation and a separately approved development plan or specific plan document.

## Practical Implications for CRE

1. **Entitlement Path:** Most commercial rezoning, CUP, and variance applications in Arizona cities proceed under procedures derived from Title 9. The sequence is: pre-application meeting → application submission → staff report → planning commission public hearing → city council (or board of adjustment for variances) → appeal period. Timeline varies by municipality but typically runs 60–120 days for a straightforward rezoning; longer for GPAs or contested applications.

2. **Development Agreements as Vesting Tools:** Title 9 development agreements are the primary mechanism to lock in entitlements for multi-phase projects. An [[Entitlement Play]] in Arizona often culminates in a DA that specifies the approved zoning, intensity, and infrastructure obligations in exchange for the developer's commitments. DA terms of 10–25 years are common in master-planned contexts.

3. **General Plan Consistency Requirement:** Because zoning must be consistent with the general plan, an out-of-plan rezoning requires a parallel GPA — a second legislative hearing before the same body. This adds time and political risk. Developers should review the adopted land-use map and housing element before committing to a site that requires a GPA.

4. **Impact Fee Due Diligence:** Arizona municipalities maintain published IIPs and fee schedules. Fees vary widely across cities and service areas; a Phoenix-area industrial project and a Chandler multifamily project may carry substantially different DIF obligations. Impact fees are typically payable at building permit issuance, which affects development proforma timing.

5. **Annexation:** Title 9 also governs annexation procedures. Unincorporated parcels on the urban fringe may be subject to pending or future annexation by an adjacent municipality, which can dramatically change the applicable zoning code and development standards. Confirm annexation status and any pre-annexation development agreements before underwriting.
