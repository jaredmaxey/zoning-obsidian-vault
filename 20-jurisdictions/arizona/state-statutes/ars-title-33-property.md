# A.R.S. Title 33 — Property

## Scope

A.R.S. Title 33 is Arizona's primary property law codification. It governs the creation, transfer, encumbrance, and termination of interests in real property and sets the statutory framework for the major property-ownership structures used in CRE product types — condominiums, planned communities, and commercial ground leases. It also governs the landlord-tenant relationship for both residential and commercial leases. For CRE developers, lenders, and investors, Title 33 defines the legal mechanics of how Arizona real estate is owned, sold, financed, and operated.

- **Conveyances and Deeds (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes the requirements for valid conveyances of real property in Arizona — deed types (warranty, special warranty, quitclaim), execution, acknowledgment, and recording. Arizona is a **notice-race** recording jurisdiction (TBD — verify current characterization). Recorded instruments provide constructive notice; priority disputes between competing claimants are resolved by reference to recording priority and notice.

- **Deeds of Trust (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Arizona is a deed-of-trust state. Mortgage financing is secured by a deed of trust naming a trustee who holds the power of sale on behalf of the lender (beneficiary). Non-judicial trustee's sale (foreclosure) is the standard remedy upon default; the statutory process involves a notice of trustee's sale, a public notice period (TBD — verify current minimum notice period), and a public auction. The relatively efficient non-judicial process is favorable to lenders and affects loan pricing.

- **Liens (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Covers mechanic's and materialman's liens. Arizona has a preliminary 20-day notice requirement (TBD — verify current requirement and timing) that contractors, subcontractors, and suppliers must serve to preserve lien rights on a construction project. Failure to serve the preliminary notice extinguishes lien rights for that claimant. This is critical for GC, sub-contractor, and lender diligence on construction loans and [[Ground-Up Development]] projects.

- **Condominiums (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Establishes the statutory framework for condominium ownership — declaration requirements, unit boundary definitions, common element governance, HOA formation and powers, and assessment obligations. Condominium regimes are used in multifamily for-sale, office condominiums, retail condo plats, and industrial condo developments. The declaration and CC&Rs must be recorded before sales can close on individual units (interacts with ADRE Public Report requirements under Title 32).

- **Planned Communities (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Governs planned residential and mixed-use communities with mandatory HOA membership and covenants, conditions, and restrictions (CC&Rs). Key provisions cover HOA governance, assessment authority, enforcement rights, and developer control transition. Large Arizona master-planned communities (MPCs) — including many in the Phoenix West Valley and East Valley — are structured under this chapter.

- **Landlord-Tenant (Residential) (Article — TBD, verify exact citation):** Arizona Residential Landlord and Tenant Act governs residential leases. While less directly relevant to CRE, it affects multifamily operators and mixed-use projects with residential units.

- **Commercial Landlord-Tenant:** Arizona commercial lease law is primarily governed by contract and common law; there is no comprehensive commercial landlord-tenant act analogous to the residential act. Commercial lease terms are largely freely negotiable. CRE practitioners rely on NNN, gross, and modified-gross lease structures as contractually defined.

- **Ground Leases and Long-Term Leases:** Title 33 recording requirements apply to long-term ground leases (generally those exceeding a specified term; TBD — verify current threshold). A memorandum of ground lease is typically recorded to provide constructive notice of the tenant's interest without disclosing confidential rent terms.

## Practical Implications for CRE

1. **Deed of Trust and Foreclosure Speed:** Arizona's non-judicial trustee's sale process is one of the faster foreclosure mechanisms in the country, which supports lender confidence and affects loan underwriting (lower foreclosure-risk premium). Developers should understand that a deed of trust default can move to trustee's sale on a relatively compressed timeline.

2. **20-Day Preliminary Notice (Construction Liens):** On any Arizona construction project, all subcontractors and suppliers must serve a preliminary 20-day notice (TBD — verify current statute) to preserve lien rights. GC contracts and construction loan documents routinely require compliance tracking. Failure creates uninsured lien exposure.

3. **Condominium and HOA Formation:** For-sale product types — residential condos, office condos, industrial condos — require Title 33-compliant declarations, properly adopted CC&Rs, and HOA formation before unit closings. ADRE Public Report issuance under Title 32 depends on Title 33 documentation being in order.

4. **CC&R Enforcement in Planned Communities:** Master-planned community CC&Rs run with the land and bind all future owners. Developers acquiring parcels within an MPC must review CC&Rs for use restrictions, architectural standards, and assessment obligations that supplement (and may be more restrictive than) municipal zoning.

5. **No Transfer Tax Environment:** Title 33's conveyance provisions operate in a no-transfer-tax environment — there is no documentary stamp tax or real property transfer tax in Arizona — which simplifies closing cost structure relative to many states.
