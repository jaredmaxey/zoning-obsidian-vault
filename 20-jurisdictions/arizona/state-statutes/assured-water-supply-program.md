# Assured Water Supply Program (Arizona)

## Scope

The **Assured Water Supply (AWS) program** is the cornerstone regulatory requirement distinguishing Arizona's development approval process from most other Sun Belt states. Administered by the **Arizona Department of Water Resources (ADWR)** under authority granted by [[A.R.S. Title 45 — Waters]] (the Groundwater Management Act), the program requires that any subdivision within an **Active Management Area (AMA)** demonstrate a physically, legally, and continuously available, and of sufficient quality 100-year water supply before the Arizona Department of Real Estate (ADRE) can issue a Public Report authorizing marketing and sales.

The AWS requirement directly gates [[Subdivision Approval]] and is the single most important water-related diligence item for any developer evaluating greenfield or land development in the Phoenix, Tucson, Prescott, Pinal, or Santa Cruz AMAs.

## The Two AWS Pathways

### 1. Certificate of Assured Water Supply (CAWS)

A CAWS is issued by ADWR **to a water provider** (a municipal utility, a private water company, or a water district). It certifies that the provider's water supply portfolio is sufficient to serve all existing customers plus all committed future development within the provider's service area for the next 100 years.

**Developer implication:** If a development site is within the service area of a CAWS-holding provider, the developer does not need to independently demonstrate 100-year supply at the project level. The provider's CAWS covers the project. This is the most common and straightforward AWS pathway in established Phoenix and Tucson metro service areas. The developer confirms: (1) the site is in the provider's certificated service area, (2) the provider's CAWS is current and not under jeopardy, and (3) the provider can physically extend service (mains, treatment capacity) to the site.

### 2. Designation of Assured Water Supply (DAWS)

A DAWS is issued by ADWR **directly to a developer or landowner** when the site is not within the service area of a CAWS-holding provider. The developer must independently demonstrate 100-year supply satisfying all four statutory criteria:

| Criterion | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Physically available** | Sufficient water volume will actually be available for 100 years — groundwater, surface water, reclaimed water, or a portfolio combination. |
| **Legally available** | The developer holds or will hold valid water rights or contracts sufficient to access the water. |
| **Continuously available** | The supply is not subject to interruption or shortage that would jeopardize availability across the 100-year horizon. |
| **Adequate quality** | The water meets applicable quality standards for the proposed use. |

**CAGRD membership as a supply tool:** Developers who lack sufficient direct water rights or utility contracts commonly satisfy the physically and legally available criteria by enrolling future lots or parcels in the **Central Arizona Groundwater Replenishment District (CAGRD)**. CAGRD membership commits the developer (and future parcel owners) to pay CAGRD to recharge an equivalent volume of water underground for any groundwater pumped in the future. CAGRD membership is disclosed in the ADRE Public Report and creates an ongoing assessment obligation on enrolled parcels — a cost that must be disclosed and underwritten.

## Active Management Areas (AMAs) Subject to the AWS Requirement

The AWS requirement applies within Arizona's five designated AMAs (`needs-verification` for current AMA boundaries and any legislative changes):

| AMA | Primary Geography | Key Development Markets |
|-----|-------------------|------------------------|
| Phoenix AMA | Maricopa County and portions of adjacent counties | Phoenix, Scottsdale, Tempe, Mesa, Chandler, Gilbert, Glendale, Peoria, Surprise, Buckeye, Queen Creek |
| Tucson AMA | Pima County | Tucson, Sahuarita, Marana, Oro Valley |
| Prescott AMA | Yavapai County (Prescott basin) | Prescott, Prescott Valley, Chino Valley |
| Pinal AMA | Pinal County | Casa Grande, Maricopa, Coolidge, Apache Junction, Queen Creek (portions) |
| Santa Cruz AMA | Santa Cruz County | Nogales area |

## Process Overview

The following is a general process flow; exact procedural requirements and timelines should be verified with ADWR (`needs-verification`):

1. **Determine AMA status:** Confirm whether the project parcel is within an AMA using ADWR's mapping tools.
2. **Identify water provider:** Determine if a CAWS-holding provider serves the site. If yes, obtain a letter confirming service area inclusion and CAWS coverage.
3. **If no CAWS provider:** Initiate DAWS application with ADWR. Assemble documentation of water rights, contracts, CAGRD membership commitments, and quality documentation.
4. **ADWR review:** ADWR reviews the application and may request additional documentation. Processing time varies; complex applications can take months.
5. **ADWR determination:** ADWR issues or denies the DAWS. A denial means the project cannot proceed to ADRE Public Report without addressing the deficiency.
6. **ADRE Public Report:** With AWS confirmed (CAWS coverage or DAWS in hand), ADRE can include water supply status in the Public Report and issue it, enabling marketing.

## Why This Matters for CRE

The AWS program is not merely a bureaucratic step — it is a **fundamental feasibility gate** for Arizona development:

- **Timeline:** On large greenfield projects with no existing water provider coverage, DAWS processing can extend the pre-development timeline by 6–24 months or more, depending on water portfolio assembly, CAGRD enrollment timing, and ADWR review backlog.
- **Cost:** CAGRD assessment obligations, water rights acquisition, and infrastructure extension costs for water service can be substantial. These must be modeled in the development proforma for any project outside an established service area.
- **Colorado River risk:** Increased Colorado River shortage declarations have raised scrutiny of CAP-dependent water supply portfolios. ADWR and CAGRD are managing portfolio diversification toward more drought-resilient sources. Developers relying on CAP-heavy supply should stress-test long-term availability assumptions.
- **Competitive dynamic:** Parcels within CAWS-holding utility service areas command a premium precisely because they eliminate the DAWS risk and timeline. This AWS "coverage premium" is a real and quantifiable component of land valuation in the Phoenix and Tucson markets.
- **[[Highest and Best Use Analysis]]:** For any Arizona land parcel analysis, AWS status (CAWS covered vs. DAWS required vs. outside AMA) is a Tier 1 diligence item that affects feasibility, timeline, and value.
