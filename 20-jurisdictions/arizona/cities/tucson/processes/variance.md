# Tucson Variance

## Summary

A variance provides relief from a specific dimensional or development standard of the Tucson Unified Development Code where strict application of the code would impose an undue hardship due to unique physical characteristics of the parcel — not simply because strict compliance would be inconvenient or costly. Variances are quasi-judicial and site-specific; they do not change the zone or the underlying permitted uses. Common variance requests involve setbacks, height limits, lot coverage, or parking requirements when an irregular lot configuration, existing structures, or topography make strict compliance impractical. See [[Variance]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

Variances are decided by the **Zoning Examiner** in Tucson. The standard is intentionally high — a variance may not be used to effectively rezone property or to accommodate a use not otherwise permitted in the zone.

## Triggering Conditions

A variance is appropriate when: (1) a specific UDC dimensional standard would be violated by the proposed development; (2) the hardship arises from unique physical site characteristics rather than the applicant's actions or economic preference; and (3) the deviation is the minimum necessary to relieve the hardship. Variances from use regulations (i.e., to permit a use not allowed in the zone) are generally not permissible — a rezoning or Special Exception is the correct vehicle in those cases.

## Decision-Maker

The **Zoning Examiner** holds a public hearing and issues a written decision. PDS staff prepares an analysis and recommendation. The process is quasi-judicial, and the Zoning Examiner's decision must be grounded in the evidentiary record. ## Findings Required

Arizona variance law (ARS Title 9) and the UDC typically require the applicant to demonstrate: (1) there are special circumstances or conditions unique to the property that do not apply generally to other properties in the same zone; (2) strict application of the UDC would deprive the property of privileges enjoyed by other properties in the same classification; (3) the hardship is not self-created; (4) the variance is the minimum necessary; and (5) granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to the public or injurious to adjacent property. ## Appeal Path

Zoning Examiner decisions on variances may be appealed to the **Board of Adjustment** within a time-certain period. Board decisions may be further challenged in Pima County Superior Court. Deadlines are TBD — verify against the UDC and ARS (`needs-verification`). See also [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to articulate a genuine physical hardship unique to the parcel; economic hardship alone does not support a variance under Arizona law.
- Requesting more relief than the minimum necessary — the Zoning Examiner may deny or reduce scope, and overreach can undermine credibility.
- Not presenting a clear before/after site plan that makes the hardship visually obvious in the record.
- Conflating variance (dimensional relief) with Special Exception (use authorization) and submitting the wrong application type.
