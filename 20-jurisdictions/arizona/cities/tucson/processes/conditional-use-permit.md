# Tucson Special Exception / CUP

## Summary

In Tucson's regulatory framework, uses that are conditionally permitted in a zone district (as opposed to permitted by-right) require a **Special Exception** — the local equivalent of what most jurisdictions call a Conditional Use Permit (CUP). A Special Exception is a quasi-judicial determination that a specific use, as proposed on a specific site, meets the standards and findings established in the UDC. Approval is tied to the site and conditions of approval, and typically runs with the land. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

Tucson's Special Exception process is administered by the **Zoning Examiner**, a hearing officer position, rather than the Planning Commission — a distinguishing feature compared to many Arizona cities. The Zoning Examiner's decision is a final administrative action subject to appeal. ## Triggering Conditions

A Special Exception is required whenever a use is listed as "conditionally permitted" (or equivalent designation) in the applicable zone district's use table. Common examples include drive-through facilities, automobile services, religious uses in commercial zones, schools, childcare centers, certain entertainment uses, and large-scale commercial developments in mixed zones. The UDC use table for each zone identifies which uses require a Special Exception.

## Decision-Maker

The **Zoning Examiner** (hearing officer) conducts a public hearing and issues a written decision approving, approving with conditions, or denying the Special Exception. This is a quasi-judicial process — the decision must be supported by findings in the record. PDS staff prepares a report and recommendation. ## Required Submittals

Typical requirements include: completed application and authorization; site plan showing the proposed use and development layout; use-specific narrative addressing each required finding; neighborhood outreach documentation; parking and circulation analysis; and application fee (TBD — verify against current PDS fee schedule, `needs-verification`). ## Typical Timeline

From application acceptance to Zoning Examiner decision, a Special Exception typically takes two to four months, encompassing staff review, public notice (mailed and posted), and the Zoning Examiner hearing. ## Findings Required

The Zoning Examiner must make affirmative findings that the proposed use meets the use-specific criteria in the UDC, is compatible with surrounding uses and the neighborhood, will not create excessive traffic or infrastructure impacts, and is consistent with Plan Tucson. ## Appeal Path

A Zoning Examiner decision may be appealed to the **Board of Adjustment** within a specified period. Board of Adjustment decisions may then be challenged in Pima County Superior Court by special action. Deadlines and procedures are TBD — verify against the UDC and ARS (`needs-verification`). ## Common Pitfalls

- Assuming a Special Exception is routine — the Zoning Examiner applies genuine scrutiny and can impose limiting conditions or deny outright based on the record.
- Inadequate findings narrative: vague or boilerplate responses to each required finding are the most common reason for continuances or denial.
- Neighborhood opposition that surfaces for the first time at the hearing; proactive outreach well before the hearing is essential.
- Proposed conditions in the approval that the applicant fails to track and satisfy before certificate of occupancy.
