# Tucson Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting in Tucson divides land into two or more lots, tracts, or parcels and records the division in the official records of Pima County. The process is governed by the Tucson UDC and by Arizona subdivision law (ARS Title 32 and Title 9). Tucson's platting process follows the standard two-phase model: preliminary plat (conceptual layout with infrastructure design) followed by final plat (recordable document with legal descriptions and dedication of public improvements). See [[Subdivision Approval]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

Subdivision platting is required for any division of land creating new lots intended for separate conveyance or development. Minor lot splits (below the threshold triggering full subdivision) may qualify for an administrative lot-split process; thresholds are TBD — verify against the UDC (`needs-verification`).

## Triggering Conditions

A subdivision plat is required when land is divided into two or more lots, blocks, or parcels for sale, lease, or development, above the minor lot-split threshold. Commercial land divisions, residential subdivisions, condominium plats, and phased master-planned developments all require the subdivision platting process. Lot combinations (assembling parcels into one lot) also flow through PDS but may follow a simplified track.

## Decision-Maker

The **Planning Commission** holds a public hearing on the preliminary plat and issues a recommendation. The **Mayor and Council** approve the preliminary plat. The final plat is reviewed administratively by PDS for conformance with the approved preliminary plat and city standards, then approved by Council and recorded with the Pima County Recorder. ## Required Submittals

Preliminary plat requirements typically include: scaled plat drawing with lot dimensions, street dedications, easements, and drainage improvements; title report; adjacent-land ownership map; DRC application; neighborhood notification; and application fee (TBD — verify, `needs-verification`). Final plat requires: recordable mylar or digital plat conforming to Pima County Recorder standards; improvement plans and completion guarantee (bond or letter of credit) for required public improvements; assurance agreements; and applicable fees.

## Typical Timeline

Preliminary plat review and Planning Commission hearing typically takes three to five months from application acceptance. Final plat review and Council recording take an additional two to four months after preliminary plat approval. Phased projects may have multiple final plats recorded over time. Preliminary plat approval typically requires findings that: the subdivision is consistent with Plan Tucson; lot dimensions and design meet UDC standards; adequate public infrastructure (roads, drainage, utilities) will be provided; and the design is compatible with the character of surrounding development. ## Common Pitfalls

- Beginning the platting process before resolving entitlement (rezoning, GPA) — the zone and plan designation must be in place before a plat can be approved.
- Infrastructure improvement assurance amounts that are miscalculated, delaying final plat recording.
- Not coordinating early with Pima County Regional Flood Control District on drainage master plans, which can require significant design revisions.
- Failing to account for Tucson's riparian habitat and environmental resource corridors, which can affect lot layout and developable area.
