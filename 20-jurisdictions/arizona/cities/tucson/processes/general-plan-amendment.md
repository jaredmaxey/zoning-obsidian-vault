# Tucson General Plan Amendment

## Summary

A General Plan Amendment (GPA) modifies Plan Tucson — the city's state-mandated general plan — by changing the land-use designation on the plan map or altering a policy element such as housing, transportation, or environmental policy. Because Plan Tucson functions as the policy foundation for all zoning and entitlement decisions, a GPA is a legislative act requiring Mayor and Council approval following a public hearing process. A concurrent GPA and rezoning is common when a development proposal requires both a policy-level land-use change and a regulatory zoning change. See also [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

GPAs are processed by the Planning & Development Services Planning Division. Arizona state law limits the frequency of privately initiated plan amendments; the specific cycle schedule for Tucson is TBD — verify against the current UDC and Plan Tucson amendment procedures (`needs-verification`).

## Triggering Conditions

A GPA is required when a proposed development or rezoning is inconsistent with the current Plan Tucson land-use designation and the project cannot be approved through a rezoning alone. Common triggers include proposals to convert low-density residential land-use designations to commercial or mixed-use, proposals that increase density or intensity beyond what the plan map permits, and comprehensive policy amendments initiated by the city. Minor rezonings that are consistent with existing plan designations do not require a GPA.

## Decision-Maker

The **Mayor and Council** hold final legislative authority to adopt or deny a GPA. The **Planning Commission** conducts a public hearing and issues a recommendation to Council. Staff prepares an analysis and recommendation. Because this is a legislative action, the Council may approve, deny, or modify the proposal. ## Required Submittals

Typical application requirements include: completed application form and authorization; plan amendment justification narrative addressing consistency with Plan Tucson goals and policies; existing and proposed land-use maps; community/neighborhood outreach documentation; environmental screening or study if applicable; and application fee (TBD — verify against current PDS fee schedule, `needs-verification`). Concurrent rezoning applications are usually submitted together.

## Typical Timeline

GPAs follow a scheduled hearing cycle. From application acceptance to final Council action, the process typically spans four to eight months, encompassing staff review, DRC coordination, Planning Commission hearing, and Council hearing. ## Findings Required

The decision-maker must find that the proposed amendment is consistent with Plan Tucson's goals and policies, compatible with surrounding land uses, and in the public interest. ## Appeal Path

As a legislative action, a Mayor and Council GPA decision is not subject to appeal through the Board of Adjustment. Challenges must be brought in Pima County Superior Court by means of a special action. The deadline for filing is TBD — verify against applicable court rules and ARS (`needs-verification`).

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to demonstrate meaningful consistency with Plan Tucson policies — a bare narrative that doesn't engage the plan's goals will struggle at the Planning Commission.
- Insufficient community/neighborhood outreach before the hearing; Tucson neighborhood associations are active and can influence Commission and Council votes.
- Underestimating the cycle schedule; missing a cycle cutoff can add months to the project timeline.
- Submitting a concurrent GPA and rezoning without aligning both applications' justification narratives.
