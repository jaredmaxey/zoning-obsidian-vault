# Tucson Rezoning

## Summary

A rezoning (zone change) is the legislative process by which a parcel or area of land is reclassified from one base zone district to another under the Tucson Unified Development Code. Rezonings are required when a proposed use or development intensity exceeds what the current zone permits and cannot be accommodated through a special exception, variance, or design adjustment. Rezonings are major entitlement actions — they permanently change the regulatory character of the land and run with the parcel, not the applicant. See [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

A rezoning must be consistent with Plan Tucson's land-use designation. If the existing plan designation does not support the proposed zone, a concurrent General Plan Amendment is required. Voluntary proffers and development agreements are sometimes used in Tucson rezonings to condition approval on specific site design commitments.

## Triggering Conditions

A rezoning is triggered when the proposed use, density, height, or intensity is not permitted in the current zone district and cannot be addressed through a special exception. Examples include rezoning a residentially designated parcel to a commercial zone for retail development, rezoning to a higher-density residential category for multifamily, or rezoning for mixed-use redevelopment. Staff may also initiate city-directed rezonings as part of area-wide planning efforts.

## Decision-Maker

The **Mayor and Council** make the final legislative decision. The **Planning Commission** conducts the public hearing and issues a recommendation. PDS staff prepares an analysis addressing consistency with Plan Tucson, compatibility, and UDC standards. The Council may approve, deny, continue, or modify the rezoning. ## Typical Timeline

From application acceptance to Mayor and Council action, a rezoning typically takes four to seven months, including staff review, DRC coordination, mailed notice, Planning Commission hearing, and Council hearing. Contested applications or those requiring environmental review may run longer. ## Findings Required

The decision-maker must find that the proposed zone change is consistent with Plan Tucson, compatible with surrounding land uses and character, serves the public interest, and meets any applicable UDC criteria for the proposed zone. ## Appeal Path

As a legislative action, a Council rezoning decision is not appealable to the Board of Adjustment. Challenges are brought in Pima County Superior Court by special action. Filing deadline is TBD — verify against ARS and applicable court rules (`needs-verification`).

## Common Pitfalls

- Submitting without a pre-application conference when PDS flags the parcel as complex — PDS will often identify Plan Tucson inconsistencies that require a concurrent GPA.
- Inadequate neighborhood outreach; Tucson neighborhood associations have formal notification rights and are influential at Planning Commission.
- Concept plans that don't clearly show how the development fits the proposed zone's development standards, leaving the Commission without a clear picture of the project.
- Failing to address infrastructure capacity (water, sewer, traffic) proactively, which can trigger conditions that delay final approval.
