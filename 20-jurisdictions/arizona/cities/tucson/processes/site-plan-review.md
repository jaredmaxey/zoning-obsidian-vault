# Tucson Development Package Review

## Summary

In Tucson, the administrative land-development review process for commercial, multifamily, and mixed-use projects is called the **Development Package (DP) Review**. A Development Package is a coordinated set of plans and documents — site plans, landscape plans, grading/drainage plans, and supporting calculations — that demonstrate compliance with all applicable UDC development standards before building permits are issued. The DP review is an administrative process managed by PDS's one-stop Development Review Center, which coordinates review comments from multiple city departments (Planning, Transportation, Engineering, Fire, Water). See [[Site Plan Review]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept note.

Development Package review is distinct from entitlement (rezoning, Special Exception) — it occurs after entitlement is secured and before the building permit is issued. For projects that are by-right under their zone, the DP review is the primary regulatory milestone.

## Triggering Conditions

A Development Package is required for new commercial construction, substantial additions or changes of use, multifamily development above a threshold (TBD — verify against UDC thresholds, `needs-verification`), and projects requiring grading permits in regulated areas. Minor interior alterations and small-scale improvements may qualify for simplified review tracks. Projects within overlay districts (Historic Preservation Zone, Rio Nuevo) may require additional design review concurrent with the DP.

## Decision-Maker

PDS staff conducts a multi-departmental administrative review. There is no public hearing for standard DP review. The review is coordinated through the Development Review Center, which consolidates comments from Planning, Transportation, Engineering, Fire, Water/Wastewater, and other departments. Approval is an administrative determination. (`needs-verification` — confirm current process structure and participating departments.)

## Required Submittals

A standard Development Package typically includes: site plan (to scale, with dimensions, setbacks, parking layout, access); landscape plan meeting UDC landscape standards; grading and drainage plan/report; water/sewer will-serve letters; utility plan; lighting photometric plan; wall and fence plan; architectural elevations (for applicable zones or overlays); and an application form with fee (TBD — verify against current PDS fee schedule, `needs-verification`). Projects in the Historic Preservation Zone require Historic Preservation review concurrently.

## Typical Timeline

PDS publishes target review cycle times, which vary based on application completeness and project complexity; specific turnaround targets are TBD — verify against current PDS service standards (`needs-verification`). After initial submission, review comments are typically provided within several weeks. Applicants respond with revised plans; the process iterates until all departments clear the package. Total elapsed time from submission to approval for a straightforward commercial project is commonly one to three months, depending on completeness and resubmittal speed.

## Findings Required

DP approval is administrative — the plans must demonstrate compliance with all applicable UDC development standards (parking, landscape, signage, lighting, walls/fences, setbacks, height, access, drainage). No discretionary findings are required; if the plans comply, the DP is approved. If a plan requests a deviation from a standard, the appropriate relief (variance, design adjustment) must be obtained separately.

## Appeal Path

An applicant may appeal an adverse staff determination on a DP to the Zoning Examiner or through the standard administrative appeal process. Further appeal is to the Board of Adjustment and then Superior Court. Specific appeal deadlines and procedures are TBD — verify against the current UDC (`needs-verification`).

## Common Pitfalls

- Submitting an incomplete Development Package; incomplete submittals reset the review clock and delay projects significantly.
- Failing to account for overlay district requirements (Historic Preservation Zone, Rio Nuevo) that add design review layers on top of the standard DP process.
- Underestimating the landscape, lighting, and parking plan detail required — PDS reviewers check calculations, not just plan layouts.
- Not coordinating with the Water Department early on utility connections, which can create schedule delays late in the review cycle.
