# Phoenix Hillside Overlay

## Summary

The Phoenix Hillside Overlay applies to properties located on or adjacent to steep terrain — mountain preserves, desert ridgelines, and hillside areas — where standard development practices would cause excessive grading, erosion, habitat disturbance, or degradation of the natural visual character of Phoenix's iconic desert mountains. The overlay modifies and supplements the base zone by imposing graduated development standards that become more restrictive as land slope increases, ultimately prohibiting construction on the steepest and most environmentally sensitive terrain.

The overlay reflects Phoenix's commitment to its Mountain Park Preserves and Desert Preserve, ensuring that hillside development blends into the natural landscape rather than dominating ridgelines or dramatically reshaping the terrain. ## Modifications to Base Zoning

The overlay imposes tiered restrictions on top of the base zone's dimensional and use standards. Restrictions increase as slope steepens:

- **Grading limits:** The total area of a lot that may be graded, cut, or filled is restricted and generally decreases as slope increases. Mass grading that reshapes the natural contours of a hillside is prohibited or sharply limited. TBD — verify specific maximum disturbed-area percentages and cut/fill depth limits by slope category.
- **Height restrictions:** Building height may be measured from natural grade rather than finished grade to prevent structures from appearing taller against the hillside backdrop. TBD — verify height measurement methodology and any absolute height caps within overlay.
- **Setbacks and building envelopes:** Larger setbacks or restricted building envelopes may apply on steep lots to protect ridge edges and drainage courses. TBD — verify specific setback modifications by slope tier.
- **Density and lot coverage:** Maximum lot coverage and impervious surface limits are typically more restrictive than the base zone. TBD — verify specific coverage standards.
- **Vegetation preservation:** Native desert vegetation removal is restricted; disturbed areas must be revegetated with native or drought-tolerant species. TBD — verify specific vegetation preservation and revegetation standards.
- **Ridgeline protection:** Structures must generally be sited below the ridgeline and may not project above the natural skyline when viewed from defined vantage points. TBD — verify specific ridgeline protection standards.

## Additional Requirements

- **Slope analysis:** Applications typically require a slope analysis map prepared by a licensed surveyor or engineer classifying the parcel into slope bands.
- **Grading plan review:** A detailed grading plan demonstrating compliance with the overlay's disturbance limits must be submitted and reviewed as part of the building permit or site plan process.
- **Retaining wall standards:** Retaining walls must generally be designed to minimize visual impact; height, terracing, and materials are subject to overlay standards. TBD — verify specific retaining wall standards.
- **Drainage:** Hillside development requires engineered drainage solutions to prevent erosion and protect downslope properties. TBD — verify drainage study requirements and standards.
- **Color and materials:** Building materials and exterior colors are typically required to blend with the natural desert landscape (earth tones, non-reflective surfaces). TBD — confirm design compatibility requirements.
- **Wildfire interface:** Hillside areas may also fall within State wildfire interface zones triggering additional building code requirements. TBD — confirm intersection with wildfire provisions.

## Process Changes

Most hillside development requires additional review beyond a standard building permit:

1. Pre-application meeting with Phoenix Planning and Development Department staff is strongly recommended to review slope categories and applicable standards.
2. Slope analysis and site plan submission demonstrating compliance with hillside overlay standards.
3. Administrative review by city staff, or Planning Commission/hearing officer review for larger projects or deviations. TBD — confirm review level thresholds.
4. Grading permit review and approval in addition to building permit.

Variances from hillside overlay standards are typically subject to heightened scrutiny and findings requirements. See [[Phoenix Variance]] for the variance process. See also [[Public Hearing Process]].
