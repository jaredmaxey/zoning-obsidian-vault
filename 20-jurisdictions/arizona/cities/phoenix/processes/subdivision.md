# Phoenix Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting is the process of dividing, replatting, or reconfiguring land into two or more parcels, lots, or tracts for the purpose of sale, transfer, development, or financing. In Phoenix, the subdivision process is governed by both Arizona state law (the Arizona Subdivision Statutes, A.R.S. Title 32 and related chapters) and the Phoenix Subdivision Ordinance (a chapter of the Phoenix City Code or Zoning Ordinance). The process establishes the legal boundaries of lots, dedicates public streets and rights-of-way, and ensures that infrastructure — utilities, drainage, street improvements — is provided or bonded to serve the new lots before the subdivision is recorded.

Subdivision approval is a prerequisite to selling or conveying individual lots in most circumstances under Arizona law. For commercial and mixed-use development, subdivision platting becomes relevant when a developer wants to sell individual pads, condominiumize a project, create a master-planned development with multiple recorded lots, or reconfigure lot lines between existing parcels. See also [[Subdivision Approval]].

## Triggering Conditions

Subdivision platting is required when:
- Land is divided into two or more lots, parcels, or tracts for purpose of sale, transfer, or development.
- Existing lot lines are reconfigured, merged, or replatted.
- A condominium plat is required for a condominium regime (commercial or residential).
- A developer creates individual pads within a commercial center for separate sale.
- A master-planned community creates individual subdivided lots.

- **Pre-application meeting:** Recommended to discuss infrastructure requirements and any special conditions.

## Findings Required

For preliminary plat approval, the decision-maker typically finds that:
- The subdivision design is consistent with the Phoenix General Plan and applicable zoning.
- The proposed lots meet minimum lot size, width, and configuration standards for the applicable zone.
- Adequate infrastructure (streets, utilities, drainage) is available or will be provided to serve the subdivision.
- The subdivision will not be detrimental to the public health, safety, or welfare.
- The plat complies with the Phoenix Subdivision Ordinance and applicable state law.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Infrastructure gaps identified late:** Discovering late that utilities (water, sewer, dry utilities) cannot serve the subdivision, or that street improvements are required beyond the project's budget, can derail or delay projects significantly. Early utility and traffic analysis is essential.
- **Right-of-way dedication surprises:** Phoenix may require dedication of additional right-of-way along frontage streets as a condition of subdivision. This can affect building envelope and project economics.
- **Improvement assurance underestimated:** The cost of required public improvements (streets, drainage, utilities) can be substantial. Assurance (bonding) of these improvements ties up capital.
- **Conditions not tracked:** Preliminary plat conditions must be fully satisfied before final plat can be approved. Losing track of outstanding conditions delays recording.
- **Phasing complexity:** Multi-phase subdivisions with complex phasing plans for infrastructure require careful coordination with city engineering and utility departments.
- **CC&R and HOA requirements:** Subdivisions with common areas, shared infrastructure, or design standards may need recorded CC&Rs and a homeowners/property owners association. TBD — confirm Phoenix requirements.
