# Phoenix General Plan Amendment

## Summary

A General Plan Amendment (GPA) is a legislative process to change the City of Phoenix's General Plan — the long-range policy document guiding land use, transportation, housing, and community development. The Phoenix General Plan establishes land use designations (such as residential, commercial, industrial, mixed-use, and open space) across the city on a map, and includes policy elements addressing growth, environmental sustainability, economic development, and infrastructure. A GPA is required when a property owner or applicant seeks a land use designation that differs from the existing General Plan map designation in a way that would be inconsistent with approving the requested rezoning or development.

GPAs are among the most significant and consequential land use actions because they reset the policy framework for an area, not just one parcel. City Council approval is required, making a GPA a legislative act subject to the political judgment of elected officials. Phoenix processes GPAs on a periodic basis — the city may have scheduled amendment cycles (e.g., annual windows) rather than allowing continuous GPA applications. ## Triggering Conditions

A GPA is required when:
- The proposed use or intensity of development is inconsistent with the current General Plan land use designation for the property.
- A rezoning is requested to a zone category that does not conform to the General Plan designation (since rezonings must generally be consistent with the General Plan in Arizona).
- The applicant seeks to change General Plan policy text (area plans, element policies) in a way that would affect permissible development.

A GPA is often processed concurrently with a rezoning application when a project requires both a policy change and a zoning change. TBD — confirm whether Phoenix requires concurrent or sequential processing.

## Decision-Maker

- **Planning Commission:** Holds a public hearing and provides a recommendation (approve, approve with conditions, or deny) to City Council. The Planning Commission's role is advisory on GPAs. TBD — confirm Planning Commission's exact advisory vs. quasi-judicial role on GPAs.
- **City Council:** Makes the final legislative decision. City Council may follow or override the Planning Commission recommendation but must hold its own public hearing. ## Findings Required

For a GPA approval, Phoenix City Council must generally find that the amendment:
- Is consistent with the overall goals, policies, and vision of the Phoenix General Plan.
- Is compatible with surrounding land use patterns and planned infrastructure.
- Serves the public interest — the amendment advances identified community goals (housing production, economic development, environmental protection, etc.).
- Is not materially detrimental to the public health, safety, and welfare.

## Appeal Path

A GPA approved or denied by City Council is a legislative act. Legislative land use decisions in Arizona are generally not subject to administrative appeal in the same way as quasi-judicial decisions. A party aggrieved by a City Council GPA decision may seek judicial review, typically by filing a special action (certiorari) in Superior Court challenging the decision as arbitrary, capricious, or beyond the city's authority. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Inconsistency with broader General Plan goals:** Applications that advance one goal (e.g., adding commercial) while conflicting with another (e.g., protecting established residential neighborhoods) face difficulty. A strong consistency analysis is essential.
- **Cycle timing:** Missing the GPA application cycle deadline can delay a project by a year or more. Confirm cycle timing early in project planning.
- **Concurrent rezoning complexity:** When a GPA and rezoning are processed together, opposition to either can derail both. Consider sequencing carefully.
- **Neighborhood opposition:** GPAs affecting residential areas are politically sensitive. Early and genuine community engagement through [[Neighborhood Outreach]] is critical.
- **Traffic and infrastructure concerns:** Failure to address infrastructure capacity (water, sewer, roads) in the justification narrative is a common deficiency that staff will flag.
- **Findings gaps:** Applicants who do not systematically address each required finding in their application narrative leave the decision-maker without a basis to approve.
