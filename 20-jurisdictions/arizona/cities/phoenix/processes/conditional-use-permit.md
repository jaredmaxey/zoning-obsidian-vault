# Phoenix Use Permit / CUP

## Summary

A Use Permit (sometimes called a Conditional Use Permit or CUP) is a quasi-judicial approval allowing a use that is not permitted by-right in a zone but is listed as conditionally permitted — meaning it may be appropriate in that zone under the right circumstances and with appropriate conditions. The Phoenix Zoning Ordinance lists certain uses as requiring a use permit in specific zones; those uses cannot proceed without an approved use permit, regardless of whether a building permit would otherwise be required.

Use permits are quasi-judicial, meaning the decision-maker applies specific adopted findings criteria to the facts of the application rather than exercising broad legislative discretion. Conditions of approval may be imposed to mitigate impacts and ensure compatibility with surrounding uses. Approved use permits run with the land (they are tied to the property, not the owner) unless the approval is personal or otherwise limited. See also [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]].

## Triggering Conditions

A use permit is required when:
- The Phoenix Zoning Ordinance lists the proposed use as requiring a use permit in the applicable base zone (e.g., a drive-through restaurant in a commercial zone, a childcare center in a residential zone, a gas station at certain locations, a cell tower, a medical marijuana dispensary, etc.). - An existing use permit's conditions need to be modified.
- Expansion of an existing conditionally-permitted use exceeds thresholds that trigger a new or amended use permit.

A use permit is distinct from a rezoning: the use permit does not change the base zone; it authorizes a specific use on a specific site within the existing zone.

## Findings Required

For a use permit approval, the decision-maker must typically find that the proposed use:
1. Is consistent with the General Plan and zoning for the area.
2. Will not be materially detrimental to the public health, safety, and welfare of persons living or working in the vicinity.
3. Will not adversely affect the public convenience and general welfare.
4. Will not adversely affect or reduce the value of adjacent properties.
5. Is compatible with the character of the surrounding neighborhood.
6. Complies with all applicable development standards, or any deviations are appropriately conditioned.

## Common Pitfalls

- **Failing to address each finding:** The single most common deficiency. Each required finding must be affirmatively addressed in the application narrative. Decision-makers cannot approve a use permit if the record does not support each finding.
- **Inadequate site plan:** A vague conceptual plan leaves neighbors and decision-makers without assurance that the use will be compatible. Detailed site plans with dimensions, screening, and parking increase approval likelihood.
- **Underestimating neighborhood concerns:** Uses with known neighbor sensitivity (drive-throughs, gas stations, group homes, entertainment venues) require proactive outreach. See [[Neighborhood Outreach]].
- **Conditions that are operationally infeasible:** If conditions are negotiated or imposed, review them carefully before accepting. A condition that cannot be complied with defeats the purpose of the approval.
- **Personal vs. transferable approval:** Confirm whether the use permit runs with the land or is personal to the applicant — this affects saleability of the asset.
- **Failure to build/open within vesting period:** An approved use permit may have an expiration date if the use is not established. TBD — verify vesting provisions.
