# Phoenix Variance

## Summary

A variance is a quasi-judicial authorization for deviation from specific dimensional or numerical development standards of the Phoenix Zoning Ordinance — setbacks, height limits, lot coverage, parking counts, fence heights, and similar standards — when strict application of those standards would create an undue hardship due to the unique physical characteristics of the property. A variance is not a tool for avoiding compliance with a regulation merely because compliance is inconvenient or expensive; it requires a genuine demonstration of hardship rooted in the land itself, not the owner's circumstances.

Variances are granted by the Zoning Adjustment Hearing Officer (for administrative/minor variances) or the Board of Adjustment (for standard variances) in Phoenix. The Board of Adjustment is an independent quasi-judicial body established to provide relief from the literal application of the zoning code in hardship cases while protecting the intent of the ordinance and the interests of neighboring property owners. ## Triggering Conditions

A variance is required when:
- A property owner seeks to build or use property in a way that complies with the applicable use regulations but cannot meet one or more of the dimensional standards (setback, height, lot coverage, parking count, etc.) of the applicable zone.
- A physical characteristic of the lot — its size, shape, topography, or relationship to surrounding development — creates a hardship that a regularly-shaped lot would not face.
- An existing nonconforming structure needs to expand in a way that would increase the degree of nonconformity.

Variances do NOT authorize:
- Uses not permitted in the zone (that requires a use permit or rezoning).
- Wholesale deviation from the intent of the ordinance.
- Relief from conditions imposed by a prior use permit or rezoning.

## Findings Required

This is the core of variance approvability. The decision-maker must find each of the following (TBD — verify exact findings language against the Phoenix Zoning Ordinance variance provisions):

1. **Unique physical hardship:** Special circumstances or conditions applicable to the specific property (size, shape, topography, location, or surroundings) exist that do not apply generally to other property in the vicinity and zone. The hardship must be inherent to the land, not self-created by the applicant.
2. **Strict application causes undue hardship:** Literal enforcement of the zoning standards would deprive the applicant of privileges enjoyed by owners of other similarly situated property.
3. **Will not be materially detrimental:** The variance will not be materially detrimental to the public health, safety, or welfare, or injurious to adjacent property or improvements.
4. **Consistent with the spirit of the ordinance:** The variance is the minimum necessary to alleviate the hardship, and granting it is consistent with the general intent of the zoning ordinance.

The Board will NOT approve a variance to accommodate a use change, to remedy a financial hardship, or to correct a condition that the applicant created (e.g., by building in violation of the code).

## Common Pitfalls

- **Insufficient hardship showing:** The single most common reason for variance denial. The hardship must be specific, physical, and tied to the land — not to the owner's desired project. A generic hardship narrative ("it would be expensive to comply") is insufficient.
- **Self-created hardship:** Applicants who purchased the property knowing of the constraint, or who created the constraint through their own actions, may be denied relief on self-created hardship grounds.
- **Requesting more than the minimum:** The variance must be the minimum necessary to alleviate the hardship. Asking for more than needed undermines credibility and findings support.
- **Failure to address each finding:** Like the use permit, every required finding must be addressed in writing. Skipping a finding leaves the record without support for that element.
- **Neighbor opposition on non-hardship grounds:** Neighbor opposition based on dislike of the project (rather than legitimate hardship concerns) generally should not defeat a technically-qualifying variance, but it can influence the decision-maker's reading of the facts.
