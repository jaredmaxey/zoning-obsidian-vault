# Gilbert Rezoning

## Summary

A rezoning (zone change) is the process by which property within the Town of Gilbert is reclassified from one zoning district to another. Rezonings are **legislative** actions because they amend the official zoning map, which has the force of law. This means the Town Council has broad discretion in its decision and the action is generally not subject to reversal via administrative appeal. See [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] for cross-jurisdictional context and [[Planned Unit Development (PUD)]] when the rezoning involves a PUD designation.

A rezoning must be consistent with the adopted General Plan. If the current General Plan designation does not support the proposed zone, a concurrent or prior [[Gilbert General Plan Amendment]] is required. Rezonings with a development plan or PUD designation effectively lock in a conceptual site layout and use mix as a condition of approval.

## Triggering Conditions

A rezoning is required whenever a property owner or developer wishes to develop under uses or dimensional standards that are not permitted in the current base zone. Common triggers: converting agricultural or residential land to commercial or employment use; intensifying density beyond what the existing zone allows; converting commercial land to mixed-use or residential; or obtaining a PUD designation to enable project-specific development standards.

## Decision-Maker

The **Planning Commission** holds a public hearing and issues a recommendation to the **Town Council**. The Town Council makes the final decision by majority vote (verify threshold against Gilbert LDC — needs-verification). Staff may recommend continuance, approval with conditions, or denial. Because rezoning is legislative, the Council is not bound by the Planning Commission recommendation.

## Required Submittals

Typical package includes: rezoning application and fee; legal description and assessor parcel number(s); narrative statement of justification (consistency with General Plan, compatibility with surrounding uses, public benefit); conceptual site plan (often required, particularly for PUD or development plan rezonings); traffic impact analysis for larger projects; and outreach documentation. ## Findings Required

Gilbert must find that the proposed zone change is consistent with the General Plan, compatible with surrounding land uses and zoning, consistent with the public health, safety, and welfare, and meets any additional criteria in the Gilbert LDC. For PUD rezonings, additional findings regarding project quality and public benefit typically apply — verify exact findings language in the LDC (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

As a legislative action, a rezoning denial (or approval) is not subject to administrative appeal through the Board of Adjustment. Judicial challenge must be filed in Maricopa County Superior Court. See [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Submitting a rezoning without confirming General Plan consistency — a GPA must precede or run concurrently, adding time and uncertainty.
- Inadequate traffic analysis for higher-intensity rezonings on arterial corridors; Gilbert is growth-oriented but scrutinizes traffic impacts.
- PUD development plans that are too rigid can create problems at the building-permit stage if plans change; PUD plans that are too conceptual can generate conditions of approval that are hard to satisfy.
- Insufficient [[Neighborhood Outreach]] — neighboring property owners and HOAs are notified and their testimony is part of the record.
