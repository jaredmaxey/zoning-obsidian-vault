# Gilbert Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting in the Town of Gilbert is the process by which land is formally divided into lots, tracts, parcels, rights-of-way, and easements for development and sale. Gilbert's subdivision regulations are consistent with Arizona's general subdivision framework (ARS § 9-463 et seq.) and the Gilbert Land Development Code. The process typically proceeds in two major stages: **preliminary plat** (planning-level approval of the proposed layout, infrastructure, and conditions) followed by **final plat** (engineering-complete, legal recordation document). See [[Subdivision Approval]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

For commercial and industrial subdivisions, the process ensures that parcels are properly configured for the intended development, that adequate infrastructure is committed, and that public rights-of-way and utility easements are properly dedicated. An **Infrastructure Improvement Agreement (IIA)** is commonly required to ensure that required public improvements are constructed or financially secured before lots are released for building permits.

## Triggering Conditions

Subdivision platting is required whenever land is divided into two or more lots, parcels, or tracts for sale, lease, or development — including commercial condominiums, industrial parks, and master-planned residential communities. Minor lot splits or lot combinations may be handled through an administrative parcel split process rather than full preliminary/final plat — verify thresholds in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification). Replatting (amending an existing recorded plat) follows a similar but sometimes abbreviated process.

## Decision-Maker

The **Planning Commission** reviews the preliminary plat and issues a recommendation; the **Town Council** gives final approval on the preliminary plat and approves the final plat. Staff at Development Services administers technical review. Verify exact decision-maker authority and routing in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification).

## Required Submittals

**Preliminary plat:** application form; preliminary plat drawing (proposed lot layout, dimensions, rights-of-way, easements, grading concept); title report; preliminary civil engineering (drainage, utilities); traffic study (if applicable); CC&Rs or HOA documentation where applicable; and a narrative addressing code compliance and General Plan consistency.

**Final plat:** completed engineering-grade plat drawing; recorded CC&Rs; approved civil construction plans; infrastructure improvement agreement (if required); title report; and all required agency signatures (Town Engineer, Development Services Director, etc.). ## Findings Required

Preliminary plat approval requires findings that the proposed subdivision is consistent with the General Plan, complies with the Gilbert LDC and subdivision regulations, provides adequate infrastructure and access, and is compatible with surrounding land uses. Arizona law (ARS § 9-463.02) establishes the statutory framework for plat approval authority — local findings criteria in the Gilbert LDC should be verified (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

Preliminary plat decisions may be appealed to the Town Council (if the Planning Commission makes the initial decision) or via judicial review. Verify the specific appeal path and deadlines in the Gilbert LDC and ARS § 9-463.02 (needs-verification). See [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to engage the Town Engineer early on drainage and infrastructure requirements — Gilbert has specific grading and stormwater management standards that can significantly affect lot yield and layout.
- Underestimating the time required to satisfy all conditions of approval and obtain all agency sign-offs before final plat can be recorded.
- IIA financial assurance requirements (letters of credit, bonds) can be a cash-flow burden for smaller developers — negotiate scope and phasing carefully.
- Not confirming that the proposed platting is consistent with an approved PUD or rezoning conditions; inconsistencies between the plat and prior entitlement approvals can require amendments before final plat approval.
