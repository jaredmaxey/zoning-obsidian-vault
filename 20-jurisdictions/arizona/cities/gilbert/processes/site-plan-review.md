# Gilbert Design Review / Site Plan

## Summary

Site plan and design review is the process by which the Town of Gilbert evaluates a proposed development project for compliance with the Gilbert Land Development Code's development standards — including site layout, architecture, landscaping, parking, access, lighting, signage, and compatibility with surrounding development. It is typically an **administrative** process for projects that comply with all applicable standards, but may be elevated to a quasi-judicial hearing for larger, more complex, or discretionary projects. See [[Site Plan Review]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

Design review in Gilbert applies standards that address both functional requirements (parking, circulation, utilities) and aesthetic/compatibility criteria (building materials, articulation, landscaping buffers). Heritage District and other overlay areas often impose more detailed design standards than the base zone alone. Meeting design standards is a prerequisite to building permit issuance.

## Triggering Conditions

Site plan review (and design review where applicable) is required for most new commercial, industrial, mixed-use, and multi-family development, and often for significant additions or changes to existing commercial sites. The specific thresholds — square footage, number of units, site alteration percentage — that trigger formal review versus administrative sign-off should be confirmed in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification). Single-family residential is typically handled through the building permit process, not a separate site plan review.

## Decision-Maker

For projects in conformance with all code standards, site plan review is typically an **administrative staff decision** handled by the Development Services Department. For projects that are part of a discretionary entitlement (rezoning, use permit, PUD) or that require design review by a board or commission, the **Planning Commission** may be the decision-maker. The routing depends on project type, overlay district applicability, and whether variances or other discretionary actions are involved — verify against the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification).

## Required Submittals

A complete site plan package typically includes: site plan (to scale, showing property lines, buildings, parking, access, landscaping, utilities); architectural elevations; landscape plan; photometric (lighting) plan; signage plan; grading and drainage plan; civil engineering plans; and a narrative addressing applicable design standards. Submittal requirements vary by project type — verify current checklist with Gilbert Development Services (needs-verification).

## Typical Timeline

Administrative site plan review timelines for complete, code-compliant projects are generally faster than discretionary entitlements. First-review turnaround, resubmittal cycles, and total duration to approval are TBD — verify against current Gilbert Development Services published timelines or pre-application meeting guidance (needs-verification). Projects involving multiple disciplines (traffic, civil, architecture, landscape) can accumulate review cycles.

## Findings Required

For administrative approvals, staff confirms compliance with all applicable code standards rather than making discretionary findings. Where design review involves a quasi-judicial body, the decision-maker typically applies findings addressing compatibility with surrounding development, conformance with design guidelines, adequacy of site organization, and consistency with any applicable overlay standards. Verify applicable findings criteria for Heritage District and other overlay areas (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

Administrative site plan approvals and conditions are typically appealable to the Planning Commission; Planning Commission decisions are appealable to the Town Council or via judicial review — confirm the exact appeal chain and deadlines in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification). See [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Submitting incomplete plan sets — missing any discipline (civil, landscape, photometric) typically triggers a completeness rejection and resets the review clock.
- Underestimating design standard requirements in overlay areas, particularly the Heritage District, which has more prescriptive façade and streetscape requirements than standard commercial zones.
- Ignoring Gilbert's landscaping buffer requirements at residential/commercial interfaces; these can significantly affect site layout efficiency.
- Parking layout errors — Gilbert enforces dimensional standards for stall size, drive aisle width, and turning radii; errors discovered late in review cost time and redesign expense.
