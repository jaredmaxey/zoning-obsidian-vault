# Gilbert Variance

## Summary

A variance grants relief from the **dimensional or physical standards** of the Gilbert Land Development Code — such as setbacks, height limits, lot coverage, or parking requirements — when strict application of the code would create an **undue hardship** due to the specific physical characteristics of the property (not the owner's personal circumstances). Variances are **quasi-judicial** decisions, meaning the Board of Adjustment applies findings criteria to the factual record; it cannot grant a variance simply because it would be convenient or economically beneficial. See [[Variance]] for cross-jurisdictional framework.

A variance does not authorize a use not otherwise permitted in the zone — that requires either a use permit or a rezoning. The hardship must be tied to the land itself (e.g., irregular lot shape, topography, substandard lot created by prior platting) rather than self-created by the applicant.

## Triggering Conditions

A variance is appropriate when a property's unique physical characteristics — unusual shape, size, topography, or pre-existing conditions — make it impossible or unreasonably difficult to meet a specific dimensional standard without effectively denying any reasonable use of the property. It is not available as a shortcut to avoid standards that are simply inconvenient or commercially undesirable. The hardship standard is intentionally high to maintain the integrity of zoning standards.

## Decision-Maker

The **Board of Adjustment** (verify exact name and composition against current Gilbert LDC — needs-verification) is the decision-making body for variances. This is an administrative, quasi-judicial process; the Board applies the hardship findings to the factual record of the specific property. Staff provides a recommendation but the Board makes an independent determination.

## Findings Required

Arizona law and the Gilbert LDC require the Board to make specific findings before granting a variance. These typically include: (1) special circumstances exist applicable to the property (size, shape, topography, location, surroundings) that do not apply generally to other properties in the same zone; (2) strict application of the code provisions deprives the property owner of privileges enjoyed by other properties in the same zone; (3) granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to adjacent properties or the public interest; and (4) the hardship is not self-created. Verify the exact findings criteria in the Gilbert LDC — this is the crux of every variance case (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

Board of Adjustment decisions on variances are final administrative actions in Arizona. Appeal is by **writ of certiorari** (special action) to Maricopa County Superior Court — the court reviews whether the Board followed proper procedures and applied the law correctly, not whether the decision was wise. Filing deadline is typically within a short period of the Board's decision — verify statutory deadline (needs-verification). See [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Framing hardship as financial rather than physical — economic disadvantage does not satisfy the hardship standard; the case must be built on the land's physical characteristics.
- Requesting a variance where the proper remedy is actually a use permit, rezoning, or design waiver — misrouting the application wastes time and fees.
- Underestimating the strength required in the findings narrative; even a sympathetic Board cannot grant a variance without adequate record support.
- Self-created hardship (e.g., the applicant designed a building that doesn't fit the lot) is explicitly not a basis for a variance.
