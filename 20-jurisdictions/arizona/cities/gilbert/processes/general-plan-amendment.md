# Gilbert General Plan Amendment

## Summary

A General Plan Amendment (GPA) modifies the Town of Gilbert's adopted General Plan — either its land-use designation map or its policy text. Because the General Plan is Gilbert's foundational land-use document, amendments are **legislative** actions that require Town Council approval and are subject to heightened procedural requirements, including public notice and hearings. Arizona law (ARS § 9-461.06) requires a two-thirds supermajority Council vote to approve a General Plan amendment, making GPAs among the most demanding entitlement actions in the town. See also [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

GPAs are often the necessary precursor to a major rezoning: if a property's existing General Plan designation does not accommodate the desired zoning or use, the applicant must first secure a GPA before or concurrently with the zone change application.

## Triggering Conditions

A GPA is required when a proposed development, rezoning, or policy change is inconsistent with the current General Plan land-use map or policy text. Common triggers include: requests to change a land-use category (e.g., residential to commercial, employment to mixed-use); density increases above what the existing designation supports; and policy text updates driven by changing community priorities or state-law mandates. Minor corrections and state-law-required amendments may follow an abbreviated process — verify with Gilbert Development Services (needs-verification).

## Decision-Maker

The **Planning Commission** holds a public hearing and issues a recommendation. The **Town Council** makes the final legislative decision. Arizona law requires a two-thirds supermajority vote of the Council to adopt a GPA (verify specific Council composition and vote threshold against current Gilbert documents — needs-verification). This is a legislative, not quasi-judicial, action.

## Required Submittals

Typical submittal package includes: completed GPA application; narrative justification explaining consistency with General Plan goals and policies; existing and proposed land-use exhibit maps; traffic impact analysis (if applicable); environmental analysis as appropriate; fiscal impact analysis for large projects; and adjacent property owner and community outreach documentation. Verify the current application checklist with Gilbert Development Services (needs-verification).

## Typical Timeline

GPA applications involve multiple public hearings and are among the longer entitlement processes. From application acceptance to Town Council decision, timelines commonly range from several months to over a year for complex amendments. ## Findings Required

The Town Council must find that the proposed amendment is consistent with the overall goals and policies of the General Plan, advances the public health, safety, and welfare of the community, and is consistent with applicable state law. Specific statutory findings under ARS § 9-461.06 and any locally required findings should be verified against the Gilbert LDC and current application guidance (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

As a legislative action, a GPA is not subject to the administrative appeals process. Legal challenges must be filed in Maricopa County Superior Court within the applicable statutory period. See [[Appeals Process]] for general context.

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to demonstrate consistency with remaining General Plan goals when proposing a narrow map change — isolated amendments that conflict with adjacent designations draw scrutiny.
- Inadequate community outreach before the hearing — Gilbert Planning Commission and Council weight neighborhood input heavily.
- Underestimating timeline — attempting to sequence a GPA and rezoning too tightly can create schedule risk if the GPA hearing is continued or denied.
- Fiscal impact concerns, particularly for large residential intensification projects, can become decisive at the Council level.
