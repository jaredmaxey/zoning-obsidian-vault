# Gilbert Use Permit

## Summary

A use permit (sometimes called a conditional use permit or special use permit in other jurisdictions) authorizes a land use that is permitted within a base zoning district but only with additional scrutiny and conditions because of its potential impacts on surrounding properties. In the Town of Gilbert, the use permit is a **quasi-judicial** process: the decision-maker applies specific findings criteria to the facts of the application, rather than exercising broad legislative discretion. Approval grants the right to operate a specific use at a specific location, subject to conditions. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

The Gilbert Land Development Code identifies which uses require a use permit within each base zone. Examples commonly include drive-throughs, gas stations, large assembly uses, childcare facilities in commercial zones, and similar uses whose design and operational characteristics must be tailored to site-specific conditions.

## Triggering Conditions

A use permit is required whenever the Gilbert LDC classifies a proposed use as a "permitted with conditions," "conditional," or "special use" within the applicable base zone. The specific list is zone-by-zone in the LDC use tables — verify which uses require a use permit in the relevant zone before assuming a use is by-right (needs-verification).

## Decision-Maker

The **Planning Commission** (or a designated hearing officer, depending on the scale and type of use) holds a public hearing and issues a decision. Confirm the decision-maker routing rules in the Gilbert LDC — some lower-impact use permits may be routed to an administrative hearing officer while others require full Planning Commission hearing (needs-verification). Use permit decisions are quasi-judicial, meaning they apply findings criteria to facts; they are not legislative.

## Required Submittals

Typical package includes: application form and fee; site plan; floor plan (if relevant to use); narrative addressing each required finding; traffic analysis for high-trip-generating uses; parking and circulation analysis; landscape plan if modifications are involved; and any use-specific studies (e.g., noise, operational hours). ## Findings Required

Gilbert's use permit findings typically require the decision-maker to find that: (1) the use is consistent with the General Plan; (2) the use is compatible with surrounding uses; (3) adequate infrastructure (access, parking, utilities) is available; (4) any detrimental impacts can be mitigated by conditions of approval; and (5) the use is consistent with the purposes of the base zoning district. Verify the exact findings language in the Gilbert LDC — these are the crux of approvability (needs-verification).

## Appeal Path

Use permit decisions (quasi-judicial) may be appealed. The appeal path — to the Board of Adjustment, Planning Commission, or Town Council depending on who made the initial decision — should be confirmed against the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification). Judicial review of final administrative decisions is to Maricopa County Superior Court. See [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to address each required finding in the narrative; decision-makers must make findings on the record and an inadequate narrative weakens the record for approval.
- Drive-through and auto-oriented use permits often face scrutiny in walkable or mixed-use areas, especially near the Heritage District.
- Conditions of approval can accumulate into operational restrictions (hours, delivery windows, lighting levels) that materially affect project economics — negotiate conditions early.
- Incomplete traffic analysis for high-traffic-generating uses is a common reason for continuance.
