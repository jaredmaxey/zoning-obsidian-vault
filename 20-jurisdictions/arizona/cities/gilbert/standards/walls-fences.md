# Gilbert Walls and Fences Standards

## Summary

The Town of Gilbert's walls and fences standards regulate the location, height, materials, and design of perimeter walls, screening walls, decorative fences, and retaining walls for commercial, industrial, and residential development. These standards serve multiple purposes: screening unsightly service areas, mechanical equipment, and loading docks from public streets and adjacent residential uses; defining property boundaries; controlling noise; and contributing to the visual quality of the built environment. Perimeter masonry walls are common in the East Valley suburban pattern, and Gilbert's standards reflect that context.

For commercial development, masonry screening walls at the rear and sides of properties — particularly where commercial uses adjoin residential zones — are typically required as a condition of development approval. Material quality, decorative treatment, and landscaping in front of perimeter walls are often design review considerations, especially in higher-visibility locations and overlay areas.

## Applicability

Walls and fences standards apply to new development and to modifications of existing screening walls. Key trigger scenarios include: commercial/residential interface perimeter walls; screening of loading areas, trash enclosures, and mechanical equipment; subdivision perimeter walls; and walls within public rights-of-way setbacks. Retaining walls may have separate height and engineering requirements. Verify specific applicability and triggers in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification).

## Exceptions

Variations from standard wall requirements may be approved through the design review process, particularly where alternative screening methods (dense landscaping berms, architectural features) achieve equivalent screening. Variances are available through the standard process (see [[Gilbert Variance]]). Heritage District and pedestrian-oriented overlay areas may have specific guidelines discouraging blank perimeter walls along street frontages and favoring transparent or articulated fencing instead.
