# Gilbert Lighting Standards

## Summary

The Town of Gilbert's outdoor lighting standards regulate the design, placement, and intensity of exterior lighting for commercial, industrial, and multi-family development. The standards are designed to provide adequate security and operational lighting while controlling light pollution, glare, and light trespass onto adjacent residential properties. Arizona municipalities generally align their outdoor lighting standards with the needs of both neighboring residents (limiting spill light) and the regional dark-sky environment — verify whether Gilbert has adopted any formal International Dark-Sky Association (IDA) or equivalent dark-sky lighting provisions (needs-verification).

Lighting plans (photometric plans) are required as part of the site plan review package for most commercial and multi-family projects. The photometric plan must demonstrate that maximum foot-candle levels at property lines and paved areas comply with the LDC, and that all fixtures use full-cutoff (shielded) optics to minimize upward light scatter.

## Applicability

Outdoor lighting standards apply to new commercial, industrial, and multi-family development and to significant lighting upgrades or additions on existing sites. Single-family residential lighting may be governed separately or only through general nuisance provisions. Parking lot lighting, building security lighting, canopy lighting (gas stations, drive-throughs), and signage illumination are all within scope — verify specific applicability in the Gilbert LDC (needs-verification).

## Exceptions

Temporary lighting for construction, seasonal displays, and special events may be addressed by separate temporary-use provisions. Variances to lighting standards are available through the standard process (see [[Gilbert Variance]]). Heritage District and other overlay areas may have specific architectural lighting design requirements.

## Calculation Examples

*Illustrative only.* A photometric plan for a commercial parking lot would model the illuminance level at every point on the lot surface (typically in a grid), identify the average and minimum foot-candle levels, and confirm that the foot-candle level at the property line stays within the maximum.
