# Tempe General Plan Amendment

## Summary

A General Plan Amendment (GPA) modifies Tempe's adopted General Plan — either the land-use designation map or the policy text — to allow a land use or intensity that the current plan does not contemplate. Because Arizona law (ARS Title 9) requires that rezonings be consistent with the adopted General Plan, a GPA is often a prerequisite step when a proposed rezoning conflicts with the existing General Plan designation. GPAs are legislative actions decided by the Tempe City Council, typically on recommendation from the planning body. They are not quasi-judicial; the council exercises broad policy discretion. See also: [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]].

## Triggering Conditions

A GPA is required when a proposed land use or development intensity on a specific parcel is inconsistent with the current General Plan land-use map designation or applicable General Plan policy. Common triggers include proposals for significantly higher density, a change from residential to commercial, or introduction of uses not contemplated by the current designation. A GPA may also be initiated by the City (e.g., area-wide policy updates or corridor studies) or by individual property owners. ## Findings Required

Because a GPA is legislative, Tempe City Council has broad policy discretion. However, the council typically considers whether the amendment: (1) advances General Plan goals and guiding policies; (2) is consistent with statewide planning requirements; (3) responds to changed conditions or a documented need not anticipated at the time of plan adoption; and (4) is compatible with surrounding land uses and community character. ## Appeal Path

Legislative land-use decisions by the City Council are generally subject to challenge in the **Superior Court of Maricopa County** via special action or certiorari rather than internal administrative appeal. Verify any internal reconsideration procedure and applicable statutory deadlines. (`needs-verification`)

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to demonstrate meaningful policy justification beyond project economics — the council must find a planning rationale, not just project merit.
- Insufficient neighborhood outreach before the public hearing; early community engagement reduces opposition at hearing. See [[Neighborhood Outreach]].
- Assuming GPA approval automatically entitles the project — a successful GPA still requires a separate rezoning and development plan review.
- Underestimating timeline: GPA + rezoning done concurrently can save time but increases complexity.
