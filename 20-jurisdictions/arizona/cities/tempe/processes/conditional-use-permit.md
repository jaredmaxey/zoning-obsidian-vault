# Tempe Use Permit

## Summary

Tempe's Use Permit (the local equivalent of a Conditional Use Permit) authorizes land uses that are listed in the Zoning and Development Code as conditionally permitted — uses that may be appropriate in a zone under certain circumstances but require individualized review due to their potential impacts on adjacent properties, traffic, or neighborhood character. The Use Permit is a quasi-judicial approval: the decision-maker applies written findings to the specific facts and circumstances of the application, distinguishing it from the broad policy discretion exercised in legislative actions like rezonings. Conditions of approval addressing mitigation, hours, site design, or operations are routinely attached. See also: [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]].

## Triggering Conditions

A Use Permit is required when a property owner proposes a use listed as "conditionally permitted" (or equivalent designation) in the applicable base zone district under the Tempe ZDC. Common examples in Tempe's context include: drive-through facilities, churches/places of worship, large-format retail, student housing in certain zones, bars and nightclubs near residential areas, childcare centers, and certain entertainment uses. The specific list of conditionally permitted uses for each zone is established in the ZDC — verify applicable uses before application. (`needs-verification`)

## Decision-Maker

The **Development Review Commission (DRC)** is the quasi-judicial body that hears and decides Use Permit applications. Staff provides a recommendation. ## Findings Required

The DRC must make affirmative written findings to approve a Use Permit. Typical findings (verify exact language against the ZDC — `needs-verification`) include: (1) the proposed use is consistent with the General Plan; (2) the use is consistent with the purpose and intent of the base zone; (3) the use will not be materially detrimental to the public health, safety, or welfare or to adjacent properties; and (4) the design, layout, and operations of the use are compatible with surrounding uses. The conditions of approval are crafted to ensure these findings can be made.

Decisions of the DRC on Use Permits are typically appealed to the **Board of Adjustment** or directly to the **City Council** — verify the current appeal path and filing deadline in the ZDC. (`needs-verification`) See also: [[Appeals Process]].

## Common Pitfalls

- Submitting an incomplete narrative — the findings are the crux of approval; a generic project description that does not address each finding directly is a common deficiency.
- Underestimating neighbor opposition for uses adjacent to residential — bars, nightclubs, and entertainment uses near ASU neighborhoods frequently draw public comment.
- Failure to propose meaningful conditions proactively; applicants who present their own draft conditions signal good faith and give the DRC a basis for approval.
- Ignoring parking and circulation analysis for uses that generate significant trip generation or parking demand.
