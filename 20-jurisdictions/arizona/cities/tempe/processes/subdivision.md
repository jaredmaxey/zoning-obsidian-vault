# Tempe Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting in Tempe establishes or modifies the legal boundaries of land parcels, dedicates public rights-of-way and easements, and ensures infrastructure requirements (streets, utilities, drainage) are addressed as a condition of development. Because Tempe is a fully built-out, landlocked city with no greenfield land, subdivision activity in Tempe is overwhelmingly of the infill replatting variety — combining, splitting, or reconfiguring existing lots to support redevelopment projects rather than creating new greenfield subdivisions. The process is governed by Arizona's subdivision statutes (ARS Title 9) and Tempe's local subdivision ordinance or ZDC provisions. See also: [[Subdivision Approval]].

## Triggering Conditions

A plat or replat is required when a property owner proposes to: divide one or more existing parcels into two or more new lots; combine existing parcels into a single legal parcel; dedicate new public rights-of-way, utility easements, or open space; or record a condominium or lot-line adjustment that changes legal parcel boundaries. The specific thresholds triggering full plat review versus a simpler lot-split or lot-line adjustment process should be verified against the Tempe ZDC and City Code. (`needs-verification`)

**Preliminary plat** review is typically administrative (staff/engineering). **Final plat** approval is generally granted by the **City Council** after City Engineer and utility sign-off. ## Findings Required

Plat approval findings are primarily technical rather than discretionary: (1) compliance with Arizona subdivision statutes; (2) consistency with the Tempe ZDC and applicable area plans; (3) adequate provision for public infrastructure (streets, drainage, utilities); and (4) conformance with Maricopa County Recorder requirements for form and format. ## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to confirm that the proposed lot configuration is buildable under current zoning — a legally platted lot still must meet minimum lot size, width, setback, and other zoning standards.
- Neglecting existing easements that may constrain development on newly platted parcels; easement review and potential vacation/relocation should be addressed early.
- Underestimating the coordination required with Maricopa County Recorder for final plat recording, which operates on its own schedule.
- In condominium projects: state condominium statute requirements (ARS Title 33) overlay the platting process and require a separate public report from the Arizona Department of Real Estate.
