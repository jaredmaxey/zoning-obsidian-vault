# Mesa, Arizona

## Overview

Mesa is Arizona's third-largest city and one of the largest cities in the United States by land area, located in the East Valley of the Phoenix metropolitan area. Its zoning framework is administered through the Mesa Zoning Ordinance, which governs land use across a diverse mix of residential neighborhoods, major employment corridors (particularly along the US-60/Superstition Freeway and the Loop 202 Red Mountain Freeway), regional retail centers, and an increasingly active downtown core. Mesa's development posture has shifted meaningfully in recent years toward infill redevelopment and transit-oriented development, driven by the extension of Valley Metro Rail through downtown Mesa.

The city's General Plan (flag: current horizon year and title `needs-verification` — verify against Mesa's adopted General Plan) provides the policy foundation for land-use decisions. Mesa has pursued mixed-use and walkable-urban zoning amendments, particularly in the downtown and transit corridors, to attract higher-density, activity-generating development consistent with light-rail investment. CRE developers should note Mesa's active use of overlay districts and specific area plans to shape development along key corridors.

## Governing Body

- **City Council** — ultimate legislative authority for zoning code amendments, general plan amendments, and rezonings that rise to the Council level.
- **Planning and Zoning Board** — quasi-judicial board that hears and makes recommendations or decisions on rezonings, special uses, variances, and conditional use permits (decision-maker role varies by application type — `needs-verification`).
- **Board of Adjustment** — hears and decides appeals from zoning administrator decisions and variance requests.
- **Zoning Administrator / Development Services Staff** — administrative approvals, interpretations, and site plan reviews that do not require board action.

## Notable Recent Code Changes

Mesa has made significant zoning investments aligned with the Valley Metro Rail extension through downtown and into Fiesta district. Transit-oriented development (TOD) zoning amendments have introduced higher-density mixed-use allowances near light-rail stations, with reduced parking minimums and urban-form standards prioritizing pedestrian activity. The downtown core has seen form-based or context-sensitive overlay language designed to encourage mid-rise and mixed-use redevelopment. Developers should check for any pending general plan updates or corridor-specific area plans that may affect project sites.
