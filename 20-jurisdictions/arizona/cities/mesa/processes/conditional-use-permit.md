# Mesa Council Use Permit / CUP

## Summary

In Mesa, certain land uses are conditionally permitted within a zone district — meaning they are allowed only upon approval of a Council Use Permit (CUP), the city's equivalent of a conditional use permit. Unlike a rezoning, a CUP does not change the zone district; it grants approval for a specific use at a specific location, typically with conditions that mitigate potential impacts. Uses that require a CUP are typically identified in the Mesa Zoning Ordinance's use regulation tables for each zone district. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

CUPs are quasi-judicial actions because they apply a general standard to a specific parcel — the decision maker must find that the proposed use meets the ordinance's approval criteria. The CUP runs with the land (it is tied to the parcel, not the applicant) unless otherwise conditioned. (`TBD — confirm that CUPs run with the land under Mesa's code` `needs-verification`)

## Triggering Conditions

A CUP is required when a proposed use appears in the Mesa Zoning Ordinance's conditional-use column for the property's zone district. Common CUP-triggering uses in commercial zones include drive-through facilities, automotive-related uses, schools, religious institutions, certain entertainment venues, medical offices in residential-adjacent zones, and higher-intensity uses that warrant a case-by-case siting and impact review. ## Findings Required

To approve a CUP, the decision maker typically must find that the proposed use is consistent with the General Plan, compatible with surrounding land uses, will not adversely affect adjacent properties or the public health/safety/welfare, and meets the specific criteria listed in the ordinance for that use type. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Incomplete use narrative:** The findings narrative must address each approval criterion specifically; a generic narrative leads to continued hearings or denial.
- **Neighbor opposition:** Uses that generate traffic, noise, or late-night activity frequently draw neighbor opposition; early outreach and operational conditions can reduce hearing friction.
- **Site plan deficiencies:** CUPs for drive-throughs, fuel stations, or other use types with specific design standards must demonstrate compliance with those standards in the submittal.
- **Condition compliance tracking:** Conditions attached to a CUP run with the land; buyers of conditioned properties inherit obligations that may not be visible without a title search of zoning records.
