# Mesa Variance

## Summary

A variance provides relief from a specific dimensional or development standard of the Mesa Zoning Ordinance when strict application of the standard would cause an undue hardship on a property owner due to unique physical characteristics of the parcel. Variances are quasi-judicial actions decided by the Board of Adjustment after a public hearing. Because variances are granted based on property-specific hardship — not general policy — they are intentionally narrow in scope. See [[Variance]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

Mesa generally recognizes two categories of variance: dimensional variances (relief from setbacks, height limits, lot coverage, parking counts, etc.) and, potentially, use variances (relief allowing a use not permitted in the zone — `TBD — verify whether Mesa allows use variances or whether only dimensional variances are available` `needs-verification`). Variances are among the more difficult entitlements to obtain because the findings are strict and the hardship must be tied to the land, not to the owner's economic preference.

## Triggering Conditions

A variance is sought when a property owner cannot comply with a specific zoning standard — most often setbacks, lot coverage, height, or parking — due to a physical constraint of the parcel (irregular shape, topography, existing encroachments, substandard lot size created before current standards). Variances are not available simply because compliance is inconvenient or economically disadvantageous.

## Findings Required

To approve a variance, the Board of Adjustment must typically find all of the following (verify exact findings against Mesa Zoning Ordinance — `needs-verification`):

1. There are special circumstances or conditions (topography, size, shape, location) applicable to the property that do not apply generally to other properties in the same zone.
2. Strict application of the ordinance would deprive the property of privileges enjoyed by other properties in the same zone.
3. The variance is necessary for the preservation and enjoyment of substantial property rights.
4. Granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to the public welfare or injurious to adjacent properties.
5. The hardship is not self-created by the applicant or property owner.

All findings must be affirmatively met; failure on any one finding is typically fatal to the application.

## Appeal Path

Decisions of the Board of Adjustment are appealed to **Maricopa County Superior Court** by way of a special action or certiorari proceeding. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Self-created hardship:** If the constraint resulted from the applicant's own prior actions (e.g., voluntarily subdividing a lot below the minimum), the Board will deny on self-created hardship grounds.
- **Economic hardship only:** Arguing that the standard is expensive to comply with is not a variance finding — the hardship must be physical and tied to the land.
- **Weak findings narrative:** Boards require a thorough findings narrative tied to the specific ordinance criteria; a generic or conclusory submittal almost always fails.
- **Seeking relief better addressed by rezoning or CUP:** Applicants sometimes pursue a variance when the correct vehicle is a zone change or conditional use permit; staff review at pre-application can clarify the right path.
