# Mesa Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting in Mesa governs the division of land into two or more parcels for development or sale. Arizona's platting law (ARS Title 9 and Title 33) establishes the framework; Mesa's subdivision ordinance and engineering standards add city-specific requirements for street design, utility extensions, drainage, dedications, and improvement guarantees. The process typically involves a preliminary plat (conceptual approval), improvement plan review (engineering), and final plat (recordation). See [[Subdivision Approval]] for cross-jurisdictional context.

For CRE projects, the subdivision process is most relevant when combining or dividing parcels for a multi-parcel development, creating a commercial or industrial subdivision, or establishing a condominium or air-rights plat for vertical or horizontal ownership. Mesa Public Works and Engineering are heavily involved alongside Development Services in the improvement plan review phase.

## Triggering Conditions

A subdivision plat is required any time land is divided into two or more parcels, lots, or tracts for sale, lease, or development. Lot splits below a certain threshold may follow a simplified administrative lot-split process rather than the full preliminary/final plat path. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Improvement plan complexity underestimated:** The improvement plan review phase (drainage, utilities, paving) is frequently the longest part of the subdivision process; engage civil engineers early and budget for multiple plan check cycles.
- **Dedication requirements not anticipated in purchase price:** Right-of-way dedications and off-site improvement obligations (sidewalks, street widening, utility extensions) are conditions of platting and can be costly; identify them at due diligence.
- **Assurance requirements:** Mesa requires improvement guarantees before final plat recordation; confirm the required form (bond, deposit, trust) early in the process.
- **Maricopa County Recorder lead time:** Final plat recordation at the County adds time after City approval; plan for this in the project schedule.
