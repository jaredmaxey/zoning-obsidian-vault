# Chandler Use Permit

## Summary

Chandler's UDM designates certain land uses as "conditionally permitted" within specific zone districts — meaning those uses require additional review and approval beyond a standard building permit. The use permit (analogous to a Conditional Use Permit in many jurisdictions) is a quasi-judicial process that evaluates whether the proposed use is appropriate given the specific site, surrounding context, and applicable conditions. Approval includes use-specific conditions of approval that run with the land. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept. Note: Chandler's UDM may use specific terminology (e.g., "Special Use Permit," "Use Permit," or another designation) — verify exact term and process against current UDM. `needs-verification`

## Triggering Conditions

Required whenever a proposed use is listed as a conditional (rather than permitted-by-right) use in the applicable zone district's use table. Common examples in commercial and industrial contexts include drive-through facilities, automobile-related uses, schools in commercial zones, fuel stations, certain outdoor storage, or uses with operational characteristics warranting case-by-case review. ## Findings Required

Quasi-judicial use permit approval typically requires findings along the following lines (verify exact language against Chandler UDM): `needs-verification`

1. The proposed use is consistent with the General Plan and the purpose of the zone district.
2. The use will not be materially detrimental to adjacent properties or the surrounding neighborhood.
3. Adequate public facilities (traffic, utilities, drainage) exist or will be provided to support the use.
4. Any proposed conditions of approval adequately mitigate potential impacts.

These findings are the crux of approvability — applicants should build their submittals around demonstrating each finding.

## Common Pitfalls

- Failing to address all required findings in the application narrative — the decision-maker cannot approve without affirmative findings; an incomplete record often results in denial.
- Proposing operational characteristics (hours, outdoor activities, noise-generating equipment) without adequate mitigation — conditions of approval can cure many concerns if proactively offered.
- Not engaging neighbors before the hearing — opposition at the hearing can be decisive for quasi-judicial bodies even when findings are technically met.
- Confusing use permit territory with rezoning territory: if the use is not listed at all in the zone (not even conditionally), a rezoning is required rather than a use permit.
