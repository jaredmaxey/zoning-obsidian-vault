# Chandler Variance

## Summary

A variance is a quasi-judicial grant of relief from the dimensional or development standards of the Chandler UDM when strict application of those standards would create an unnecessary hardship due to unique physical characteristics of the subject property. Variances are not an entitlement and are not automatically granted when a hardship is claimed — the applicant must satisfy a multi-part findings test. Variances run with the land, not the owner. See [[Variance]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept. Note: Chandler's UDM may distinguish between area variances (dimensional standards) and use variances — verify whether use variances are available under current Chandler code. `needs-verification`

## Triggering Conditions

Required when a proposed development cannot comply with one or more of the UDM's dimensional standards (setbacks, height, lot coverage, parking count, etc.) and the applicant believes the deviation is justified by unique, hardship-creating physical circumstances specific to the parcel — such as unusual shape, topography, or prior lot configuration. A variance is NOT a mechanism to override use restrictions or permit a use not allowed in the zone; that requires a use permit or rezoning.

## Findings Required

Arizona law and Chandler's UDM require the Board to make affirmative findings (exact language TBD — verify against Chandler UDM): `needs-verification`

1. **Unique physical hardship:** Strict application of the standard creates an unnecessary hardship due to unique physical characteristics of the property (size, shape, topography, existing structures) — not applicable to other properties in the zone generally.
2. **No self-created hardship:** The hardship was not created by the applicant's own actions (e.g., a voluntary subdivision that created the undersized lot).
3. **Minimum relief:** The variance requested is the minimum necessary to alleviate the hardship.
4. **No detriment:** Granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to adjacent properties or the public welfare.
5. **Consistency with intent:** The variance is consistent with the intent of the UDM and the General Plan.

Meeting all findings is essential — the Board cannot grant a variance on equitable arguments alone if the legal standard is not satisfied.

## Common Pitfalls

- Arguing financial hardship (the property is more valuable without the setback) rather than physical hardship — economic inconvenience is not a legally sufficient grounds for a variance in Arizona.
- Failing to demonstrate uniqueness: if the alleged constraint affects many lots in the zone, it is a code problem for the City to fix, not a variance situation.
- Requesting more than the minimum variance — boards scrutinize overbroad requests; tailor the request to the minimum deviation actually needed.
- Self-created hardship: if the applicant or a predecessor created the lot configuration or physical constraint through their own action, the finding typically fails.
- Conflating a variance with a use permit — attempting to get a prohibited use approved through a variance is reversible error.
