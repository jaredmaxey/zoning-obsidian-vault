# Scottsdale General Plan Amendment

## Summary

A General Plan Amendment (GPA) modifies Scottsdale's adopted General Plan — either its Future Land Use Map designation for a specific parcel or the plan's policies, goals, or text. Because the General Plan is the foundational land-use policy document in Arizona (ARS § 9-461 et seq.), a rezoning that conflicts with the current GP designation will typically be denied unless a concurrent or preceding GPA is approved first. Developers therefore use the GPA process to unlock a higher-intensity or differently typed land-use designation before or simultaneously with a rezoning application. See also [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] for the cross-jurisdictional concept overview.

## Triggering Conditions

A GPA is triggered when a proposed development or rezoning is inconsistent with the Future Land Use Map designation or the applicable policy framework of the current General Plan. Common scenarios include a request to develop a commercially designated site at residential densities not contemplated by the plan, intensification of a site beyond the plan's allowed envelope, or a use category change (e.g., low-density residential to mixed-use commercial). Arizona law imposes limitations on how often and how extensively a general plan may be amended in any given year — verify the specific cycle and threshold rules against ARS § 9-461.06 and the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance. ## Decision-Maker

The **City Council** makes the final legislative decision on general plan amendments, typically acting on a recommendation from the **Planning Commission** following a public hearing. Because a GPA is a legislative act (not quasi-judicial), the standard of review is whether the amendment is consistent with the public interest and sound planning principles — findings are policy judgments, not legal tests. ## Findings Required

Because a GPA is a legislative act, there are no rigid quasi-judicial findings in the variance sense; rather, the decision-makers weigh whether the amendment is in the public interest and consistent with the plan's own goals. Common criteria applied include: (1) the amendment is consistent with remaining General Plan policies and elements; (2) changed conditions since plan adoption justify the amendment; (3) the amendment promotes the health, safety, and welfare of the community. Verify exact findings or criteria, if any, against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance and General Plan administrative procedures. (needs-verification)

## Appeal Path

General plan amendments are legislative acts. There is no administrative appeal — a dissatisfied party may challenge the action in Maricopa County **Superior Court** on grounds of procedural irregularity or violation of state law. The deadline to file is short; verify the applicable limitations period. (needs-verification)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Inconsistency with GP policies beyond the FLUM:** Changing the map designation alone is insufficient if the project narrative conflicts with specific GP policy language — address all relevant policies in the justification.
- **Timing relative to rezoning:** Filing a GPA and rezoning concurrently is common but adds process complexity; confirm whether Scottsdale allows parallel processing.
- **Annual cycle restrictions:** Arizona law may limit when large-scale GPAs can be processed — check timing early.
- **Neighborhood outreach:** Scottsdale applicants are strongly advised to conduct meaningful [[Neighborhood Outreach]] before the formal hearing; late opposition can derail a schedule.
- **Incomplete findings narrative:** Staff and commissioners scrutinize the justification heavily — generic narratives rarely succeed.
