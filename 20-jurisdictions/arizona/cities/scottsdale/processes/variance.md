# Scottsdale Variance

## Summary

A variance grants relief from a specific dimensional or development standard in the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance — such as a setback, lot coverage, height limit, or parking requirement — when strict application of the standard would cause an unnecessary hardship attributable to unique physical characteristics of the parcel, not to general conditions in the area or to the owner's self-created situation. Variances are among the highest-bar entitlements in any jurisdiction: the applicant must clear a strict set of findings, and approvals are conditioned not to confer special privilege or undermine the ordinance's overall intent. See [[Variance]] for the general concept. The Board of Adjustment is the decision-maker in most Scottsdale variance cases — verify against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance. (needs-verification)

## Triggering Conditions

A variance is sought when a proposed development cannot comply with a specific dimensional standard without relief, and the non-compliance stems from the unique physical attributes of the lot (e.g., irregular shape, steep topography, width, depth, or physical encumbrances like drainage easements). Variances are not available to authorize a use that is otherwise prohibited in the zone — that requires a rezoning or CUP. They are also generally not available simply because compliance is inconvenient or reduces profitability.

## Decision-Maker

**Board of Adjustment** — a quasi-judicial body that applies the ordinance's findings criteria to the record. The Board's decision is final at the local level; further challenge goes directly to Superior Court. ## Findings Required

The variance findings are the legal test. Exact language must be verified against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance, but Arizona municipalities typically require the Board to find all of the following: (1) special circumstances apply to the property (size, shape, topography, location, or surroundings) that do not apply generally to other properties in the same zone; (2) because of those special circumstances, strict application of the ordinance would deprive the owner of privileges enjoyed by other properties in the same zone; (3) the variance is necessary for the preservation and enjoyment of a substantial property right; (4) granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to the public welfare or to adjacent property; and (5) the variance is the minimum necessary to afford relief. Self-created hardship is a bar to approval — the Board will scrutinize whether the applicant or a prior owner created the condition. (needs-verification)

## Appeal Path

Board of Adjustment decisions are final at the local administrative level. A dissatisfied party — including neighbors who opposed the grant — may file a **special action** in Maricopa County **Superior Court**. The court reviews whether the Board acted within its jurisdiction and whether its decision is supported by substantial evidence. Verify the applicable filing deadline. (needs-verification)

## Common Pitfalls

- **Arguing financial hardship:** Economic inconvenience is not a legal basis for a variance — frame the case around physical site characteristics.
- **Self-created hardship:** If a prior owner or the applicant voluntarily created the condition (e.g., built a structure that consumed setback), the Board will likely deny on self-creation grounds.
- **Over-requesting:** Requesting more relief than is minimally necessary signals that the project should instead be redesigned; the Board may reduce or deny a variance that exceeds minimum necessity.
- **Neighbor opposition:** While the Board applies legal findings, vocal organized opposition can influence outcomes; address neighbor concerns before the hearing.
- **Incomplete findings narrative:** Each finding must be addressed with site-specific evidence — a conclusory narrative will fail.
