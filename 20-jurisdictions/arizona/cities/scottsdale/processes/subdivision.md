# Scottsdale Subdivision Platting

## Summary

Subdivision platting in Scottsdale is the process of dividing land into two or more lots, tracts, or parcels for development or sale, governed by the Arizona Subdivision Statutes (ARS Title 32 Chapter 20 and ARS Title 9 Article 6-1) and Scottsdale's local subdivision ordinance. The process involves a preliminary plat for policy and design review, followed by a final plat that is recorded and establishes legal lot boundaries, rights-of-way, easements, and improvement requirements. Subdivision approval also triggers impact fee assessment and infrastructure dedication requirements. See [[Subdivision Approval]] for the general concept.

For smaller divisions (e.g., splitting a lot into two), a lot-split or administrative plat process may be available in lieu of a full subdivision — verify thresholds against the Scottsdale Subdivision Ordinance. (needs-verification)

## Triggering Conditions

The full subdivision process is generally required when dividing land into five or more lots, or when public streets or utilities must be dedicated. Smaller splits may qualify for a simpler lot-line adjustment or administrative division process. Condominium projects undergo a separate condominium plat process under ARS Title 33. Verify the exact thresholds and available tracks in the Scottsdale Subdivision Ordinance. (needs-verification)

## Decision-Maker

**Planning Commission** reviews the preliminary plat and makes a recommendation. **City Council** typically gives final approval to both the preliminary and final plat. Staff in Planning and Development Services and the City Engineer's office review for technical compliance. ## Findings Required

Preliminary plat approval typically requires findings that: (1) the plat is consistent with the General Plan and applicable area plans; (2) the design meets all applicable subdivision ordinance standards for streets, lots, drainage, and utilities; (3) adequate public facilities are available or will be provided; and (4) the subdivision will not be detrimental to adjacent properties or the public interest. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Improvement assurance timing:** The subdivision improvement agreement and bonding requirements must be satisfied before the final plat can be recorded — this can delay project financing closings if not anticipated.
- **ESLO / hillside compliance:** Scottsdale's Environmentally Sensitive Lands Ordinance can impose significant constraints on lot layout and coverage in hillside areas — check early.
- **Impact fee calculations:** Impact fees are assessed at platting and can be substantial — model them into the land pro forma before acquiring the site.
- **Infrastructure off-site:** Scottsdale may require off-site improvements (street widening, utility extensions) as a condition of plat approval — scope these before committing.
- **Condominium plat vs. subdivision plat:** Multi-family for-sale projects need to confirm which plat type applies; mixing these up causes significant processing delays.
