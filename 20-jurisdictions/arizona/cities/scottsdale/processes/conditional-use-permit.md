# Scottsdale Conditional Use Permit

## Summary

A Conditional Use Permit (CUP) — sometimes called a Special Use Permit or Use Permit depending on Scottsdale's current ordinance terminology — authorizes a use that is listed as conditionally permitted in a base zone but requires case-by-case review to ensure it is compatible with its specific context. Unlike a rezoning, a CUP runs with specific conditions tied to the project rather than permanently changing the underlying zoning. It is a quasi-judicial process: the decision-maker must apply written findings to the facts of the application, rather than exercising broad legislative discretion. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] for the general concept.

## Triggering Conditions

A CUP is triggered when a desired use appears in the base zone's use regulations as "conditionally permitted" (or equivalent) rather than by-right. Common CUP uses in commercial and mixed-use contexts include drive-throughs, outdoor dining, entertainment venues, automobile-related services, schools, places of worship, group homes, and certain industrial uses near sensitive receptors. The specific list of conditional uses for each zone is enumerated in the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance — do not rely on generalized lists. (needs-verification)

## Findings Required

CUP findings are the crux of approvability. Although exact findings must be verified against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance, conditional use permits in Arizona municipalities commonly require that the decision-maker find: (1) the use is consistent with the General Plan and applicable policies; (2) the use will not be detrimental to the public health, safety, or welfare; (3) the use is compatible with adjacent uses and neighborhood character; (4) the site is adequate in size, shape, and location; (5) the site has adequate access and infrastructure; and (6) any proposed conditions are sufficient to mitigate impacts. ## Common Pitfalls

- **Use not actually conditionally permitted:** Confirm the use is listed in the zone's CUP column before filing — if it is not listed at all, a rezoning is required.
- **Underdeveloped findings narrative:** The applicant bears the burden of demonstrating each finding is met; a generic narrative will fail. Tie every finding to site-specific evidence.
- **Operations details matter:** For uses like entertainment venues or auto services, the decision-maker will scrutinize hours of operation, noise control, and screening — address these proactively.
- **Conditions negotiation:** Proposed conditions are often circulated in draft before the hearing; engage staff on unreasonable conditions before the record closes.
- **CUP runs with the use, not the land:** Confirm whether Scottsdale's CUP is tied to the operator or the property — transferability matters for underwriting.
