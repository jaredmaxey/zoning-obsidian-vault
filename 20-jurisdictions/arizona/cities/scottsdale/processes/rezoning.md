# Scottsdale Rezoning

## Summary

A rezoning (zone change) reclassifies a parcel or group of parcels from one base zone district to another, permanently changing the allowed uses and dimensional standards applicable to the land. In Scottsdale, rezoning is a legislative act initiated by a property owner or developer (or by the City). It requires consistency with the General Plan Future Land Use Map — an inconsistent rezoning almost always requires a concurrent [[Scottsdale General Plan Amendment]]. Scottsdale also permits planned development (PD) or planned community district rezonings that tie approval to a specific development program; these are common for larger mixed-use and master-planned projects. See [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] for the general concept.

## Triggering Conditions

A rezoning is required when a property owner wants to develop a use or at an intensity not permitted under the current base zone, and the desired outcome cannot be achieved through a conditional use permit or variance. Typical triggers include: converting a residential-designated site to commercial or mixed-use, increasing density beyond the existing zone's envelope, or moving from a lower-intensity commercial zone to a higher-intensity one. Planned development rezonings are also commonly used when a project warrants custom standards.

## Decision-Maker

**City Council** makes the final decision following a recommendation from the **Planning Commission**, which holds the initial public hearing. Both hearings are public. Because rezoning is legislative, Council has broad discretion. Some minor or administrative zone changes may be handled differently — verify the specific delegation and any Zoning Administrator authority against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance. ## Findings Required

Rezoning is legislative, so the decision-makers weigh policy criteria rather than applying strict quasi-judicial findings. Common considerations include: (1) consistency with the General Plan Future Land Use Map and applicable policies; (2) compatibility with surrounding uses and the character of the area; (3) adequacy of infrastructure capacity; (4) public health, safety, and welfare. For planned development rezonings, the approved development program becomes a condition of the zoning. Verify whether Scottsdale's ordinance enumerates specific findings or criteria. ## Appeal Path

As a legislative act, a rezoning decision by City Council is not subject to administrative appeal. A party seeking to challenge the action must file in Maricopa County **Superior Court**. Verify the applicable limitations period. (needs-verification)

## Common Pitfalls

- **General Plan inconsistency:** Applications not consistent with the FLUM designation face denial unless a GPA is filed concurrently — identify and address the GP conflict early.
- **Insufficient conceptual program:** For planned development rezonings, an under-developed site concept leads to conditions or deferrals; invest in the concept before filing.
- **Neighborhood opposition:** Scottsdale has engaged neighborhoods; early [[Neighborhood Outreach]] and design responsiveness can make or break Council votes.
- **Inadequate infrastructure analysis:** Traffic, utilities, and school capacity issues discovered late in the process create costly delays.
- **Conditions of approval:** Rezoning approvals routinely carry conditions — review the draft conditions during the process and negotiate unreasonable ones before the hearing.
