# Scottsdale Historic Preservation Overlay

## Summary

Scottsdale's historic preservation program designates individual landmarks and historic districts that are subject to additional review requirements for alterations, demolition, new construction, and changes in use. The program is administered through a **Historic Preservation Commission** (or equivalent body — verify current name and authority against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance and City Code). (needs-verification) Properties listed on the City of Scottsdale Historic Register, the Arizona State Historic Property Register, or the National Register of Historic Places may be subject to these requirements; the exact trigger for local review versus federal Section 106 consultation depends on whether public funding or permits are involved. For CRE development, historic overlay constraints most commonly arise in the Old Town/Downtown area where mid-century modern and early Arizona commercial architecture is prevalent.

## Geographic Extent

Historic overlay requirements apply to individually designated landmarks and to any locally designated historic districts. Identifying whether a specific parcel is subject to the overlay requires checking the Scottsdale Historic Property Register, available through the Planning and Development Services Department or the official GIS. The overlay boundary for any historic district follows the legally adopted district boundaries — verify current designations. (needs-verification)

## Modifications to Base Zoning

The historic preservation overlay modifies the standard development review process by adding a layer of review specifically focused on the retention of historic character-defining features. The overlay generally does not change base-zone dimensional standards (setbacks, height, FAR) independently; rather, it imposes design review and approval requirements that constrain how those standards may be met in practice. Key modifications include (verify against Scottsdale's historic preservation ordinance — needs-verification):

- **Demolition review and possible denial:** Demolition of a designated landmark or a contributing structure within a historic district requires Historic Preservation Commission review. The Commission may recommend denial or conditions, and City Council may ultimately act on a demolition application. TBD — verify exact procedure.
- **Certificate of Appropriateness:** Alterations to designated properties (exterior changes, additions, rehabilitation) typically require a Certificate of Appropriateness (COA) or equivalent approval demonstrating conformance with the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for Rehabilitation or locally adopted design guidelines — verify the current mechanism.
- **New construction within historic districts:** Infill construction within a designated historic district is typically reviewed for compatibility with the district's historic character, scale, and materials, even if the new building itself is not historic.

## Additional Requirements

Applicants proposing work on designated properties should consult the Secretary of the Interior's Standards for the Treatment of Historic Properties, which are frequently incorporated by reference in local historic preservation ordinances. Federal Historic Tax Credits (20% credit for income-producing certified historic structures) and Arizona state historic tax credits may be available for qualifying rehabilitation projects — these incentives can materially affect the economics of adaptive reuse deals involving designated properties. Consult a historic preservation architect and tax credit specialist early in the project. (needs-verification)

## Process Changes

The Certificate of Appropriateness process adds a review step — and potentially a public hearing — to work on designated properties. Timeline and process details should be verified against the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance and the Historic Preservation Commission's procedures. (needs-verification) For projects that also require standard development review or building permits, the COA must typically be obtained before or concurrently with the building permit — do not assume the building permit process alone is sufficient.
