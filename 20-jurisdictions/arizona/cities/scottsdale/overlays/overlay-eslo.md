# Scottsdale Environmentally Sensitive Lands Overlay

## Summary

The Environmentally Sensitive Lands Ordinance (ESLO) is one of Scottsdale's most consequential development regulations, applying to properties that include sensitive environmental features such as hillside slopes, ridgelines, riparian corridors, rock outcroppings, and natural desert vegetation. The ESLO overlay limits developable coverage based on slope steepness, requires native plant preservation and on-site salvage or transplanting, restricts lighting and wall heights to minimize visual and ecological impacts, and may require additional environmental studies before development can proceed. Properties in northern Scottsdale near the McDowell Mountains, the McDowell Sonoran Preserve buffer, or hillside terrain are particularly likely to carry significant ESLO constraints that materially affect project economics and design. ESLO compliance is not optional and is reviewed as part of development review. ## Geographic Extent

The ESLO applies to parcels that contain Environmental Resource Preservation areas, hillside slopes above a threshold steepness, riparian corridors, or other mapped sensitive environmental features as defined in the Scottsdale Zoning Ordinance. Determining ESLO applicability requires overlaying the parcel against Scottsdale's environmental resources map, available through Planning and Development Services or the official GIS. ESLO constraints vary in intensity depending on the specific resource category present — a parcel can have multiple categories applying simultaneously. (needs-verification)

## Additional Requirements

- **Native plant inventory and salvage:** An inventory of significant native plants (saguaro, palo verde, ironwood, etc.) is required; plants that can be salvaged must be transplanted on-site or donated to approved programs.
- **Hydrological/drainage study:** Development in or near riparian corridors requires a hydrological analysis demonstrating no adverse impact on drainage function.
- **Visual impact analysis:** Hillside projects may require viewshed analysis and renderings demonstrating that structures will not create undue visual impact from public viewpoints.
- **Lighting:** Highly restricted — full-cutoff fixtures, low mounting heights, minimized lumen output. See [[Scottsdale Lighting Standards]].
- **Walls and grading:** Retaining walls must be terraced to limit individual wall heights; colors and materials should blend with the natural landscape.

## Process Changes

ESLO projects typically require additional environmental studies as pre-application or concurrent review items. Environmental Resource Review may be required before or as part of the standard development review application. A Biotic Resources Survey or equivalent may be required in areas with significant native vegetation or potential for protected species. Projects with significant ESLO constraints should budget additional time and consultant costs compared to unconstrained urban infill sites. (needs-verification)
