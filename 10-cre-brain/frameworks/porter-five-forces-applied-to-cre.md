# Porter Five Forces Applied to CRE

## Purpose

Michael Porter's Five Forces framework, originally designed to assess industry profitability, translates naturally to commercial real estate when the "industry" is redefined as the competitive market for a specific type of space in a defined submarket. The five forces — threat of new entrants (new supply), bargaining power of buyers (tenants), bargaining power of suppliers (land/labor/capital), threat of substitutes (alternative space or locations), and competitive rivalry (existing competitive inventory) — map directly onto the structural questions that determine whether a property's income stream is durable and growing or fragile and under pressure.

Institutional analysts apply Porter's framework to CRE primarily during acquisition underwriting, asset strategy review, and market-entry screening. Its value is in adding a qualitative structural layer on top of quantitative metrics: a market may show strong current rent growth (a lagging indicator) but score poorly on all five forces (a leading indicator of future rent pressure). The framework is particularly powerful for retail and office assets, where substitution and competitive rivalry are most acute, and for any asset class where a large pipeline threatens the pricing power of existing owners.

## Inputs Required

- **Asset class and submarket:** the defined competitive set for the subject property (per [[Submarket Definition and Selection]]).
- **Supply pipeline:** new entrants delivering into the submarket; from [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]].
- **Barriers to entry:** land scarcity, entitlement complexity, construction cost versus replacement value, zoning constraints.
- **Tenant base:** lease terms, tenant credit quality, lease expiration schedule, tenant concentration.
- **Substitute space:** availability of functionally similar space in adjacent submarkets, alternative asset types, or remote/digital alternatives (especially for office and retail).
- **NOI composition:** fixed versus variable lease income, embedded rent escalators, expense recovery structures.

## Method

1. **Force 1 — Threat of New Entrants (New Supply).** Assess how easily new competitive supply can be delivered into the submarket. High barriers (scarce land, difficult entitlement, high replacement cost, long construction timelines) make new supply slow and limited — a favorable structural condition for existing owners. Low barriers (abundant entitled land, streamlined permitting, efficient construction methods) mean any demand uptick will attract competing supply within 18–36 months.

   Rate: **Low / Moderate / High threat.** For each qualifier note whether barriers are structural (geography, zoning) or cyclical (current credit cost).

2. **Force 2 — Bargaining Power of Tenants (Buyers).** Assess how much leverage tenants have to push rents down or extract concessions. Factors favoring tenant power: high vacancy alternatives in the submarket, commodity space that is easily replicated elsewhere, short lease terms, and large tenants that anchor entire buildings. Factors favoring landlord power: low vacancy, unique physical attributes (last-mile locations, specialized infrastructure), long weighted-average lease term (WALT), and mission-critical space for the tenant.

   Evaluate tenant concentration risk: if the top two tenants represent >40% of revenue (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24), tenant bargaining power is a concentration risk, not just a structural force.

3. **Force 3 — Bargaining Power of Suppliers (Land, Labor, Capital).** In CRE, suppliers are land sellers, construction contractors, and capital providers. High contractor demand (busy construction cycle) raises hard costs; limited entitled land raises land prices; tight debt markets raise financing costs. These forces directly affect the cost of new supply (Force 1) and therefore the replacement cost floor under existing asset values.

   In the operating phase, supplier power manifests as operating expense inflation: utility costs, property management fees, insurance premiums, and property tax assessments. Track the trend in each OpEx line against rent growth — if expenses inflate faster than rents, NOI margin compresses structurally.

4. **Force 4 — Threat of Substitutes.** Identify space types or locations that tenants could use instead of the subject property or its competitive set. For retail, substitutes include e-commerce fulfillment, other shopping centers, and experience venues. For office, substitutes include suburban campuses, coworking, and permanent remote work. For industrial, substitutes include owner-occupied facilities and offshore warehouse consolidation. For multifamily, substitutes are single-family rentals and homeownership (rate-sensitive).

   Rate based on availability, switching cost, and current adoption rates. The higher the substitution pressure, the more structural the demand risk — this is distinct from cyclical vacancy, which recovers; structural substitution may not.

5. **Force 5 — Competitive Rivalry (Existing Inventory).** Examine the existing competitive inventory within the submarket. A submarket with many similarly-aged, similarly-amenitized properties competing for the same tenant pool creates intense rivalry and compresses rents toward marginal cost. Differentiating factors that reduce rivalry: superior location, unique physical attributes (floor plates, ceiling height, parking ratio), recent capital investment, and proximity to demand generators.

   Map the competitive set. Identify what percentage of inventory is direct competition versus differentiated enough to compete in a different tier.

6. **Synthesize into a structural assessment.** Rate each force Low / Moderate / High threat and write a one-sentence rationale. Then synthesize into an overall structural position: **Strong** (majority of forces are low-threat — durable income, pricing power), **Moderate** (mixed forces — income is defensible but requires active management), or **Weak** (majority of forces are high-threat — income at risk, caution warranted).

7. **Translate to underwriting implications.** Strong structural position supports aggressive NOI growth assumptions and cap rate compression in [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]]; Weak structural position warrants flat or declining NOI and cap rate expansion stress in [[Sensitivity Analysis]].

## Outputs

- A five-force rating table (Low/Moderate/High for each force).
- An overall structural position (Strong / Moderate / Weak) with supporting rationale.
- A qualitative positioning memo identifying the two or three forces most critical to NOI durability for the specific asset.
- Underwriting implication flags: where to stress-test assumptions based on identified structural weaknesses.

## Example Walkthrough

*All figures below are illustrative/hypothetical.*

**Asset:** Class A last-mile industrial facility, 250,000 SF, infill submarket of a Sun Belt metro.

| Force | Rating | Rationale |
|---|---|---|
| New Supply Threat | Low | Submarket is fully developed; remaining land is constrained by highway/rail. Entitlement 2+ years. Replacement cost $175/SF versus existing transactions at $210/SF. |
| Tenant Bargaining Power | Low-Moderate | Single tenant on a 10-year NNN lease with 3% annual escalators; strong credit (investment grade). However, single-tenant concentration is a risk at lease expiration. |
| Supplier Power | Moderate | Construction costs elevated (2024–2026 cycle); any redevelopment/expansion would face high hard costs, which supports existing asset value. |
| Substitute Threat | Low | Last-mile location critical to 2-hour delivery radius; no functionally equivalent sites available in the submarket. |
| Competitive Rivalry | Low | Only 3 comparable last-mile facilities in submarket; all are 95%+ occupied. |

**Overall: Strong structural position.** The asset benefits from high barriers to new supply, minimal substitution threat, and low competitive rivalry. The primary risk is tenant renewal risk at Year 10 — underwriting should include a lease-up scenario.

**Underwriting implication:** underwrite going-in cap rate at market (illustratively 5.25%) with modest cap rate compression to 5.0% by Year 5 given structural demand. Stress test a lease-up scenario at Year 10 with 12 months of vacancy and $45/SF leasing cost (illustrative).

## Limitations

Porter Five Forces is a qualitative framework — it does not produce a number. Two analysts may reasonably disagree on ratings, particularly for "Moderate" assessments. The framework benefits from being paired with quantitative market metrics from [[Absorption Modeling]], [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]], and [[Demand Driver Analysis]] rather than used in isolation.

The framework is better suited to market-level analysis than individual asset analysis. A single well-located asset within a weak structural market can still be a good investment. Conversely, a mediocre asset in a strong structural market can underperform if its specific competitive positioning is poor. Always supplement with asset-level [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] and [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] analysis.

The framework does not capture macro forces (interest rates, credit availability, regulatory changes) that can override structural fundamentals. A market with excellent Five Forces scores can still underperform in a rising-rate environment where cap rate expansion overrides NOI growth.
