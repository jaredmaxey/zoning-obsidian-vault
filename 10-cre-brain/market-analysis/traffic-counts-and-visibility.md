# Traffic Counts and Visibility

## Purpose

Traffic counts and visibility analysis evaluates the quantity and quality of vehicular exposure a commercial site receives, translating roadway traffic volume and site physical characteristics into a judgment about the site's suitability for traffic-dependent commercial uses. Many retail, restaurant, quick-service restaurant (QSR), convenience, gas station/C-store, car wash, hotel, and drive-through financial uses are fundamentally traffic-driven businesses: the volume of vehicles passing the site on a daily basis is one of the single strongest predictors of sales performance for these categories. A site with 40,000 Average Annual Daily Traffic (AADT) on its primary road typically supports a different and broader range of uses than a site with 10,000 AADT, all else equal.

Traffic analysis does more than count vehicles: it assesses visibility (can a driver see the site and its signage from the road?), access (can a driver safely and conveniently enter and exit?), and traffic quality (is the count driven by commuters passing at highway speed, or by local residents shopping and eating?). These qualitative factors can make a high-count site perform below expectations or a moderate-count site perform above expectations. Analysts reach for this framework when evaluating retail or hospitality ground leases, convenience retail pad sites, net-lease QSR investments, and development feasibility for any use where a national or regional tenant will conduct their own traffic study before committing. The output feeds [[Site Selection Scorecard]] and [[Trade Area Analysis (Retail)]].

## Inputs Required

- **AADT counts:** Annual Average Daily Traffic from the state Department of Transportation (DOT) or local Metropolitan Planning Organization (MPO). Most states publish interactive traffic count maps online; counts are typically updated on a rotating 2–5 year schedule for major roads. Data is available at no cost from state DOT websites or the Federal Highway Administration (FHWA) Highway Performance Monitoring System (HPMS).
- **Intersection turning movement counts (TMC):** Available from local MPO or city traffic engineering departments; shows peak-hour directional flows at intersections, important for evaluating channelization (left-turn pockets, dedicated turn lanes) and site access safety.
- **Site visibility assessment:** Field observation or aerial photography (Google Maps, state DOT aerial) to evaluate sight lines from the road, distance at which the site is first visible to approaching drivers, obstructions (vegetation, grade changes, building setbacks), and signage pylon opportunity.
- **Ingress/egress configuration:** Number and type of access points, shared vs. dedicated access, distance from nearest signalized intersection, presence of median cuts, U-turn opportunities, and whether right-in/right-out limitations apply.
- **Road classification and speed limit:** Federal Functional Classification (principal arterial, minor arterial, collector, local) and posted speed limit. Higher speed limits reduce effective "windows of opportunity" for drivers to notice and enter a site.
- **Planned roadway improvements:** State DOT Statewide Transportation Improvement Program (STIP) and local MPO Transportation Improvement Program (TIP) for planned widening, new interchange, or access management projects that could materially change traffic patterns.

## Method

1. **Identify the primary and cross-street road segments.** Determine which road(s) provide primary vehicular access to the site and which cross-streets form the nearest intersection. Typically the primary road is the one providing the most direct access and exposure.

2. **Pull AADT counts for all relevant road segments.** Download or look up counts from the state DOT traffic count map for the segment(s) immediately adjacent to the site and for 1–2 segments in each direction. Note the count year; if counts are more than 5 years old, apply a trend adjustment using the MPO's modeled growth rate for the corridor.

3. **Assess traffic composition.** Where truck percentage or transit usage data is available (from DOT or MPO), note whether the count is primarily passenger vehicle (retail-relevant) or heavy commercial (less relevant for retail, more relevant for truck-stop or industrial uses).

4. **Evaluate visibility.** Conduct (or require) a field observation at typical operating hours. Score visibility on three dimensions: (a) distance from which the site is visible to approaching drivers (150+ feet = good, < 75 feet = poor), (b) availability of signage opportunity (monument sign, pylon, building-mounted), and (c) obstructions (grade change, sight-distance restriction, heavy landscaping). Note whether signage permits are available.

5. **Evaluate access configuration.** Identify the number of access points, their type (full access, right-in/right-out, shared), and proximity to the nearest signalized intersection. A site with one right-in/right-out access 500 feet from the nearest signal performs meaningfully worse than one with a full-access driveway at a signalized intersection, even at the same AADT.

6. **Benchmark against use-type thresholds.** Compare the site's AADT to minimum traffic count thresholds published or commonly cited for the contemplated use type. Note: all thresholds vary by market, brand, and trade area demographics; these are common ranges as of 2026-05-24 but vary by market and vintage:
   - Convenience store/C-store: typically seeks 15,000–30,000+ AADT on primary road
   - QSR drive-through: typically seeks 25,000–45,000+ AADT
   - Fast-casual restaurant: typically seeks 20,000–35,000+ AADT
   - Regional hotel (branded): varies widely; often 30,000–60,000+ AADT for freeway adjacent
   - Neighborhood/community retail center: typically 20,000–40,000 AADT on primary road
   These ranges vary significantly by brand, market size, income, and competitive density.

7. **Assess planned infrastructure changes.** Review the state STIP and local TIP for any planned changes to the road network that could increase or decrease traffic volume, or materially change access configuration, within the next 5 years.

8. **Summarize the traffic and visibility profile.** Produce a one-page summary with the AADT table, a visibility/access quality rating, the use-type benchmark comparison, and a narrative conclusion about whether the site meets the traffic and visibility thresholds for the contemplated use.

## Outputs

The primary output is a **traffic count summary table** showing AADT for the primary road and all relevant cross-streets, with count year noted. This is accompanied by a **visibility and access quality rating** (scored or qualitative), a **use-type benchmark comparison**, and a **narrative conclusion** (1–2 paragraphs) addressing whether the site's traffic and visibility profile supports the intended use. Red flags (access limitations, sight-distance restrictions, near-term construction disruption) and upside factors (planned road improvement, traffic growth trend) are called out explicitly.

## Example Walkthrough

The following numbers are illustrative and hypothetical, labeled as such throughout.

Suppose the subject is a hypothetical 1.2-acre pad site at the corner of a primary arterial and a secondary collector road in a suburban trade area. The analyst pulls the state DOT traffic count map and finds a hypothetical AADT of 38,000 on the primary arterial and 12,000 on the cross street (both hypothetical; varies by market and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24). A field observation confirms strong visibility: the site is at a signalized intersection, the pad is elevated slightly above road grade with a clear monument sign easement. Access is full-access via the cross street and right-in/right-out on the arterial. The analyst benchmarks the AADT against typical QSR drive-through thresholds (25,000–45,000) and community pad-site retail thresholds and concludes the site exceeds minimum thresholds for QSR and fast-casual. An MPO TIP review reveals a planned arterial widening from 4 to 6 lanes beginning in 3 years, which is expected to add 10–15% to AADT upon completion — a modest upside factor. No access limitations or sight-distance restrictions are identified.

## Limitations

AADT counts reflect all vehicles passing the site, but not all vehicles are equally relevant to a specific use. A count driven primarily by through-traffic at highway speeds (interstate frontage road) may be less valuable than a lower count on a busy local shopping street where drivers are already in shopping/dining mode. Traffic counts also change with land use changes, new interchange construction, and road closures. Counts older than 5 years should be treated with caution in fast-growing or fast-changing corridors. Visibility and access quality are inherently subjective; different analysts may score the same site differently. This framework is most reliable for use types where the tenant or operator has established traffic threshold criteria — for more complex or experience-driven retail (boutique, entertainment, destination dining), traffic alone is a poor predictor of performance.
