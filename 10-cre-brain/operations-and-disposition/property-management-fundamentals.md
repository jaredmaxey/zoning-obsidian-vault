# Property Management Fundamentals

## Definition

Property management is the day-to-day operational stewardship of a real estate asset on behalf of its ownership. A property manager's mandate spans four domains: physical building operations (maintenance, janitorial, vendor contracts, life-safety systems), tenant relations (leasing, move-ins/move-outs, service requests, lease enforcement), financial reporting (rent collection, accounts payable, monthly operating statements), and compliance (building codes, ADA, environmental obligations, local licensing). The discipline exists at the intersection of hospitality—tenants are customers—and disciplined expense management, because every controllable dollar saved flows directly to [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]].

Property management is typically delivered either in-house by an owner's operating platform or outsourced to a third-party firm under a property management agreement. Third-party fees vary by asset class and market but generally run in the range of 3–8% of collected effective gross income for most commercial and multifamily assets, with lower percentages applying to larger portfolios and higher percentages to smaller or more operationally intensive properties (current as of 2026-05-24; varies by market and vintage). Fee structures may be flat monthly fees, percentage-of-collections, or hybrid models.

The property manager is the owner's eyes and ears on the ground. Their decisions directly affect tenant satisfaction and retention, physical asset condition (and thus future capital needs), and the reliability of the revenue stream. A well-run property will show low vacancy, collected rents that match scheduled rents, and expense ratios in line with underwriting benchmarks, whereas a poorly run property will exhibit chronic deferred maintenance, higher-than-market [[Vacancy and Collection Loss]], and surprises at disposition.

## When It's Used

Property management is active from the day a property is acquired or placed into service through disposition. It becomes especially consequential during lease-up of a new or repositioned asset, when tenant retention is the primary value-protection lever. Asset managers and equity sponsors hire or retain property managers as the first operational decision after closing; the selection of a capable PM firm is often a precondition to lender approval on agency and CMBS financings. Investors reviewing an asset's operational performance during due diligence will scrutinize property management quality through trailing income statements, delinquency reports, and maintenance logs. Brokers marketing an asset for sale will present the PM platform as part of the investment thesis, since a takeable in-place management structure reduces friction for buyers.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The scope of "property management" shifts materially by asset class. In multifamily, it dominates day-to-day operations—unit turns, amenity management, online reviews, and rent collection are intensely labor-driven. In single-tenant net-lease retail or industrial, the tenant handles most building maintenance and the PM role is largely administrative (rent collection, CAM reconciliations, insurance tracking). In office and multi-tenant retail, PM is complex because of common-area cost recovery, tenant improvement coordination, and the need for active relationship management across a larger tenant roster. Hospitality asset management is sometimes conflated with property management but involves a separate franchise/brand relationship and revenue management function that exceeds typical PM scope.

Some owners blur the distinction between property management and [[Asset Management vs. Property Management|asset management]], especially vertically integrated operators. In those platforms, the "property manager" may also set leasing strategy and approve capital decisions that would normally rest with an asset manager. Understanding which role owns which decisions is critical before assigning accountability for performance shortfalls.

## Common Mistakes

The most common error is treating property management as purely a cost center rather than a NOI-protection function—cutting PM fees by hiring the cheapest vendor often results in deferred maintenance, higher vacancy, and tenant non-renewals that cost far more than the fee savings. Owners also frequently fail to establish clear performance benchmarks (occupancy floor, delinquency ceiling, expense ratio target) in the property management agreement, making it impossible to hold the PM firm accountable.

A second frequent mistake is under-resourcing the leasing function within property management. In markets with elevated vacancy, the PM firm's ability to attract and close new tenants is as important as expense control, yet many contracts treat leasing fees separately and do not incentivize the PM team to prioritize it. Finally, owners sometimes ignore the [[Operating Expense Ratio]] trend over time, missing early warning signs of cost creep or vendor fraud that compound before disposition.
