# 1031 Exchange Overview

## Definition

A 1031 exchange (also called a "like-kind exchange" or "Starker exchange") is a provision under Section 1031 of the Internal Revenue Code that allows an owner of investment real property to defer federal income taxes on the gain realized from a sale, provided the net sale proceeds are reinvested into qualifying "like-kind" replacement property within strict statutory timelines. The tax deferral applies to both capital gains tax (which for real property held longer than one year applies at the long-term capital gains rate, currently 20% federal for high-income taxpayers plus applicable state taxes) and depreciation recapture (25% federal on accumulated depreciation). For a property with a low cost basis—the result of years of depreciation deductions—a forced taxable sale can absorb 30–40% of gross proceeds before the investor can reinvest, making a 1031 exchange a powerful wealth-preservation tool.

The mechanics of a 1031 exchange require strict compliance with IRS rules. The **45-day identification window** begins on the date the relinquished property closes: the exchanger must formally identify (in writing, delivered to the qualified intermediary) one or more potential replacement properties within exactly 45 calendar days. The **180-day exchange period** is the outer deadline by which the replacement property must actually close—again measured from the relinquished property's close date. Both deadlines are absolute and statutory; missing either deadline, even by one day, disqualifies the exchange and the deferred gain becomes immediately taxable. Extensions are not available for ordinary circumstances, though disaster-area relief has historically been provided by IRS notice in certain declared disasters.

The use of a **Qualified Intermediary (QI)** is mandatory—the exchanger cannot directly receive sale proceeds between the relinquished and replacement property closings. The QI holds the proceeds in escrow, disburses them to fund the replacement property acquisition, and coordinates the exchange documentation. Choosing a financially secure QI is critical; QI insolvency (which has occurred historically) can result in loss of exchange funds and an unintended taxable event with no tax funds in hand to pay the liability.

## When It's Used

The 1031 exchange is used whenever a real estate investor sells a property with a gain and wants to continue investing in real estate rather than taking a taxable distribution. It is most powerful for: (1) long-hold investors with heavily depreciated low-basis assets, where the deferred tax liability is largest as a percentage of proceeds; (2) investors upgrading from smaller to larger properties ("trading up") who need to deploy all after-tax proceeds to acquire a larger asset; and (3) investors consolidating multiple smaller properties into a single larger asset or geographic rebalancing within a portfolio.

The 1031 exchange interacts directly with [[Hold vs. Sell Analysis]]: a taxable sale may produce net after-tax proceeds insufficient to justify selling over holding, while a 1031 exchange (which defers the tax) may make the economics of selling and reinvesting favorable. The decision must always be modeled on an after-tax basis for private investors.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Boot**: Any proceeds not reinvested into like-kind replacement property—either cash received or net debt relief (selling a $5M property with a $2M loan and buying a $4M property with a $1M loan produces $1M of "mortgage boot")—are taxable as "boot" in the year of the exchange, even if the rest of the gain is deferred. Boot rules require careful structuring: the replacement property's total acquisition price (not just equity) must equal or exceed the relinquished property's total sale price to defer all gain.

**Delaware Statutory Trust (DST)**: A fractional ownership structure that qualifies as like-kind replacement property for 1031 purposes, enabling an exchanger to invest in a professionally managed institutional property without the operational responsibilities of direct ownership. DSTs are popular with investors seeking to exit active management while maintaining 1031 deferral, though they have illiquid, fixed-term structures and limited investor rights.

**Reverse exchange**: A structure where the replacement property is acquired before the relinquished property is sold, using an "exchange accommodation titleholder" to hold one property while the other is marketed. Reverse exchanges are more complex and expensive than forward exchanges, require IRS safe-harbor compliance (Rev. Proc. 2000-37), and are limited to a 180-day window from the parking arrangement to the sale of the relinquished property.

**TIC (Tenancy-in-Common)**: Co-ownership structures that qualify as like-kind property. IRS Revenue Procedure 2002-22 governs when a TIC will be respected as like-kind rather than a partnership interest (which does not qualify). TICs were common before DSTs became the preferred co-ownership vehicle; they are now less frequently used for new exchanges.

## Common Mistakes

The most frequently fatal mistake is missing the 45-day identification deadline because the exchanger underestimates the difficulty of locating replacement property in a competitive market. The 45-day window is extremely tight in a seller's market where desirable properties receive multiple competing offers; exchanges must begin property identification before or concurrent with selling the relinquished property. A common mitigation strategy is to identify the maximum allowable number of replacement properties (3 under the "3-property rule," or more under the "200% rule") to preserve optionality.

A second common error is failing to properly title the replacement property acquisition—if the exchanger takes title in a name or entity different from the relinquished property seller, the IRS may disqualify the exchange on the basis that the taxpayer is different. Entity structuring for 1031 purposes requires legal counsel familiar with both tax and real estate law. Investors also frequently underestimate the "taxable gain deferral compounding" benefit—the deferred tax is effectively an interest-free loan from the government that earns returns if invested, making the 1031 a permanent benefit (not merely a deferral) if exchanges are chained through an investor's lifetime and the asset is held until death (step-up in basis rules).
