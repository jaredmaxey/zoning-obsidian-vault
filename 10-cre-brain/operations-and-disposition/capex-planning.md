# CapEx Planning

## Definition

Capital expenditure (CapEx) planning is the asset-management function of identifying, budgeting, sequencing, and funding capital improvements to a property over a defined investment horizon, typically 1–10 years. CapEx falls into two broad categories. **Maintenance CapEx** (also called "capital replacements" or "deferred maintenance remediation") addresses items that wear out over time—roofs, HVAC systems, parking lot resurfacing, elevator modernization, plumbing infrastructure, exterior painting—and is necessary to preserve the asset's physical condition and prevent accelerating deterioration. **Value-add CapEx** includes discretionary improvements that are expected to generate incremental revenue (unit renovations, amenity upgrades, lobby modernization, technology infrastructure) and is the primary driver of value creation in repositioning strategies.

A rigorous CapEx plan begins with a property condition assessment (PCA) at acquisition, which is a third-party engineering inspection that catalogs the physical condition of major building systems and estimates remaining useful life and replacement cost for each. The PCA output feeds a multi-year CapEx schedule that, when combined with revenue and expense projections, determines how much equity the deal will consume during the hold period beyond the initial purchase price. Reserve levels for replacement are typically modeled at $150–$400 per unit per year for multifamily (varies by vintage, unit type, and market; current as of 2026-05-24) and as a percentage of replacement cost for commercial properties, but the PCA should drive actual budgeting rather than generic benchmarks; see [[Reserves for Replacement]].

CapEx planning is the connective tissue between operations and investment strategy. Because capital expenditures are excluded from [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] (they are below-the-line in the operating statement), owners sometimes underestimate their true drag on returns. A thorough CapEx plan makes the capital commitment visible and ensures it is reflected in equity multiple and [[Internal Rate of Return (IRR)]] calculations.

## When It's Used

CapEx planning is performed initially during acquisition underwriting (to assess the total cost to execute the business plan) and then maintained as a living document throughout the hold period. It is revisited at least annually during budget preparation and whenever a significant change in strategy occurs (accelerating the renovation program, deciding to sell early, or responding to a major unplanned system failure). Lenders, especially bridge and construction lenders, will often require a capital budget as part of the loan draw schedule; agency lenders require a funded capital reserve account at closing for any identified deferred maintenance items. At disposition, the remaining CapEx schedule becomes part of the seller's disclosure and buyer's due diligence, affecting pricing and the negotiation of repair credits.

Value-add sponsors rely on CapEx planning as the core of their investment thesis. The underwritten renovation cost per unit, renovation timeline, and resulting rent premium drive the model's returns. If per-unit renovation costs run 15–20% over budget or the rent premium is below the underwritten assumption, returns compress materially—making CapEx execution one of the highest-risk elements of value-add strategy.

## Variations & Edge Cases

CapEx classification—whether an item is expensed (above-the-line operating expense) or capitalized (below-the-line CapEx)—affects both the NOI calculation and tax treatment. Under GAAP and IRS rules, expenditures that extend the useful life of an asset or add new functionality are capitalized; routine repairs and maintenance are expensed. In practice, owners apply a materiality threshold (often $2,500–$10,000 per item, varying by entity accounting policy) below which items are expensed regardless of their nature. Sophisticated buyers during due diligence will normalize the seller's presentation by reclassifying any improperly expensed capital items back above the line (which reduces NOI) or capitalizing items that were incorrectly expensed—a component of [[Expense Normalization]].

In value-add multifamily, the distinction between "unit renovation CapEx" and "building systems CapEx" is critical for return attribution. Unit renovation is discretionary, drives rent bumps, and is funded from the acquisition equity budget; building systems replacement is non-discretionary, does not increase rent, and must be funded from reserves or additional equity calls. Owners who commingle these buckets in their reporting often understate the true cost of the value-add program.

## Common Mistakes

The most common CapEx planning error is underestimating scope at acquisition—accepting a PCA that identifies only "critical" items (those failing immediately) while ignoring "near-term" and "deferred" items that will become critical within the hold period. Sophisticated buyers treat the PCA as a starting point and add a contingency buffer of 10–20% for unknown conditions (asbestos abatement, lead paint, structural surprises) that PCAs routinely miss.

A second recurring mistake is failing to sequence CapEx to match the leasing program. In a multifamily value-add, renovating units before the leasing team has demonstrated that the rent premium is achievable in the market—what practitioners call "proving out the renovation thesis"—risks over-investing before confirming demand. Similarly, completing common-area upgrades before unit renovations is often a mistake, because the amenity premium cannot be captured until units are also upgraded. Getting the sequencing wrong delays return realization and strains equity capital.
