# Lease Administration

## Definition

Lease administration is the systematic process of abstracting, tracking, and enforcing the rights and obligations created by every executed lease in a property's tenancy roster. While [[Rent Roll Maintenance]] captures the high-level occupancy snapshot, lease administration goes deeper: it is the ongoing stewardship of each lease document's provisions—rent escalations (CPI adjustments, fixed bumps, percentage rent), option rights (renewal options, expansion rights, rights of first refusal), critical dates (lease expirations, option exercise windows, rent commencement), tenant improvement allowance (TIA) disbursement schedules, and landlord/tenant default and cure procedures. In commercial properties, lease administration also encompasses CAM (common area maintenance) billing, reconciliation, and audit rights.

A well-run lease administration function creates a lease abstract for every executed lease: a standardized summary that distills the key economic and legal terms into a reference document the operations team can act on without re-reading the full lease every time. Abstracts are typically maintained in a property management software platform (Yardi, MRI, RealPage, Entrata, etc.) and reviewed at least quarterly for upcoming critical dates. Missing an option exercise window—which can be as short as 30–180 days—can be legally fatal: the tenant may lose their renewal right or, conversely, the landlord may lose the right to recapture space.

The financial stakes of lease administration are high. Missed rent escalations directly suppress [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] and, at a 5% cap rate, every $10,000 in annual NOI represents $200,000 in asset value. Sloppy CAM reconciliation on a multi-tenant retail or office property can result in under- or overbilling tenants, disputes, credit loss, and reputational damage with long-term tenants whose goodwill is a meaningful retention factor.

## When It's Used

Lease administration is an always-on function for any property with executed leases. It becomes especially critical at three junctures. First, at acquisition: the buyer's due diligence team should audit every lease against the rent roll and identify discrepancies, undisclosed options, and encumbrances (co-tenancy clauses, exclusives, kick-out provisions) that could impair income or limit leasing flexibility after close. Second, during annual operating cycles: CAM reconciliations for the prior year are typically completed in Q1 of the following year and must be delivered to tenants within the time frame specified in each lease (often 90–120 days after year-end). Third, at renewal and re-leasing: the lease admin function must surface expiration dates and option windows 12–24 months in advance so leasing strategy can be set proactively.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Lease administration complexity scales sharply with asset class. **Multifamily** leases are standardized, short-term (12-month), and relatively simple—most lease admin work is tracking concessions (free rent, move-in specials) and ensuring rent escalations are applied correctly at renewal. **Office and retail** leases are highly negotiated, long-term (5–15 years), and laden with economic provisions (TI packages, free-rent periods, base-year expense stops, CPI escalations, co-tenancy clauses) that require careful abstraction and ongoing monitoring. **Industrial** leases are typically NNN (triple-net), shifting most operating expense responsibility to the tenant, but still require tracking CAM and insurance cost recovery, especially in multi-tenant flex buildings.

A notable edge case is **co-tenancy clauses** common in retail leases: a tenant's rent obligation (or right to terminate) may be contingent on an anchor tenant remaining open. If the anchor closes, lease admin must immediately identify all co-tenancy-affected leases, calculate the rent impact, and notify the asset manager. Similarly, **percentage rent** provisions in retail leases require the lease admin team to collect and audit annual gross-sales reports from tenants, a function that is easy to neglect and difficult to recover retroactively.

## Common Mistakes

The most common and costly mistake is failing to calendar option exercise windows accurately—both the window open date and the deadline. Renewal options, purchase options, and rights of first refusal are frequently buried in lease amendments and side letters rather than the main lease document, and they are missed when the abstract is prepared from the main lease only. A comprehensive lease administration system requires abstracting every amendment and side letter, not just the base lease.

A second pervasive error is allowing CAM reconciliation to slip past contractual deadlines. Some leases provide that if the landlord fails to deliver the reconciliation within the stated period, the landlord waives the right to collect additional amounts for that year. Owners also routinely fail to audit tenant-controlled CAM audits—many office and retail leases give tenants the right to audit CAM charges, and sophisticated tenants exercise this right; landlords should maintain clean documentation and consistent methodology to defend against challenges.
