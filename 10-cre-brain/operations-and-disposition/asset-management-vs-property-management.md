# Asset Management vs. Property Management

## Definition

Asset management (AM) and property management (PM) are two distinct organizational roles in the CRE ownership hierarchy, and conflating them is a persistent source of confusion in the industry. **Property management** is the execution layer: collecting rent, managing vendors, maintaining the physical plant, enforcing leases, and producing monthly financials. **Asset management** is the strategic layer: it sets investment objectives, directs leasing strategy, approves capital expenditures, manages lender and investor reporting, and decides when to hold, refinance, or sell. The asset manager answers to the fund or ownership entity; the property manager answers to the asset manager (or directly to the owner if no separate AM layer exists).

The AM role is most visible in institutional and fund-sponsored ownership structures. A sponsor managing a value-add multifamily portfolio, for example, will have asset managers overseeing each property's business plan—setting rent targets, approving renovation scopes, tracking returns versus underwriting—while a property management firm (in-house or third-party) handles the operational execution. Asset management fees for institutional platforms typically range from 0.5–2.0% of equity or gross asset value (varies by market, strategy, and fund structure; current as of 2026-05-24), though fee conventions differ from PM fees, which are usually percentage-of-revenue.

In smaller or private ownership structures, a single principal may perform both AM and PM functions informally, or a PM firm may be given broader discretion that bleeds into asset management territory (e.g., approving leasing concessions or recommending capital projects). This blurring can work in simpler situations but creates accountability gaps in complex portfolios where investment performance deviates from plan.

## When It's Used

The AM/PM distinction becomes critical in three contexts. First, at acquisition: the asset manager writes or reviews the business plan and underwrites the deal, then delegates operational execution to the PM team. Second, during the hold period: the asset manager reviews PM-produced reports, identifies variances from plan, and makes corrective decisions (lease-up incentives, capital reallocation, lender modifications). Third, at disposition: the asset manager leads the sale or refinance decision, coordinates broker selection, and manages the disposition process—functions entirely outside the PM scope. Understanding who is responsible for each function also matters for lender and investor reporting, since lenders typically want AM-level certifications on major decisions (lease approvals above threshold, capital draws, financial covenants) rather than PM-level sign-offs.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The scope of each role varies considerably by ownership type. **Owner-operators** (vertically integrated platforms like large apartment REITs) often do not distinguish AM from PM organizationally—regional managers perform both functions. **Institutional fund managers** (private equity real estate, open-end funds) keep AM and PM strictly separated, sometimes outsourcing PM entirely to third parties while maintaining a lean AM layer internally. **Family offices and private owners** often have an informal "owner as asset manager" structure with a third-party PM, which can work well if the owner is engaged and financially literate.

There is also an ongoing industry debate about where "leasing" belongs—some treat it as a PM function (especially in multifamily, where on-site staff lease units), while others treat it as an AM/ownership function (especially in office and retail, where the AM team sets leasing strategy, selects brokers, and approves LOIs). The answer usually depends on deal complexity and the capability of the PM platform.

## Common Mistakes

The most damaging mistake is allowing the PM firm to make strategic decisions—approving below-market leases, deferring major capital indefinitely, or accepting concessions that impair the asset's long-term value—because there is no active asset manager directing strategy. The PM's incentive is to keep tenants happy and retain the management contract; the AM's incentive is to maximize risk-adjusted return. These incentives differ, and without AM oversight the asset drifts toward occupancy stability at the expense of value creation.

A second common error is failing to define the decision authority matrix in the property management agreement. Without written thresholds (e.g., AM approval required for any lease above $X or capital expenditure above $Y), the PM may act on operational instinct rather than investment strategy. Additionally, owners sometimes conflate PM-produced operating statements with AM-level performance reporting—the PM statement shows income and expenses; the AM report should also show business-plan variance, capital position, debt metrics, and projected returns versus underwriting.
