# Bank Debt

## Definition

Commercial banks are the most broadly active lenders in CRE, covering a wide range of product types from small community bank construction loans to large money-center bank balance-sheet permanent mortgages. Unlike life companies (which focus on permanent portfolio mortgages) or the agencies (multifamily permanent only), banks serve the full spectrum of CRE financing needs — construction, bridge, permanent, land, and specialty — and are present in markets of every size. Banks hold CRE loans on their own balance sheets rather than securitizing, meaning the lender-borrower relationship is direct and the lender retains ongoing credit exposure to the loan's performance.

Bank CRE lending is significantly regulated. Domestic commercial banks are subject to capital reserve requirements under Basel III/IV frameworks, and federal and state banking regulators have implemented Concentration Guidance that limits CRE loans (particularly construction and development loans) as a percentage of bank capital to reduce systemic risk. These concentration limits — typically CRE loans should not exceed 300% of risk-based capital, and construction/land development loans should not exceed 100% — constrain bank CRE lending capacity, particularly at community and regional banks that have historically been significant construction lenders. When banks approach their regulatory limits, they pull back from CRE origination regardless of deal quality, creating cyclical scarcity in bank credit supply.

Bank CRE loans are typically floating-rate (indexed to SOFR or a prime rate equivalent, plus a spread) for construction and bridge products, though banks also originate fixed-rate portfolio permanent loans (often swapped to fixed using an interest rate swap arranged with the bank's derivatives desk). Pricing spreads for bank construction and bridge loans tend to run somewhat below institutional debt fund pricing for comparable transactions, reflecting the bank's lower cost of funds from deposits, though banks' regulatory constraints and internal credit policies can offset this advantage.

## When It's Used

Community and regional banks dominate construction lending below approximately $25M–$50M, particularly for local and regional developers with existing bank relationships. The relationship component is critical: banks extend credit to borrowers they know, track record they can verify, and markets where they have existing knowledge. Cold-approaching a community bank for a large construction loan without an existing relationship is rarely competitive; these lenders are relationship-driven, and borrowers with long track records and deposit relationships have materially better access and pricing.

Larger regional and money-center banks originate both construction and permanent balance-sheet loans for institutional borrowers. For a core institutional investor or large developer, a bank relationship can provide construction financing, bridge financing, and a pathway to permanent portfolio loan — creating a "cradle to grave" relationship that is attractive for both parties. Bank permanent loans are particularly useful for assets that require flexibility — lease modifications, partial releases, property improvements — that would be cumbersome to obtain from a CMBS servicer.

Bank debt is also the primary provider of CRE revolving credit facilities and unsecured lines of credit to REITs and large real estate operating companies. These facilities — unsecured revolvers backed by unencumbered asset pools — function as general corporate credit rather than property-specific mortgage financing and are a critical liquidity tool for large real estate platforms.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Banks draw a meaningful internal distinction between "recourse" and "non-recourse" CRE loans. Construction loans are almost universally full recourse to the development entity and typically require personal guarantees from principals for both completion and repayment. Bridge loans are often recourse, especially for smaller loans or newer borrower relationships. Large non-recourse permanent loans are available from money-center and large regional banks but typically require stronger credit metrics, larger loan sizes, and more established borrower relationships than CMBS non-recourse products.

Bank participation loans — where two or more banks share a single loan, with one "lead" bank managing the relationship and administration — are common for larger construction and bridge loans that exceed any single bank's legal lending limit to a single borrower. The lead bank originates, underwrites, and administers; participating banks fund portions of the loan based on their own credit approval. From the borrower's perspective, a participation loan looks and functions like a bilateral loan.

## Common Mistakes

The most common mistake with bank debt is treating a relationship term sheet as equivalent to a commitment. Banks conduct formal credit approval after the term sheet stage, and approval timelines can be unpredictable — especially if the bank's CRE portfolio is approaching regulatory concentration limits or if the credit committee has concerns about deal structure or sponsor financials. Borrowers who sign purchase contracts contingent on bank financing without a fully approved bank commitment risk failed closings.

A second error is ignoring financial covenant compliance during the loan term. Bank loans frequently contain financial covenants — minimum liquidity, net worth, and global DSCR requirements at the guarantor level — that must be maintained throughout the loan term. Sponsors who structure personal guarantees on multiple loans simultaneously can find themselves in covenant default at the guarantor level even if the underlying assets are performing, triggering technical defaults and lender acceleration rights.

## Sources

Bank CRE lending regulation and concentration guidance are published by the Federal Reserve, OCC, and FDIC in their joint Interagency CRE Concentration Guidance (2006, still operative). Basel III/IV capital requirements affecting bank CRE lending are detailed in BIS publications and U.S. banking agency implementing regulations. The Mortgage Bankers Association and FDIC publish annual data on bank CRE origination volumes and delinquency rates. CREFC publishes periodic analysis of bank vs. non-bank CRE credit market share.
