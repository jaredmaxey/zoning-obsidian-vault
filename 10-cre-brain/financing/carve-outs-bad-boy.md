# Bad-Boy Carve-Outs

## Definition

Bad-boy carve-outs (also called "recourse carve-outs" or "non-recourse carve-outs") are provisions in an otherwise non-recourse CRE loan that impose personal liability on the guarantor — typically the sponsor, managing member, or key principal — for specific enumerated events or actions. The underlying principle is that non-recourse protection is a benefit extended to borrowers in exchange for operating the property and managing the loan in good faith; if the borrower commits specified acts of wrongdoing or voluntarily takes actions that impair the lender's collateral or remedies, the non-recourse shield is pierced and the lender may pursue the guarantor personally.

There are two structural categories of bad-boy carve-outs. "Carve-out events" (also called "springing recourse" triggers) are events that cause the entire loan balance to become the personal obligation of the guarantor — the non-recourse protection converts to full recourse for the full loan amount. These typically include: (1) voluntary bankruptcy filing by the borrowing entity; (2) collusive or non-collusive bankruptcy filing with the guarantor's knowledge or consent; (3) misappropriation of insurance or condemnation proceeds; (4) environmental contamination caused by the borrower; and (5) unauthorized transfer of the property or ownership interests. "Carve-out obligations" (sometimes called "indemnity carve-outs") are losses or liabilities for which the guarantor is personally responsible on a dollar-for-dollar basis, without causing the full loan to become recourse. These typically include: (1) waste or willful misconduct that damages the property; (2) fraud or material misrepresentation in loan origination; (3) misappropriation of rents, security deposits, or insurance proceeds; (4) failure to maintain required insurance; and (5) failure to comply with environmental laws.

The practical importance of bad-boy carve-outs is that in a distressed scenario, lenders routinely assert that carve-out events have occurred as leverage in workout negotiations, even when the events are genuinely ambiguous. The threat of full recourse — with the guarantor's personal assets at risk — creates powerful incentives for borrowers to negotiate rather than litigate. The scope of the carve-out list is therefore a critical negotiating point, and sponsors should push to narrow carve-outs to genuinely wrongful acts rather than accepting overbroad triggering events.

## When It's Used

Bad-boy carve-outs are a standard feature of virtually every non-recourse CRE mortgage in the institutional market — they are so universal that a non-recourse loan without any carve-outs would be unusual. They appear in CMBS loans, agency loans (Fannie/Freddie), life company loans, bank non-recourse loans, and debt fund bridge loans alike. The specific list of carve-out events and obligations varies by lender type, loan type, and vintage — CMBS loans typically have the most standardized and extensive lists, while bank portfolio loans may have shorter, more negotiated lists.

In loan origination, the carve-out guaranty is typically a separate legal document from the non-recourse loan itself — a "Guaranty of Recourse Obligations" signed by the individual principal(s) or the GP entity. Identifying who must sign the guaranty — which individuals or entities — and what their net worth and liquidity must be to satisfy the lender's requirements is a routine part of loan structuring. Key principals who refuse to sign (or who cannot satisfy financial requirements) can block loan approval.

Carve-out guaranties are particularly significant in CMBS loans because the servicer (often a special servicer in a distressed situation) may have strong incentives to assert carve-out claims. CMBS B-piece buyers and special servicers control the enforcement of carve-out claims and may pursue them aggressively to recover value for bondholders in ways that a bilateral lender with relationship considerations might not.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The most contested carve-out in litigation has historically been the "bankruptcy filing" springing recourse. Courts have split on the enforceability of springing recourse triggered by a voluntary bankruptcy filing, with some courts holding the provision unenforceable as a violation of public policy (since it punishes the borrower for seeking the protection of federal bankruptcy law) and others enforcing it strictly. The outcome varies by jurisdiction. Given this uncertainty, the bankruptcy filing carve-out has become an intense point of negotiation — sponsors may seek to carve it out entirely, limit it to collusive filings, or add qualifications requiring bad faith before the trigger fires.

"Environmental carve-outs" are another frequently contested category. Full indemnification for environmental contamination can expose a guarantor to liability vastly exceeding the loan balance, particularly for industrial or brownfield properties. Sponsors often negotiate caps, time limits, or carve-outs for pre-existing contamination known to the lender at origination.

Some lenders, particularly private credit funds, use "full recourse" construction loans rather than non-recourse-with-carve-outs, in which case the carve-out framework does not apply — the entire loan is personal. As the deal de-risks (completion, lease-up), the sponsor may negotiate a transition to a non-recourse permanent loan, effectively "burning off" the construction recourse at stabilization.

## Common Mistakes

The most serious recurring mistake is sponsors signing carve-out guaranties without understanding which events trigger full loan recourse vs. indemnity obligations. Misappropriation of rents in a cash-constrained situation (e.g., using property cash flow to fund a capital call rather than paying the lender as required) is a carve-out indemnity event that can expose the guarantor to hundreds of thousands or millions of dollars of personal liability, even if done under financial duress and without fraudulent intent. Understanding the scope of each carve-out before signing is essential.

A second common error is failing to negotiate the "controlled entity" or "organizational document" carve-out scope. Many lenders draft carve-outs that apply to actions by any "affiliate" of the borrower or guarantor, potentially sweeping in actions by other funds or related entities that the guarantor cannot control. Narrowing carve-outs to actions within the borrower entity itself — not affiliated entities — is an important protection for sponsors running multiple vehicles.

## Sources

Bad-boy carve-out structure, scope, and litigation is addressed extensively in the American Bar Association Real Property, Trust and Estate Law Section publications and in ACREL practitioner commentary. Significant case law includes Cheyne (N.Y.), Blue Hills Office Park (Mass.), and numerous post-GFC decisions addressing the enforceability of springing recourse and the scope of carve-out indemnities. CREFC publishes standardized loan documents (the "Model Loan Agreement") that include a benchmark carve-out list used as a starting point in many CMBS transactions.
