# Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)

## Definition

Agency debt refers to multifamily mortgage loans originated under the programs of Fannie Mae (Federal National Mortgage Association) and Freddie Mac (Federal Home Loan Mortgage Corporation), the two government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) that dominate the U.S. multifamily lending market. Fannie Mae originates and securitizes multifamily loans through its Delegated Underwriting and Servicing (DUS) program, in which approved lender-partners bear a first-loss position on each loan they originate, creating a strong incentive for rigorous underwriting. Freddie Mac operates its equivalent through the Optigo program. Together, Fannie and Freddie consistently account for a substantial share of all multifamily mortgage origination — their market share has at times exceeded 40%–50% of total multifamily originations by dollar volume, though this varies with market conditions and policy direction (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24).

Agency loans carry several structural advantages that make them the benchmark financing product for stabilized multifamily: (1) they offer the deepest liquidity of any multifamily lender, enabling loans on properties ranging from 5 units to thousands; (2) they price at spreads to Treasuries that are typically among the tightest available in the market because of the implicit government backing; (3) they offer high leverage — LTV up to 80% on certain products, with competitive DSCR floors; and (4) loan terms extend to 10–12 years with amortization of up to 30 years, offering long-term rate certainty. These features make agency debt the clear refinancing target after a multifamily value-add or ground-up lease-up.

The programs are exclusively focused on multifamily residential properties (5+ units) and specifically exclude commercial and mixed-use properties where the ground-floor commercial component exceeds defined thresholds. Both programs offer products for market-rate, affordable (LIHTC and Section 8), seniors housing, student housing, and manufactured housing communities, each with distinct underwriting criteria. Both also offer "small balance" programs for loan sizes below defined thresholds, which feature streamlined underwriting and documentation.

## When It's Used

Agency debt is the primary permanent financing vehicle for stabilized multifamily across the United States. Sponsors completing value-add renovation programs, developers completing ground-up lease-up, and core investors purchasing stabilized apartments all target agency debt as their long-term hold financing because of the combination of rate, leverage, and liquidity. The deep secondary market for agency mortgage-backed securities (MBS) means lenders can consistently offer competitive pricing that balance-sheet lenders cannot match.

Agency loans are underwritten by the approved DUS or Optigo lender-partner, who submits the application to the relevant GSE for review. The underwriting process — especially for loans above certain size thresholds — is thorough and time-consuming, typically taking 45–90 days from application to closing on a standard deal. The process involves third-party property condition assessments, environmental reports, appraisals, and financial underwriting of both the property's current income and the borrower's financial strength.

Prepayment mechanics are a defining feature of agency loans and have significant exit-strategy implications. Most Fannie DUS loans require yield maintenance or defeasance for prepayment during the loan term, which can be extremely costly in a declining-rate environment (when outstanding Treasuries are valued at a premium). Step-down prepayment schedules (e.g., 5-4-3-2-1) are available on some products and offer more predictable exit cost, but typically at slightly higher pricing.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Both agencies offer interest-only (IO) periods for qualified borrowers and deals: typically, IO is available at lower LTV ratios (≤65%–70%) and is a coveted feature for sponsors who want to maximize near-term cash flow without amortizing the balance. IO loans carry more refinancing risk at maturity since the full original balance remains outstanding.

Green financing programs (Fannie Mae Green Rewards and Freddie Mac Green Advantage) offer additional loan proceeds or pricing improvements in exchange for borrower commitments to reduce property energy or water consumption by specified percentages. These have become increasingly important for sponsors pursuing ESG mandates and for maximizing loan proceeds on eligible properties.

Affordable housing programs — loans on LIHTC properties, HAP contract (Section 8) properties, and naturally occurring affordable housing (NOAH) — carry distinct underwriting criteria, often more favorable LTV and DSCR thresholds in exchange for affordability covenants running with the property. Mission-driven borrowers and affordable housing developers rely heavily on agency programs and their favorable pricing for affordable product.

## Common Mistakes

A common mistake is assuming agency financing is available at the time of acquisition of a transitional or lease-up property. Agency underwriting standards require stabilized occupancy (typically 90%+) maintained for a specified period (often 90 days) before an application is viable. Sponsors who rely on agency take-out financing in their exit underwriting but acquire a property with meaningful lease-up risk may find themselves unable to refinance on the assumed timeline, extending the bridge period and increasing financing cost.

A second recurring error is failing to model the prepayment obligation in the hold period model. If the deal underwriting assumes a 5-year hold but places a 10-year agency loan, the cost of defeasance or yield maintenance in years 2–5 can represent a significant reduction in net sale proceeds. This must be computed explicitly using the specific prepayment schedule in the loan agreement, not assumed to be minimal.
