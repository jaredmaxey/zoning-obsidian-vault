# Private Credit

## Definition

Private credit (also called "private debt" or "debt fund lending") refers to CRE financing originated by non-bank, non-agency investment funds — typically credit-focused real estate fund managers, asset managers, insurance company credit subsidiaries, or specialty finance companies — that source capital from institutional investors and deploy it as mortgage loans, bridge loans, mezzanine debt, or preferred equity. Unlike banks (which are deposit-funded and heavily regulated for capital adequacy), private credit funds raise equity from LPs or issue their own bonds and CLO structures, giving them more flexibility to operate at leverage, risk, and loan-type parameters that banks cannot. The private credit market has grown dramatically since the 2008 financial crisis as bank regulatory tightening created persistent unmet demand for transitional and higher-leverage CRE debt.

Private credit fills the "credit gap" between what regulated banks will do and what capital markets (CMBS, agency) require in terms of property stabilization and loan standardization. A value-add deal at 75%–80% LTC in a secondary market with a sponsor track record below what a large bank would need, or a heavy-value-add renovation requiring a flexible draw structure that a CMBS product cannot accommodate — these are the natural borrowers in the private credit market. In exchange for this flexibility and speed of execution, private credit lenders charge a premium: spreads over benchmark are typically 200–500+ bps wider than bank or CMBS execution, and origination fees of 1%–2% are standard (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24).

The private credit sector encompasses a wide range of managers, from small single-strategy bridge lenders to multi-strategy global credit managers overseeing tens of billions of dollars across senior, mezzanine, preferred equity, and equity tranches. The largest institutional players include Blackstone Real Estate Debt Strategies, Ares Real Estate, Starwood Capital, KKR Real Estate Credit, Benefit Street Partners, and many others. The proliferation of private credit has been a defining structural shift in CRE finance over the past decade, and private credit is now estimated to account for a substantial share of all transitional CRE debt origination.

## When It's Used

Private credit is the dominant provider of transitional senior bridge and construction financing above the threshold where community banks operate ($25M+) and below the threshold where CMBS standardization applies. This "missing middle" of CRE debt is where debt funds have built scale. Value-add acquisitions of office, retail, industrial, and multifamily in secondary and tertiary markets, heavy renovation programs requiring flexible draw structures, and transitional ground-up development outside the bank's underwriting appetite are the core borrower profile.

Private credit also dominates the mezzanine debt and preferred equity market. When a deal requires leverage above what a senior lender will provide, the sponsor turns to a private credit fund for gap financing. Most of the institutional mezzanine and preferred equity deployed in CRE transactions comes from debt funds or credit affiliates of large asset managers, not from banks or insurance companies. The terms, structure, and pricing of private credit mezzanine vary widely; the most competitive terms are available to sponsors with established relationships and strong track records.

Private credit execution speed is a significant competitive advantage. While a bank may take 45–60 days to complete credit approval, and CMBS underwriting requires a full third-party report package, a private credit lender with an established relationship can term-sheet and close a bridge loan in 20–30 days, which is meaningful in competitive acquisition processes where closing certainty is a differentiator.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Private credit funds are structured in several ways that affect borrower experience and loan terms. Closed-end funds with finite investment periods and LP commitments have deployment pressure that can translate to competitive pricing and willingness to underwrite more aggressively at certain points in the fund lifecycle. Evergreen or permanent capital vehicles (BDCs, mortgage REITs, insurance credit arms) have more stable deployment capacity and can be more consistent counterparties but may price less aggressively in highly competitive environments.

CRE CLOs (collateralized loan obligations backed by floating-rate bridge loans) have become a significant funding vehicle for large private credit platforms. A CLO allows the originator to securitize a pool of bridge loans, recycle capital, and continue originating — analogous to how banks securitize consumer loans. The CLO market's openness directly affects private credit capacity and pricing: when CLO execution is favorable, private credit firms can offer tighter pricing; when CLO markets close (as they did briefly in 2020 and are subject to doing during credit dislocations), private credit capacity can contract sharply.

## Common Mistakes

Private credit borrowers frequently focus exclusively on rate and fail to model the total cost of capital: origination fees, exit fees, extension fees, and the cost of the required interest reserve all add substantially to the effective financing cost. A bridge loan at SOFR + 300 bps looks cheaper than one at SOFR + 350 bps, but if the first has a higher origination fee, a required exit fee, and a larger interest reserve, the total effective cost can be higher. Always model the all-in cost on a full-term basis.

A second error is treating all private credit lenders as fungible. The quality of the counterparty relationship matters enormously in a distressed or modified scenario. A reputable institutional lender with a long operating history and alignment incentives will behave very differently from a smaller, more aggressive fund in a workout situation. The track record, fund structure, and institutional reputation of the lender are as important as the headline economics.
