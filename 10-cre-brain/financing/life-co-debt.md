# Life Company Debt

## Definition

Life insurance companies are among the oldest and largest institutional commercial real estate lenders. They originate fixed-rate permanent mortgages held on their own balance sheets (rather than securitizing), funded by the long-duration premium and reserve assets that naturally match the long-duration, predictable cash flows of high-quality real estate loans. The largest life company lenders include MetLife, Prudential Financial (PGIM), New York Life, Principal Financial, and TIAA (now Nuveen Real Estate), collectively deploying tens of billions of dollars annually into CRE mortgages. Smaller life companies and mortgage-focused insurance affiliates also participate, particularly in regional markets (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24).

Life company loans are characterized by several features that distinguish them from both agency and CMBS products. First, they are portfolio loans — the originating insurer holds the loan to maturity, meaning the relationship between borrower and lender is direct and continuous rather than intermediated by a servicer. This creates genuine flexibility: a life company relationship manager can approve consent requests, lease modifications, and partial releases with a level of responsiveness and pragmatism unavailable in a securitized structure. Second, pricing is typically competitive with or tighter than CMBS conduit for high-quality, lower-leverage transactions because the insurer's cost of funds from insurance liabilities is low. Third, underwriting is conservative: life companies typically target lower LTV (55%–65%, sometimes up to 70% for top-tier assets), higher DSCR minimums, and top-market, high-quality assets that match their long-term portfolio mandates.

Life company loan allocation is subject to each insurer's internal allocation limits, property-type concentrations, and geographic preferences. Demand for life company allocations routinely exceeds supply in favorable market conditions, making relationships with life company correspondents (mortgage banking firms that originate on behalf of life companies) a meaningful competitive advantage for institutional borrowers. Loan sizes typically range from $5M to $500M+, with large insurers focusing at the higher end.

## When It's Used

Life company debt is the preferred financing vehicle for core institutional investors acquiring high-quality, stabilized, long-hold assets across office, industrial, retail, and multifamily property types. A pension fund or insurance company acquiring a class-A industrial portfolio or a stabilized suburban office campus at conservative leverage would typically seek life company financing because of the alignment between the insurer's long-term investment horizon and the investor's long-term hold strategy. The conservative LTV and strong DSCR requirements are acceptable in the context of a low-leverage core strategy.

Life company lenders are also the primary source of financing for certain specialty asset types that fall outside CMBS standardization — large single-tenant net-lease properties, NNN portfolios, medical office buildings, and large grocery-anchored retail centers. Their portfolio nature allows underwriting accommodations (e.g., shadow-anchored retail, single-tenant credit risk analysis) that conduit lenders would have difficulty justifying.

Borrowers seeking maximum leverage or transitional financing are not natural candidates for life company debt. Life companies are disciplined underwriters of stable, income-generating assets at moderate leverage — not providers of high-LTC construction loans or 80% LTV bridge transactions. For those needs, banks, debt funds, or CMBS transitional products are more appropriate.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Most life company mortgage loans carry yield maintenance prepayment provisions — the borrower pays a penalty sufficient to make the lender whole on their expected yield through the loan term if the loan is retired early. Yield maintenance can be extremely expensive on large loans when Treasury rates have fallen since origination (the replacement portfolio of Treasuries costs more to assemble). Some life companies offer "lockout" periods (no prepayment permitted for a specified period) followed by a step-down structure, which is more predictable but less flexible.

"Forward commitments" are a distinctive life company product where a life insurer commits in advance to fund a permanent loan at a fixed rate upon completion and stabilization of a development project. Forward commits allow developers to lock in permanent financing rates before the project is built, eliminating the take-out rate risk during the construction and lease-up period. Life company forward commitments are particularly important in markets and periods where rate uncertainty is high.

Some life companies participate in construction lending (typically only for pre-leased or built-to-suit projects) and mezzanine lending, though these are smaller programs relative to their core permanent mortgage volumes. Their participation in non-core products typically reflects specific portfolio management needs rather than a broad market mandate.

## Common Mistakes

Borrowers who approach life companies with properties that fall outside the insurer's core mandate — secondary markets, lower-quality product, transitional assets — will find they are not competitive recipients of life company capital. Wasting time and goodwill by submitting deals outside a specific life company's appetite is a common broker and sponsor error. Understanding each life company's current geographic focus, property type preferences, and leverage limits before approaching is essential.

A second common mistake is underestimating the importance of the correspondent relationship. Most life company loans are originated through approved mortgage banking correspondents who have existing allocations and established trust with the insurer's loan committee. Direct approaches from unknown borrowers are rarely competitive; working through an established correspondent is typically required to access the best pricing and allocation.
