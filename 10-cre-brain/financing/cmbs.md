# CMBS

## Definition

Commercial Mortgage-Backed Securities (CMBS) are bonds backed by pools of commercial real estate mortgages that are originated, pooled, and securitized through a trust structure. The process begins when a conduit lender originates a fixed-rate commercial mortgage, typically conforming to securitization eligibility requirements, then sells the loan into a pool with other mortgages. The pool is placed into a Real Estate Mortgage Investment Conduit (REMIC) trust, which issues bonds ("tranches") with varying credit ratings and yields — AAA-rated senior tranches are first in line for principal and interest payments, with successively junior tranches absorbing losses first. Investors in each tranche are compensated according to their position in the "waterfall" of the bond capital structure.

For borrowers, CMBS conduit lending offers several advantages: (1) fixed-rate execution at spreads tied to the 10-year Treasury that are competitive with life company pricing for many product types; (2) non-recourse structure (except for bad-boy carve-outs) at leverage levels that a balance-sheet lender might not offer on a non-recourse basis; (3) geographic and asset-class breadth — CMBS lenders will finance retail, office, industrial, hospitality, multifamily (non-agency), self-storage, and specialty property types across essentially any market; and (4) relatively standardized underwriting criteria that are transparent and navigable. CMBS loan sizes typically range from approximately $2M to hundreds of millions, with the "conduit" segment focusing on standard institutional product and "single-asset, single-borrower" (SASB) deals addressing large loans and trophy assets.

The CMBS market has historically been highly cyclical. The original CMBS market developed in the 1990s; the 2005–2007 vintage was characterized by aggressive underwriting and high leverage, contributing to significant losses during the 2008–2012 downturn. Post-GFC reform created "risk retention" rules (Dodd-Frank Act) requiring CMBS sponsors to retain a minimum 5% economic interest in each deal, aligning incentives between originators and investors. The market has since developed a healthier underwriting culture, though cycles continue (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24).

## When It's Used

CMBS is the dominant fixed-rate, non-agency permanent financing vehicle for commercial properties other than multifamily. Retail centers, office buildings, industrial portfolios, hotels, and mixed-use properties that do not qualify for agency programs or that are too small, specialized, or geographically distributed for life company appetites frequently finance via CMBS conduit. For properties that do qualify for life company debt, CMBS may still be preferred because of higher leverage availability or broader asset-type coverage.

Large single-asset transactions — typically trophy office, flagship retail, or large hotel transactions — are frequently financed via SASB CMBS, where a single loan collateralized by one property or a small portfolio is securitized with bespoke bond tranches rated by credit agencies. SASB transactions allow sophisticated underwriting of complex collateral and can accommodate loan sizes from $100M to $1B+. The structure allows the borrower to access capital markets liquidity without the standardized constraints of the conduit market.

Borrowers should understand that once a loan is securitized, the original lender no longer controls the loan; the loan is administered by a designated servicer (a "master servicer" for ongoing administration and a "special servicer" for defaulted loans). The servicer's discretion is governed by the Pooling and Servicing Agreement (PSA), which can significantly constrain the servicer's flexibility to accommodate loan modifications, extensions, or consent requests compared to a bilateral bank relationship. This "servicer rigidity" is one of the key structural disadvantages of CMBS financing versus balance-sheet alternatives.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The CMBS market encompasses several distinct product categories: "conduit" CMBS (diversified pools of loans under $50M–$75M each), SASB CMBS (single large assets), "agency CMBS" (Fannie/Freddie MBS, distinct from non-agency conduit), and CRE CLOs (collateralized loan obligations backed by floating-rate bridge and transitional loans, a growing segment). CRE CLOs are structurally similar to CMBS but back floating-rate loans originated by debt funds and banks rather than fixed-rate conduit product.

B-piece (or "junior bond") buyers in CMBS pools hold the most subordinate, highest-risk tranches and receive the highest yield. B-piece buyers typically conduct thorough re-underwriting of all loans in the pool and can reject up to a specified number of loans (the "kick-out" right) before closing. B-piece buyers are a powerful check on underwriting quality in the conduit market and can affect what loans are financeable on what terms.

## Common Mistakes

Borrowers frequently underestimate the complexity and inflexibility of CMBS servicing. Seemingly routine requests — lease consent, partial release, property improvement plans — that a bank would approve quickly require formal servicer approval processes that can take 60–120 days, cost significant fees, and may be refused if the PSA terms do not permit the requested action. Borrowers who require ongoing operational flexibility should carefully evaluate whether CMBS is the appropriate product given their asset management needs.

Modeling defeasance or yield maintenance costs accurately is another frequent gap. Sponsors who model "prepay at par" for a CMBS loan are systematically overstating net sale proceeds. Defeasance is computed based on the cost of assembling a portfolio of Treasury securities that exactly replicates the loan's remaining cash flows — in a low-rate environment, this portfolio can cost materially more than the outstanding loan balance.
