# Recourse vs. Non-Recourse

## Definition

In CRE finance, "recourse" refers to the lender's right to pursue the borrower's assets beyond the mortgaged property if the loan goes into default and the property value at foreclosure is insufficient to satisfy the outstanding debt. A "full recourse" loan allows the lender, after foreclosing and selling the property, to pursue the borrower entity and — if a personal guaranty is in place — the individual guarantors for any remaining deficiency. "Non-recourse" loans limit the lender's remedy solely to the collateral — if the property value at foreclosure is less than the outstanding loan, the lender absorbs the shortfall, and the borrower has no further personal obligation.

The distinction is fundamental to sponsor liability management and deal structuring. Non-recourse financing is highly valued by institutional sponsors and equity investors because it "ring-fences" the loss on any single investment: a failed deal cannot jeopardize the sponsor's other assets, investor capital in separate deals, or personal wealth. Recourse loans create contingent liabilities that must be disclosed on financial statements and factored into guarantor net worth and liquidity calculations — a sponsor with $100M in recourse construction loan guaranties carries a very different financial profile than one with equivalent non-recourse exposure.

In practice, virtually all CRE loans exist on a spectrum rather than at the extremes. Non-recourse loans almost universally include "recourse carve-outs" — a list of specific events or actions that, if committed by the borrower, cause the loan to become full recourse or trigger a separate repayment guaranty obligation. These carve-outs are addressed in detail in [[Bad-Boy Carve-Outs]]. Conversely, even recourse loans typically limit the guarantors' exposure via negotiated caps, burn-offs, or sunset provisions.

## When It's Used

The availability of non-recourse financing is one of the defining structural advantages of institutional CRE capital structures. Non-recourse financing is standard for:

- **CMBS conduit loans** — essentially all CMBS loans are non-recourse with carve-outs by convention; the securitized structure makes full recourse impractical.
- **Agency loans (Fannie/Freddie)** — non-recourse with standard bad-boy carve-outs and completion guaranties for certain construction products.
- **Large permanent portfolio loans** from life companies and money-center banks for institutional borrowers — typically non-recourse for stabilized, lower-leverage transactions.
- **Large institutional bridge loans** from debt funds — often structured non-recourse for established sponsors at moderate leverage.

Recourse is standard for:

- **Construction loans** at all lender types — completion guaranties (and often repayment guaranties) are nearly universal for ground-up construction; the unbuilt collateral creates a level of lender risk that cannot be fully mitigated without personal accountability.
- **Small balance loans** from community and regional banks — below certain thresholds, banks routinely require recourse as a credit enhancement, particularly from sponsors without extensive track records.
- **High-leverage or transitional loans** to sponsors with limited track records — lenders who cannot fully rely on collateral performance use recourse as a compensating factor.

## Variations & Edge Cases

"Non-recourse with completion guaranty" is a hybrid structure common in construction lending: the loan is nominally non-recourse, but the sponsor guarantees that the project will be completed to a specified standard and that all mechanics' liens will be cleared. If the project is not completed (e.g., the sponsor abandons it or runs out of funding), the completion guaranty is triggered. This is a substantial but time-limited personal obligation that burns off upon project completion and certificate of occupancy.

"Springing recourse" is a provision in otherwise non-recourse loans where certain events cause the loan to become full recourse: a bankruptcy filing by the borrower entity (since a bankruptcy filing alone can destroy collateral value through delay), a transfer of the property in violation of due-on-sale provisions, or the borrower's failure to maintain required insurance. Unlike a true carve-out (which creates a separate personal obligation), a springing recourse converts the entire loan balance to full recourse.

Recourse burn-offs — provisions that eliminate a personal guaranty after the loan achieves specified performance metrics (e.g., the property reaches stabilized occupancy for 90 consecutive days and achieves a minimum DSCR) — are negotiated in some bank and debt fund loans. These are valuable to sponsors because they reduce contingent liability over time as the deal de-risks.

## Common Mistakes

A common error by new sponsors is failing to understand the full scope of their personal exposure under a nominally "non-recourse" loan. Non-recourse loans with standard carve-outs can in practice expose sponsors to full personal liability for environmental contamination, fraud, misrepresentation, unauthorized transfers, and misappropriation of rents — events that are far more commonly asserted by lenders in distressed situations than the "bad actor" label suggests. Every guaranty in a CRE loan should be reviewed by legal counsel before signing.

A second mistake is ignoring recourse exposure in portfolio financial models. A sponsor who has guarantied five construction loans simultaneously may be within each individual bank's underwriting standards but has created a total contingent liability that exceeds their liquid net worth. A simultaneous stress event across the portfolio — a market downturn that affects all projects at once — could trigger calls on all guaranties simultaneously. Tracking total recourse exposure across the portfolio is an essential risk management discipline.
