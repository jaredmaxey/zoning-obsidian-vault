# Financing Index

## Overview

This folder covers the full spectrum of capital sources and structuring frameworks used in institutional commercial real estate transactions — from first-lien senior mortgage debt through mezzanine, preferred equity, and common equity, and the analytical frameworks for designing capital stacks, distribution waterfalls, and joint venture structures. Notes are organized into three groups: debt instruments, equity instruments, and structures/frameworks.

## Contents

### Debt Instruments

- [[Senior Debt]] — First-lien mortgage debt secured by a property; holds the highest repayment priority in the capital stack and typically offers the lowest cost of capital.
- [[Bridge Loan]] — Short-term, floating-rate senior debt used to finance transitional or value-add properties until they stabilize and qualify for permanent financing.
- [[Construction Loan]] — Short-term, draw-based financing that funds ground-up or major renovation construction, repaid from permanent financing or sale proceeds upon project completion.
- [[Permanent Loan]] — Long-term, typically fixed-rate first-lien mortgage placed on stabilized income-producing property after construction or business plan execution is complete.
- [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] — Government-sponsored enterprise multifamily mortgage programs offering deep liquidity, competitive fixed-rate pricing, and high leverage for stabilized apartment properties.
- [[CMBS]] — Securitized commercial real estate debt pooled into bonds of varying credit quality; the primary non-agency fixed-rate permanent financing vehicle for income-producing properties.
- [[Life Company Debt]] — Fixed-rate portfolio loans originated by life insurance companies targeting high-quality, lower-leverage stabilized assets; known for relationship flexibility, tight pricing, and conservative underwriting.
- [[Bank Debt]] — Balance-sheet commercial real estate loans originated by commercial banks; covers construction, bridge, and portfolio permanent products with relationship-driven flexibility but regulatory leverage limits.
- [[Private Credit]] — Non-bank, fund-based debt capital providing bridge, construction, mezzanine, and preferred equity financing; fills the gap where bank regulation and agency/CMBS constraints leave unserved demand.
- [[Mezzanine Debt]] — Subordinate debt positioned between senior mortgage and equity; secured by a pledge of the borrowing entity's ownership interests rather than a direct property lien.
- [[Recourse vs. Non-Recourse]] — The distinction between loans where the lender can pursue the borrower's other assets upon default (recourse) versus those limited to the property collateral (non-recourse).
- [[Bad-Boy Carve-Outs]] — Exceptions to non-recourse protection that impose personal liability on sponsors for specific wrongful acts or voluntary actions that impair the lender's collateral or remedies.

### Equity Instruments

- [[Preferred Equity]] — Equity investment carrying a fixed preferred return and repayment priority over common equity, positioned in the capital stack between senior debt and common equity.
- [[Common Equity]] — The residual ownership layer of the capital stack, bearing first losses and receiving distributions only after all debt and preferred equity obligations are satisfied.

### Structures and Frameworks

- [[Capital Stack Overview]] — Framework for visualizing and analyzing the layered financing structure of a CRE transaction, mapping each capital source's priority, cost, and risk-return position.
- [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] — Framework for structuring and computing the distribution waterfall in a CRE joint venture, including preferred return hurdles, catch-up provisions, and the GP promote split.
- [[Joint Venture Structures]] — Framework for designing and evaluating CRE joint venture entity structures, governance rights, contribution splits, and alignment mechanisms between operating partners and capital partners.
- [[Preferred Return Mechanics]] — Framework for computing accrual, payment, and compounding of the LP preferred return hurdle in a CRE joint venture waterfall, including simple, compounding, and look-back variants.
- [[GP Co-Invest and Promote Crystallization]] — Framework for sizing GP co-investment obligations and determining when and how promote economics crystallize into realized GP cash distributions at the deal and fund level.
