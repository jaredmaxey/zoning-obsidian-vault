# Multifamily — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Multifamily underwriting converts a property's rent roll and expense history into a stabilized [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] and then applies a capitalization rate to derive value, or projects a multi-year cash flow for a [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] valuation. The starting point is always the current rent roll — unit-by-unit, lease-by-lease — combined with trailing 12-month (T-12) and trailing 3-month (T-3) income and expense actuals. See [[Rent Roll Analysis]], [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]], and [[Expense Normalization]] for the analytical mechanics.

Institutional underwriting of multifamily is characterized by explicit hedging of market-level assumptions (cap rates, rent growth, expense inflation) against a range of scenarios, with sensitivity tables showing the impact of cap rate and rent growth variance on projected returns. All ranges below represent broad institutional norms across U.S. markets and vintages as of 2026-05-24; individual asset underwriting must be calibrated to the specific submarket, vintage, quality tier, and current supply/demand balance. Consult current market data, broker opinions of value, and lender underwriting guidelines before relying on any range herein.

## Typical Ranges

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Going-in cap rate | 4.0% – 6.5% | Class A gateway closer to 4.0–4.5%; Class B Sun Belt suburban 5.0–6.0%; Class C value-add 5.5–7.0%; varies by market and vintage |
| Stabilized physical vacancy | 4% – 8% | Lease-up assets may run 5–15% temporarily; Class A urban tends toward 4–5%; suburban value-add 6–8% |
| Economic vacancy (all-in loss) | 7% – 13% | Includes physical vacancy + concessions + bad debt + collection loss; total EGI haircut varies by market and vintage |
| Operating expense ratio (OpEx / EGI) | 35% – 50% | Garden suburban at the low end; urban high-rise with doormen and structured parking at the high end; varies by market and vintage |
| Exit cap rate spread to going-in | +25 bps – +100 bps | Shorter holds (3–5 years) often underwrite +25–50 bps; longer holds (7–10 years) underwrite +50–100 bps to reflect asset aging and uncertainty |
| Annual rent growth assumption | 2.0% – 4.0% | Long-run blended; short-run varies widely — could be negative in oversupplied markets or 5–7% in tight markets; varies by market and vintage |
| Expense growth assumption | 2.5% – 4.0% | Insurance and property taxes can grow faster in certain states; payroll inflation is a key driver |
| Replacement reserves | $250 – $450/unit/year | Lenders often require $250–$300/unit minimum; higher for older assets (pre-1990) or Class A high-rise; varies by market and vintage |
| Loan-to-Value (stabilized) | 55% – 70% | Agency (Fannie/Freddie) typically 65–70% at stabilized underwriting; bridge loans 65–80% on as-is or cost basis |
| DSCR (Agency at stabilization) | 1.20x – 1.30x minimum | Fannie/Freddie typically require 1.20–1.25x at underwritten NOI; lender-specific |
| Construction cost per unit (garden) | $150,000 – $250,000 | All-in hard + soft; wide range driven by land cost, labor market, and program; varies by market and vintage |
| Construction cost per unit (mid-rise podium) | $250,000 – $450,000 | Structured parking adds meaningfully; varies by market and vintage |
| Construction cost per unit (high-rise) | $400,000 – $800,000+ | Concrete/steel frame; gateway urban markets at top of range; varies by market and vintage |

Ranges current as of 2026-05-24. Verify against current market data before underwriting.

## Lease Structures

Multifamily leases are gross leases — the landlord pays all operating expenses including property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and common area utilities, while the tenant pays rent plus any individually metered utilities (electric, gas) allocated directly to their unit. This is the fundamental structural difference from NNN commercial leases, and it creates two critical underwriting implications: (1) operating expense growth directly erodes NOI unless offset by rent growth; and (2) utility cost recovery strategies (submetering, RUBS — ratio utility billing systems) can be a meaningful value-add lever in properties currently paying tenant utility costs.

Lease terms are almost universally 12 months with month-to-month renewal thereafter. A small percentage of units in any stabilized community are on short-term (3–6 month) leases at a premium; these are underwritten at stabilized market rates for projection purposes. Month-to-month units carry higher turnover risk and are typically underwritten at market rent with a slight concession adjustment. The 12-month lease cycle means that a property with 300 units will turn over 200–250 units per year at normal 65–80% retention rates — creating substantial leasing and make-ready expenses that must be captured in the operating expense budget.

Concessions — free rent, move-in discounts, gift cards — are common in lease-up and oversupplied markets. Institutional underwriting normalizes concessions to the current market equilibrium level and models them burning off (or escalating) based on supply/demand forecasts. Loss-to-lease — the aggregate discount from market rent embedded in existing leases — is a key value-add underwriting input when in-place rents are below market.

## Operating Expense Norms

Multifamily operating expenses are typically grouped into the following categories. Percentages are expressed as a share of [[Effective Gross Income (EGI)]] and represent institutional norms; actual figures vary by market, vintage, and property type.

**Property Taxes:** The largest single operating expense in most markets, commonly representing 15–25% of EGI. Highly variable by state (Texas and Illinois are among the highest; Colorado, Arizona, and Nevada are lower); subject to reassessment risk on sale. Institutional underwriting models property tax based on the assessed value implied by the acquisition price in the year of purchase, then inflates annually at the jurisdiction's historical reassessment rate.

**Insurance:** Typically 3–6% of EGI and rising materially in coastal and wildfire-exposed markets. Property, general liability, and umbrella policies are standard; windstorm and flood are separate and can be significant in Gulf Coast, Florida, and coastal markets. Insurance cost inflation is a current NOI headwind in many Sun Belt and coastal markets.

**Payroll and Management:** On-site staffing (property manager, leasing agents, maintenance technicians) typically represents 12–18% of EGI. Professional third-party management fees add 4–6% of collected revenue (varies by portfolio size and operator). Larger communities benefit from payroll scale — the cost per unit of a single property manager is lower at 300 units than 80 units.

**Repairs and Maintenance (R&M):** Routine maintenance excluding capital improvements; typically $600–$1,200 per unit per year ($50–$100/unit/month) in stabilized Class A–B communities; higher in older Class C properties. Includes appliance repairs, plumbing, HVAC service, landscaping, and common area cleaning.

**Utilities:** Common area electricity, gas, water/sewer, and trash; typically $400–$800 per unit per year for properties with individually metered units; higher where landlord pays for all utilities. Water and sewer costs are rising faster than general inflation in many western U.S. markets.

**General and Administrative (G&A):** Leasing commissions (on new leases), marketing and advertising, legal, accounting, and administrative costs; typically 3–5% of EGI.

**Replacement Reserves (CapEx):** See table above. Institutional underwriting and Agency lenders treat reserves as a below-NOI line item; some underwriting presentations show NOI before reserves and then deduct separately. Major CapEx items include roof replacements (15–20 year cycles), HVAC systems (15–20 years), parking lot/drive re-surfacing (7–12 years), and unit interior renovations in value-add programs.

Ranges current as of 2026-05-24. Verify against current market data before underwriting.
