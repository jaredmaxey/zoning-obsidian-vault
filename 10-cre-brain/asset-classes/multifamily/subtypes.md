# Multifamily — Subtypes

## Overview

The multifamily asset class is internally diverse — each subtype carries its own construction economics, operating model, financing profile, and investor base. Understanding subtype distinctions is essential for accurate underwriting, appropriate capital stack sizing, and realistic risk assessment. The six primary subtypes recognized in institutional practice are garden apartment, mid-rise, high-rise, build-to-rent/single-family rental, student housing, and senior housing. Affordable housing (via LIHTC or project-based Section 8) overlaps several subtypes and is treated separately in [[Affordable Housing & LIHTC]]. See [[Garden vs. Mid-Rise vs. High-Rise]] for a direct comparison of the density-tier subtypes.

---

### Garden Apartment

Garden-style apartments are two- to three-story wood-frame or light-gauge-steel buildings on larger suburban land parcels, typically with surface or carport parking. Unit counts in stabilized garden communities commonly range from 150 to 400+ units, though smaller communities of 50–150 units are common in secondary and tertiary markets. Common amenities include a leasing office, fitness center, pool, and laundry facilities; newer communities add coworking spaces, pet-washing stations, and package-locker systems. The physical profile emphasizes horizontal spread over density — garden projects typically achieve 15–30 units per acre depending on zoning and unit mix.

From a deal perspective, garden apartments are the dominant product type in U.S. institutional multifamily by unit count. They are the preferred vehicle for value-add acquisition strategies because the mid-century to early-2000s vintage stock (often 1970s–1990s construction) represents the largest segment of existing supply and offers the widest spread between in-place rents and market rents. Construction cost per unit is the lowest among multifamily types, making ground-up development more feasible in markets with lower land costs. The investor universe is the broadest of any multifamily subtype: REITs, institutional open-end and closed-end funds, private equity, family offices, and individual investors all participate. Financing via Agency programs (Fannie Mae DUS, Freddie Mac Optigo) is readily available for stabilized product.

### Mid-Rise Apartment

Mid-rise apartments are four- to eight-story buildings, typically concrete podium or steel-frame construction above a ground-level parking structure or retail base. They are most common in urban infill neighborhoods, transit-adjacent suburban locations, and mixed-use developments where higher density is required to justify land cost but where the full structural complexity of high-rise construction is not warranted. Parking is usually structured (podium or garage) rather than surface, adding $20,000–$50,000+ per space in cost depending on market and construction type. Unit counts generally range from 100 to 350 units; density of 40–80+ units per acre is typical.

Mid-rise product occupies a middle ground in both cost and demand: it is significantly more expensive per unit than garden but serves a broader renter demographic than high-rise luxury. Mixed-use podium buildings — retail or office at grade, residential above — are a common mid-rise form and introduce management complexity (shared structure, separate leases, HOA or condo association mechanics in some cases). Developers, local and regional operators, and national apartment REITs all own mid-rise product. Ground-up development is more sensitive to interest rates and construction cost volatility than garden because the higher per-unit cost compresses feasibility margins. Value-add mid-rise renovation typically targets common area improvements, unit interior upgrades, and energy-efficiency programs that reduce operating expenses.

### High-Rise Apartment

High-rise apartments are nine-plus-story, elevator-served towers with structured parking integrated into the building's podium or underground. Construction is concrete or steel frame; costs per unit are the highest of any multifamily type and in major gateway markets can exceed $500,000–$800,000+ all-in per unit (varies widely by market, site, and program). Unit counts range from as few as 75–100 units in boutique urban infill projects to 400–600+ in large tower developments. Amenity packages are extensive — rooftop decks, concierge services, coworking lounges, hotel-quality fitness centers, and curated food-and-beverage activations are typical in Class A product.

Demand is concentrated in the highest-income renter cohort — renters-by-choice who can afford homeownership but prioritize urban location, amenity access, and lifestyle flexibility. Urban gateway markets (New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Seattle, Miami) and dense Sun Belt downtowns support the strongest high-rise rent levels. From a deal perspective, high-rise development requires institutional equity or public subsidy in most markets because the all-in cost per unit is difficult to justify at cap rates buyers will pay on completed product. Core acquisitions of Class A high-rise trophy assets in gateway markets attract sovereign wealth funds, insurance companies, and open-end core funds. Value-add repositioning is less common because renovation of occupied high-rise buildings is operationally complex and expensive.

### Build-to-Rent / SFR

Build-to-rent (BTR) communities and single-family rental (SFR) portfolios are purpose-built or assembled portfolios of detached or attached single-family homes and townhomes intended for long-term rental. The BTR format — developing horizontal communities of 50–500+ homes specifically for rent, with shared amenities, professional management, and a unified ownership structure — emerged as a major institutional product type in the 2010s and accelerated through the 2020s. The SFR format involves acquiring scattered or clustered single-family homes for rent, either at scale (iBuyers and large SFR REITs) or in boutique portfolios.

BTR appeals to renters who want the physical experience of a single-family home (private yards, garages, extra space) but lack the capital for a down payment or prefer the flexibility of renting. The renter demographic skews toward families, young professionals, and remote workers seeking suburban or exurban locations. From an underwriting and operations standpoint, BTR communities behave more like apartment communities — centralized management, common amenities, and institutional reporting — than like scattered SFR portfolios. However, per-unit maintenance and CapEx costs are higher because each unit has its own mechanical systems, roof, and exterior. See [[Build-to-Rent / SFR]] for detailed treatment.

### Student Housing

Student housing is purpose-built or repositioned multifamily housing serving university and college enrollees, located on or near campus. The product is typically leased by the bedroom rather than by the unit, which can generate higher revenue per square foot relative to conventional apartments. Amenity packages are often aggressive — resort-style pools, gaming rooms, study lounges, and high-speed internet are standard in competitive markets. Occupancy is tied to the university enrollment calendar, typically running August through July; off-peak months and enrollment declines are the primary risk.

The investor universe includes specialist student housing REITs and operators (American Campus Communities was the dominant public REIT before going private), private equity funds with niche student-housing expertise, and increasingly general multifamily operators who recognize the favorable yield spreads relative to conventional apartments. Supply risk is concentrated at universities with active on-campus housing development programs, which can materially impair the market for off-campus private student housing. Enrollment trends — particularly demographic headwinds from declining birth rates, international student enrollment policy, and competition among institutions — are a growing medium-term risk for the sector. See [[Student Housing (Multifamily)]] for detailed treatment.

### Senior Housing

Senior housing spans a wide spectrum from age-restricted independent living communities (55+, with no care services) through active adult, independent living (IL), assisted living (AL), memory care (MC), and skilled nursing facilities (SNF). The physical and operational complexity — and therefore the risk and return profile — increases as care intensity rises along this spectrum. Age-restricted active adult communities most closely resemble conventional multifamily in their operating model; SNFs are regulated healthcare facilities with fundamentally different economics, staffing requirements, and reimbursement structures.

From an investment perspective, the independent-living through memory-care segment (sometimes called the "seniors housing" definition used by NCREIF and major institutional benchmarks) is the most common institutional target. These properties require specialized operators — national platforms like Brookdale, Sunrise, and Five Star Senior Living, or regional owner-operators — and are typically structured as operating leases or joint ventures between a real estate owner and a licensed operator. RIDEA structures (where the owner participates directly in operating income rather than receiving a flat lease) are common for larger institutional portfolios. Demand is driven by the aging Baby Boomer cohort, one of the most powerful demographic tailwinds in CRE over the next 15–20 years. Supply risk is real in certain markets; high barriers to entry (licensing, construction cost, specialized labor) constrain oversupply relative to conventional multifamily. See [[Senior Housing Spectrum]] for detailed treatment; see also the [[Senior Housing]] asset-class overview in the CRE brain.
