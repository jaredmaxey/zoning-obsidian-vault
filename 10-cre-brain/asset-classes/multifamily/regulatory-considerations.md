# Multifamily — Regulatory Considerations

## Overview

Multifamily is subject to a broader and more interventionist regulatory environment than most other CRE asset classes because it provides housing — a politically and socially sensitive service. Regulatory risk in multifamily is primarily a state and local phenomenon: while federal law (Fair Housing Act, ADA) sets a floor of requirements, the most impactful rules — rent control, just-cause eviction, landlord-tenant notice requirements, and housing element mandates — vary dramatically by jurisdiction. Investors and operators must conduct jurisdiction-specific legal due diligence as part of every acquisition or development decision. The sections below address the principal regulatory categories; always verify current law with qualified local counsel before transacting.

---

## Rent Control and Rent Stabilization

Rent control — broadly, government-imposed limits on rent increases — is the single highest-impact regulatory risk for multifamily investors in affected jurisdictions. The specific form and stringency varies:

**Strict rent control** (e.g., New York City's Rent Stabilization program) ties permissible rent increases to annual guideline percentages set by a Rent Guidelines Board, limits lease renewal premiums, restricts vacancy decontrol (the ability to reset rents to market on turnover), and in some cases caps the rent level that can be charged regardless of tenant turnover. These regimes most severely constrain NOI growth and asset appreciation, and most institutional investors treat strict rent-stabilized portfolios as a separate investment category with distinct return expectations.

**Rent stabilization / just-cause eviction (without strict caps)** programs — as in California's statewide AB 1482 (2020), Oregon's statewide rent stabilization law, and many city ordinances — impose annual rent increase caps (commonly CPI + some fixed percentage, e.g., CPI + 5%), require just-cause for non-renewal of leases, and often apply to buildings older than 15 years (with exemptions for new construction to preserve development incentives). These regimes moderate but do not eliminate rent growth, primarily affecting value-add strategies that rely on above-cap rent increases or lease non-renewal to achieve mark-to-market.

**Preemption states** (Texas, Florida, Arizona, Colorado historically, among others) bar local governments from enacting rent control by state statute — a significant pro-investment feature. However, political winds shift: Florida removed its preemption protections in 2023 for certain jurisdictions, and ongoing legislative activity in many states creates uncertainty. Acquisition underwriting in preemption states should account for the possibility that the regulatory environment changes during the hold period.

Key institutional considerations: (1) confirm whether the specific asset is covered by the applicable ordinance (new construction exemptions, building size thresholds, and owner-occupancy exemptions vary); (2) model NOI growth under the applicable rent cap rather than unconstrained market assumptions; (3) assess the political trajectory — cities and states that are moving toward greater regulation represent elevated regulatory risk even if current law is permissive.

## Fair Housing

The federal Fair Housing Act (FHA) prohibits discrimination in housing on the basis of race, color, national origin, religion, sex, disability, and familial status (the seven protected classes). Many states and localities have expanded protections to include sexual orientation, gender identity, source of income (prohibiting refusal to accept Section 8 vouchers), marital status, and other characteristics.

For multifamily operators, fair housing compliance primarily governs: (1) marketing and advertising — cannot indicate any preference or limitation based on protected class; (2) tenant screening — criteria must be facially neutral, applied consistently, and not have unjustified disparate impact on protected classes; (3) reasonable accommodation — landlords must make reasonable accommodations for tenants with disabilities (e.g., allowing assistance animals despite a no-pets policy, allowing lease modifications for medical necessity); (4) accessibility — the FHA requires specific physical accessibility features (e.g., accessible common areas, adaptable design) in multifamily buildings built after March 1991 with four or more units.

**ADA (Americans with Disabilities Act):** ADA applies to the common areas and public accommodations aspects of multifamily (leasing offices, clubhouses, pools) but not to individual dwelling units (those are governed by FHA and design standards). Properties undergoing renovation of common areas must bring those areas into ADA compliance.

Fair housing violations can result in civil penalties, private right-of-action lawsuits, HUD complaints, pattern-or-practice investigations by DOJ, and reputational damage. Institutional owners conduct annual fair housing training for all on-site staff, implement written screening policies reviewed by fair housing counsel, and maintain documentation of all leasing decisions.

## Landlord-Tenant Law and Eviction Process

Landlord-tenant law — governing lease requirements, security deposit handling, notice periods for rent increases and lease termination, habitability standards, and eviction procedures — is entirely state and local in its specifics. The COVID-19 eviction moratoriums (2020–2022) exposed how dramatically these rules can be altered by emergency orders. Key variables by jurisdiction:

**Notice periods:** Many states require 30–90 days written notice before rent increases above a threshold; notice periods for non-renewal (month-to-month tenancies) range from 30 to 90 days. Some jurisdictions require longer notice periods for long-term tenants.

**Security deposits:** State laws cap allowable security deposit amounts (commonly 1–2 months' rent), prescribe how deposits must be held (separate interest-bearing accounts in some states), and set deadlines for return with itemized deductions (commonly 14–30 days after move-out). Non-compliance exposes landlords to liability for multiple damages.

**Habitability and maintenance:** All states recognize an implied warranty of habitability — the landlord must maintain units in livable condition. Failure to maintain habitability exposes landlords to rent withholding claims, repair-and-deduct remedies, and tenant-initiated lease termination.

**Eviction process:** Varies from rapid (Texas: 3-day notice to quit; judicial process can take 3–4 weeks) to lengthy (New York, California: just-cause requirements, mandatory cure periods, and judicial process that can take months to years). Understanding the local eviction timeline is critical for modeling bad debt and estimating the cost of tenant defaults.

**Short-term rental regulation:** Many cities (New York, San Francisco, Los Angeles, Denver) have enacted ordinances restricting operators from using multifamily units for Airbnb-style short-term rentals. Lease provisions and HOA rules may separately prohibit subletting. Institutional operators should verify local STR ordinance applicability.

## LIHTC and Affordable Housing Compliance

The Low Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program — administered by state housing finance agencies under IRS Section 42 — is the primary federal mechanism for financing affordable multifamily construction. LIHTC properties are subject to 15-year initial compliance periods (often followed by extended use agreements of 15–30+ additional years) during which the property must meet income and rent restriction requirements: units must be rented to households earning at or below a specified percentage of Area Median Income (AMI, typically 40%, 50%, or 60%), and rents are capped at 30% of the applicable AMI income limit.

LIHTC compliance is technically complex: operators must track tenant income certifications at move-in and annually, maintain unit set-asides, comply with student rule limitations, and report to the state housing finance agency. Non-compliance can trigger credit recapture (the IRS claws back previously claimed credits from investors), penalty assessments, and loss of the extended-use restriction waiver. See [[Affordable Housing & LIHTC]] for detailed treatment.

Mixed-income properties — where LIHTC-restricted units are combined with market-rate units — require careful operational separation and documentation. Project-Based Section 8 (PBS8) properties have similar compliance requirements under HUD HAP (Housing Assistance Payment) contracts.

## Building Codes and Life Safety

Multifamily construction and renovation is governed by state-adopted building codes (typically the IBC — International Building Code — with state amendments), fire codes (IFC or NFPA), and, for HUD-financed properties, HUD Minimum Property Standards. Key areas:

**Fire and life safety:** Sprinkler systems, smoke detectors, fire alarm systems, fire-rated construction assemblies, and emergency egress requirements. High-rise buildings face the most stringent requirements. Retroactive sprinkler mandates have been enacted in some jurisdictions.

**Energy codes:** ASHRAE 90.1 (commercial), IECC (residential), and California's Title 24 are the primary energy efficiency standards. New construction must meet current energy codes; significant renovations often trigger partial or full code upgrade requirements.

**Accessibility:** As described in Fair Housing above; Type A and Type B unit requirements for multifamily buildings depend on building type, height, and number of units.

**Lead and asbestos:** Pre-1978 buildings with renovation work triggering RRP (EPA Renovation, Repair and Painting rule) must use certified renovators and contain lead dust. Pre-1980 buildings with ACM require asbestos abatement management plans before any disturbing work.

## Condo Conversion Regulation

Condominium conversion — converting an existing rental apartment building into for-sale condominiums — is a common multifamily exit strategy in appreciating markets where condo prices per unit exceed apartment building investment value. However, many jurisdictions have enacted conversion regulations that: require long notice periods to existing tenants (90–180+ days in many cities); give existing tenants right-of-first-refusal to purchase their unit; impose relocation assistance payments to displaced tenants; require condominium conversion permits that may be subject to quotas or moratoria; and mandate certain property improvements (habitability upgrades) as a condition of conversion approval.

The viability of a condo conversion exit depends entirely on the local regulatory environment — it can be a straightforward value-creation strategy in permissive markets or practically infeasible in heavily regulated ones. Underwriting a condo conversion exit requires confirmed legal analysis of current conversion regulations, tenant notification and relocation cost modeling, and a realistic timeline for the conversion permitting process.
