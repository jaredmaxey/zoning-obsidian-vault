# Affordable Housing & LIHTC

## Overview

Affordable housing — residential rental housing restricted to income-qualified households at below-market rents — is a major institutional real estate sector in its own right. The primary federal mechanism for financing affordable multifamily development in the United States is the Low Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) program, established by the Tax Reform Act of 1986 under IRC Section 42. LIHTC has financed the development or preservation of approximately 3–4 million affordable housing units since its inception and allocates roughly $10+ billion in annual federal tax credits to qualifying projects. Understanding LIHTC mechanics is essential for any CRE professional active in multifamily development, as LIHTC transactions regularly intersect with conventional multifamily markets, ground-up development, and adaptive reuse.

Affordable housing development involves a different set of motivations, participants, and financial structures than market-rate multifamily. Tax credit investors (typically large financial institutions and corporations) seek to use the tax credits generated by the property to offset their federal tax liability; developers pursue affordable housing for fee income, community impact, and long-term management revenue; nonprofit and public housing authority partners provide mission-driven capital and sometimes land. The financial engineering required to make below-market rents support development costs is complex and involves multiple layers of subsidy, soft debt, and equity.

## The LIHTC Program: Mechanics

**Credit Types:** LIHTC comes in two forms: the **9% credit** (formally a 70% present value credit) for new construction and substantial rehabilitation, and the **4% credit** (formally a 30% present value credit) for acquisition and rehabilitation using tax-exempt bond financing. The 9% credit generates approximately $0.90–$1.00+ of equity per dollar of eligible basis (the qualified cost of the project), making it the more powerful subsidy; the 4% credit generates less equity and is typically paired with tax-exempt bond financing.

**State Allocation:** Annual 9% credits are allocated by each state's housing finance agency (HFA) through a competitive Qualified Allocation Plan (QAP) process. Each state's allocation is based on a per-capita formula set by Congress. States prioritize applications based on factors such as location (qualified census tracts, difficult development areas), targeting of special needs populations, energy efficiency, public housing involvement, and developer experience. Competition for 9% credits is intense in most states; many projects require multiple application cycles before receiving an allocation.

**Tax-Exempt Bonds and 4% Credits:** Projects financed with at least 50% of qualifying costs through private activity bonds (PABs) issued by a state or local authority automatically qualify for 4% credits without competing for state credit allocations. The combination of PABs and 4% credits has become an increasingly important affordable housing financing tool as demand for 9% credits outstrips supply.

**Eligible Basis and Qualified Basis:** Only costs that qualify as "eligible basis" (generally hard and soft costs of construction or rehabilitation, excluding land and certain other costs) generate tax credits. The "qualified basis" is the portion of eligible basis attributable to income-restricted (low-income) units; the credit is calculated on qualified basis. Income averaging elections (introduced in 2018) allow mixing income tiers (e.g., 20%, 40%, 60%, and 80% AMI) to achieve greater flexibility in tenant income targeting while meeting the "average income test."

**Credit Period and Compliance:** Credits are claimed by investors over a **10-year credit period** following the placed-in-service date. The property must remain in compliance with income and rent restrictions for a **15-year initial compliance period** (after which "extended use agreements" typically require an additional 15–30+ years of affordability). During the compliance period, the state HFA monitors the property for compliance; non-compliance can trigger IRS credit recapture.

## Income and Rent Restrictions

LIHTC income restrictions require that qualifying units be occupied by households earning at or below a specified percentage of **Area Median Income (AMI)**. Common targeting structures include:

- **60% AMI**: The most common targeting level. Households must earn no more than 60% of AMI to qualify. Rents are capped at 30% of 60% of AMI for the applicable household size.
- **50% AMI**: Deeper affordability, common in projects targeting very low-income households.
- **80% AMI (with income averaging)**: The 2018 "average income test" allows some units at higher AMI levels (up to 80%) if the average of all restricted unit AMIs is 60% or below.

AMI is determined annually by HUD for each metropolitan statistical area (MSA) and is adjusted for household size. Maximum rents under LIHTC are published annually by the HFA and updated as AMI levels change. Tenants must be re-certified annually to confirm continued income eligibility; households that exceed the income limit upon recertification typically have an "income averaging" grace period or are subject to a next-available-unit rule.

## Deal Structure and Capital Stack

A LIHTC transaction's capital stack is layered and complex, typically including multiple debt and equity sources:

**Tax Credit Equity:** The largest equity source in most 9% transactions. Tax credit investors (banks, insurance companies, corporations with federal tax appetite) purchase the tax credits at a "credit price" (typically expressed as cents per dollar of credit, e.g., $0.85–$1.05/credit, varying by market conditions). This credit price multiplied by the annual credit amount multiplied by 10 years produces the total equity contribution. Tax credit investors receive the economic benefits (credits plus depreciation deductions) and typically hold a 99.99% limited partnership interest in the ownership entity.

**Permanent Debt:** LIHTC properties can finance a portion of their cost with conventional debt, though because rents are restricted below market, debt capacity is limited by the NOI generated at restricted rents. Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and FHA all have LIHTC-specific debt programs. HUD Section 8 Project-Based Rental Assistance (PBRA) contracts, if attached to the property, increase NOI predictability and can support higher loan amounts.

**Soft Debt and Grants:** The gap between total development cost and the sum of tax credit equity plus supportable debt is typically filled by "soft money" — forgivable or deferred-payment loans from state and local housing agencies, HOME funds (federal formula grants administered by states and localities), Community Development Block Grant (CDBG) funds, and other public sources. Soft debt typically accrues interest (if any) but is not required to be repaid from operations, making it the critical gap-filling tool in affordable housing finance.

**Developer Fee:** The developer earns a developer fee (typically 8–15% of eligible basis, subject to limits set by the HFA) as compensation for the development process. A portion of the developer fee is typically deferred — paid from future cash flow — and the remainder is paid at construction completion.

**Nonprofit Basis Boost:** Nonprofit co-developers or sponsors in a 9% transaction can qualify for a basis boost of up to 130% of eligible basis, increasing the credit generation for the same level of physical development and improving financial feasibility.

## Compliance Management

LIHTC compliance is the ongoing obligation that distinguishes affordable housing operations from market-rate multifamily. Key operational requirements:

- **Move-in certification:** Every new resident must be certified as income-eligible before or at move-in; documentation requirements are specific (verifiable income, assets, student status) and must be maintained in the property file.
- **Annual recertification:** Existing tenants are recertified annually (though some programs allow streamlined recertification for under-threshold households).
- **Unit set-asides:** The number and targeting level of income-restricted units must be maintained throughout the compliance period; failing to maintain set-asides is a compliance violation.
- **Rent limits:** Rents charged must not exceed the published limits for the applicable income tier and unit size; rent increases must be tracked against updated AMI limits.
- **Annual reporting:** HFAs require annual audits or reviews of tenant files and financial statements; non-compliance findings can result in cure periods, penalty assessments, or credit recapture referral to the IRS.
- **Extended use compliance:** After the 15-year initial compliance period, extended use agreements (recorded as deed restrictions) continue to govern the property for an additional 15–30+ years.

## Investment Considerations

Affordable housing development and investment offers a distinct profile compared to market-rate multifamily: lower absolute returns (restricted rents limit NOI growth), but lower revenue volatility (restricted rents are more stable than market rents, and demand typically exceeds supply in most markets) and a deep government subsidy backstop. Developer fee income front-loads returns; ongoing cash flow from operations is typically modest.

Corporate tax credit investors earn returns primarily from the tax benefit (credits and depreciation deductions), not from property cash flow or appreciation — this makes the investment structure fundamentally different from market-rate equity investment. Institutional investors in LIHTC (banks, insurance companies) are primarily motivated by CRA (Community Reinvestment Act) credit and federal tax liability management.

Affordable housing development is technically and administratively complex: assembling subsidy sources, navigating HFA application processes, managing compliance, and coordinating multiple public partners requires specialized expertise. Projects regularly take 3–5 years from conception to construction commencement. Market-rate developers entering affordable housing typically partner with experienced affordable housing specialists or nonprofits to manage the complexity.
