# Multifamily — Risks & Considerations

## Overview

Multifamily is widely regarded as the most resilient CRE sector through economic cycles — housing demand is relatively inelastic, leases re-price quickly, and Agency financing provides a stable liquidity floor. However, the sector is not without significant risk. The risks below are organized by category: market conditions, operational execution, capital and financing structure, and physical/structural characteristics. Each risk is paired with the standard institutional mitigants. For regulatory-specific risks (rent control, fair housing, LIHTC compliance), see [[Multifamily — Regulatory Considerations]].

---

### Market

**Supply Overshoot / New Deliveries Compression.** Multifamily development cycles are notoriously prone to oversupply: when market conditions are favorable, multiple developers start projects simultaneously, and because construction timelines run 18–36 months, supply delivered 2–3 years later often hits a market that has already softened. Sun Belt metros (Austin, Phoenix, Charlotte, Nashville) experienced significant supply overhang in 2023–2025 after record deliveries compressed rent growth and occupancy. *Mitigants:* Submarket-level supply pipeline analysis (see [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]]); underwriting conservative rent growth during near-term delivery peaks; acquiring in supply-constrained submarkets with high entitlement barriers; phasing investments to avoid peak supply cycles.

**Demand Cyclicality / Employment Shock.** Multifamily occupancy and rent growth are correlated with job growth and in-migration. A major employer departure or regional economic recession can materially reduce household formation, increase vacancies, and force concessions. *Mitigants:* Diversified employment base in the target submarket; underwriting conservative absorption scenarios; holding adequate operating reserves; stress-testing at recessionary vacancy assumptions (see [[Stress Testing]]).

**Rent-to-Income Compression.** When asking rents approach affordability ceilings (typically 30–35% of median household income), organic rent growth stalls. The ceiling is hardest in markets with limited wage growth, high cost-of-living, or large lower-income renter populations. *Mitigants:* Tracking rent-to-income ratios by submarket and unit type; underwriting rent growth at levels supportable by median income growth; diversifying across price points (workforce vs. luxury).

**Interest Rate Sensitivity / Cap Rate Expansion.** Rising interest rates tend to push cap rates upward, compressing values even when NOI is stable or growing. A 50 bps cap rate expansion on a $50M property can erase $4–7M of equity value. This is particularly acute for assets acquired at compressed cap rates during low-rate environments. *Mitigants:* Conservative going-in cap rate assumptions; purchasing yield cushion (sufficient in-place cash flow); long-term fixed-rate financing to de-link debt cost from value; avoiding over-leverage that amplifies mark-to-market sensitivity.

**Submarket-Level Deterioration.** Crime increases, school quality decline, employer departures, or deteriorating infrastructure can make a submarket less competitive over time, reducing renter demand and depressing effective rents relative to the broader metro. *Mitigants:* Rigorous submarket-level due diligence ([[Submarket Definition and Selection]]); diversification across submarkets; monitoring leading indicators (school ratings, permit activity, employment data) during hold.

### Operational

**Property Management Execution Risk.** Multifamily is operationally intensive: high unit counts, high turnover (65–80% of units may turn annually), resident relations, maintenance responsiveness, and leasing execution all depend on on-site staffing quality. Poor management leads to rising vacancies, higher bad debt, and deferred maintenance. *Mitigants:* Rigorous property manager selection and contract negotiation; clear performance benchmarks (occupancy, renewal rate, bad debt, response times); regular asset management oversight (see [[Asset Management vs. Property Management]]); benchmarking against competing properties.

**Deferred Maintenance and CapEx Creep.** Older vintage properties (pre-1990 especially) often have deferred maintenance that was not visible during due diligence: aging plumbing, outdated electrical systems, failing HVAC units, or deteriorating foundations. These surprises can consume value-add equity returns. *Mitigants:* Thorough pre-acquisition property condition assessment (PCA) by a qualified engineer; robust CapEx reserve underwriting; phased renovation programs that surface hidden deferred maintenance early; contingency budgets of 10–15% on value-add programs.

**Value-Add Execution Risk.** Renovation programs can face contractor delays, cost overruns, permitting issues, and lower-than-projected rent premiums. The thesis that tenants will pay $150–$200/month more for renovated units may prove overly optimistic if the competitive set offers equal quality at lower rents. *Mitigants:* Market-testing renovated unit rents before committing to a full program; fixed-price contracts with qualified GCs; realistic renovation timelines that account for occupied versus vacant unit phasing; conservative underwriting of premium capture.

**Turnover and Leasing Cost.** High turnover at a large community generates substantial recurring costs — unit make-ready, marketing and leasing agent fees, and vacancy downtime. At $1,500–$3,000 per turn and 60–70% annual turnover on a 300-unit property, annual turnover costs can easily exceed $300,000. *Mitigants:* Retention programs (renewal incentives, responsive maintenance); pricing discipline (avoid outsized rent increases that drive out satisfied tenants); efficient make-ready processes.

**Fraud and Tenant Screening Risk.** Application fraud — falsified income documentation, synthetic identities — is an ongoing challenge. Placing a poorly qualified tenant leads to eviction costs, legal fees, and unit damage. *Mitigants:* Robust screening protocols (income/employment verification, credit check, rental history); third-party screening services with fraud detection; consistent, fair housing-compliant criteria applied uniformly.

### Capital & Financing

**Floating Rate Exposure.** Bridge loans and adjustable-rate Agency loans expose borrowers to payment increases when benchmark rates rise. A 300 bps increase in SOFR on a $30M bridge loan increases annual interest expense by $900,000 — a significant NOI drag. *Mitigants:* Interest rate caps (purchased at origination, renewed as needed); early refinancing into fixed-rate permanent debt when stabilization is achieved; stress-testing cash flow at rates 200–300 bps above the current strike (see [[Stress Testing]]).

**Refinance Risk.** Value-add deals underwritten on the assumption of refinancing into Agency permanent debt at stabilization face risk if stabilization takes longer than projected, if cap rates have expanded (reducing appraised value), or if the financing market tightens. *Mitigants:* Conservative stabilization timelines; adequate equity cushion (not over-leveraged at acquisition); extension options on bridge loans; relationship with multiple Agency lenders to maximize competitive execution.

**Construction Loan Maturity / Cost Overrun.** Development deals face the risk that construction takes longer or costs more than projected, causing the construction loan to mature before stabilization. *Mitigants:* Adequate contingency reserves (10–15% hard cost contingency); fixed-price GMP contracts; extension options; conservative draw schedules tied to verified completion milestones.

**LP Capital Call Risk.** Value-add and development JVs may require additional equity if pro forma assumptions are missed. LPs may resist or be unable to fund capital calls, creating friction with the GP and potentially forcing distressed asset management decisions. *Mitigants:* Conservative underwriting with explicit downside scenarios; adequate preferred return structures that align LP and GP interests; clear capital call provisions in the JV agreement; maintaining GP co-investment relationships with high-quality LP partners.

**Insurance Cost Escalation.** Property and casualty insurance costs have increased dramatically in coastal, Gulf, and wildfire-exposed markets. In some Florida markets, premiums have risen 50–100%+ in 3–5 years. *Mitigants:* Factor realistic insurance cost growth into underwriting; obtain competitive bids from multiple carriers; consider geographic diversification away from high-climate-risk markets; explore captive insurance or large deductible structures for large portfolios.

### Structural & Physical

**Deferred Capital Expenditures in Aging Stock.** Value-add garden and mid-rise properties from the 1970s–1990s often have aging roofs, plumbing systems (galvanized steel or polybutylene in some vintage properties), original electrical panels, and HVAC systems that are near end of life. *Mitigants:* Engineering-level PCA at acquisition; budget for full-system replacement programs; prioritize safety-critical capital items (plumbing, electrical, fire safety); track CapEx run rate relative to reserves.

**Environmental Risk.** Older multifamily properties (pre-1980) may contain asbestos-containing materials (ACM) or lead-based paint; sites may have underground storage tanks (USTs) or prior contaminated uses. Renovation of pre-1978 properties triggers lead paint RRP (Renovation, Repair, and Painting) federal compliance requirements. *Mitigants:* Phase I Environmental Site Assessment at acquisition; Phase II if Phase I identifies recognized environmental conditions (RECs); asbestos and lead surveys on pre-1980 properties before renovation; contractual representations and indemnities from sellers.

**Natural Disaster and Climate Risk.** Flood, hurricane, wildfire, and earthquake risk can cause material physical damage and business interruption. Climate risk is intensifying in many markets — coastal Florida flood and wind risk, California wildfire exposure, and Texas freeze risk (as demonstrated in 2021) are institutional-level concerns. *Mitigants:* Flood zone analysis and FEMA map review at acquisition; site-specific natural hazard assessments; adequate catastrophic insurance coverage; geographic diversification across risk zones; pro forma stress-testing for business interruption scenarios.

**Seismic Risk (West Coast / Pacific Northwest).** Soft-story wood-frame buildings — common in 1960s–1980s multifamily construction in Los Angeles, San Francisco, Seattle, and other seismic regions — are vulnerable to significant structural damage in earthquakes. Many California jurisdictions have adopted mandatory soft-story retrofit ordinances. *Mitigants:* Seismic inspection and structural engineering review at acquisition; budget for retrofit compliance if required; confirm insurance coverage for earthquake damage; understand the local ordinance compliance timeline.
