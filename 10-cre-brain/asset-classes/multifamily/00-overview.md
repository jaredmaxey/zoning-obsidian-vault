# Multifamily

## Overview

Multifamily residential — purpose-built rental housing with five or more units — is widely considered the most liquid and institutionally accessible commercial real estate sector. The asset class encompasses a spectrum from garden-style suburban communities to high-rise urban towers, plus newer residential formats such as build-to-rent (BTR) single-family communities and niche segments like student and senior housing. Institutional demand for the sector is underpinned by several structural advantages: operating leases of one year or less give landlords relatively rapid re-pricing ability during inflationary periods; the product type qualifies for government-sponsored enterprise (GSE) financing through Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, providing deep, consistent liquidity at competitive rates; and the United States housing supply deficit — structural underbuilding relative to household formation over the past 15 years — supports long-term occupancy and rent fundamentals across most major markets.

Multifamily trades across all four risk buckets. Core institutional capital targets stabilized, Class A properties in gateway and high-growth Sun Belt metros, compressing cap rates to levels that reflect the sector's bond-like income profile. Value-add investors pursue 1970s–2000s vintage garden and mid-rise communities where organic rent growth is augmented by interior renovation programs (unit upgrades, amenity enhancements) that can drive meaningful mark-to-market rent uplifts. Opportunistic and development-oriented capital targets ground-up construction in supply-constrained markets or adaptive reuse of office and hotel product. Sentiment is sensitive to new supply cycles: when development pipelines surge — as they did in the early 2020s across Sun Belt metros — rent growth softens even in fundamentally strong markets, making vintage selection and submarket precision critical.

The sector's headline thesis is demographic: millennials and Gen Z household formation, high single-family ownership costs (elevated home prices plus elevated mortgage rates), and growing renter-by-choice behavior among higher-income cohorts all support sustained rental demand. Counterbalancing risks include the concentration of near-term supply deliveries in certain metros, affordability constraints limiting rent growth, and legislative pressure around rent regulation in coastal and progressive jurisdictions. Institutional investors must also distinguish between workforce/affordable housing and luxury Class A product, as regulatory, financing, and operational dynamics differ substantially between segments.

## Subtypes

Multifamily breaks into several distinct product types with different construction profiles, capital requirements, and investor bases. See [[Multifamily — Subtypes]] and [[Garden vs. Mid-Rise vs. High-Rise]] for detailed treatment.

- **Garden Apartment** — Two- to three-story, surface-parked suburban communities typically built on larger land parcels. Lowest construction cost per unit; the dominant product type by unit count nationwide.
- **Mid-Rise Apartment** — Four- to eight-story buildings, often with structured or podium parking. Common in urban infill and transit-adjacent suburban locations; higher cost than garden but broader market reach than high-rise.
- **High-Rise Apartment** — Nine-plus stories, elevator-served, with structured parking. Concentrated in dense urban cores; highest per-unit cost and most complex operations.
- **Build-to-Rent / SFR** — Purpose-built for-rent single-family homes or horizontal apartments, marketed to renters preferring the privacy and space of a single-family home. See [[Build-to-Rent / SFR]].
- **Student Housing** — Proximity-to-campus communities (on- or off-campus) leased by the bedroom to university enrollees. See [[Student Housing (Multifamily)]].
- **Senior Housing** — A spectrum from active adult (55+, age-qualified) to independent living, assisted living, memory care, and skilled nursing. Regulatory and operational complexity increases with care intensity. See [[Senior Housing Spectrum]].

## Demand Drivers

The primary demand driver is household formation — the rate at which individuals and couples establish independent residences. Household formation is a function of population cohort size (the Millennial generation is the largest in U.S. history), age (formation peaks in the 25–34 cohort), income, and the relative cost of owning versus renting. When mortgage rates are elevated relative to rents, the rent-versus-buy math skews toward renting, expanding the effective renter pool beyond structurally price-excluded households to include households that could own but choose not to.

Employment and income growth are leading indicators because job gains drive in-migration, household formation, and the wage growth necessary to absorb rent increases. Markets with diversified, tech-forward, or healthcare-heavy employment bases tend to exhibit more resilient multifamily fundamentals through cycles. Net migration patterns — particularly the sustained Sun Belt in-migration from higher-cost coastal metros — have reshaped the geography of institutional multifamily demand over the past decade. Secondary and tertiary markets with strong university presences, regional medical centers, or military installations can punch above their demographic weight in occupancy stability.

Affordability is a demand governor as much as a demand driver. When asking rents approach 30–35% of median household income in a submarket, organic rent growth stalls or reverses as households double up, seek cheaper submarkets, or accept lower-quality product. Tracking rent-to-income ratios at the submarket level — combined with home price-to-income and effective mortgage payment-to-income — gives the clearest picture of sustainable rent growth runway. Supply absorption rates (how quickly newly delivered units reach stabilization) are the most actionable near-term indicator of demand depth.

## Supply Dynamics

Multifamily development cycles are driven by the spread between development yields and market cap rates, the availability and cost of construction financing, and the pace of entitlement approvals. When the going-in yield on a stabilized new project (yield on cost) exceeds prevailing cap rates by a sufficient margin — historically 150–200+ basis points — development activity accelerates. Developers begin projects when that spread compresses, often miscalculating how much additional supply their comps will face by the time their project delivers 24–36 months later. The resulting supply overshoot is a recurring multifamily cycle feature, most visible in markets with permissive zoning and low construction barriers (Phoenix, Dallas, Austin in the early 2020s) versus those with constrained entitlement (coastal California, New York, Seattle).

Construction costs — driven by labor availability, materials prices, and land cost — are the primary feasibility gate. Garden-style construction is meaningfully cheaper per unit than podium mid-rise or high-rise, which is why suburban markets attract more development activity in cost-sensitive cycles. High-rise product in gateway cities carries the highest all-in cost and often requires institutional equity or public subsidy to pencil, limiting its responsiveness to demand signals. Development timelines range from 18–24 months for garden communities to 30–48 months for complex urban high-rise projects, meaning the supply entering a market today largely reflects decisions made 2–4 years ago.

Barriers to entry vary enormously: zoning, neighborhood opposition, impact fees, and available land are the dominant constraints. Rent-controlled or rent-stabilized jurisdictions effectively cap return potential on new development, reducing supply incentives over time and contributing to affordability deterioration in those markets. |--------|---------------|-------|
| Going-in cap rate | 4.0% – 6.5% | Class A urban core tighter; Class B/C suburban wider; varies significantly by market and vintage |

| Replacement reserves | $250 – $450/unit/year | Varies by age, type, and lender requirement |

## Key Operating Metrics

See [[Multifamily — Key Metrics]] for full definitions and formulas.

The core multifamily performance metrics are effective rent per unit, physical occupancy, economic occupancy, loss-to-lease, concessions as a percent of asking rent, and expense ratio. Revenue per available unit (RevPAU) aggregates rent and ancillary income against the total unit count, providing a single-line comparable across properties. The delta between physical occupancy (units occupied / total units) and economic occupancy (collected rent / potential rent at full occupancy with no concessions) reveals the true revenue drag from concessions, bad debt, and rent delinquency.

## Common Deal Structures

Multifamily acquisitions are structured as all-cash or levered deals; most institutional acquisitions use 55–65% LTV senior debt. Agency debt (Fannie Mae DUS, Freddie Mac Optigo) is the dominant financing source for stabilized assets — it is non-recourse, long-term (5–10 year fixed or adjustable), and carries the most competitive pricing of any CRE debt product. Bridge loans and construction loans are used for transitional and development assets, respectively. See [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]], [[Bridge Loan]], and [[Construction Loan]].

Joint venture structures are common: an operator/sponsor contributes 5–20% of equity and earns a promote (carried interest) above a preferred return hurdle, with institutional LP capital providing the majority of equity. Waterfall structures typically include a 6–8% preferred return, a 50–80% LP catch-up, and a 20–35% GP promote thereafter. See [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] and [[Joint Venture Structures]].

Leases are gross (tenant pays rent, landlord pays all operating expenses including utilities) or modified gross (tenant pays some utilities directly). Standard residential leases run 12 months with rolling month-to-month thereafter. The 12-month lease term is simultaneously an asset (re-pricing ability) and a management burden (high turnover friction relative to long-term NNN leases).

## Major Risks

See [[Multifamily — Risks & Considerations]] for the full risk register.

Key risks include: supply-driven rent softening in overdeveloped submarkets; regulatory risk from rent control or stabilization legislation; interest rate sensitivity (cap rate expansion when rates rise compresses values even when NOI is stable); operational complexity (high unit count × high turnover = significant property management burden); and physical/deferred maintenance risk in older vintage product. Workforce and affordable housing segments face additional pressure from rising operating costs (insurance, property taxes) that can erode NOI in jurisdictions with limited rent growth.

## Regulatory Considerations

See [[Multifamily — Regulatory Considerations]] for full coverage.

The most significant class-specific regulatory risks are rent control/stabilization (active in California, New York, Oregon, New Jersey, and many major cities), just-cause eviction ordinances, fair housing compliance (Fair Housing Act, ADA accessibility), LIHTC compliance for affordable properties (see [[Affordable Housing & LIHTC]]), and local landlord-tenant law governing notice periods, security deposits, and habitability standards. High-rise and urban infill projects face more intensive building code and fire/life-safety requirements. Condo conversion regulations affect exit optionality.

## Notable Markets

Gateway markets (New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Boston, Washington D.C.) historically commanded the lowest cap rates due to supply constraints, deep liquidity, and tenant demand — though affordability ceilings and regulatory risk have moderated their institutional appeal. Sun Belt growth markets (Dallas, Atlanta, Phoenix, Charlotte, Nashville, Tampa) attracted the heaviest development activity in the 2020s, delivering significant new supply that softened near-term rent growth but supports long-term demand as population migration continues. Midwest markets (Minneapolis, Columbus, Indianapolis, Kansas City) offer wider cap rates and more stable demand without the supply/regulatory extremes of gateway or Sun Belt markets. Supply constraints are most severe in coastal California cities and other jurisdictions with slow entitlement timelines and significant NIMBYism.
