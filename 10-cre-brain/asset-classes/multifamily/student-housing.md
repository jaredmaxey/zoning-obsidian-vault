# Student Housing (Multifamily)

## Overview

Student housing is purpose-built or repositioned multifamily housing serving university and college enrollees, physically located on or adjacent to campus. It is a specialized niche within multifamily, sharing the sector's fundamental operating model — leasing units, collecting rent, maintaining property — while exhibiting distinctive characteristics that create both higher yield potential and higher operational risk than conventional apartments. The by-the-bed leasing structure, academic calendar dependency, enrollment-correlated demand, and specialized amenity requirements define the asset class and differentiate it from conventional apartment investment.

The institutional student housing sector grew rapidly in the 2000s and 2010s, driven by American Campus Communities (ACC), the dominant public REIT (taken private by Blackstone in 2022), and a large cohort of regional and national private operators. Today, institutional ownership of off-campus student housing is concentrated at the largest and most enrollment-stable university markets — the Power Five athletic conference institutions, large state university systems, and Ivy League and elite private universities.

---

## By-the-Bed Leasing Structure

The defining structural difference from conventional multifamily is that student housing is typically leased **by the bedroom**, not by the unit. A 4-bedroom, 4-bathroom apartment is leased as four separate contracts: each occupant signs an individual lease for their bedroom and a proportional share of common areas. Each tenant is individually responsible for their own rent — there is no joint-and-several liability across roommates. This structure:

- **Increases revenue per square foot** relative to conventional multifamily, since the effective rent is collected on a per-bed basis, and beds can be priced individually
- **Reduces collection risk** from roommate disputes or co-tenant default (one roommate's non-payment does not affect others' obligations)
- **Increases management complexity** because each bed has a separate lease, lease-up must achieve pre-lease rates across all beds rather than units, and parental guarantors are common
- **Requires specialized property management software** that tracks bed-level occupancy, lease terms, and payments

Some student housing properties also offer by-the-unit leasing (particularly graduate student-oriented or more affordable properties), but by-the-bed is the dominant institutional format for new construction.

## Pre-Leasing and the Academic Calendar

Student housing operates on an academic calendar — most leases commence in August or September and run 12 months. Pre-leasing activity (signing leases for the following academic year) begins 8–12 months in advance, typically in October–January for August occupancy. Pre-lease rates — the percentage of beds leased by a specific date (e.g., "85% pre-leased as of March 1") — are the most closely watched real-time performance metric and a key indicator of competitive positioning relative to peers.

A property that achieves 90%+ pre-lease by April is in strong competitive position; one that reaches lease commencement below 85% faces concession pressure and potential occupancy risk. The academic calendar also creates seasonality risk: if a property fails to lease up by the August occupancy date, it faces a full year of partial vacancy before the next lease cycle — there is no mid-year recovery path of the type available to conventional apartments (which re-lease units continuously throughout the year).

## Demand Drivers

**University enrollment** is the foundational demand driver — student housing demand is derived demand from the university's ability to attract and retain students. Markets with large flagship state universities (25,000–60,000+ students) offer the deepest and most resilient demand bases. Enrollment trends — demographic (U.S. high school graduation rates peaked in the early 2010s and are projected to decline through the early 2030s in many states), policy-driven (international student enrollment depends on visa policy), and competitive (inter-institutional competition for declining domestic enrollment) — are the primary long-term risk for the sector.

**On-campus housing competition** is a critical supply consideration. Universities actively compete for student housing revenue; when a university builds new residence halls, it can materially reduce the addressable market for private off-campus housing. Institutional due diligence must include analysis of university on-campus housing plans, current on-campus bed counts relative to enrollment, and stated university policy on housing requirements.

**Proximity to campus** — measured in walk-time or transit distance — is the single most important site selection criterion. Properties within 0.5 miles of the core campus command the highest rents and achieve the fastest pre-lease rates; properties 1+ mile away must compete primarily on price. "On-campus" properties developed on university-owned land via ground lease arrangements (common with American Campus Communities) command the deepest demand.

**Amenities and lifestyle** compete as a secondary demand driver. Student housing amenities arms races in competitive markets have produced resort-style pools, gaming rooms, group study spaces, fitness centers with professional equipment, and rooftop decks. Top-of-market amenities drive premium rent positioning; mid-market properties can compete on value and location without matching the luxury amenity profile.

## Supply and Competition

Student housing supply risk is concentrated at markets where new off-campus development has outpaced enrollment growth, or where the university has expanded on-campus capacity. The 2010s saw significant oversupply in several major student housing markets (Tempe, AZ; Tallahassee, FL; various Texas markets), driven by aggressive capital deployment and relatively low development barriers in college towns. Markets with natural or regulatory supply constraints (limited suitable land within walkable distance, historic preservation zones, university-controlled land buffers) exhibit more supply discipline.

Unlike conventional multifamily markets, student housing competitive sets are geographically limited by the proximity-to-campus constraint — a new luxury property 0.8 miles from campus directly competes with a comparable property 0.3 miles away because students' location decision is dominated by walk-time. This makes submarket-level supply analysis extremely granular — "how many new beds are delivering within 0.5 miles of the main campus gate in the next 24 months" is the operative question.

## Underwriting Considerations

Student housing underwriting adapts conventional multifamily methods to the by-the-bed structure. Revenue is modeled on a per-bed basis (effective rent/bed × total beds × economic occupancy); operating expenses are broadly similar to conventional multifamily, with higher marketing and leasing costs (pre-lease campaigns, digital marketing to incoming students), slightly higher turnover costs (students leave at end of academic year in large numbers), and utilities that may be bundled in rent (a common student housing offering that adds both marketing value and expense management complexity).

Key underwriting metrics:
- Pre-lease rate by week/month (leading indicator of occupancy)
- Rent per bed by bedroom configuration (1BR/1BA studio beds command the highest rents; 4BR/4BA beds the lowest on a per-bed basis)
- Occupancy at lease commencement versus move-in (students sometimes sign and do not move in)
- Operating expense ratio (typically 45–55% of EGI in student housing, higher than comparable conventional multifamily due to amenity intensity and turnover)
- University enrollment growth (5-year trend and projected)

Agency financing (Fannie/Freddie) is available for stabilized student housing; CMBS is an alternative for larger assets. Lenders apply more scrutiny to enrollment trends, on-campus competition, and pre-lease velocity than in conventional multifamily underwriting.

## Institutional Landscape and Risk Summary

Institutional student housing investment is concentrated at universities with 20,000+ students, historical enrollment stability, and robust academic reputations that attract sustained enrollment demand. The asset class has historically offered yield premiums of 50–150 bps over comparable conventional multifamily, reflecting the operational complexity and enrollment risk premium. The major risks are: (1) enrollment decline — demographic or institutional; (2) university on-campus housing expansion; (3) new supply oversaturation; (4) academic calendar inflexibility (no mid-year re-leasing opportunity); and (5) per-unit expense intensity. The sector's tailwinds are the higher yield, strong amenity-driven pricing power at top-tier institutions, and the structural underbuilding of quality near-campus housing at many major universities.
