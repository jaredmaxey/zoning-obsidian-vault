# Senior Housing Spectrum

## Overview

Senior housing is not a single asset type but a spectrum of residential and care settings designed for older adults, distinguished primarily by the level of care and services provided. Moving from least to most intensive, the spectrum runs: age-restricted active adult (55+) communities, independent living (IL), assisted living (AL), memory care (MC), and skilled nursing facilities (SNF). The physical design, licensing requirements, staffing models, revenue sources, and investor profiles differ substantially at each level of care. This note covers the full spectrum with emphasis on the segments most commonly targeted by real estate investors — active adult through memory care — while noting the distinct healthcare-facility dynamics of skilled nursing.

Senior housing is often discussed in the same breath as multifamily because the lower-acuity formats (active adult, IL) are residential housing products with operations closely resembling conventional apartment management. As care intensity increases through AL, MC, and SNF, the asset transitions from a real estate investment into a healthcare operating business where operating profitability depends on staffing, clinical outcomes, and government reimbursement as much as real estate fundamentals.

---

## Active Adult / Age-Restricted (55+)

Active adult communities are age-restricted apartment or single-family communities where all or a defined percentage of residents must be 55 or older (per the Housing for Older Persons Act, or HOPA). No care or healthcare services are provided — residents are independent, active, and do not require assistance with activities of daily living (ADLs). The product is marketed on lifestyle: resort-style amenities, active programming (fitness, social events, travel, lifelong learning), and a community of peers at a similar life stage. Private patios or yards, accessible unit design, and single-story options are physical features that attract the active adult renter.

Active adult is the closest segment to conventional multifamily in its operating model — residential leases, rent-based revenue, no healthcare services, no licensing beyond standard apartment regulations. It is the fastest-growing and most straightforward senior housing investment format for generalist multifamily investors and developers. Demand is driven by the leading edge of the Baby Boomer cohort (born 1946–1964) entering their late-60s and 70s — the largest senior cohort in U.S. history. Cap rates and investment characteristics are similar to conventional Class A multifamily, with a modest premium reflecting the niche demand base and slightly higher operating costs for programming and amenities.

---

## Independent Living (IL)

Independent living communities provide housing, meals (typically one to three meals per day in a communal dining room), and lifestyle amenities to seniors who are capable of self-care but desire a maintenance-free environment with social programming and optional support services. IL residents are typically 75–85 years old, do not require assistance with ADLs, and choose IL primarily for social connection, safety, and freedom from home maintenance. Monthly fees are typically all-inclusive (housing + meals + amenities + some transportation) rather than base-rent-plus-services, and fees are typically paid privately (not covered by Medicare or Medicaid in most forms).

IL operations are more complex than active adult: food service (a commercial kitchen, dining room, and staff) represents a major operating cost and a significant quality differentiator. IL facilities typically require state licensing (standards vary) and must maintain certain staffing ratios and safety protocols. Revenue visibility is higher than conventional apartments because all-inclusive monthly fees are typically paid in advance and move-in deposits are common. The primary risk is occupancy — maintaining 85–95%+ occupancy is essential to covering the high fixed operating costs.

From an investment perspective, IL is commonly bundled with AL in continuing-care campuses and marketed to investors as "seniors housing" (using the NCREIF/NIC definition). RIDEA structures — in which the real estate owner shares directly in operating income rather than receiving a flat lease — are common for institutional IL/AL portfolios. Cap rates for IL typically range modestly above conventional multifamily, reflecting the operating complexity and care licensing requirement.

---

## Assisted Living (AL)

Assisted living provides housing plus assistance with ADLs — bathing, dressing, medication management, mobility assistance — for residents who need help with daily functions but do not require 24-hour skilled nursing care. AL facilities are licensed by state health departments and must meet staffing ratios, training requirements, and inspection standards that vary significantly by state. Monthly fees are typically privately paid (private pay), though Medicaid waiver programs in some states pay for the care component for qualifying low-income residents. Medicare generally does not cover AL.

AL operations are distinctly more complex than IL or active adult: care staff (CNAs, medication technicians, and supervisors) represent 50–60% or more of operating costs; occupancy is directly driven by referrals from hospitals, physicians, and senior living advisors; and resident length of stay is unpredictable (average 22–30 months nationally, though highly variable). The private-pay revenue model provides relative insulation from government reimbursement risk compared to SNF, but makes demand more sensitive to general economic conditions (families paying $4,000–$8,000+/month from savings) and competitive pricing.

Institutional investment in AL is typically through operator-owner partnerships — the real estate owner provides capital and owns the facility; a specialized operator provides clinical staff, management systems, and compliance expertise. Operator selection and contract structure are among the most critical investment decisions in AL. RIDEA structures are standard for institutional portfolios.

## Memory Care (MC)

Memory care is a specialized form of assisted living designed for residents with Alzheimer's disease or other forms of dementia. Physical design features — secure perimeters, way-finding aids, non-stimulating color palettes, safe wandering paths — distinguish MC from standard AL. Staffing ratios are higher than AL due to the supervision requirements of cognitively impaired residents. Monthly fees are typically 20–40% higher than comparable AL at the same location, reflecting the higher care intensity and specialized environment.

Memory care is typically provided in stand-alone MC facilities or dedicated wings of IL/AL campuses. The demand trajectory is exceptionally strong — the prevalence of dementia increases sharply with age, and the 80+ population cohort will grow substantially through the 2040s. Supply discipline is important because MC is a more specialized product with a narrower catchment area. Regulatory and licensing requirements are the most intensive of any AL format.

---

## Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNF)

Skilled nursing facilities (SNFs, also called nursing homes) provide 24-hour licensed nursing care and rehabilitation services to residents requiring post-acute care (following hospital discharge) or chronic long-term care. SNFs are Medicare and Medicaid certified healthcare providers — unlike AL, a substantial portion of SNF revenue is reimbursed by Medicare (for post-acute rehabilitation stays) and Medicaid (for long-term custodial care for low-income residents). This government reimbursement dependency fundamentally distinguishes SNF from all other senior housing formats.

SNF investment is primarily undertaken by specialized healthcare real estate investors and operators. Cap rates are typically higher than IL/AL to compensate for reimbursement risk, regulatory intensity, and operating complexity. The healthcare and real estate aspects are deeply intertwined — staffing levels directly affect Medicare quality ratings, which affect referral volumes and occupancy, which drive revenue and NOI. Triple-net leases (where the operator pays rent to a REIT or property owner) were the traditional SNF investment structure, though RIDEA and joint venture structures have grown. Generalist multifamily investors generally avoid SNF investment absent specialized operational capability.

## Demand Tailwinds and Investment Thesis

The senior housing sector's headline demand thesis is among the strongest in all of CRE: the Baby Boomer cohort (approximately 72 million individuals, born 1946–1964) is entering peak senior housing demand age (75–85+) between approximately 2020 and 2040. The 80+ population in the U.S. is projected to approximately double over this period. Meanwhile, the industry delivered relatively little new supply in 2020–2023 (disrupted by COVID-19's devastating operational impact, which reset investor confidence and caused widespread financial distress in IL/AL operators), creating a setup for supply/demand imbalance favoring landlords and operators from the mid-2020s forward.

The investment thesis — buy or develop quality IL/AL/MC assets in supply-constrained markets with strong aging demographics, partner with high-quality operators, and benefit from occupancy recovery and rate escalation as post-COVID demand normalizes — has been the organizing institutional narrative for the sector. Key risks include operating cost inflation (labor is the dominant operating cost, and caregiving labor markets are structurally tight), operator quality and financial stability, regulatory and licensure risk, and the lingering reputational and occupancy legacy of COVID-19 on the industry.
