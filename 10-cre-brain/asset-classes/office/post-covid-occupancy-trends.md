# Post-COVID Occupancy Trends

The COVID-19 pandemic and its aftermath constitute the most significant structural disruption to U.S. office demand in modern commercial real estate history. While prior demand shocks (dot-com bust, Global Financial Crisis) were cyclical — demand contracted, then recovered as employment growth resumed — the post-2020 shift involves a change in the relationship between headcount and space demand that has not fully reversed and may be permanent in its altered equilibrium.

## Physical Utilization vs. Leased Occupancy

A critical distinction for office market analysis is the difference between physical utilization (what percentage of seats are occupied on any given day) and leased occupancy (what percentage of rentable square footage is under signed lease). Leased occupancy declined significantly from pre-pandemic highs in most markets but stabilized at elevated levels (15–25%+ vacancy in many CBDs, varies by market and vintage) by 2024–2025. Physical utilization, as measured by badge access data, building security systems, and third-party mobility data, has remained well below 2019 norms in most U.S. cities.

National average physical office utilization in most U.S. CBDs has ranged from 40–60% of pre-pandemic baseline even in periods of strong economic conditions (varies by market, industry composition, and season). This gap between leased occupancy and physical utilization signals that a substantial portion of currently leased space is functionally underused — companies are holding space under existing lease terms but actively right-sizing as leases roll. The "shadow vacancy" represented by subleased space actively marketed by existing tenants is a leading indicator of future direct vacancy as leases expire.

## Hybrid Work Normalization

The normalization of hybrid work (typically 2–3 in-office days per week for knowledge workers, varies widely by industry and company culture) has two contradictory effects on office demand. On the one hand, hybrid allows companies to consolidate to smaller headquarters while providing employees greater location flexibility. On the other hand, companies implementing "hoteling" (unassigned seating) can potentially absorb some portion of space reduction through denser utilization on the days when employees do come in.

In practice, most institutional studies suggest that the net effect of hybrid normalization has been negative for aggregate square footage demand, primarily because: (1) real-world utilization rates in hoteling environments rarely achieve the theoretical desk-sharing ratios used to justify downsizing; (2) conferencing and collaboration space requirements often offset individual workstation reductions; and (3) many companies have been slow to fully implement space reductions during multi-year lease terms, meaning the downsizing happens at renewal rather than immediately.

Industry composition matters enormously. Financial services, professional services, and healthcare-adjacent companies have largely returned to higher in-office attendance rates and are closer to pre-COVID space-per-worker norms. Technology companies — which drove enormous space demand in the 2015–2020 period — have been the most aggressive adopters of hybrid and remote policies, and their lease consolidations represent a disproportionate share of the sublease supply that drove market-level vacancy increases in Bay Area, Austin, Seattle, and Denver office markets.

## Flight to Quality

The most consistent observed trend in post-COVID leasing activity is "flight to quality" — tenants that are reducing overall square footage are concentrating the retained space in the highest-quality buildings with the best amenities, energy efficiency, and location. Class A and trophy buildings in well-located submarkets have maintained substantially stronger occupancy and rent levels than the overall market averages would suggest, because shrinking tenants have upgraded building quality as they downsized.

This dynamic has bifurcated the market sharply: best-in-class product has held value and in some markets seen rent growth; Class B and C commodity product has faced compounding vacancy and functional obsolescence challenges with little prospect of reversal without major capital investment or conversion. For investors, the practical implication is that headline market vacancy statistics are less informative than building-level and quality-tier analysis — a market with 20% overall vacancy may have 10% vacancy in its top quartile of buildings and 30–40% effective vacancy in the bottom half.

## Sublease Availability as a Leading Indicator

Sublease availability — space under lease by existing tenants that is being actively marketed for sublease to subtenant occupiers — is the most widely followed leading indicator of future direct vacancy. When companies put significant space on the sublease market, it signals their intention to surrender or downsize that space at lease expiration. Sublease space typically trades at a 15–30% discount to direct leased space of equivalent quality (varies by market and vintage), and the presence of large blocks of sublease supply in a submarket suppresses face rents and extends re-tenanting timelines for competing buildings.

Investors and lenders analyzing any office acquisition should assess: current sublease availability in the subject building and immediate competitive set; the term of existing subleases and when that space will ultimately be surrendered; and whether large tenants in the building have initiated sublease marketing that has not yet appeared in public availability data.

## Return-to-Office Mandates and Policy Evolution

From 2023 onward, a growing number of large corporate employers — financial institutions, law firms, retail corporations, and government agencies — implemented formal return-to-office (RTO) policies requiring 3–5 days per week attendance. These mandates have modestly improved physical utilization in affected markets and submarkets but have not fully reversed the structural decline. Major technology companies have remained more mixed in their policies. The long-run equilibrium appears to be settling around 3 days per week as the mainstream hybrid norm for professional office-using industries, with significant variation by employer culture and industry.

For underwriting purposes, investors should resist assuming a full return to pre-COVID demand norms and instead build underwriting scenarios around the current structural equilibrium (2–3 day hybrid norms, higher space efficiency targets, continued right-sizing at lease renewal) while running upside scenarios assuming incremental improvement. The downside scenario — where a renewed emphasis on remote work (driven by a future recession, technology platform improvements, or cultural shift) further compresses demand — should be modeled as a stress test rather than dismissed as a tail risk.

## Geographic Divergence

Post-COVID occupancy trends are not uniform nationally. Sun Belt CBDs and suburban markets (Dallas, Austin, Houston, Nashville, Charlotte, Phoenix, Atlanta, Miami) have demonstrated materially stronger recovery than legacy gateway markets. The combination of population in-migration, younger workforce demographics, lower cost-of-living (reducing commute resentment), newer building stock, and diverse industry base has supported faster return to office in these markets. Gateway CBDs — particularly San Francisco, parts of Chicago, and select other legacy urban cores — face layered challenges combining structural demand reduction, public safety perception issues, and commercial-to-residential conversion pressure. Investors applying gateway market norms to Sun Belt contexts (or vice versa) will systematically misjudge market conditions.
