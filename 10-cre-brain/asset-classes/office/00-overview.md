# Office

## Overview

Office is one of the foundational institutional commercial real estate asset classes, encompassing buildings and campuses designed for professional, administrative, creative, and knowledge-work tenancy. The class spans a wide spectrum — from landmark Class A trophy towers in dense urban CBDs to suburban garden campuses, from life-sciences lab clusters to flex/creative loft conversions. Historically, office has attracted core and core-plus institutional capital because of its long-lease, creditworthy-tenant profile; large corporate headquarters leases anchoring major submarkets provided stable, bond-like cash flows that appealed to life companies, pension funds, and open-end core funds.

The post-2020 period has fundamentally challenged that thesis. The rapid normalization of hybrid and remote work has structurally reduced aggregate demand for traditional office space in most U.S. markets. Net absorption has been persistently negative in many CBDs, vacancy rates have risen well above historic norms, and a wide bid-ask gap has suppressed transaction volume. Against this backdrop, institutional investors have broadly re-rated office as a higher-risk, less-liquid asset class requiring disciplined submarket and subtype selection. Well-located, amenity-rich, energy-efficient buildings ("flight to quality") have captured most remaining demand, while older commodity buildings face secular obsolescence and potential conversion.

Despite the headwinds, select office subsectors retain compelling fundamentals. [[Medical Office Buildings (MOB)]] benefit from healthcare demand that is largely non-discretionary and immune to work-from-home dynamics. [[Life Sciences & Lab Space]] has experienced strong demand from biotech/pharma growth, though rapid construction deliveries in some markets have created near-term oversupply. [[Flex Office]] and serviced office models continue to grow as occupiers seek portfolio agility. Investors approaching office today must apply sharper sub-market, vintage, and subtype filters than were needed in prior cycles.

## Subtypes

See [[Office — Subtypes]] for detailed profiles. The major subtypes are:

- **CBD / Class A Trophy:** High-rise or landmark assets in dense urban cores — highest rents, highest TI/LC costs, most amenity-intensive. Subject to greatest WFH-related demand risk but also most resilient within that tier.
- **Suburban Office:** Campus or low-/mid-rise assets in suburban nodes. Highly varied; some submarkets (Sun Belt, life-sciences corridors) remain healthy while older Rust Belt or isolated suburban parks face distress.
- **Medical Office Building (MOB):** Purpose-built or converted space for outpatient healthcare. Demand driven by demographics (aging population) rather than knowledge-work trends; see [[Medical Office Buildings (MOB)]].
- **Life Sciences / Lab:** Wet lab, dry lab, and flex lab space clustered in established innovation districts. Capital-intensive to build or convert; strong but cyclical demand; see [[Life Sciences & Lab Space]].
- **Flex Office:** Shorter-term, operator-managed suites and coworking. Demand-elastic and growing; see [[Flex Office]].
- **Creative / Loft:** Adaptive reuse of industrial or older warehouse buildings into open-plan office. Often in urban infill; strong appeal to media, tech, and creative tenants.

## Demand Drivers

Office demand is fundamentally a derivative of employment — specifically, the count and density preferences of knowledge workers. Leading indicators include total nonfarm payrolls (white-collar industries: finance, professional and business services, tech, healthcare), corporate profit margins (which drive hiring and space expansions), and market-level office-using employment forecasts. Secondary indicators include net migration to a market, educational attainment of the labor pool, and the concentration of anchor employers (universities, government, hospital systems) that generate less cyclical demand.

The structural shift to hybrid and remote work has complicated the relationship between headcount and square footage demand. Utilization surveys consistently show average physical attendance below 60% of pre-pandemic norms in most U.S. CBDs, yet many companies have not yet completed lease consolidations. This "shadow vacancy" — space held under lease but functionally underutilized — complicates projections. Markets with younger demographics, in-migration, and Sun Belt growth dynamics have recovered faster; gateway markets with high housing costs and long commutes have been slower. Watching return-to-office mandates from large employers, sublease availability as a leading indicator of future vacancy, and tech-sector layoff/expansion cycles are all essential inputs.

## Supply Dynamics

Office supply is delivered slowly relative to other property types. Major new construction requires 24–48 months from entitlement to delivery, a multi-year pre-leasing runway, and significant equity commitment (office land, hard costs, and TI packages make spec development economically challenging in most markets). The result is that supply overhangs typically build during periods of easy credit and lease-up euphoria, then take years to absorb. In the current cycle, very little speculative new supply is under construction outside of a handful of pre-leased headquarters builds and life-sciences campuses, which is a long-run positive for the highest-quality existing stock.

The more pressing supply issue in many markets is functional obsolescence: a large proportion of existing inventory — particularly pre-2000 commodity suburban and Class B/C CBD space — no longer meets tenant expectations for amenities, sustainability credentials, column-free floorplates, and technology infrastructure. This obsolete stock competes poorly for shrinking demand, widens the effective vacancy overhang, and in some cases is most economically viable as an office-to-residential conversion target. Investors must distinguish between headline vacancy (which includes structurally impaired buildings) and competitive vacancy (vacancy in buildings that actually compete for creditworthy tenants).

## Key Operating Metrics

See [[Office — Key Metrics]] for full definitions. Core metrics include:

- **Net Effective Rent (NER):** Face rent less TI amortization and free rent — the true economic rent.
- **Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT):** Duration-weighted average of remaining lease terms; a key risk indicator.
- **TI/LC Amortization:** Annual cost of tenant improvement and leasing commission capital spread over lease term.
- **Occupancy / Availability Rate:** Both physical and economic occupancy matter; sublease availability is a key shadow metric.
- **Load Factor (Add-on Factor):** Ratio of rentable to usable area; affects tenant economics on gross-lease buildings.
- **Parking Ratio:** Spaces per 1,000 RSF; critical for suburban assets where parking scarcity is a competitive disadvantage.
- **Expense Recovery Ratio:** Proportion of operating expenses recovered from tenants; varies by lease structure.

## Common Deal Structures

Office transactions commonly involve institutional equity (pension funds, REITs, private equity) combined with [[Senior Debt]] — traditionally [[Life Company Debt]] for core stabilized assets and [[CMBS]] or [[Bank Debt]] for transitional or value-add plays. [[Bridge Loan]] financing is prevalent for repositioning or re-leasing plays where the asset cannot yet service permanent debt. [[Joint Venture Structures]] with operating partners who bring leasing or management expertise are common on value-add and opportunistic deals.

Lease structures vary by subtype and market: full-service gross leases dominate in CBD high-rise (landlord pays all operating expenses and recovers via base year or expense-stop provisions); modified gross leases are common in suburban mid-rise; NNN or absolute-net leases appear primarily in single-tenant net-leased office and MOB contexts. WALT is a critical underwriting input — office deals with near-term rollover carry significant re-leasing risk and must be underwritten conservatively on downtime, TI cost, and rent reset assumptions.

## Major Risks

See [[Office — Risks & Considerations]] for a structured discussion. Key risks include:

- **WFH / Hybrid Demand Shock:** Structural reduction in office-using demand that may not fully reverse.
- **Tenant Concentration:** Single large tenants create binary rollover risk; losing an anchor tenant can impair an entire building.
- **Capital Intensity of Re-tenanting:** TI packages, free rent, and leasing commissions for large office leases can equal 2–4 years of base rent, destroying near-term cash flow.
- **Obsolescence:** Aging HVAC, low ceilings, poor energy efficiency, and lack of amenities render many buildings functionally uncompetitive.
- **Financing Availability:** Lenders have broadly reduced office exposure; acquisition financing for anything other than high-quality stabilized product is difficult and expensive.

## Regulatory Considerations

See [[Office — Regulatory Considerations]] for the detailed discussion. Key regulatory domains include ADA compliance (accessibility standards for public buildings), energy benchmarking mandates (mandatory disclosure laws in many major cities), zoning and parking minimums (which affect density and conversion viability), and office-to-residential conversion incentive programs proliferating in many cities as a policy response to elevated CBD vacancy.

## Notable Markets

Office fundamentals vary dramatically by geography. Sun Belt metros (Atlanta, Dallas, Austin, Phoenix, Charlotte) have demonstrated stronger absorption and lower vacancy than gateway CBDs, driven by population and employment migration. Life-sciences clusters (Boston/Cambridge, San Francisco Bay Area, San Diego, Research Triangle, Seattle) retain strong demand for lab-enabled product. Legacy gateway CBDs (San Francisco, Chicago, parts of Manhattan) carry elevated vacancy and ongoing distress pressure. Secondary markets with dominant single employers (government, university, healthcare system) often show more stability than their size would suggest.
