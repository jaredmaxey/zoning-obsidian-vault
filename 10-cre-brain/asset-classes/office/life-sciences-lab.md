# Life Sciences & Lab Space

Life sciences and lab space is among the most technically specialized segments of the commercial office universe, requiring building infrastructure substantially beyond that of standard office and commanding corresponding rent premiums. The sector serves biotech, pharmaceutical, medical device, genomics, and academic research tenants who require controlled environments and specialized utilities that cannot be accommodated in conventional office buildings.

## Physical Requirements and Building Specifications

The defining physical characteristic of lab-enabled buildings is the mechanical, electrical, and plumbing infrastructure that supports wet lab operations. Key differentiators from standard office include:

- **HVAC:** Lab suites require significantly higher air-change rates (10–20+ air changes per hour vs. 2–4 for office), exhaust fume hood ductwork connected to high-speed exhaust fans, and often building-level air handling that provides 100% outside air (no recirculation) for biosafety compliance.
- **Structural loading:** Heavy mechanical equipment, chemical storage, and analytical instrumentation require floor load capacities of 100–150+ pounds per square foot (versus 50–75 lb/SF for office).
- **Vibration dampening:** Sensitive analytical equipment (electron microscopes, NMR machines) requires vibration isolation that may require special floor slab design or active isolation systems.
- **Utilities:** Deionized water, house vacuum, piped gas systems (compressed air, nitrogen, carbon dioxide), emergency power (backup generator), and enhanced electrical capacity (3–5 watts/SF vs. 1–2 watts/SF for office).
- **Plumbing:** Laboratory waste systems with acid neutralization and traps compliant with local environmental regulations; biosafety hood drainage systems.
- **Biosafety:** BSL-2 and select BSL-3 suite design requires interlocked door systems, negative pressure rooms, and HEPA filtration.

These requirements make ground-up construction or conversion of office to lab expensive and technically challenging. Lab conversion costs premium over standard office construction typically range widely (varies significantly by market, starting baseline, and the scope of lab infrastructure required), and the feasibility of converting any given building depends heavily on its structural capacity, floor-to-floor heights (minimum 14–15 ft is generally required for lab HVAC), and shaft space for exhaust systems.

## Demand Drivers

Life sciences demand is driven by a distinct set of factors compared to traditional office:

- **NIH and federal research funding:** National Institutes of Health grant awards directly fund academic and research hospital lab space demand in clusters built around research universities.
- **Venture capital and private equity investment in biotech:** VC funding cycles drive startup formation and lab space demand from early-stage companies. The 2021 biotech VC peak drove unprecedented demand; the 2022–2023 VC pullback created significant subleasing and demand softening in top clusters.
- **Large pharma and biotech expansion:** Late-stage clinical pipelines and M&A by large pharmaceutical companies drive demand for owned and leased R&D campuses.
- **Geographic clustering:** Life sciences demand is highly concentrated in a handful of established innovation ecosystems — Boston/Cambridge (Kendall Square and adjacent submarkets), San Francisco Bay Area (South San Francisco, Mission Bay, Emeryville), San Diego (Torrey Pines/UTC), Research Triangle Park (NC), Seattle (South Lake Union), and a growing list of secondary clusters. Demand outside these established ecosystems is thin and underwriting should reflect limited exit liquidity.

## Rent and Underwriting Norms

Life sciences lab space commands a substantial rent premium over standard office in the same market, reflecting the cost to build the requisite infrastructure and the limited supply of appropriately configured buildings. Gross lab rents in established clusters have ranged from $60–$150+/RSF annually (varies significantly by cluster and vintage), versus $40–$80/RSF for high-quality conventional office in the same submarkets.

Key underwriting considerations:

- **Tenant credit quality:** Early-stage biotech tenants (pre-revenue, VC-backed) carry significant credit risk. Lease guarantees from the sponsoring VC fund are rarely available. Underwriters should model credit loss scenarios and diversify tenant credit risk across the rent roll.
- **Supply pipeline sensitivity:** Established clusters have seen significant speculative construction delivering simultaneously with demand slowdowns (2023–2025 period). Absorption timelines for new spec lab deliveries in oversupplied submarkets have extended materially.
- **Conversion optionality:** Buildings designed with convertible lab/office floor plans (infrastructure roughed in but not fully built out) provide flexibility to serve either market based on demand; fully fit-out wet lab suites have narrower demand.
- **Yield on cost:** Development deals are underwritten on [[Yield on Cost]] vs. stabilized cap rate spread (see [[Development Spread]]); lab development requires substantial spread above going-in cap rates to justify the construction risk and specialized infrastructure investment.

## Ownership and Market Structure

Life sciences real estate is dominated by a small number of specialized platforms with deep cluster relationships, long development pipelines, and tenant relationships spanning multiple cycles. Alexandria Real Estate Equities (ARE) is the preeminent publicly traded life sciences REIT with concentrated ownership in the primary clusters. Healthpeak Properties has a significant life sciences portfolio. Private equity platforms including Longfellow Real Estate Partners and others have built active development pipelines in secondary clusters.

The specialized nature of the asset class — deep market knowledge required, long development timelines, complex tenant buildout management, and limited transaction comparables — creates high barriers to entry for generalist investors. New entrants to the sector are most successful when partnering with established operators who have cluster-specific tenant relationships and operational expertise.

## Key Risks

- **VC funding cycle dependency:** Demand from early-stage biotech is highly correlated with VC biotech investment, which is volatile. The 2022–2023 biotech funding correction demonstrated how quickly demand can turn to sublease supply.
- **Geographic concentration:** Life sciences demand outside established clusters is shallow; assets in secondary or emerging clusters face limited exit liquidity.
- **Speculative overbuilding:** The 2021–2024 construction cycle delivered a large volume of new lab space in top clusters relative to near-term demand, creating elevated vacancy conditions in some submarkets.
- **Conversion cost and obsolescence:** Lab infrastructure has a shorter functional life than conventional office systems; major re-tenanting may require significant reinvestment in HVAC, plumbing, and utility systems.
- **Large pharma consolidation risk:** Mergers and acquisitions among large pharma and biotech companies can result in consolidated lab footprints, creating large mid-lease or rollover vacancies in affected buildings.
