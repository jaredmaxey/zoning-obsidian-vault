# Office — Subtypes

Office encompasses a range of building types that differ materially in tenant profile, lease structure, capital requirements, and risk-return expectations. Understanding subtype distinctions is essential for accurate underwriting and portfolio positioning, because aggregated office statistics often mask significant divergence between the highest-quality CBD towers and distressed suburban commodity stock.

---

### CBD / Class A Trophy

**Profile.** CBD Class A and trophy office comprises the highest-quality, most amenity-rich, often architecturally prominent towers in a market's central business district. Typical characteristics include large floor plates (20,000–50,000 RSF+), modern HVAC and building systems, LEED or equivalent sustainability certifications, ground-floor retail and food service, conference facilities, fitness centers, and proximity to transit. Trophy product typically commands a rent premium of 20–50%+ over Class B space in the same market, varies by market and vintage, and attracts the most creditworthy corporate tenants — financial institutions, law firms, professional services, and large technology tenants.

**Deal Profile.** Trophy office has historically been a core/core-plus institutional play: long WALTs, investment-grade credit tenants, and cap rates that reflect the asset's perceived durability. In the current environment, even prime CBD product faces elevated vacancy as tenants right-size footprints and defer leasing decisions. Re-tenanting a large block in a CBD tower requires substantial TI (ranges of $80–$150+/SF for build-out, varies by market and vintage), free rent periods of 6–18 months, and multi-year leasing timelines. Underwriters must stress both downtime and rent resets — face rents may hold in headline terms while effective rents compress materially due to concessions.

**Typical Owners.** Sovereign wealth funds, pension fund separate accounts, open-end core funds (REITs and private), major institutional real estate investment managers. The trophy segment has historically had very low transaction velocity — assets trade rarely, and when they do, at compressed cap rates that reflect scarcity and durability premiums.

### Suburban Office

**Profile.** Suburban office encompasses low-rise (typically 2–6 story) and mid-rise campus or park settings outside the urban core. Quality ranges enormously — from well-amenitized corporate campus product in desirable suburban nodes to aging, underparkingd, poorly-maintained commodity parks on arterial roads. Suburban demand is closely tied to local employment concentrations, major corporate campuses, and the preference of businesses to locate near the workforce rather than in congested CBDs. Parking ratios (typically 4–6 spaces per 1,000 RSF) are a key competitive variable versus CBD product.

**Deal Profile.** Suburban commodity office has been among the most distressed segments post-2020. Smaller tenants in these buildings have been quicker to reduce footprints, and the economics of re-tenanting older suburban stock (significant TI required for dated finishes, common areas, and building systems) often does not pencil without substantial price basis reductions. Value-add and opportunistic buyers have targeted suburban product at deep discounts to replacement cost, but leasing traction has been inconsistent. Conversion to residential, industrial, or mixed-use is increasingly evaluated as the economically superior outcome for functionally obsolete suburban parks; see [[Adaptive Reuse]].

**Typical Owners.** Regional operators, private equity, value-add and opportunistic funds, and some REITs with suburban platforms. Core capital has largely exited older suburban commodity product; well-located newer suburban campuses in growth markets retain institutional interest.

### Medical Office Building (MOB)

**Profile.** MOBs are purpose-built or purpose-converted office buildings housing outpatient healthcare providers — physician practices, ambulatory surgery centers, imaging centers, dialysis clinics, and ancillary health services. Key differentiators from traditional office: tenant demand is non-discretionary (driven by patient volumes, not office utilization mandates), leases are long (typically 7–15 years, NNN or modified gross), and on-campus or near-hospital locations create significant switching costs for clinical tenants who have invested in specialized buildouts. See [[Medical Office Buildings (MOB)]] for the full deep-dive.

**Deal Profile.** MOBs trade at tighter cap rates than traditional office, reflecting perceived income stability. On-campus (hospital-owned land) MOBs carry an additional premium due to the implicit sponsorship relationship with the health system. Re-tenanting risk is lower because clinical buildouts are expensive and physicians do not relocate casually. The primary risks are health system credit dependency, reimbursement policy changes affecting tenant economics, and the capital intensity of specialized clinical buildouts.

**Typical Owners.** Healthcare REITs (Healthpeak, Physicians Realty — now Healthpeak), institutional core funds, private equity targeting healthcare real estate, physician groups (owner-occupied), and health systems themselves. MOB has become a standalone institutional sub-sector rather than an edge case of general office.

### Life Sciences / Lab

**Profile.** Life sciences space comprises wet lab, dry lab, clean room, and research-and-development facilities used by biotechnology, pharmaceutical, and research institutions. Key physical requirements include enhanced HVAC (higher air-change rates, humidity/temperature control), vibration dampening, specialized plumbing (lab waste systems), increased electrical capacity, and biosafety infrastructure. These requirements make conversions expensive and result in significant switching costs for tenants who have built out custom lab configurations. Demand is clustered in established innovation ecosystems with major research universities and NIH/private research funding flows. See [[Life Sciences & Lab Space]] for the full deep-dive.

**Deal Profile.** Life sciences has been among the strongest-performing office subtypes over the past decade, but rapid speculative construction deliveries in top clusters (Boston/Cambridge, Bay Area, San Diego) created significant near-term oversupply conditions from 2023 onward. Underwriting must carefully model competitive supply pipelines and tenant credit quality — early-stage biotech tenants carry meaningful credit risk (VC-backed, pre-revenue). Development yields on life-sciences product have historically justified spec construction, but developer returns hinge heavily on lease-up timing and lab conversion costs (ranges of $150–$400+/SF conversion premium over standard office, varies by market and vintage).

**Typical Owners.** Life sciences–focused REITs (Alexandria Real Estate Equities, Healthpeak), core and value-add institutional funds, life sciences–focused private equity platforms, universities, and anchor health systems.

### Flex Office

**Profile.** Flex office includes operator-managed coworking, serviced offices, and enterprise flexible space, where the underlying landlord leases to an operator (WeWork, IWG/Regus, Industrious, etc.) or operates the flex platform directly, and end users take short-term memberships or licenses. Demand is driven by enterprise tenants seeking portfolio optionality, small and medium businesses without credit for direct leases, and remote/hybrid workers needing occasional dedicated workspace. Flex space typically occupies 5–20% of a building, with some assets running entirely as flex. See [[Flex Office]] for the full deep-dive.

**Deal Profile.** Flex operators are generally viewed by lenders and appraisers as higher-risk tenants than credit-worthy direct occupiers — operator lease structures often include revenue-share or termination provisions that create cash flow volatility for landlords. The underlying asset underwriting must be done on the building's fee-simple value, not the operator's contracted rent alone. In an era of elevated vacancy, some landlords have added flex amenity floors as a competitive differentiator.

**Typical Owners.** Institutional landlords who self-operate or partner with flex operators, private equity, and specialized flex-focused funds. Pure-play flex operators who also own real estate (less common post-WeWork restructuring).

### Creative / Loft Office

**Profile.** Creative or loft office is an adaptive reuse of older industrial, warehouse, or manufacturing buildings into open-plan, high-ceiling, exposed-structure office space with character finishes. This product type is attractive to media, advertising, technology, design, fashion, and nonprofit tenants who prefer the aesthetic and spatial flexibility over commodity Class A space. Key characteristics: high ceilings (14–18 ft+), exposed concrete or timber structure, polished or stained concrete floors, abundant natural light through large windows, and location in urban infill neighborhoods with walkability and proximity to amenities.

**Deal Profile.** Creative/loft office typically requires an [[Adaptive Reuse]] development or repositioning play — acquisition of an underperforming industrial or older office building, seismic or structural upgrade, and creative buildout. Rents can be competitive with or above Class B traditional office in markets where this product is scarce. The primary risks are the cost unpredictability of adaptive reuse (hidden structural, environmental, or seismic issues), the narrowness of the demand pool in weaker markets, and sensitivity to broader office market headwinds. Parking is typically constrained and must be supplemented by transit access or shared-parking arrangements.

**Typical Owners.** Value-add and opportunistic private equity, local and regional developers with adaptive reuse expertise, family offices. Larger institutional capital is rare unless the asset is stabilized with creditworthy tenants on long-term leases.
