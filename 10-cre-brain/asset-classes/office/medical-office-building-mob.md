# Medical Office Buildings (MOB)

Medical office buildings (MOBs) are purpose-built or purpose-converted office facilities housing outpatient healthcare providers, including physician practices, ambulatory surgery centers, imaging centers, physical therapy clinics, dialysis centers, and other ancillary health services. MOBs have emerged as a distinct institutional asset sub-sector within the broader office category, generally underwritten and priced more similarly to healthcare real estate than to traditional office.

## Structural Demand Differentiation

The fundamental differentiator between MOB and conventional office is demand character. MOB demand is driven by patient volumes, provider capacity, and demographic composition of the surrounding population — not by knowledge-worker utilization rates, corporate hiring cycles, or work-from-home adoption. An aging U.S. population (the Baby Boomer cohort moving through peak healthcare consumption years) creates a structural tailwind for outpatient healthcare demand that is largely non-discretionary: patients need to see their doctors, have imaging performed, and receive physical therapy regardless of economic conditions or work-location preferences.

The transition of healthcare delivery from inpatient hospital settings to lower-cost outpatient settings (driven by payer pressure, technology enabling outpatient procedures, and patient preference) further strengthens MOB demand. Ambulatory surgery centers, advanced imaging, and procedure-based specialties that historically required hospital-based delivery have migrated to purpose-built outpatient facilities. MOBs are the primary real estate vehicle for this migration.

## Location and Physical Characteristics

MOBs cluster in two primary configurations: **on-campus** (located on or immediately adjacent to a hospital campus, often on health-system-owned land) and **off-campus** (standalone community locations serving a defined patient catchment area). On-campus MOBs carry a premium because the proximity to the affiliated hospital creates maximum convenience for both physicians and patients, and the health system's land ownership (which may result in a ground lease structure for the real estate investor) provides an implicit tenancy guarantee.

Physical requirements for MOBs are more specialized than standard office: reinforced structural capacity for heavy medical equipment (imaging systems, linear accelerators), enhanced plumbing and medical gas systems, backup power (generator systems for surgical suites and life-safety equipment), accessible design throughout (ADA requirements are particularly stringent given patient population), and radiation shielding for imaging suites. These physical features create high switching costs — a radiology group that has invested in vault construction and lead lining for its CT and MRI suite is not going to relocate casually. This capital stickiness is a key underwriting advantage.

## Lease Structure and Tenant Economics

MOBs typically feature longer leases than conventional office — 7–15 year terms are common, often with NNN or modified-gross structures where tenants absorb most operating expenses. NNN leases are preferred by institutional buyers because they insulate the landlord from expense inflation and simplify management. Medical tenant buildouts are expensive (ranges of $80–$200+/RSF for clinical fit-out, varies by market and vintage), which creates high renewal probability — a physician group investing $500,000 in a custom clinical buildout will strongly prefer renewal over relocation. This high renewal rate reduces re-tenanting capital costs and downtime risk.

Tenant credit quality in MOBs is a spectrum: large health systems and hospital affiliates as anchor tenants carry investment-grade or near-investment-grade credit; independent physician practices range from creditworthy established groups to smaller practices with limited track records. Underwriters should evaluate the mix of anchor health system tenancy vs. independent practice tenancy, because the health system anchor provides implicit support to the overall building's desirability and stabilizes base demand even when smaller tenants turn over.

## Underwriting Norms

MOBs trade at tighter cap rates than conventional office, reflecting their perceived income stability. On-campus MOBs with health system credit anchors have historically traded at cap rates that approach core industrial or grocery-anchored retail in their compression (varies by market and vintage). Key underwriting considerations include:

- **Vacancy assumption:** Lower than conventional office; stabilized vacancy for well-located MOBs is typically in the 5–12% range (varies by market and vintage), versus 10–20%+ for conventional.
- **TI on renewal:** Lower than new-tenant TI because existing clinical buildouts are preserved; renewal TI ranges $10–$40/RSF (varies by market and vintage).
- **Lease rollover risk:** Lower probability of downtime due to high switching costs; model realistic downtime of 3–9 months for tenant replacement rather than the 12–24 months appropriate for conventional office (varies by market and vintage).
- **Exit cap:** Tighter spreads over going-in than conventional office; strong demand from healthcare REITs and core funds provides a liquid exit market for quality product.

## Major Risks

The primary risks specific to MOBs include:

- **Health system credit concentration:** On-campus MOBs anchored by a single health system are vulnerable to that system's financial health — health system bankruptcies or consolidations can impair MOB value.
- **Reimbursement policy risk:** Changes in Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement rates affect physician practice economics and may reduce tenants' ability to sustain high rents.
- **Physician group practice consolidation:** Continuing consolidation of independent practices into health system employment may shift demand from independent-group tenancy to health-system-controlled decisions about location.
- **Regulatory and licensing:** Ambulatory surgery centers and specialty clinics operate under state licensing and CMS certification requirements; a tenant losing its license creates an immediate vacancy.

## Notable Owners and Transaction Activity

Healthcare REITs have been the primary institutional buyers of MOB portfolios — Healthpeak Properties (formerly HCP) and Physicians Realty Trust (merged with Healthpeak in 2024) collectively hold large MOB portfolios. Private equity healthcare real estate platforms have been active acquirers of off-campus community MOB portfolios. MOB has been one of the few office sub-sectors to sustain transaction volume and liquidity through the broader office market slowdown, reflecting its differentiated fundamental profile.
