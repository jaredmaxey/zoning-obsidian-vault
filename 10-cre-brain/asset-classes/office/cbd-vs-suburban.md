# CBD vs. Suburban Office

The central business district (CBD) vs. suburban office distinction is one of the most consequential submarket choices in office investment. The two contexts differ not only in physical form but in tenant economics, demand driver composition, parking dynamics, capital cost structure, and performance through cycles. Understanding these differences is essential because headline office statistics often mask enormous dispersion between a strong Sun Belt suburban market and a struggling legacy CBD.

## Defining the Contexts

**CBD office** is characterized by dense urban location, vertical building form (mid-rise to high-rise), reliance on public transit, walking access to amenities (restaurants, hotels, retail), and tenant profiles dominated by professional services (law firms, financial institutions, consulting), large technology companies, and creative industries. Floor plates are typically larger per floor but the entire building's rentable area is stacked vertically. Parking is limited, expensive, and often accessed through shared structures or monthly contracts rather than dedicated surface lots.

**Suburban office** spans a wide range from well-amenitized master-planned business parks with structured parking and on-site restaurants to aging strip-commercial low-rise buildings with inadequate systems and deteriorating common areas. Suburban buildings are generally low-rise (2–6 stories), often in garden or campus configurations, with dedicated surface or structured parking. The tenant base skews toward smaller to mid-size professional, technology, healthcare-adjacent, and government-related occupiers. Demand is less concentrated in financial services and more tied to local employment growth, corporate campus preferences, and proximity to workforce concentrations.

## Demand Drivers Compared

| Dimension | CBD | Suburban |
|-----------|-----|---------|
| Primary demand driver | Financial services, professional services, technology, creative | Regional employment base, corporate headquarters/campus, healthcare-adjacent, government |
| Commute dependency | Transit-accessible; less car-dependent | Largely car-dependent; transit often absent |
| Talent pool served | Younger workers preferring urban amenities | Suburban homeowners; regional workforce |
| WFH impact | Higher — CBD demand dropped more sharply | Varied — some suburban markets absorbed well; others distressed |
| New supply | Very limited (high land cost, entitlement complexity) | More feasible; some markets significantly oversupplied |

The post-2020 experience showed that CBD and suburban markets diverged significantly — but not always in the intuitive direction. Some Sun Belt suburban markets outperformed troubled CBDs because population and employment migration brought new demand to suburban nodes (Dallas/Fort Worth Uptown, Atlanta Perimeter, Phoenix/Scottsdale) while gateway CBDs like San Francisco's Financial District experienced severe demand contraction. Investors must avoid generalizing from one geographic context to another.

## Physical and Operational Differences

**Parking.** The most operationally significant physical difference. Suburban buildings typically provide 4.0–5.0 spaces per 1,000 RSF as an expectation; CBD buildings offer far less and rely on transit or paid parking structures. For suburban landlords, parking quantity and quality (covered vs. surface, lighting, proximity to entrance) are active leasing variables — buildings falling below submarket parking norms face tenant resistance.

**Floor Plate Efficiency.** CBD high-rise buildings often have less efficient floor plates (higher core factor, more structural columns, longer elevator lobbies) relative to newer suburban product with wide-span structural systems and central core designs. This affects load factor and tenant occupancy cost.

**Building Systems Age.** The suburban stock includes a large proportion of 1980s–1990s vintage buildings whose HVAC, electrical, and plumbing systems are approaching or past useful life. CBD Class A buildings generally have been maintained and upgraded more aggressively due to competitive pressure from institutional owners. Suburban value-add deals often carry larger capital backlogs than they appear.

**Amenities.** CBD buildings compete on proximity to retail, restaurants, transit, and urban amenities that are inherently off-site. Suburban buildings must either provide on-site amenities (café, fitness center, conference facilities, outdoor space) or accept that tenants will benchmark them against nearby competitors offering those features.

## Underwriting Differences

Underwriting CBD vs. suburban office requires different vacancy benchmarks, TI assumptions, and exit cap assumptions:

- **CBD Class A trophy:** Lower stabilized vacancy assumption for the most competitive buildings (varies by market and vintage); highest TI costs per RSF but high face rents to absorb them; exit caps tightest in the class; longest WALT typical.
- **CBD Class B/C:** Highest effective vacancy rates in the current cycle; functional obsolescence makes re-tenanting economics challenged; often priced at significant discounts to replacement cost or conversion value.
- **Suburban Class A/B (well-located, strong submarket):** Vacancy assumptions vary by submarket; moderate TI costs; competitive parking; more achievable re-tenanting timelines than CBD B/C.
- **Suburban commodity (older, weak submarket):** Most distressed segment; underwriting must assume extended downtime, high TI, and potential conversion write-down scenarios; exit cap spreads are widest.

## Current Market Dynamics (as of 2026)

CBDs in legacy gateway markets (San Francisco, Chicago, parts of Manhattan, parts of Washington D.C.) continue to face elevated vacancy, limited new demand, and ongoing distressed sales. These markets require contrarian conviction, ability to hold through extended lease-up periods, and willingness to underwrite conversion scenarios. Sun Belt CBDs (Austin, Charlotte, Nashville, Dallas/Fort Worth) have performed materially better and more closely resemble historically normal conditions.

Suburban office is bifurcated: strong Sun Belt suburban submarkets with population growth and diverse employment are absorbing space; isolated or aging suburban parks in Rust Belt and weak gateway suburban markets are struggling to find buyers willing to assume re-tenanting risk at any price. The conversion of suburban office parks to mixed-use, multifamily, or light industrial is an active trend in the strongest suburban real estate markets where alternative use demand is sufficient.

Both CBD and suburban institutional buyers have adopted a "flight to quality" positioning — concentrating investment in the top tier of product in each submarket rather than accepting commodity exposure for higher initial yield.
