# Office — Risks & Considerations

Office carries a broader and more complex risk profile than most other institutional CRE asset classes, reflecting both cyclical demand dynamics and structural secular shifts in how and where knowledge workers choose to work. A disciplined risk framework must distinguish between cyclical risks (resolvable as the economy and employment recover) and structural risks (where recovery may be partial or permanent). The severity of each risk category varies substantially by building class, subtype, and submarket; investors must apply subtype-specific and geography-specific filters rather than treating "office" as a monolithic risk category.

---

### Market

**WFH / Hybrid Demand Shock.** The most consequential structural risk in office is the persistent reduction in demand for space-per-worker driven by hybrid and remote work normalization. Even if companies do not fully abandon office space, the trend toward higher desk-sharing ratios, activity-based working, and hoteling means that headcount growth no longer translates 1:1 into SF demand growth. Many institutional projections pre-2020 assumed 200–250 RSF per office-using worker; current tenant buildouts often target 100–150 RSF per worker in premium urban product. This compression, applied across millions of square feet of lease renewals, represents a structural demand reduction that does not reverse with economic expansion.

*Mitigants:* Focus on submarkets and subtypes with structural demand insulation (healthcare, life sciences, government anchor tenancy); underwrite to conservative stabilized vacancy assumptions rather than pre-2020 norms; target flight-to-quality assets where tenants consolidate rather than exit entirely; require shorter initial underwriting holds to reduce terminal value uncertainty.

**Tenant Concentration / Rollover Risk.** Large-floor-plate office buildings may have one or two tenants representing 40–70%+ of revenue. The loss or contraction of an anchor tenant creates a sudden, large vacancy that takes years to re-tenant and requires massive TI/LC capital outlays. Mid-lease contraction (sublease marketing by a large tenant) can suppress market rents, affect appraisal values, and trigger DSCR covenant concerns on existing debt.

*Mitigants:* Prefer diversified rent rolls across multiple tenants; underwrite large-tenant rollover scenarios explicitly in sensitivity analysis; negotiate tenant early-warning provisions in leases (rights of first refusal, kick-out protections); structure acquisition pricing to assume 12–18+ months of downtime on anchor rollover.

**Submarket Deterioration.** CBD and suburban submarkets can deteriorate rapidly when anchor employers relocate, crime and public-safety perceptions decline, or infrastructure investments fail to materialize. Unlike industrial or multifamily, the office tenant cannot easily move to a competitor submarket mid-lease, but at renewal will leave if the submarket no longer serves their talent attraction needs.

*Mitigants:* Rigorous submarket selection (see [[Submarket Definition and Selection]]); favor submarkets with multiple large employers in non-cyclical industries; monitor leading indicators (transit ridership, restaurant/retail traffic, sublease inventory) during hold.

### Operational

**Capital Intensity of Re-tenanting.** Office is among the most capital-intensive asset classes to re-tenant. A large tenant vacancy requires: (1) TI allowances to restore and customize the space (ranges of $50–$150+/RSF for new leases, varies by market and vintage); (2) leasing commissions paid to both tenant and listing brokers (ranges of 4–6% of total lease value, varies by market and vintage); (3) free rent periods of 6–18 months that suspend cash flow even once a lease is signed; and (4) potential base building capital upgrades (HVAC, electrical, common area renovation) needed to make the space competitive. The combined cost can equal 2–4 years of base rent, and must be funded from reserves, additional equity, or construction financing during the downtime period.

*Mitigants:* Model full re-tenanting costs in DCF underwriting; maintain adequate CapEx reserves; structure acquisition debt to allow for TI/LC funding; prefer longer-WALT assets that delay large capital outlays; value assets on net effective rent, not face rent.

**Property Management Complexity.** Multi-tenant office buildings require intensive property management: lease administration for complex gross-lease documents, operating expense reconciliations, CAM audits by tenants, building systems maintenance, and tenant service management. Poorly managed buildings lose tenants at renewal due to service quality perception. Management costs are higher per dollar of revenue for smaller buildings (<50,000 RSF) and functionally obsolete assets that require disproportionate maintenance.

*Mitigants:* Hire experienced, market-dedicated property managers; establish proactive tenant relationship programs; budget accurately for management fees (3–5% of EGI is a typical range for institutional office, varies by market and vintage).

**Functional Obsolescence.** Buildings with low ceilings (below 9 ft), poor natural light, outdated mechanical systems (single-zone HVAC), inefficient floor plates (high core factor, low usable area), and inadequate technology infrastructure are increasingly non-competitive with tenants who have options. Obsolescence is difficult to cure without capital investment that often exceeds economic justification.

*Mitigants:* Conduct thorough physical due diligence; quantify the capital investment required to make a building competitive; stress-test economic viability of renovation versus conversion alternative.

### Capital & Financing

**Lender Appetite Contraction.** Office has experienced a broad pullback in lender appetite since 2020. Banks, life companies, and CMBS conduits have reduced office exposure limits, tightened underwriting criteria (lower LTVs, higher debt yields, stronger DSCR requirements), and in some cases exited the sector entirely for anything other than best-in-class stabilized product. This lender retreat creates refinancing risk for maturing office loans underwritten under pre-2020 standards, and acquisition financing risk for buyers seeking leverage on value-add deals.

*Mitigants:* Underwrite conservative refinancing assumptions at maturity; target lower initial LTV (50–60% vs. prior 65–75%); consider bridge-to-permanent structures with clear lease-up milestones; maintain lender relationships with specialized commercial real estate lenders who have maintained office programs.

**Valuation Volatility.** Office cap rates have widened significantly in the current cycle, compressing asset values. Buyers underwriting at 2019 cap rates and exit assumptions face meaningful mark-to-market risk. The wide bid-ask spread between sellers anchored to prior values and buyers requiring risk-adjusted pricing has suppressed transaction volume, making current comp-based appraisals uncertain (thin transaction data leads to appraised values that may not reflect true market clearing prices).

*Mitigants:* Use conservative exit cap assumptions (+50–100+ bps over going-in, stress for wider); run downside scenarios on both NOI and exit cap simultaneously; require wider discount rates for office DCF than for other asset classes.

**Interest Rate Sensitivity.** Office, as a long-duration asset with long lease terms and significant upfront capital costs, is particularly sensitive to discount rate changes. Rising interest rates compress values through both cap rate expansion and higher debt service costs, while also increasing the hurdle rate for re-tenanting capital.

*Mitigants:* Prefer fixed-rate or rate-capped debt structures; stress test debt coverage ratios under various rate scenarios; model negative leverage scenarios for value-add deals where going-in cap rates are below all-in debt costs.

### Structural & Physical

**Deferred Maintenance and Capital Backlog.** Many office buildings — especially those with long-term, below-market leases or extended vacancy periods — have accumulated significant deferred maintenance: aging HVAC equipment past useful life, outdated elevators, deteriorating facades, and outdated plumbing. These capital backlogs are often understated in seller-prepared underwriting and only become apparent in thorough property condition assessments.

*Mitigants:* Commission independent property condition assessments (PCA) by qualified engineers; adjust purchase price for deferred maintenance identified; model realistic CapEx budgets based on PCA findings rather than rule-of-thumb reserves.

**Sustainability and Energy Performance Risk.** Environmental benchmarking mandates (New York Local Law 97, California AB 802, similar laws in Boston, Seattle, and Chicago) impose financial penalties on buildings that fail to meet energy efficiency standards. Buildings with poor Energy Use Intensity (EUI) scores face compliance costs, potential retrofits (mechanical upgrades, window replacement), and reputational risk with sustainability-conscious tenants. Green-certified buildings (LEED, ENERGY STAR) command a measurable rent premium and superior tenant retention in many markets.

*Mitigants:* Evaluate energy performance early in due diligence; budget for energy efficiency capital improvements; target LEED/ENERGY STAR certification where achievable; factor compliance costs into NOI projections in jurisdictions with active benchmarking mandates.

**Seismic, Structural, and Environmental Risk.** Older office buildings in seismic zones (California, Pacific Northwest) may require seismic retrofits to meet current life-safety standards or tenant requirements. Environmental issues (asbestos, lead paint, PCBs in older glazing systems, underground storage tanks) are common in buildings constructed prior to 1980 and require Phase II environmental assessments. These costs can be substantial and may make a project economically non-viable if not properly quantified in due diligence.

*Mitigants:* Phase I and Phase II environmental assessments on all acquisitions; seismic evaluation in applicable zones; budget remediation costs at conservative high-end of engineer estimates; negotiate seller representations and escrow arrangements for known issues.
