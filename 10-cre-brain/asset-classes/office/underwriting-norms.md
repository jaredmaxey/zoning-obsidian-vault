# Office — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Underwriting office investments requires careful calibration of four interdependent variables: rent (face rate vs. net effective), vacancy (current vs. stabilized vs. structural), capital costs (TI, LC, CapEx), and hold-period financing assumptions. Office is distinctively capital-intensive on the revenue side — concessions packages for new leases can consume two to four years of base rent, and failure to model this correctly inflates projected cash flows. The post-2020 demand shift has widened the range of reasonable assumptions significantly; investors operating at the extremes of the historically "normal" range now face material underwriting risk. The discipline of separating stabilized from stressed assumptions and running explicit sensitivities on vacancy, rent reset, and downtime has become table stakes.

All figures below are ranges reflecting mainstream institutional practice. Actual assumptions must be derived from current submarket data, rent comps, and comparable lease transaction analysis. Varies by market and vintage throughout.

## Lease Structures

Office lease structures directly determine how operating expenses are allocated between landlord and tenant, and must be modeled precisely in a [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] analysis.

**Full-Service Gross (FSG).** The landlord pays all operating expenses (taxes, insurance, utilities, maintenance, janitorial, management). The tenant pays a fixed base rent. Most common in CBD high-rise and Class A multi-tenant buildings. For underwriting, the key variable is the base-year or expense-stop provision: leases written with a base-year stop require the landlord to absorb operating expense increases above the first-year baseline. In a high-inflation or rising-tax environment, this erodes NOI. Underwriters must project expense escalation and model the expense recovery shortfall under gross structures.

**Modified Gross.** A hybrid structure in which some expenses (commonly utilities and janitorial) are passed to tenants while others (taxes, insurance, structural maintenance) remain with the landlord. Common in suburban mid-rise and older CBD Class B buildings. Modified gross leases should be underwritten by explicitly modeling which expense categories are recovered from tenants and which are not; blanket "modified gross" without further detail is insufficient.

**Triple Net (NNN) / Absolute Net.** The tenant pays all operating expenses directly or reimburses the landlord for their pro-rata share. Common in MOB, single-tenant net-leased office, and some suburban parks. Underwriting is simpler on the expense side (landlord retains only non-recoverable items and management fee), but care must be taken with landlord reserves (roof, structure, capital replacements that tenants are not obligated to fund) and management fee drag.

**Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT).** WALT is calculated as the sum of (remaining lease term × tenant square footage) divided by total leased square footage. A WALT below 3–4 years signals imminent rollover risk and requires the underwriting to model TI, LC, downtime, and rent reset for a significant portion of the rent roll within the hold period. Office assets with short WALTs are generally financed as transitional (bridge) rather than permanent loans. See [[Office — Key Metrics]] for formula detail.

## Operating Expense Norms

Office OpEx consists of controllable and non-controllable components. Non-controllables (real estate taxes, insurance) are passed to tenants in NNN structures and must be modeled at market escalation rates regardless. Controllables (management fees, utilities, maintenance) vary significantly by building class, age, and management quality.

**Typical controllable OpEx categories** (per RSF per year, full-service gross building; varies by market and vintage):
- Janitorial: $1.50 – $3.50/RSF
- Utilities (common areas, HVAC): $2.00 – $5.00/RSF
- Maintenance and repairs: $1.00 – $3.00/RSF
- Management fee: 3% – 5% of EGI
- Security: $0.50 – $2.00/RSF
- Landscaping/parking/exterior: $0.25 – $1.00/RSF

**Non-controllable OpEx** (varies sharply by jurisdiction):
- Real estate taxes: highly jurisdiction-dependent; 20–50% of total OpEx in high-tax markets
- Insurance: $0.30 – $0.80/RSF; varies with building age, construction type, and market

Expense normalization (see [[Expense Normalization]] and [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]]) is critical in office underwriting because sellers may have deferred maintenance, allowed vacancies to temporarily suppress certain variable expenses, or benefited from non-recurring insurance or tax adjustments. Underwriters should normalize to a stabilized, fully-occupied expense base before applying vacancy and collection loss to derive stabilized NOI.
