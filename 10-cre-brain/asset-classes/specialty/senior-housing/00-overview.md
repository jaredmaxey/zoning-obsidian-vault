# Senior Housing

## Overview

Senior housing encompasses a broad continuum of residential and care facilities designed for older adults, ranging from lifestyle-oriented independent living communities to medically intensive skilled nursing facilities. Unlike conventional multifamily, senior housing blends real estate investment with operating business risk: occupancy, revenue, and profitability depend heavily on the quality and cost of care delivery, staffing, and local competitive positioning — factors that require specialized operating expertise. This hybrid nature differentiates senior housing from passive-investment real estate and demands that investors either engage best-in-class operators or develop operational capability themselves.

The structural demand thesis is exceptionally compelling: the US population aged 75 and over — the primary demand cohort for most care levels — is growing faster than any other age segment, driven by the aging of the baby boomer generation. The 2025–2035 decade represents a demographic wave that will sharply increase demand for senior housing capacity. Against this backdrop, new supply has been constrained since 2020 by construction cost inflation, labor shortages, and financing tightening, creating favorable supply/demand dynamics in primary markets as of 2026. Occupancy has recovered substantially from COVID-19 lows and continues to climb.

Institutional investors include specialized healthcare REITs (Welltower, Ventas, Healthpeak), private equity firms with operating platforms, and family office capital. The RIDEA (REIT Investment Diversification and Empowerment Act) structure, which allows REITs to participate in operating income through operating company (OpCo) / property company (PropCo) JV arrangements, has become the dominant structure for institutional investment in assisted living and memory care.

## Subtypes

**Independent Living (IL):** Age-restricted (typically 55+ or 62+) apartment communities offering optional amenities — dining, housekeeping, concierge services, and social programming — but without personal care or medical services included in the base fee. Residents are self-sufficient. Revenue is primarily driven by monthly service fees rather than care reimbursement; the business model is closest to conventional multifamily with enhanced amenities.

**Assisted Living (AL):** Facilities providing residents with assistance in activities of daily living (ADLs: bathing, dressing, medication management, mobility) and 24-hour staffing, while preserving resident autonomy and privacy. Revenue is predominantly private-pay (out-of-pocket) with limited Medicaid waiver participation in some states. Staffing cost is the dominant operating expense.

**Memory Care (MC):** Secure, specialized environments for residents with Alzheimer's disease or other dementias. Higher staff-to-resident ratios than AL, reinforced safety and wayfinding design, and specialized programming. Revenue per occupied room (RevPOR) is typically higher than AL due to enhanced care requirements. State licensure requirements are distinct from AL in most jurisdictions.

**Continuing Care Retirement Community (CCRC / Life Plan):** Campus communities offering the full care continuum — IL, AL, MC, and sometimes SNF — under one roof. Residents often pay substantial entrance fees (ranging widely from $100,000 to $1,000,000+) plus monthly fees, with contractual rights to move up the care continuum as needs increase. Actuarial risk (matching entrance fee refund obligations to future care costs) requires sophisticated financial management.

**Skilled Nursing Facility (SNF):** The most medically intensive senior housing product; provides post-acute rehabilitation and long-term custodial care. Highly dependent on Medicare (post-acute, short-stay) and Medicaid (long-term, low-income) reimbursement. Regulatory intensity and reimbursement risk are highest in this subtype; many institutional investors have reduced SNF exposure in favor of IL/AL/MC.

## Demand Drivers

The primary demand driver is demographic inevitability: the US 75+ population will grow by approximately 50% between 2025 and 2035 as the Baby Boom cohort ages into the core senior housing demand years. This wave is uniquely large, well-documented, and largely predictable. Secondary drivers include increasing average lifespans (which extend years in senior housing), rising rates of Alzheimer's and dementia (directly driving memory care demand), increasing female workforce participation in prior generations (reducing availability of informal family caregiving), and consumer preference for purpose-built senior communities over aging in place alone.

For SNFs specifically, Medicare reimbursement policy and post-acute referral patterns from hospitals are critical variables. Value-based care reforms incentivizing hospitals to reduce readmissions have generally been positive for high-quality SNFs that demonstrate outcomes. Geographic migration of seniors to Sun Belt markets drives spatial shifts in demand. Operating performance is also sensitive to flu season severity, COVID-19 variants, and infection control costs.

## Supply Dynamics

Senior housing development is constrained by construction costs, labor economics (new communities require staffing investment before revenue ramps), and financing availability. The construction pipeline contracted sharply after 2020, and new starts have not kept pace with demand recovery. In primary markets (coastal, Sun Belt), barriers to entry are further reinforced by land cost and, for SNFs and AL in CON states, certificate-of-need restrictions limiting new supply.

Development timelines run 24–42 months for mid-scale AL/MC communities; CCRC development is longer (5+ years given complexity). Lease-up risk is significant: new AL/MC communities typically take 12–30 months to reach stabilized occupancy (85–92%), and cash flow burn during lease-up is substantial. Under-capitalized developers are particularly vulnerable. Unlike multifamily, a senior housing community cannot simply fill up on rent alone — the cost of care staffing means that sub-optimal occupancy generates operating losses, not just reduced NOI.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Revenue per Occupied Room (RevPOR):** Total monthly revenue per occupied unit, including base service fee plus care tier add-ons. The headline revenue metric for AL and MC. `RevPOR = Total Monthly Revenue / Occupied Rooms`.

$$\text{RevPOR} = \frac{\text{Total Monthly Revenue}}{\text{Occupied Rooms}}$$

**Care-Level Mix:** The distribution of residents across care tiers (e.g., Level 1 / light assist vs. Level 3 / heavy assist) within an AL community, or across IL/AL/MC within a CCRC. Higher care mix generates more revenue per room but requires more staffing and creates operational complexity.

**Occupancy Rate:** Occupied units / total licensed units. The fundamental operating health indicator. Industry stabilized occupancy benchmark is typically 85–92%; below 80% generates operating losses in most AL/MC configurations.

**Labor Cost Ratio:** Total labor expense (wages, benefits, agency/temp staffing) as a percentage of total revenue. This is the most critical P&L driver in AL and MC, where labor typically represents 50–60% of revenue. `Labor Cost Ratio = Total Labor Expense / Total Revenue`.

$$\text{Labor Cost Ratio} = \frac{\text{Total Labor Expense}}{\text{Total Revenue}}$$

**Agency / Contract Staffing Percentage:** The share of total labor hours filled by contract (agency) staffing vs. permanent employees. High agency usage inflates labor cost by 30–60% vs. equivalent permanent staff and signals operational instability.

**EBITDAR Coverage:** Earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and rent, divided by rent. The primary metric for lenders and operators assessing cash flow adequacy to cover rent obligations in a triple-net or RIDEA structure. `EBITDAR Coverage = EBITDAR / Annual Rent`.

**Move-In / Move-Out Rate:** The velocity of resident turnover. High turnover inflates marketing costs, staffing transition costs, and indicates potential care quality issues. For SNFs, average length of stay (short-stay Medicare vs. long-stay Medicaid) drives reimbursement mix.

## Common Deal Structures

Senior housing acquisitions use [[Core Acquisition]] for stabilized IL and AL communities with strong operator history, and [[Value-Add Acquisition]] for communities with occupancy or operational upside. The dominant institutional structure is the RIDEA structure (REIT Investment Diversification and Empowerment Act), where the REIT owns the real estate (PropCo) and partners with an operating company (OpCo) via a TRS (taxable REIT subsidiary), sharing in operating upside rather than receiving a fixed triple-net rent. This aligns incentives between owner and operator but exposes the investor to operational risk.

Triple-net leases to national senior housing operators are common for more passive investors, particularly in IL and SNF; in this structure the operator bears operational risk and pays a fixed or CPI-adjusted rent regardless of operating performance. RIDEA structures are typical for value-add and growth-oriented strategies. [[Ground-Up Development]] is pursued by sponsors with established operator relationships. Financing includes [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] for qualifying IL properties, HUD/FHA Section 232 debt for SNF and AL (low rate, long amortization), [[Life Company Debt]] for stabilized communities, and [[Bridge Loan]] financing during lease-up.

## Major Risks

**Labor Market Risk:** Staffing shortages, wage inflation, and agency staffing costs are the dominant operational risk in AL and MC. Communities in tight labor markets may experience compressing margins even at high occupancy if agency labor is required to maintain minimum staffing ratios.

**Regulatory / Survey Risk:** AL communities are licensed by state agencies with regular survey inspections. A deficiency citation can trigger remediation costs, reputational damage, and in severe cases, license suspension. SNF CMS survey results are public (Care Compare ratings) and directly affect referral volumes.

**COVID-19 and Infectious Disease Risk:** The sector learned acutely from COVID-19 that infectious disease can devastate occupancy and dramatically increase operating costs simultaneously. Infection control infrastructure and protocols are now a permanent operating cost and underwriting consideration.

**Reimbursement Risk (SNF):** Medicare and Medicaid rate changes, PDPM (Patient-Driven Payment Model) methodology updates, and state Medicaid appropriations can materially affect SNF revenue with limited notice. Medicaid underfunds true cost of care in many states, creating structural margin pressure for Medicaid-heavy operators.

**Operator Risk:** In RIDEA structures, the performance of the community is directly tied to operator quality. Operator changes are expensive and disruptive. Underwriting must assess management team depth, track record, regional density, and financial stability.

**CON Exposure (SNF):** In certificate-of-need states, existing SNF licenses are valuable assets — but CON elimination or waiver by state legislatures can materially affect the value of these licenses.

## Regulatory Considerations

Senior housing is among the most heavily regulated CRE asset classes. AL and MC are licensed by state departments of health or social services under state-specific regulations that govern staffing ratios, training requirements, admission criteria, physical plant standards, and resident rights. SNFs are federally certified under Medicare and Medicaid Conditions of Participation (42 CFR Part 483) and subject to annual CMS surveys, with Five-Star Quality Rating implications for referral volumes. CCRCs are regulated by state insurance departments in most states (entrance fee refund obligations are treated as insurance liabilities), with significant financial disclosure and actuarial reserve requirements.

Fair Housing Act and Americans with Disabilities Act apply to IL communities (with age-restriction exemptions under the Housing for Older Persons Act). Many states have mandatory minimum liability insurance and staffing ratio laws. Environmental regulations for communities with laundry, kitchen, and hazardous medical waste apply. Certificate-of-need laws remain in force for SNF licensing in approximately 35 states, affecting both new construction and acquisition of bed licenses.

## Notable Markets

**Florida (Miami, Sarasota, Naples, Boca Raton, Tampa Bay):** The largest US senior housing market by volume; driven by retiree migration, warm climate, and no state income tax. High density of luxury IL and CCRC product.

**Arizona (Scottsdale, Chandler, Sun City, Tucson):** Major retirement destination market; active Senior Living development driven by population growth and retiree in-migration.

**Sun Belt broadly (Georgia, Texas, North Carolina, Tennessee):** Rapidly growing senior populations driven by in-migration of working-age families who will age in place.

**California (coastal markets):** High-cost, supply-constrained markets with wealthy demographics and strong private-pay revenue, but difficult entitlement and high construction cost.

**Midwest markets (Chicago suburbs, Columbus, Minneapolis):** Dense existing senior housing inventory; less growth-driven but with established operator ecosystems.
