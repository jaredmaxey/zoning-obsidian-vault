# Student Housing

## Overview

Student housing encompasses purpose-built residential communities and adapted multifamily properties serving the residential needs of college and university students. As a distinct institutional asset class, purpose-built student housing (PBSA) is characterized by bed-level leases (rather than unit-level leases), annual lease cycles aligned with the academic year, amenity packages targeting the student demographic, and investment returns that are structurally tied to university enrollment trends. The asset class has matured significantly from its origins as informal near-campus apartment operations into a sophisticated sector served by specialized REITs, institutional sponsors, and national operators.

Student housing offers relatively stable demand anchored by enrollment at well-positioned, research-intensive universities — demand that is structurally insulated from economic cycles because students attend college regardless of GDP growth. The sector's downside case, however, is a structural one: long-term US college enrollment trends show demographic pressure from declining high school graduate cohorts in many regions, and some universities have pursued aggressive on-campus housing programs or P3 partnerships that compete with off-campus operators. International student enrollment, a significant demand driver at many flagship universities, is subject to immigration policy volatility.

Institutional ownership is led by American Campus Communities (acquired by Blackstone in 2022), Greystar, The Scion Group, Landmark Properties, and Campus Advantage. The sector attracts value-add sponsors pursuing renovation plays on older near-campus communities and core buyers seeking long-WALT, well-occupied assets at flagship universities.

## Subtypes

**Purpose-Built Student Housing (PBSA) — Off-Campus:** Newly constructed or recently renovated purpose-built communities immediately adjacent to or within walkable distance of a major university. Features bed-by-the-bed leasing, roommate matching, academic amenities (study rooms, computer labs), and lifestyle amenities (pools, fitness centers, game rooms) targeting the student demographic. The institutional "gold standard" product type.

**On-Campus P3 Development:** Public-Private Partnership (P3) development where a private developer finances, builds, and operates student housing on university-owned land under a long-term ground lease (typically 40–75 years). The university retains land ownership; the developer earns revenue from student rents. Risk profile combines real estate development risk with counterparty dependency on the university.

**Conventional Apartment Communities Near Campus:** Standard multifamily properties that happen to be located near a university and informally attract student tenants. Not purpose-built; lack specialized amenities and bed-level lease structures. Represent the value-add conversion opportunity when renovated to student-focused product.

**Single-Family Rental Near Campus:** Individual houses or small multi-unit properties near campus rented to students by private landlords. Fragmented, primarily mom-and-pop owned; not a primary institutional product type.

## Demand Drivers

Total enrollment at 4-year universities is the foundational demand driver; institutional quality (flagship state universities, R1 research institutions) provides the most durable demand because these institutions have maintained or grown enrollment through cycles and attract national and international students. International student enrollment — concentrated at top research universities — has been one of the fastest-growing demand segments and carries immigration policy risk. Graduate student enrollment is growing faster than undergraduate at many institutions, creating demand for higher-quality, more private housing configurations.

Off-campus housing absorption is additionally driven by university-mandated policies restricting on-campus housing to freshmen (the largest cohort push for off-campus demand is sophomore-and-above students no longer housed on campus), and the gap between on-campus housing capacity and total enrollment. Markets where the university cannot house a significant portion of its student body (typically universities where on-campus beds represent less than 25–35% of enrollment) have the strongest off-campus demand.

Leading indicators: university enrollment trends, international student application volumes, on-campus bed expansion plans, state university system funding, and demographic data for the 18–22 year-old population.

## Supply Dynamics

New PBSA supply is generally concentrated near universities with strong enrollment and demonstrated rent growth, and requires walking or biking distance access to campus for competitive positioning. Land acquisition near major universities is challenging and expensive; entitlement in dense college towns often involves community opposition to dense student housing developments. Development timelines run 24–36 months for mid-rise product; high-rise takes longer.

The sector has experienced periodic supply overbuilds at specific universities when multiple developers simultaneously target the same market. Markets with declining enrollment or aggressive university P3 programs face structural supply/demand imbalances. The overall US PBSA pipeline contracted post-2020 as financing costs rose and labor and materials inflation increased construction costs, supporting occupancy recovery at existing communities in tight supply markets.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Bed Occupancy Rate:** Occupied beds / total beds. Student housing leases by the bed (each resident signs an individual lease, even in shared units), making bed-level occupancy the fundamental operating metric — distinct from unit occupancy in conventional multifamily. `Bed Occupancy = Occupied Beds / Total Beds`.

$$\text{Bed Occupancy} = \frac{\text{Occupied Beds}}{\text{Total Beds}}$$

**Revenue per Available Bed (RevPAB):** Total annual revenue divided by total available beds. Analogous to RevPAR in hospitality; normalizes across different unit configurations (2BR/2BA vs. 4BR/4BA). `RevPAB = Total Revenue / Total Available Beds`.

**Pre-Leasing Rate by Date:** The percentage of beds pre-leased for the upcoming academic year as of a specific calendar date (e.g., 90 days prior to move-in). The single most important leading indicator for occupancy and revenue risk. Industry benchmarks vary by university tier and market seasonality.

**Distance to Campus (Minutes Walking / Biking):** Proximity to the university's main academic core is the strongest determinant of competitive positioning and rent premium. A community within 5–10 minutes walking typically commands 10–20% rent premium over comparable product at 15–20 minutes.

**Beds per Unit Ratio:** Average number of beds per apartment unit. Higher ratios (3–4 beds per unit) reduce cost per bed for tenants and can improve revenue per square foot for operators; lower ratios (1–2 beds/unit) appeal to older students and graduate students but reduce occupancy diversification.

**Annual Lease Expiration Risk:** Because student housing leases typically expire simultaneously in May or August, the operator faces complete re-leasing of the entire property annually. Pre-leasing execution determines occupancy for the subsequent academic year; a poor pre-lease season has an outsized annual revenue impact.

## Common Deal Structures

Student housing acquisitions typically use [[Core Acquisition]] for well-occupied, flagship-university assets with strong pre-leasing history, or [[Value-Add Acquisition]] for older near-campus communities requiring renovation and rebranding. [[Ground-Up Development]] by specialized developers adjacent to high-enrollment universities is the primary new supply mechanism. P3 on-campus developments use ground lease structures with the university as ground lessor; these assets require university credit analysis in addition to real estate underwriting.

Lease structures are individual bed leases (each occupant signs independently) rather than unit leases, providing operators with protection against a single occupant's default affecting the full unit. Leases are typically 12-month academic-year terms aligned with the university calendar; some properties offer semester-length options at a premium. Management is typically contracted to specialized student housing operators (Greystar, Campus Advantage, Cardinal Group) or performed by integrated sponsor-operators. Financing uses [[Senior Debt]] from banks and [[CMBS]], with [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] available for qualifying student housing properties that meet conventional underwriting standards.

## Major Risks

**Enrollment Decline:** A university's enrollment contraction directly reduces demand for off-campus housing. Some institutions face secular enrollment pressure from demographic decline (particularly smaller private colleges and regional state universities), and overbuilds at declining schools can cause severe occupancy and valuation damage.

**International Student Policy Risk:** Immigration policy changes — visa restrictions, processing delays, or political tensions affecting source-country enrollment — can rapidly reduce enrollment at flagship universities heavily dependent on international students. This risk was demonstrated during the first Trump administration and remains a structural vulnerability.

**University On-Campus Competition:** When a university builds or expands on-campus housing (particularly via P3 partnerships), it directly competes with off-campus operators. Mandatory on-campus housing requirements for additional classes amplify the impact. On-campus housing policy changes require ongoing monitoring.

**Annual Re-Leasing Risk:** Unlike conventional multifamily with staggered lease expirations, student housing faces complete portfolio re-leasing each year. A poor pre-leasing season (low velocity due to competitive supply, enrollment softness, or operator mis-execution) directly impacts annual NOI with limited ability to course-correct mid-year.

**Operational Intensity:** Student tenants generate above-average maintenance, turnover, and legal costs. Summer vacancy periods require intensive maintenance and turnover work. Insurance and liability for student-occupied properties can be elevated.

## Regulatory Considerations

Student housing is subject to standard multifamily regulatory frameworks (Fair Housing Act, ADA, local landlord-tenant law) plus university-specific requirements in P3 structures. Many college towns have specific zoning overlays or use restrictions limiting high-density residential near campuses, protecting neighborhood character. Some municipalities have adopted student housing-specific licensing requirements, occupancy limits, or noise ordinances that affect operating costs. HUD rules allow universities to restrict dormitory-style housing to students without triggering Fair Housing single-occupancy issues, but off-campus private operators must comply with full Fair Housing Act requirements. International building codes (IBC) and local fire codes apply to all facilities; high-occupancy student buildings may trigger life-safety requirements above standard residential.

## Notable Markets

**Austin, TX (UT Austin):** One of the largest and fastest-growing student housing markets; UT Austin's large enrollment, strong national brand, and Austin's broader growth make it among the most sought-after institutional markets.

**Columbus, OH (Ohio State):** Consistently high transaction volume given OSU's enormous enrollment (the largest single-campus enrollment in the US).

**Gainesville, FL / Baton Rouge, LA / College Station, TX:** Major flagship Southeastern Conference markets with large enrollments and active institutional investment.

**State College, PA (Penn State) / Tuscaloosa, AL (Alabama) / Knoxville, TN (UT):** Classic Midwestern and Southern flagship markets with dedicated student housing investment ecosystems.

**Berkeley, CA / Ann Arbor, MI / Madison, WI:** Supply-constrained, high-barrier-to-entry flagship markets with dense institutional demand.
