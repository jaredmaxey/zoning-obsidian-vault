# Data Center

## Overview

Data centers are purpose-built or highly specialized facilities designed to house computing hardware — servers, networking equipment, and storage systems — along with the critical mechanical and electrical infrastructure necessary to power and cool that hardware continuously. Within institutional CRE, data centers have evolved from niche corporate facilities into a mainstream alternative asset class driven by explosive demand for cloud computing, AI workloads, streaming, and enterprise digitization. The asset class is broadly bifurcated between hyperscale campuses serving a single cloud provider (Amazon Web Services, Microsoft Azure, Google Cloud, Meta) and multi-tenant colocation facilities that lease IT capacity by the cabinet, cage, kilowatt, or megawatt to many users simultaneously.

Investor sentiment as of 2026 remains strongly constructive despite rising construction costs, power procurement constraints, and interest-rate pressure. AI training and inference workloads have materially extended the demand runway beyond prior forecasts, with hyperscalers routinely pre-leasing capacity years in advance. The headline thesis for data centers is a long-duration, contractually bonded income stream backed by mission-critical tenants with high switching costs, combined with secular demand tailwinds from digitization, AI, and cloud migration. Key risks center on technological obsolescence, power availability and cost, and water usage in drought-prone markets.

Institutional ownership is concentrated in REITs (Equinix, Digital Realty, Iron Mountain, QTS/Blackstone), infrastructure funds, and a growing cohort of private equity sponsors. The asset class increasingly attracts sovereign wealth and pension capital seeking inflation-linked, long-duration cash flows. Sale-leaseback and build-to-suit structures remain common entry points for private investors.

## Subtypes

**Hyperscale / Build-to-Suit:** Single-tenant campuses developed or acquired to serve one hyperscale cloud provider. Characterized by large power contracts (50–500+ MW per campus), long initial lease terms (10–20 years), and triple-net or absolute-net structures. Development risk is mitigated by pre-lease or full pre-commitment before construction commences.

**Wholesale Colocation:** Multi-tenant facilities leasing capacity in large blocks — typically 250 kW to 10 MW per tenant — to enterprises and cloud providers needing dedicated space. Tenants bring their own IT equipment; the landlord provides power, cooling, physical security, and connectivity. Lease terms typically run 5–10 years with periodic escalations.

**Retail Colocation:** Facilities leasing smaller increments (1–50 kW, individual cabinets or cages) to a large number of tenants on shorter terms. Revenue per square foot is highest in this subtype, but management intensity, churn, and credit diversity are also highest. Connectivity density and carrier neutrality are primary competitive differentiators.

**Edge / Single-Tenant Enterprise:** Smaller facilities (1–10 MW) placed close to end users for latency-sensitive workloads (financial services, gaming, CDN). Also includes facilities built or acquired by a single enterprise to house its own IT infrastructure. These assets carry concentrated occupancy risk but often strong credit.

## Demand Drivers

The primary demand driver is growth in digital workloads: cloud adoption by enterprises transitioning from on-premise IT, hyperscaler investment in AI training infrastructure (GPUs requiring dense power loads of 30–100+ kW per rack versus 5–15 kW for traditional compute), streaming media, fintech, and the proliferation of IoT devices. AI inference at scale is the most recent demand accelerant and is expected to drive outsized power absorption through the late 2020s.

Secondary drivers include enterprise digital transformation mandates, data sovereignty and regulatory requirements compelling in-country or in-jurisdiction data storage, and the ongoing shift from corporate-owned data centers to leased colocation. Financial services, healthcare, and government sectors all face regulatory regimes that specify where and how data must be stored, creating captive demand in specific markets.

Leading indicators to watch: hyperscaler capital expenditure budgets, megawatt absorption rates in primary markets, power utility interconnection queue lengths (a leading indicator of supply constraints), and enterprise IT spending growth.

## Supply Dynamics

New supply is constrained primarily by power availability — the most acute bottleneck in nearly every major data center market. Securing utility interconnection agreements and power purchase agreements (PPAs) can add 18–48 months to a development timeline. Purpose-built construction is highly specialized; shell-and-core industrial shells are not meaningfully convertible without extraordinary investment. Full development timelines (land, entitlements, power, construction) typically run 3–6 years for greenfield campuses.

Secondary supply constraints include cooling water availability, fiber connectivity density, and zoning — data centers consume substantial acreage, power, and water while generating minimal employment and tax revenue per acre, creating municipal resistance in some jurisdictions. Barriers to entry are high, providing pricing power for established operators in supply-constrained markets. Historical supply cycles show periodic excess in legacy markets (Northern Virginia, Silicon Valley) when utility queues open up, compressing cap rates in the short term before demand reabsorbs.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Critical IT Load (MW):** Total megawatts of power available or contracted to IT equipment. The fundamental sizing metric; drives lease economics, construction cost, and valuation. Formula: `Critical IT Load = Total Utility Power × (1 – Overhead Ratio)`.

**Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE):** The ratio of total facility power to IT equipment power. A lower PUE indicates greater efficiency. Formula: `PUE = Total Facility Power (MW) / IT Load (MW)`. World-class hyperscale facilities approach PUE of 1.10–1.20; older facilities may run 1.40–1.80. Tenants increasingly require contractual PUE commitments.

$$\text{PUE} = \frac{\text{Total Facility Power (MW)}}{\text{IT Load (MW)}}$$

**Revenue per kW ($/kW/month):** The lease rate normalized to power capacity. The primary rent metric for wholesale and hyperscale. Formula: `Revenue/kW = Annual Rent ÷ (Leased kW × 12)`. Ranges vary widely by subtype and market.

**Megawatt Absorption:** Net new power leased in a market over a period (quarter or year). Analogous to net absorption in office/industrial; the single most-watched supply/demand metric. Reported by JLL, CBRE, and specialized data center advisory firms.

**Annualized Revenue per Leased MW:** Total annual revenues divided by leased critical IT load in megawatts. A normalization metric enabling cross-asset comparison.

**Colocation Revenue per Cabinet / Cage:** For retail colo, lease rates are often quoted per cabinet per month, ranging from a few hundred to several thousand dollars per cabinet depending on power density and market.

**Watts per Square Foot (W/SF):** Power density of the raised-floor or white-space, indicating how AI-capable the facility is. Legacy facilities may support 100–200 W/SF; modern hyperscale designs can support 500–1,000+ W/SF for GPU-dense AI deployments.

## Common Deal Structures

Data center acquisitions most commonly take the form of [[Core Acquisition]] or [[Core-Plus Acquisition]] of stabilized, long-leased assets by REITs and infrastructure funds, or [[Build-to-Suit]] developments where a hyperscale tenant pre-commits before construction. [[Sale-Leaseback]] structures are common when enterprises sell corporate data centers to operators and lease back the space. [[Value-Add Acquisition]] of older, under-powered facilities with re-leasing or re-powering potential is an emerging strategy.

Lease structures are predominantly NNN or absolute-net for wholesale/hyperscale; retail colo leases are typically gross or modified-gross with pass-through of power costs at cost-plus-margin. Management contracts are uncommon; most institutional owners self-operate or engage specialized operators. Joint ventures with hyperscale tenants are increasingly used to share development risk on large campuses, with the tenant contributing a long-term lease in exchange for below-market rents or build cost contributions.

Financing typically involves [[Senior Debt]] from life companies, banks, or private credit lenders; data center-specific CMBS pools have emerged. LTV thresholds tend to be lower than conventional real estate given the specialized nature of the asset. [[Construction Loan]] financing for development transitions to [[Permanent Loan]] or REIT balance-sheet funding at stabilization.

## Major Risks

**Technological Obsolescence:** Power density requirements are advancing rapidly, particularly for AI workloads. Facilities designed for 5–10 kW/rack loads can become functionally obsolete for GPU-dense tenants within one lease cycle. Mitigation requires modular design, flexible cooling infrastructure (rear-door heat exchangers, liquid cooling readiness), and active re-investment.

**Power Availability and Cost:** The scarcity of utility interconnection capacity in primary markets is the sector's most acute near-term constraint. Utility rate increases, carbon-pricing mechanisms, and renewable energy mandates add operating cost uncertainty. On-site generation and PPA structures partially hedge but add complexity.

**Tenant Concentration:** Hyperscale campuses often have a single tenant representing 100% of revenue. Loss of that tenant at lease expiration can render an asset non-income-producing while a replacement is found; re-leasing timelines for large assets can run 12–36 months.

**Water Usage:** Evaporative cooling systems consume millions of gallons annually. Regulatory pressure in water-stressed markets (Phoenix, Las Vegas, parts of the Pacific Northwest) may restrict water use or impose surcharges. Air-side economization and dry coolers reduce risk but increase capital cost.

**Entitlement and Community Opposition:** Some municipalities have restricted data center development due to the mismatch between power and water consumption and low job creation. Zoning constraints and utility moratoriums have emerged in Northern Virginia and parts of the Pacific Northwest.

## Regulatory Considerations

Data centers are generally zoned as light industrial or special-use; CUPs may be required in suburban locations. Utility interconnection agreements involve extensive public-utility commission processes in regulated markets. Environmental review (NEPA/CEQA or state equivalents) is triggered by large power draws and water usage in environmentally sensitive areas. Energy efficiency and renewable portfolio standards imposed by states affect operating costs; some jurisdictions require on-site renewable generation or purchase agreements for large power consumers.

FERC and state public utility commission rules govern interconnection timelines and priority queuing; delays of 3–7 years in some queues have become headline risk. In the EU, data center sustainability reporting under the EU Energy Efficiency Directive imposes disclosure and efficiency requirements that increasingly affect multi-national operators.

## Notable Markets

**Northern Virginia (Loudoun County / "Data Center Alley"):** The world's largest data center concentration; driven by proximity to federal government, major internet exchange points, and decades of fiber infrastructure investment. Utility constraints and community opposition have emerged as significant supply limitators.

**Phoenix / Chandler, AZ:** A major growth market driven by low land cost, low-cost utility power, business-friendly entitlement, and proximity to California demand. Water scarcity is an increasing constraint on evaporative cooling.

**Dallas–Fort Worth:** A large, diversified market with strong utility infrastructure, central US connectivity, and enterprise demand from Fortune 500 firms headquartered in the region.

**Chicago:** A key interconnection hub for east–west traffic; strong colocation market with diversified tenant base.

**Silicon Valley / Bay Area:** Legacy concentration of enterprise and colocation facilities; constrained by land cost, utility capacity, and seismic risk. Net absorption has shifted to other markets.

**Atlanta, Columbus OH, Portland, Amsterdam, Frankfurt, Singapore:** Other major global data center markets with distinct regulatory and utility characteristics.
