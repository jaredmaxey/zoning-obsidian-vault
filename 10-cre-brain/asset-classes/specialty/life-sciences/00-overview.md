# Life Sciences

## Overview

Life sciences real estate encompasses laboratory, research and development, and biomanufacturing facilities purpose-built or adapted to serve biotech, pharmaceutical, genomics, medical device, and academic/research tenants. Unlike conventional office buildings, life sciences facilities require extensive specialized infrastructure — high-bay spaces to accommodate tall equipment, reinforced floor loads (150–300 lbs/SF), dedicated HVAC systems maintaining precise temperature, humidity, and biosafety conditions, 100% outdoor-air exhaust, above-standard electrical capacity, chemical storage and waste handling systems, and abundant vivarium space in some configurations. The capital intensity of building out functional laboratory space creates powerful switching costs for tenants and high barriers to entry for landlords.

The sector experienced a historic expansion cycle between 2020 and 2022, fueled by the COVID-19 pandemic, record NIH funding, and venture capital pouring into life sciences startups. By 2023–2024, a correction set in as VC funding contracted, early-stage startups downsized, and excess sublease space entered the market in secondary clusters. As of 2026, the market is differentiating sharply: established primary clusters (Cambridge, San Francisco Bay Area, San Diego) show resilient demand from large pharma and established biotechs, while secondary markets face meaningful lease-up risk on newly delivered speculative supply.

Institutional investors have broadly embraced life sciences as a long-term conviction play on biomedical innovation, an aging global population, and the pipeline of therapeutic breakthroughs in oncology, immunology, and gene therapy. The asset class offers above-market rents relative to conventional office, long lease terms, and durable demand from a knowledge-economy tenant base that is relatively insulated from remote-work disruption — lab work is inherently on-site.

## Subtypes

**Wet Lab / Research Lab:** The core life sciences product type. Space equipped for bench-level laboratory work including chemistry, biology, and genomics research. Requires specialized HVAC (100% OA, high air changes per hour), fume hoods, plumbing, chemical-resistant surfaces, and biosafety infrastructure. Most institutional life sciences investment targets this subtype.

**cGMP / Biomanufacturing:** Current Good Manufacturing Practice facilities used for clinical-scale or commercial-scale production of biologics, cell therapies, or pharmaceuticals. Higher technical specifications than R&D labs; regulatory inspections by FDA and equivalent agencies add compliance layers. Development timelines are longer; tenants include contract development and manufacturing organizations (CDMOs) and large pharma.

**Hybrid Lab-Office (Flex Lab):** Buildings designed to accommodate varying proportions of laboratory and office space, often on a floor-by-floor basis. Appeal is broadest to startup and growth-stage companies that need lab access but haven't determined their final space configuration. Many incubator and accelerator products fall in this category.

**Incubator / Accelerator:** Shared laboratory facilities targeting early-stage companies with short-term licenses rather than long-term leases. Operated by universities, economic development agencies, or private sponsors. Revenue per square foot is highest, but occupancy is more volatile and management intensity is greatest.

## Demand Drivers

Federal research funding (NIH budget), National Science Foundation grants, and institutional academic research expenditures are the foundational demand drivers — they underwrite the talent pipeline and basic science that fuels biotech formation. Venture capital investment into life sciences startups is the most cyclically volatile driver; it translates NIH-funded discoveries into laboratory-using companies. Large pharmaceutical companies and their clinical-stage pipeline drive demand for GMP manufacturing and specialized R&D space.

Demographic tailwinds are structural: aging Baby Boomers increase demand for therapeutics, diagnostics, and medical devices. Breakthroughs in cell and gene therapy, mRNA platforms (accelerated by COVID-19), and AI-driven drug discovery create new categories of lab user. Patent cliffs forcing large pharma to acquire pipeline from biotechs sustain M&A activity that reshuffles but sustains overall tenant demand in major clusters.

Leading indicators: NIH appropriations, quarterly venture capital funding into biotech/pharma, FDA approval rates, Biotech stock index performance (XBI), sublease availability rates in primary clusters.

## Supply Dynamics

Supply in life sciences is structurally constrained in primary clusters by the concentration of specialized talent and academic anchor institutions. Life sciences tenants are highly location-sensitive: they must be near world-class research universities, teaching hospitals, and the talent pools they generate. Cambridge, MA (MIT, Harvard, MGH) and Mission Bay in San Francisco (UCSF) are the canonical examples. These factors concentrate demand in geographies where land is scarce and entitlement is challenging.

Development timelines for purpose-built lab buildings run 3–5 years from land acquisition to occupancy, due in part to specialized MEP coordination and longer entitlement processes in dense urban markets. Construction costs for life sciences shells are substantially above conventional office — typically 30%–80% above comparable office shell costs, and fit-out costs for tenant improvements run $150–$400/SF or more. Conversion of existing office or industrial buildings to lab space is possible in some cases (e.g., floor-to-floor heights of 13+ feet are generally required) but involves significant capital and technical risk. The 2021–2023 speculative development wave has left excess supply in some secondary markets that will take several years to absorb.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Rent per Square Foot (Annual NNN):** The headline metric; laboratory rents are typically quoted on a NNN or modified-gross basis and run substantially above office rents in the same market due to specialized infrastructure. `Lab Rent Premium = Lab Rent/SF – Comparable Office Rent/SF`.

**Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT):** Life sciences leases, particularly for larger established tenants, tend to run 7–15 years; WALT is critical to valuation stability. Shorter WALT increases re-leasing risk given the specialized buildout.

**Lab-Ready vs. Warm Shell vs. Cold Shell:** A spectrum of infrastructure readiness that determines TI requirements. Lab-ready (fully fit-out) commands highest rents; cold shell (base building only) requires largest TI allowance. The landlord's upfront investment and rent premium must be modeled explicitly.

**Vacancy by Product Type (Wet Lab vs. Dry/Office):** Projects with hybrid wet-dry configurations should track vacancy separately for each component; dry/office vacancy in life sciences buildings has risen sharply post-2023 while wet lab space remains tighter.

**Tenant Credit / Stage:** The credit quality spectrum runs from pre-revenue Series A startups (high default risk, short leases) to publicly-traded large pharma (investment-grade). WALT weighted by credit tier is a more meaningful metric than simple WALT.

**Sublease Availability Rate:** In life sciences clusters, the sublease rate is a leading indicator of demand health — rising sublease space (common when startups downsize or fail) compresses effective rents and signals oversupply before direct vacancy rises.

## Common Deal Structures

Life sciences acquisitions typically follow [[Core Acquisition]] or [[Value-Add Acquisition]] structures; [[Build-to-Suit]] and [[Ground-Up Development]] are common for sponsor-developer platforms in established clusters. [[Adaptive Reuse]] of older office or industrial buildings with sufficient floor-to-floor heights and floor loading is an active strategy. [[Sale-Leaseback]] is used by large pharma or research institutions monetizing owned campuses.

Lease structures are predominantly NNN or modified-gross, with landlords providing significant TI allowances (often amortized into the rent) in exchange for longer terms. Rent bumps of 2%–3% annually or fixed step-ups are standard. JV structures pairing institutional capital with specialized operators (Alexandria Real Estate Equities, BioMed Realty, Healthpeak, and numerous private sponsors) are common; operator expertise in managing specialized tenant requirements is a genuine differentiator.

Financing uses [[Senior Debt]] from banks, life companies, and [[Private Credit]], often at conservative LTV given the specialized nature; [[Bridge Loan]] financing is common during lease-up of newly delivered or converted space. CMBS execution is possible for stabilized, long-leased assets with institutional tenants.

## Major Risks

**Cluster Dependency:** Life sciences demand is profoundly cluster-dependent. Secondary markets that attempted to replicate Cambridge or San Diego by delivering speculative supply without an anchor academic institution face multi-year lease-up risk. Location selection is the single most consequential underwriting decision.

**Venture Capital Funding Cyclicality:** Early-stage tenant demand tracks VC investment closely. A sharp VC contraction (as seen in 2022–2023) eliminates demand from startups and growth-stage companies and pushes sublease space into the market. This cyclicality is a distinguishing risk versus other asset classes.

**Tenant Failure:** Pre-revenue biotech tenants can exhaust their capital and vacate; credit analysis requires more than balance-sheet review — pipeline milestones, burn rate, and cash runway matter. A single major tenant failure can have an outsized NOI impact on a single-tenant or small-tenant building.

**Obsolescence of Lab Infrastructure:** Rapid evolution of laboratory technology (especially AI-driven drug discovery platforms with different space and power needs) can render specific buildouts obsolete before lease expiration. Flexibility of MEP systems and floor plates is a key underwriting consideration.

**Regulatory / Clinical Risk:** Tenant revenue is contingent on FDA approvals and clinical trial outcomes. A Phase 3 failure can trigger a tenant's downsizing or failure, with direct occupancy impact on the landlord.

## Regulatory Considerations

Life sciences facilities are subject to biosafety regulations (CDC/NIH biosafety levels BSL-1 through BSL-4 for research using biological agents), chemical safety regulations (EPA, OSHA Hazard Communication), and — for GMP manufacturing — FDA Current Good Manufacturing Practice standards (21 CFR Parts 210/211 for pharmaceuticals, 21 CFR Parts 820 for medical devices). Vivarium spaces (animal research) require IACUC certification and are subject to USDA Animal Welfare Act compliance, affecting building operations significantly. Local fire codes have specific requirements for chemical storage (NFPA 45). Environmental review for large campuses may require hazardous materials management plan approval.

Building permits for life sciences facilities are more complex than conventional office due to chemical fume hood exhaust, vivarium exhaust, and biohazardous waste systems. Entitlement in dense urban clusters (Cambridge, San Francisco) involves lengthy community review processes. Some municipalities offer life sciences overlay zones or specific plans to streamline approvals.

## Notable Markets

**Cambridge / Boston:** The world's preeminent life sciences cluster; anchored by MIT, Harvard, Dana-Farber, MGH, and a dense network of venture firms and large pharma offices. Rents are among the highest globally; vacancy is structurally low for top-quality lab space despite cyclical softness.

**San Francisco Bay Area (South San Francisco / Mission Bay):** The second major US cluster; anchored by UCSF and legacy biotech firms (Genentech, Gilead). Strong VC ecosystem; constrained land supply.

**San Diego (Torrey Pines / UTC / Sorrento Valley):** A top-three US market with anchors at UCSD and Scripps Research; favorable regulatory environment and land availability relative to Boston/SF.

**Research Triangle Park, NC:** Growing market anchored by Duke, UNC, and NC State; benefiting from corporate relocations and lower cost base.

**Philadelphia / King of Prussia:** Proximity to Penn, CHOP, and Jefferson Health systems; LIHTC-funded biotech incubators growing the cluster.

**Maryland (Rockville / Bethesda):** Proximity to NIH headquarters drives federal research demand; home to major clinical-stage companies.
