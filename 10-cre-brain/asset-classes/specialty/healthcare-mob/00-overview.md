# Healthcare / Medical Office (MOB)

## Overview

Medical office buildings (MOBs) are purpose-built or adapted commercial properties designed to house outpatient healthcare services including primary care, specialty physician practices, surgical centers, imaging and diagnostic facilities, physical therapy, behavioral health, and a growing spectrum of ambulatory care settings. The defining operational characteristic is proximity to or affiliation with a hospital or health system, which drives patient referral patterns and establishes the tenant's revenue base. MOBs occupy a distinct position in the CRE landscape: structurally similar to conventional office in form, but functionally more akin to critical infrastructure due to the embedded nature of healthcare delivery, the regulatory framework governing tenants, and the high switching costs created by medical infrastructure build-outs.

Institutional sentiment toward MOBs is durably positive. Healthcare real estate benefits from non-discretionary demand, an aging US population with rising per-capita healthcare consumption, and a powerful secular shift from inpatient hospital-based care to lower-cost outpatient settings. Payers (Medicare, Medicaid, private insurers) actively incentivize outpatient delivery, and health systems are aggressively expanding their ambulatory network footprints. These tailwinds provide a relatively stable income stream compared to conventional office, and MOB vacancy has historically held well through economic downturns.

The investor universe includes specialized healthcare REITs (Healthpeak, Ventas, Physicians Realty Trust / DocuSign Healthcare, NNN Healthcare), private equity sponsors with healthcare operating expertise, and institutional buyers attracted by the long lease terms and health-system credit quality of top-tier MOBs. Portfolio acquisitions from health systems via [[Sale-Leaseback]] have been a significant source of institutional deal flow.

## Subtypes

**On-Campus MOB (Health System–Owned or Affiliated):** Buildings located on hospital campuses or immediately adjacent, typically leased to or affiliated with the anchor health system. On-campus positioning creates structural demand through patient referral continuity; the health system often serves as master tenant or credit backstop. These assets carry the tightest cap rates and most favorable financing terms.

**Off-Campus / Community MOB:** Buildings in retail corridors, suburban medical parks, or stand-alone locations. Tenant base is typically private physician groups, national practice management companies, or health-system satellites. More vulnerable to tenant attrition than on-campus MOBs, but benefit from proximity to patient population centers.

**Ambulatory Surgery Center (ASC):** Dedicated outpatient surgical facilities performing procedures that formerly required hospital admission (orthopedic, ophthalmology, GI, ENT, etc.). Revenue per square foot is highest among MOB subtypes; tenant mix often includes physician equity ownership creating alignment. ASCs face regulatory scrutiny under Medicare Conditions for Coverage.

**Imaging / Diagnostic Center:** Facilities housing MRI, CT, PET, and other imaging equipment. High capital build-out cost creates tenant stickiness; imaging reimbursement rates are under ongoing pressure from CMS, creating revenue risk for tenants and by extension landlords.

**Behavioral Health / Specialty Clinic:** A growing segment driven by unmet demand for mental health and substance use disorder treatment. Typically lower infrastructure intensity than traditional MOB; regulatory requirements vary significantly by state and license type.

## Demand Drivers

The aging US population is the foundation of MOB demand: adults over 65 consume healthcare services at roughly 3–4x the rate of younger adults, and this cohort is growing as a share of the total population. The Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services (CMS) policy to shift care to lower-cost ambulatory settings — through bundled payment reforms, outpatient-only procedure listings, and site-neutral payment policies — structurally increases demand for outpatient facilities while reducing hospital occupancy. Health system strategic expansion of ambulatory networks to capture market share and improve population health management drives new MOB development and lease demand.

Secondary drivers include physician consolidation into large practice management platforms and health-system employment models (which strengthen tenant credit), the growth of value-based care arrangements requiring primary care infrastructure, and consumer preferences for convenient community-based care. Telehealth adoption slightly offsets demand for routine primary care visits but has minimal impact on procedural and imaging demand.

Leading indicators: CMS reimbursement rate announcements (especially ambulatory surgery fee schedules), health system operating margins (which affect expansion capacity), physician employment trends, and population growth in suburban and Sun Belt markets.

## Supply Dynamics

New MOB supply is primarily health-system-driven — the anchor health system's network strategy determines where buildings are needed, how quickly they are developed, and how they are occupied. Speculative development without a health system tenant is uncommon and carries substantial lease-up risk. Barriers to entry include health system relationships (often required for on-campus development rights), medical-grade construction requirements (reinforced floors, plumbing upgrades, higher electrical capacity), and HIPAA-compliant building systems.

Development timelines run 18–36 months for new construction; conversion of conventional office to MOB is possible but requires significant capital investment in HVAC, plumbing, and electrical. The supply pipeline is moderately constrained in high-demand suburban markets due to land cost, entitlement timelines, and the health-system relationship requirement. Unlike conventional office, MOB has not seen destructive speculative oversupply cycles in most markets; the concentrated tenant base and health-system oversight of expansion limit excess supply.

## Key Operating Metrics

**On-Campus vs. Off-Campus Classification:** The most fundamental valuation differentiator. On-campus buildings trade at 50–150 bps tighter cap rates than comparable off-campus assets due to structural demand and credit benefits. Defined by direct adjacency to or ground-lease on the hospital campus.

**Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT):** Critical for MOB valuation. Longer WALT with health-system credit commands premium pricing; short WALT with private-practice tenants introduces re-leasing risk. Formula: `WALT = Σ(Lease Term Remaining × Tenant Rent) / Total Rent`.

$$\text{WALT} = \frac{\sum_{i}(\text{Remaining Term}_i \times \text{Annual Rent}_i)}{\sum_{i} \text{Annual Rent}_i}$$

**Health-System Credit Percentage:** The share of NOI attributable to investment-grade or nationally recognized health-system tenants vs. private physician groups. Higher credit percentage supports lower cap rates and broader lender appetite.

**Revenue per Square Foot:** Annual gross revenue (base rent plus any expense recoveries) divided by rentable square footage. Benchmarks against comparable medical office in the same market.

**Occupancy Rate:** Percentage of rentable SF leased. Persistent high occupancy (typically 90–97% for on-campus MOBs) is a quality indicator; below 85% signals either asset quality or market weakness.

**Lease Rollover Profile:** The percentage of leases rolling within the next 12, 24, and 36 months. Critical for refinancing risk and hold/sell decisions. High near-term rollover in a softening market or with marginal tenants is a significant risk factor.

**TI per Lease Year:** Total TI allowance divided by lease term in years. Normalizes TI across different lease lengths for comparison. `TI per Lease Year = Total TI / Lease Term (years)`.

## Common Deal Structures

MOB acquisitions are dominated by [[Core Acquisition]] of stabilized, long-leased assets by REITs, pension funds, and core-plus private equity. [[Sale-Leaseback]] from health systems is the primary source of large portfolio deal flow — health systems monetize real estate to deploy capital into clinical operations, while retaining operating control via long-term net leases. [[Build-to-Suit]] development for expanding health system networks is the primary development entry point; ground-up speculative development is rare. [[Value-Add Acquisition]] of older MOBs with lease rollover or capital needs is a smaller but active strategy.

Lease structures are predominantly NNN or gross-modified NNN, with health-system credit tenants often demanding institutional-grade build-outs in exchange for longer terms. Ground leases on hospital campuses (ground rent typically 8–12% of land value annually) are common for on-campus buildings, adding lease complexity but enhancing occupancy stability. JV structures pairing healthcare REIT operating expertise with institutional equity capital are standard for platform acquisitions.

Financing uses [[Life Company Debt]], [[CMBS]], and [[Senior Debt]] from banks. On-campus, long-WALT, health-system-leased assets access the best terms in the market; off-campus community MOBs are typically financed by commercial banks or bridge lenders during lease-up.

## Major Risks

**Health System Financial Stress:** Hospital operating margins are under persistent pressure from labor costs, Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement, and post-pandemic cost structures. Financially stressed health systems have reduced network expansion, renegotiated leases, or sold assets at distress — all risk factors for MOB landlords. Tenant concentration in a single health system is the primary credit risk.

**Reimbursement Rate Risk:** CMS and private insurer reimbursement rates directly affect tenant revenue. Site-neutral payment policies reducing outpatient reimbursement to hospital-based levels, or procedure-level rate cuts, can impair tenant EBITDA and lead to lease renegotiations or failures, particularly for ASC and imaging tenants.

**Physician Practice Volatility:** Private-practice physician groups have shorter lease terms and lower credit than health systems; group dissolution, retirement waves, or hospital employment transitions can create sudden vacancy in off-campus MOBs without health-system backstop.

**Regulatory Changes:** Changes to Medicare coverage (including outpatient-only procedure lists, which can shift procedures from hospital to ASC or vice versa) alter the economics of specific MOB subtypes materially. HIPAA amendments and state privacy laws may require capital investment in building systems.

**Functional Obsolescence:** Aging MOB buildings with inadequate floor-to-floor heights, outdated HVAC, or insufficient plumbing density may require substantial capital reinvestment as tenants' technology and infection-control requirements evolve.

## Regulatory Considerations

Healthcare tenants are subject to extensive federal and state licensure: CMS Conditions of Participation govern ASCs and other Medicare/Medicaid-certified providers; state health department certificate-of-need (CON) laws restrict the opening of new ASCs and imaging centers in approximately 35 states, limiting supply in those markets and protecting existing facilities. ADA compliance is more stringent for healthcare facilities than standard office (accessible exam rooms, accessible equipment standards). HIPAA requires physical safeguards in clinical areas. Lab and imaging spaces handling radioactive materials are subject to NRC and state radiation control regulations.

Local zoning may require healthcare-specific use permits (CUPs) for ASCs, behavioral health facilities, or clinics with overnight observation. Building codes (IBC Chapter 4, NFPA 101, and FGI Guidelines for Design and Construction) impose life-safety requirements for occupancy classification that are more demanding than standard office. Ground lease structures on hospital campuses may include use restrictions, reversion clauses, or change-of-control provisions that affect transferability.

## Notable Markets

**Sun Belt metros (Phoenix, Dallas, Houston, Atlanta, Nashville, Orlando, Raleigh):** The highest-growth MOB markets driven by population in-migration, aging demographics, and health system expansion. Low barriers to outpatient development relative to Northeast/West Coast markets.

**Suburban Chicago, suburban New York, suburban Philadelphia:** Established MOB markets with dense existing inventory and strong health-system anchors; less growth-driven but durable demand.

**Washington DC metro / Northern Virginia:** Strong demand from federal employee/contractor demographics and proximity to NIH and major academic medical centers.

**Nashville:** A national healthcare industry headquarters hub (HCA, Acadia, Community Health Systems) with disproportionate MOB transaction activity.
