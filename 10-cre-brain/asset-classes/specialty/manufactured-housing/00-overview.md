# Manufactured Housing

## Overview

Manufactured housing communities (MHCs), sometimes called mobile home parks or land-lease communities, are residential properties where the community owner owns the land and underlying infrastructure (streets, utilities, common areas) while individual residents own their manufactured home units and pay a monthly lot rent to the community. This land-lease structure creates a uniquely resilient income stream: residents have invested tens of thousands of dollars in their home, which is expensive and logistically difficult to move, creating extremely high retention rates and making manufactured housing one of the lowest-turnover residential asset classes. The landlord's capital is deployed in land and infrastructure rather than the home, dramatically reducing the per-unit investment required.

Manufactured housing has emerged as an institutional asset class primarily because of the US housing affordability crisis. Manufactured homes are the largest source of unsubsidized affordable housing in the United States, serving households that cannot access conventional single-family ownership or market-rate apartment renting. Lot rents in most markets are substantially below comparable apartment rents, and the combination of homeownership (of the unit) and lower monthly housing costs attracts a sticky, working-class tenant base. Institutional investors have recognized the combination of affordability-driven demand, extremely low capital expenditure requirements, and secular tailwinds from housing cost pressures.

Institutional ownership is led by Sun Communities, Equity LifeStyle Properties, and UDR in the public REIT space, plus an active cohort of private equity sponsors including RHP Properties, Yes! Communities, and Havenpark Communities. The sector has attracted significant controversy regarding rent increases imposed by institutional landlords, with political and regulatory responses emerging in multiple states.

## Subtypes

**Land-Lease Community (Tenant-Owned Homes):** The dominant subtype. Residents own their manufactured home and lease only the lot from the community operator. The landlord receives lot rent with minimal operating expense — no home maintenance, minimal turnover cost. Value creation through lot rent increases is the primary strategy.

**Community-Owned Rental Homes:** The operator both owns the land/lots and the manufactured homes, renting the complete unit (home + lot) to residents. Higher capital intensity per pad, higher revenue per pad, but operating expense more closely resembles conventional multifamily. Enables faster lease-up of vacant sites and serves residents who cannot or choose not to purchase a home.

**Age-Restricted (55+) Community:** Communities restricted to residents 55 and older under the Housing for Older Persons Act (HOPA) exemption to Fair Housing. Higher-amenity communities in Sun Belt retirement destinations often appeal to retirees seeking affordable homeownership with social programming.

**Lifestyle / Amenity-Driven Community (RV Crossover):** High-amenity communities that may serve a mix of full-time residents, seasonal residents, and RV travelers. More complex operations; revenue streams include short-term RV site rentals as well as long-term lot leases. Closest analog to resort-oriented land uses.

## Demand Drivers

Workforce housing affordability is the structural demand driver: when single-family homeownership becomes inaccessible due to home prices or mortgage rates, and when market-rate apartment rents exceed household budgets, manufactured housing absorbs demand from the "missing middle" of working-class households. As of 2025–2026, housing unaffordability is at multi-decade highs in most US markets, creating sustained demand for MHC lot rents priced at a discount to both for-sale housing and conventional apartments.

Retiree demand is growing for age-restricted communities in Sun Belt markets: manufactured housing communities offer homeownership lifestyle and community amenities at a fraction of conventional single-family or condo pricing. Baby boomer retirements support this demand trajectory through the 2030s. Secondary drivers include immigration (workforce housing demand from new arrivals), job growth in markets where workforce housing supply is constrained, and the growing acceptance of HUD-code manufactured homes as comparable in quality to site-built housing following improvements in building standards.

## Supply Dynamics

New MHC supply is severely constrained — more so than almost any other residential asset class. Permitting new manufactured housing communities is politically contentious; many municipalities have outright banned new MHC development, and existing communities benefit from decades of grandfathered zoning that would not be approved today. This supply constraint means that portfolio concentration of existing communities is the primary institutional investment strategy; greenfield development of new MHCs is rare and site-specific. Development of new communities in permissive jurisdictions (rural areas, some Sun Belt submarkets) does occur, with development timelines of 18–30 months for infrastructure and initial fill-up.

Infill density — adding sites to existing communities by subdividing underutilized parcels — is a low-risk, high-return value-add strategy that does not require new entitlements in many cases. Site fill-up (placing new homes on vacant lots within existing communities) is another capital-light value-creation lever. These strategies make acquired communities with below-market occupancy or infill potential attractive value-add targets.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Lot Rent:** Monthly rent charged per pad/lot (land only, in tenant-owned home model). The fundamental revenue driver. Benchmarks against comparable MHC communities in the local market. Formula: `Gross Potential Revenue = Total Lots × Monthly Lot Rent × 12`.

**Homeowner vs. Rental Lot Mix:** The percentage of lots occupied by tenant-owned homes vs. community-owned rental homes. Higher tenant-owned percentage typically implies lower operating costs (no home maintenance) and higher NOI margin. A high rental home percentage may indicate value-add through home disposition (selling rental homes to residents at favorable terms, converting them to lot-rent income with higher margin).

**Occupancy Rate (Lots):** Occupied lots / total lots. Vacant lots generate zero revenue and are a direct value-creation opportunity. `Lot Occupancy = Occupied Lots / Total Lots`.

$$\text{Lot Occupancy} = \frac{\text{Occupied Lots}}{\text{Total Lots}}$$

**Infill Density / Vacant Sites:** The number of sites available for additional home placement within the existing community footprint. Each infill site represents a low-capital value-add opportunity.

**Revenue per Occupied Site:** Total annual revenue (lot rent + utility income + ancillary fees) divided by occupied sites. Normalizes across communities with different utility structures (some communities sub-meter utilities; others include utilities in lot rent).

**Utility Income Ratio:** The percentage of total revenue from utility billing (water, sewer, electric pass-through) vs. lot rent. High utility income can indicate sub-metering upside in communities that currently include utilities in lot rent.

**Home Sales Volume (Operator-Assisted):** Some operators actively facilitate home sales and resales within the community, earning commissions and providing financing. Active home sales programs fill vacant lots and improve community health metrics; this income stream should be tracked separately from base lot rent.

## Common Deal Structures

MHC acquisitions are dominated by [[Core Acquisition]] and [[Core-Plus Acquisition]] of stabilized communities by REITs and institutional sponsors, with [[Value-Add Acquisition]] of communities with below-market lot rents, high vacancy, or infill potential commanding the widest universe of buyers. [[Ground-Up Development]] of new communities is rare but pursued in permissive jurisdictions by experienced sponsors.

The asset class is particularly well-suited to buy-and-hold strategies given the extreme stability of cash flows and low capital expenditure requirements. Portfolio acquisitions from long-term mom-and-pop owners (who often lack succession plans and have held communities for decades at below-market rents) have been the primary source of institutional deal flow. JV structures are common for larger platforms; the GP typically provides operating expertise while the LP provides bulk capital.

Financing uses [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] for qualifying communities, [[Life Company Debt]] for high-quality stabilized assets, and [[CMBS]] for portfolio-level executions. MHC has among the lowest default rates in the real estate lending universe, making it attractive to conservative lenders.

## Major Risks

**Rent Increase Political Risk:** Aggressive rent increases by institutional operators have generated state and local legislative responses including rent stabilization ordinances for MHCs, right-of-first-refusal (ROFR) requirements allowing resident cooperatives to purchase communities before sale to investors, and notice requirements for rent increases. This regulatory risk is concentrated in states with organized tenant advocacy (California, Colorado, Oregon, Washington) but is spreading.

**Resident Displacement and Reputation Risk:** The asymmetric power dynamic — residents own their home but not the land, making relocation costly — creates significant reputational and political risk for operators who pursue aggressive rent increases or site redevelopment. ESG-conscious capital has reduced willingness to invest in operators with poor tenant relations records.

**Infrastructure Age:** Many MHCs were developed in the 1960s–1980s with aging water, sewer, and electrical systems. Infrastructure replacement capital (utility upgrades) can be a material unbudgeted cost in older vintage communities; due diligence on utility infrastructure condition is essential.

**Title and Legal Complexity:** MHC title is often complicated by home title issues (manufactured homes may be titled as personal property rather than real property, complicating mortgage financing for residents), easements, and sometimes disputed lot boundaries in older communities.

**Regulatory Closure Risk:** Municipal redevelopment or infrastructure expansion (roads, utilities) can lead to community closure orders. Tenant relocation assistance requirements in many states significantly increase the cost of community closure to operators.

## Regulatory Considerations

Manufactured housing is governed by the HUD Manufactured Home Construction and Safety Standards (Title 24, Part 3280), which regulate the factory-built homes themselves, and by local zoning for the communities. The Manufactured Housing Improvement Act of 2000 established dispute resolution and installation standards. The Federal Housing Administration (FHA) Title I and Title II programs provide financing for manufactured home purchases; Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac have manufactured housing mortgage programs (MH Advantage, CHOICEHome) that affect the pool of qualified residents.

Tenant protections vary widely by state: California, Oregon, and several other states have enacted "just cause" eviction requirements and right-of-first-refusal for MHC sales. Many states require advance notice periods (60–180 days) before rent increases above a threshold and before community closure. ADA Title II applies to common areas; Fair Housing Act applies to all rental activities. Mobile home park acts in most states establish minimum lease terms, required disclosures, and rent dispute processes.

## Notable Markets

**Florida (Tampa Bay, Orlando, Central Florida):** The largest US MHC market by pad count; combination of affordable retiree demand, working-class workforce housing, and large RV/seasonal communities.

**Arizona (Phoenix metro, Tucson, Sun City):** Major retirement and workforce housing market; significant age-restricted community concentration.

**California:** Highest rent communities in the US (in many markets lot rents exceed apartment rents in comparable markets); also the state with the most restrictive tenant protections. Strong demand from housing-cost-driven households.

**Texas, North Carolina, South Carolina, Georgia:** Growing Sun Belt markets with active development and value-add acquisition activity.

**Midwest (Michigan, Ohio, Indiana):** High pad-count states with older vintage communities and active value-add investment.
