# Marina

## Overview

Marinas are specialized real property assets providing boat owners and recreational boaters with water-access facilities including boat slips (wet berths where vessels float at dock), dry stack storage (rack storage buildings where boats are retrieved by forklift), fuel docks, boat ramps, and supporting retail and service operations. As a CRE investment, marinas combine real property value with an operating business: the real asset (waterfront land, docks, infrastructure) is inseparable from the operating platform (slip rentals, fuel sales, marine services, boat sales, restaurants). This hybrid nature makes marinas more analogous to hospitality than conventional real estate — operating skill drives a meaningful portion of returns.

Marinas represent one of the most supply-constrained real estate niches in the US. New marina development requires securing permits from the Army Corps of Engineers, state coastal commissions, the EPA, and local authorities with jurisdictional overlap on water rights and submerged lands — a process that can take 7–15 years or result in outright denial due to environmental opposition. Existing marinas in high-demand coastal markets benefit from essentially permanent competitive moats: no new competitor can be built without a decade of regulatory effort, and the existing asset controls the only available water access in its trade area.

Institutional interest in marinas has grown substantially since 2018. Specialists including Safe Harbor Marinas (acquired by Sun Communities), Suntex Marinas, and IGY Marinas have assembled large national portfolios via acquisition, while private equity platforms seek individual marinas and small portfolios as defensive, supply-constrained income assets with recreational demand tailwinds.

## Subtypes

**Wet Slip Marina:** Traditional marina configuration where boats remain in the water in assigned slips at fixed or floating docks. Largest category by market value and institutional investment interest. Revenue primarily from slip rental (annual or monthly), fuel, and ancillary services. Waterfront real estate and slip count are the primary value drivers.

**Dry Stack / Dry Storage Marina:** Rack-storage buildings (similar to multi-story self-storage) housing boats out of the water; boats are retrieved by forklift and launched on demand. Lower waterfront footprint per boat; can accommodate more boats per acre of water access. Operating-intensive (dedicated forklift operators and scheduling systems required). Popular in Southeast and Gulf Coast markets for smaller powerboats.

**Mixed-Use Marina (Marina + Retail / Hotel):** Marinas combined with restaurant, retail, hotel, or residential components to maximize the value of the waterfront location. Common in urban waterfront redevelopment contexts. Requires sophisticated mixed-use development and management capability.

**Yacht Club / Membership Marina:** Facilities operated under membership model with annual dues, reciprocal privileges with other clubs, and social programming. Revenue is membership-based rather than transaction-based; occupancy is effectively structural. Typically operated as nonprofit or cooperative entities; occasional for-profit conversion opportunities arise.

**Working / Commercial Marina:** Facilities serving commercial fishing, charter boats, water taxis, or maritime industry users rather than recreational boaters. Revenue is more predictable than recreational marinas but is tied to commercial fishing quotas, tourism spending, and maritime industry health.

## Demand Drivers

Recreational boating participation is the foundational demand driver. The US has approximately 100 million boating participants and 17–18 million registered recreational vessels; boating participation tends to correlate with disposable income, warm climate, and water access availability. Affluent, coastal, and lakeside markets generate the strongest slip demand. Baby boomer retirements have been a tailwind for recreational boating as retirees activate long-deferred purchases; a younger Gen X/millennial cohort is entering prime boating years.

Geographic constraints are the structural driver of pricing power: the number of boats seeking slips in any given coastal or lakeside market is limited only by boat ownership, but the number of slips is fixed by the existing stock of marinas and no new supply can realistically be added in most markets. In popular markets (South Florida, Chesapeake Bay, Pacific Northwest, Lake Tahoe), waitlists for slips can run 5–10 years, supporting both high rents and premium pricing for any available slip inventory. Dry stack demand is driven by convenience (no boat maintenance in water) and the availability of waterfront land for more fuel-efficient rack storage.

## Supply Dynamics

Marina supply is among the most permanently constrained in all of CRE. New wet slip construction requires federal (Army Corps of Engineers Section 10/404 permits), state coastal permits, and local approvals, with environmental review addressing impacts on water quality, marine habitat, and navigation. In California, the Coastal Commission has not approved a new marina in decades in most jurisdictions. Florida, the Carolinas, and Gulf Coast states are somewhat more permissive but still require multi-year permitting timelines and are subject to frequent litigation.

Existing marina infrastructure — docks, pilings, bulkheads, fuel systems — ages over time and requires ongoing capital reinvestment. Significant redevelopment or expansion (increasing slip count, replacing floating docks) typically triggers full re-permitting, effectively capping existing marinas at their current configuration. This constraint means that the slip count of a market is essentially fixed at current levels, and demand growth translates directly into rate increases rather than new supply.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Revenue per Slip:** Annual gross revenue (slip rent + fuel + services) divided by total slip count. The normalized revenue metric for cross-asset comparison. `Revenue per Slip = Total Annual Revenue / Total Slips`.

$$\text{Revenue per Slip} = \frac{\text{Total Annual Revenue}}{\text{Total Slips}}$$

**Slip Occupancy Rate:** Occupied/leased slips as a percentage of total available slips. Trophy coastal marinas may run 95–100% with waitlists; seasonal inland marinas experience greater fluctuation.

**Fuel Revenue per Gallon / Fuel Margin:** Fuel sales are a high-volume, moderate-margin component of marina revenue. Margin per gallon varies by pump location, competition, and wholesale contract. Fuel revenue should be tracked separately from slip rent given its volatile cost base.

**Annual vs. Monthly/Transient Mix:** The breakdown of slip rentals between annual contracts (predictable, high retention) and monthly or transient rentals (higher rate per day but volatile occupancy). Annual slip holders generate stable base revenue; transient income captures revenue from cruising traffic and seasonal visitors at premium rates.

**Dry Stack Utilization:** For dry stack facilities, the percentage of rack spaces occupied. Dry stack operations have higher operating costs (forklift labor, maintenance) but can accommodate more vessels per waterfront foot than wet slips.

**Ancillary Revenue Ratio:** Non-slip revenue (fuel, boat repair, concessions, boat storage, launch fees, pump-out services) as a percentage of total revenue. Higher ancillary ratios indicate greater operating complexity but also diversification from slip-rent-only income. Value-add strategies often focus on adding or improving ancillary revenue streams.

## Common Deal Structures

Marina acquisitions are typically structured as [[Core Acquisition]] or [[Value-Add Acquisition]] of stabilized or below-market-rent assets. The scarcity of tradeable assets means off-market transactions are common; national operators (Safe Harbor, Suntex, IGY) often acquire through direct outreach to long-time private owners. [[Ground-Up Development]] is rare given permitting barriers.

Ownership structures range from fee-simple (where the operator owns the upland and underwater land) to leasehold marinas built on state-owned submerged lands under long-term leases from state agencies (common in Florida, California, and the Carolinas). Leasehold marinas require careful analysis of lease term, renewal options, and rent escalation provisions. Financing typically involves [[Senior Debt]] from regional banks and SBA 504 programs (for smaller acquisitions), with [[Private Credit]] and bridge lenders for larger transactions. Life company and CMBS are less common given the specialized nature and smaller average deal size.

JV structures are standard for institutional acquisitions, typically pairing operating expertise with institutional capital. Management contracts with national marina operators are used by passive investors; integrated operating platforms are preferred by sophisticated investors because operating income contribution (fuel, services, ancillary) is significant and requires day-to-day expertise.

## Major Risks

**Hurricane / Catastrophic Weather Risk:** Coastal marinas are exposed to severe storm damage. Floating docks, vessels, and fuel systems are highly vulnerable; property insurance (including wind/flood) is expensive and may become unavailable or prohibitively costly in high-risk coastal markets (South Florida, Gulf Coast). Comprehensive post-storm business interruption analysis is required.

**Environmental Liability:** Marina operations involve fuel storage, fuel spills, engine waste, antifouling paint, and wastewater from vessels — all sources of soil and water contamination. Phase I and Phase II environmental assessments are essential; undisclosed environmental liability can significantly exceed asset value. EPA and state environmental agencies impose cleanup obligations regardless of whether the current owner caused the contamination.

**Regulatory / Permitting Complexity:** The multi-agency permitting environment makes any significant capital improvement or slip expansion highly uncertain. Changes in federal or state coastal regulations (including sea-level rise adaptation requirements) may impose capital costs or restrict uses. Army Corps and state agency renewal of permits for existing docking structures may be denied or conditioned on expensive environmental mitigation.

**Seasonality:** Marinas in northern markets experience dramatic seasonal revenue swings; winter months may generate minimal revenue while spring/summer peak season drives the majority of annual cash flow. Budgeting must account for seasonal working capital requirements. Insurance and maintenance obligations are year-round.

**Slip Size Obsolescence:** The trend toward larger boats over time (median powerboat length has increased over decades) means older marinas configured for smaller slips may face functional obsolescence as their target vessel fleet grows too large. Reconfiguration requires dredging and permitting.

## Regulatory Considerations

Marina development and operation is governed by an unusually dense regulatory framework: the Army Corps of Engineers (Section 404/Section 10 of the Rivers and Harbors Act) controls any structure in or over navigable waters; state coastal commissions or equivalent agencies regulate in coastal zones; the EPA governs fuel storage (underground storage tank regulations, spill prevention, containment and countermeasure plans), stormwater discharges, and vessel sewage pump-out requirements. The Clean Vessel Act provides federal funding for pump-out stations; the Clean Water Act and state equivalents impose discharge limits. Submerged land leases (for marinas built on state-owned underwater land) are negotiated with state agencies and include use restrictions. Local zoning governs upland uses, signage, parking, and hours of operation.

ADA requirements apply to marina facilities, including accessible pathways to docks, accessible restrooms, and — in some interpretations — accessible slip accommodations. Marinas with more than a certain volume of fuel dispensing are subject to EPA SPCC (Spill Prevention, Control, and Countermeasure) plan requirements.

## Notable Markets

**South Florida (Fort Lauderdale, Miami, Palm Beach):** The largest US luxury marina market; Fort Lauderdale is the "yachting capital of the world" with enormous megayacht infrastructure. Strong international demand; high insurance costs.

**Chesapeake Bay (Annapolis, MD / Northern Neck, VA):** The most active recreational boating market on the East Coast; dense marina supply serving the DC/Baltimore/Richmond boating public.

**Pacific Northwest (Puget Sound / Seattle):** Strong year-round boating culture; premium slip rents; significant kayak and powerboat demand.

**Great Lakes (Chicago, Milwaukee, Cleveland, Toronto):** Large freshwater boating market; shorter season but substantial slip demand in major metros.

**Gulf Coast (Naples, Tampa Bay, Mobile Bay):** Active powerboat and fishing markets with strong seasonality; hurricane risk is material.

**California Coastal (Marina del Rey, Dana Point, Sausalito):** High-value, extremely supply-constrained markets with multi-year waitlists.
