# Education Facilities

## Overview

Education facilities encompass a broad spectrum of properties used for learning and childcare: charter school campuses, early childhood education (ECE) and daycare centers, private K-12 schools, higher education academic buildings (non-university-owned), and vocational or trade school facilities. As a CRE asset class, education facilities are primarily analyzed through a net-lease framework — where an educational operator (a charter school management organization, a national ECE chain, or a private school) leases the physical facility from an investor-owner — combined with mission-capital and public-finance structures for government-adjacent educational uses.

The investment thesis for education facilities rests on several pillars: long-term net leases (often 10–25 years) providing stable, bond-like income streams; captive, non-discretionary demand (enrollment-driven occupancy); and strong community acceptance and political durability for educational uses. The sector has attracted specialized REITs and private equity platforms (Citywide Investors, Equus Capital, Purpose Built Schools Atlanta, Raza Development Fund, and others) and a growing cohort of impact and mission-driven capital that values the combination of financial return and social purpose.

Charter schools are the largest single segment of investor-owned educational real estate. The charter school movement has generated significant demand for private real estate capital because charter schools, despite receiving public per-pupil funding, frequently cannot access traditional tax-exempt municipal bond financing and must rely on private real estate capital for their facilities. This structural gap between charter school demand for space and the absence of traditional public financing creates a real estate investment opportunity.

## Subtypes

**Charter School (Net-Leased / Investor-Owned):** The dominant institutional product. A Charter Management Organization (CMO) leases a school building from an investor under a long-term NNN lease. The CMO operates the school and receives per-pupil public funding; the investor collects rent. Credit analysis focuses on CMO track record, enrollment stability, charter renewal probability, and state/local political environment for charter education. Specialized lenders and impact investors are most active in this space.

**Early Childhood Education (ECE) / Daycare Center:** National operators (KinderCare, Learning Care Group, Primrose Schools, Bright Horizons) lease purpose-built or converted commercial spaces for daycare and preschool programs. Revenue is driven by enrollment capacity, tuition rates, and government subsidy participation (Head Start, Child Care Development Fund). Net-lease structures with national operators are the primary institutional product; cap rates are competitive with other single-tenant net-lease retail given operator credit quality.

**K-12 Private School Campus:** Independent schools (parochial, college-preparatory, Montessori, Waldorf) owning or leasing campus facilities. Most institutional schools are owner-occupants; sale-leaseback opportunities arise when schools seek to monetize real estate for endowment investment. Investor-owned facilities are less common; the combination of nonprofit status and limited creditworthiness constrains conventional investment appetite.

**Higher Education Building (Non-University-Owned):** Academic, laboratory, or administrative buildings leased by universities or colleges from private developers. P3 (public-private partnership) structures are increasingly common for university housing, dining, and academic buildings. The university credit is typically strong, making these assets attractive to institutional investors.

**Vocational / Trade School:** Facilities serving workforce training programs (community colleges, for-profit trade schools, coding bootcamps, culinary institutes). Demand tied to workforce development policy, employer partnerships, and tuition reimbursement programs. For-profit trade school regulation has increased significantly since the 2015–2016 gainful employment rule cycle; credit analysis of for-profit operators requires evaluation of regulatory compliance.

## Demand Drivers

Public school enrollment trends are the foundational demand driver for charter schools — total enrollment in a district determines per-pupil funding available to charter operators, and population growth in family-formation age cohorts in suburban and Sun Belt markets drives enrollment growth. School choice policies and charter authorization environments vary dramatically by state; states with broad charter laws (Arizona, Florida, Texas, Indiana, Ohio) have the most active markets, while states with restrictive charter laws limit the investment opportunity.

For ECE facilities, demand is driven by working-parent labor force participation rates, childcare affordability (government subsidy availability and tuition costs relative to income), and birth rates in the surrounding area. The federal childcare shortage is widely documented; demand exceeds supply in most markets. For private K-12, demographic growth in affluent suburban markets, school quality dissatisfaction in public systems, and faith community growth are key drivers.

## Supply Dynamics

Education facility supply is primarily driven by demand from existing operators seeking to expand, replace inadequate facilities, or enter new markets. New charter school supply is constrained by authorization limits — charter authorizers (typically state boards of education or designated authorizers) limit the number of charters that can be issued in a given year and geography. ECE facility supply is constrained by zoning (many jurisdictions require CUPs for childcare centers in commercial zones) and staffing availability (high licensing requirements for ECE teachers limit operator expansion speed).

Purpose-built education facilities are specialized assets; conversion of commercial or industrial space to school use requires significant capital investment in code compliance (egress, restrooms, classroom configuration, playground) and zoning approval. Adaptive reuse of surplus office or retail space for charter schools has increased as urban charter networks seek space in supply-constrained markets. Development timelines for purpose-built schools run 18–30 months from land acquisition to opening.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Enrollment as % of Capacity:** For charter schools and ECE operators, the percentage of enrollment relative to licensed capacity. Consistently below 80% suggests demand risk; near-capacity enrollment indicates strong community demand and operator execution.

**Per-Pupil Revenue:** For charter schools, state per-pupil funding is the primary revenue stream. `Annual Revenue ≈ Total Enrollment × Per-Pupil Allocation (state-funded)`. The per-pupil amount is set by state legislation and varies widely ($5,000–$20,000+ per pupil depending on state and special education mix).

$$\text{Annual Revenue} \approx \text{Enrollment} \times \text{Per-Pupil Allocation}$$

**EBITDAR Rent Coverage:** Operator EBITDAR (earnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, amortization, and rent) divided by annual rent obligation. The primary credit metric for net-leased education. `Coverage = EBITDAR / Annual Rent`. Coverage below 1.1x indicates high stress; above 1.4x is considered comfortable.

$$\text{Rent Coverage} = \frac{\text{EBITDAR}}{\text{Annual Rent}}$$

**Charter Renewal Probability:** Charter schools operate under 3–5 year charters issued by authorizing bodies. Non-renewal or revocation terminates the school's right to operate and, in most leases, triggers a termination or default event. Renewal track record of the CMO and the quality of its relationship with the authorizer are key qualitative metrics.

**Waitlist Length:** Strong charter schools develop enrollment waitlists that provide forward visibility into demand and indicate the brand's appeal in the community. A multi-year waitlist is the strongest enrollment stability indicator.

**Facility Condition Score / Remaining Useful Life:** Purpose-built schools have specific code requirements (egress, HVAC for classroom air quality, restroom ratios, accessibility). The condition and remaining useful life of key building systems directly affects re-leasing capability and capital expenditure risk.

## Common Deal Structures

Charter school investments are typically structured as [[Core Acquisition]] or [[Build-to-Suit]] of NNN-leased facilities, with the CMO as tenant. [[Adaptive Reuse]] of commercial, industrial, or surplus religious properties for charter school use is active. [[Sale-Leaseback]] transactions from CMOs or private schools that have owned their facility and wish to monetize real estate for reinvestment in programming are a primary acquisition source. New Markets Tax Credits (NMTC) are frequently used for charter school financing in qualifying census tracts, enabling below-market debt financing. ECE investments closely resemble STNL retail acquisitions; [[Core Acquisition]] of stabilized, long-leased national operator facilities is the dominant institutional strategy.

Financing structures include [[Senior Debt]] from community development financial institutions (CDFIs), impact-focused banks, and specialized lenders; NMTC structures combining conventional debt with NMTC equity; and [[Private Credit]] for acquisitions of smaller or less credit-qualified operators. Charter schools in states that allow charter school tax-exempt bond financing (using conduit issuers) may access lower-cost public-market debt.

## Major Risks

**Charter Non-Renewal / Revocation:** The existential risk for charter school investments is non-renewal or revocation of the charter. Non-renewal typically gives 1–2 years of notice, providing time to find a replacement tenant; revocation can be abrupt. Re-leasing a purpose-built school to a non-educational user typically requires significant capital investment and zoning change.

**State Policy Risk:** Charter school expansion, funding, and per-pupil allocation are subject to state legislative and gubernatorial action. Hostile political environments can freeze new charter authorizations, reduce per-pupil funding, or impose unfavorable facility requirements that affect operator viability and therefore landlord NOI.

**Enrollment Volatility:** Population shifts (suburban out-migration, demographic change, competitive alternative schools) can reduce enrollment below financially viable levels. Charter schools are more enrollment-volatile than conventional public schools because families can disenroll mid-year and the school's revenue falls immediately.

**For-Profit Operator Regulatory Risk:** For-profit ECE and vocational school operators are subject to heightened regulatory scrutiny and public/political criticism. Regulatory action (license revocation, program elimination, gainful employment rule enforcement for trade schools) can rapidly impair the operator's ability to continue as a tenant.

**Building Re-Use Limitations:** Purpose-built school buildings are difficult to re-lease for non-educational uses due to their distinctive layout (many small classrooms, dedicated hallways, restroom ratios, playground requirements). Adaptive reuse to office or residential is possible but capital-intensive, limiting re-leasing optionality compared to conventional commercial real estate.

## Regulatory Considerations

Charter schools are subject to state charter school authorization statutes, which vary enormously in their permissiveness, oversight requirements, and appeals processes. The Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA) and Individuals with Disabilities Education Act (IDEA) impose federal compliance requirements on all schools receiving public funding, including special education obligations that affect facility layout and staffing costs. ADA Title II applies to charter schools receiving federal funding (requiring accessible facilities and programs); ADA Title III applies to private schools open to the public. State and local building codes impose school-specific occupancy requirements: IBC Group E occupancy for K-12 (specific egress, classroom size, restroom ratio, fire suppression requirements), playground safety standards (ASTM F1292, CPSC guidelines), and state Department of Education facility standards.

ECE operators are licensed by state departments of social services or children's family services agencies, with facility inspection requirements (space per child, outdoor play area, kitchen/food service standards, emergency egress). Child:staff ratios are fixed by state regulation and directly affect operating costs and facility layout requirements. USDA Child and Adult Care Food Program (CACFP) participation affects kitchen requirements. For-profit vocational schools must comply with Department of Education gainful employment regulations if they participate in federal student aid programs.

## Notable Markets

**Arizona:** The most permissive US charter authorization environment; Phoenix metro has the highest density of charter school real estate investment activity in the country. Strong ECE demand driven by population growth.

**Florida (Miami, Orlando, Tampa, Jacksonville):** Large charter sector; strong ECE demand from working-family demographics and immigrant communities; active NMTC deployment.

**Texas (Dallas, Houston, San Antonio):** Large state charter sector; significant urban population growth driving ECE and charter demand.

**Ohio, Indiana, Michigan:** Midwestern states with permissive charter laws and active nonprofit and for-profit CMOs; lower land costs than coastal markets.

**California:** Large charter sector despite increasing state and district resistance; high land costs and tight entitlement environment for new ECE facilities; significant NMTC activity in underserved communities.

**Washington DC:** Charter schools serve approximately 50% of DC public school enrollment; the most mature and densely invested urban charter real estate market in the country.
