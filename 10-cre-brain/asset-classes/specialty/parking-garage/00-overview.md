# Parking Garage

## Overview

Structured parking garages are commercial real estate assets providing off-street vehicle storage in multi-level structures, generating revenue from a mix of transient (hourly, daily) parking users and contract (monthly) parkers under longer-term arrangements. As an institutional asset class, parking garages sit at an unusual intersection: they generate stable, predictable cash flows in the near term, but face a structural demand risk horizon tied to the potential widespread adoption of autonomous vehicles and ongoing shifts in urban commuting patterns accelerated by remote and hybrid work. This long-term obsolescence risk has differentiated parking garage investing into two broad strategies: income-focused buyers who underwrite current cash flows conservatively, and land-banking investors who value the parking garage primarily as a monetizable land asset beneath the structure, using parking income as carry while awaiting the right redevelopment window.

Parking garages tied to captive demand — hospital campuses, airports, large event venues — exhibit the most resilient demand and command institutional interest. Urban CBD parking tied to office commuter demand has declined structurally since 2020, as hybrid work reduced 5-day commuter parking volumes. Event-driven and destination-driven demand (stadiums, arenas, entertainment districts) has been more resilient. Parking management expertise is critical: parking is an operationally intensive asset, and the choice between self-management, third-party management (IMPARK, LAZ Parking, Propark), and management/lease contracts significantly affects NOI.

## Subtypes

**Urban CBD Parking Garage:** Multi-level structures in downtown cores serving commuters, office workers, residents, and visitors. Revenue mix of monthly contract and daily/hourly transient. Demand most affected by office commuting patterns; WFH/hybrid has structurally reduced 5-day commuter volumes in many CBDs. Land value often exceeds parking income value in prime locations, driving adaptive reuse consideration.

**Hospital / Medical Campus Parking:** Garages serving hospital campuses with captive demand from patients, visitors, and employees. Among the most resilient parking demand in the sector — healthcare is non-discretionary, in-person, and generates consistent daily user traffic across all days of the week, insulated from WFH trends. Typically operated under management agreements with the hospital or health system.

**Airport Parking:** Garages or surface lots serving airports and generating revenue from short-term (hourly/daily) and long-term (extended stay) travelers. Demand tied directly to airline travel volumes; highly cyclical around economic and pandemic shocks. Can be operated by airport authorities, concessionaires, or private operators under concession agreements.

**Suburban Mixed-Use Parking:** Garages serving retail, residential, or mixed-use developments where parking is a required component of the development program. May be operated under shared parking agreements with multiple users (daytime office, evening retail, residential). Revenue can be structured as part of a mixed-use financial model rather than as a standalone parking income stream.

**Surface Parking Lot (Value-Hold / Land Banking):** Single-level surface parking on high-value urban land. Parking income covers carrying costs while the owner waits for zoning, market conditions, or capital to align for redevelopment. Primarily a [[Land Banking]] or [[Entitlement Play]] strategy; parking is incidental to the land investment thesis.

## Demand Drivers

Vehicle ownership and commuting patterns are the foundational drivers for transient parking demand. Urban density, transit accessibility, and employer parking policies determine what share of workers drive to their workplace and therefore require parking. The shift to hybrid and remote work has permanently reduced 5-day commute parking volumes in most CBDs, but event, retail, restaurant, and medical demand have partially offset this. Population growth, tourism, and event venue activity in specific markets can drive parking demand growth even as commuter parking declines.

For hospital parking specifically, patient and visitor volume and the health system's growth plans are the primary drivers. Airport parking demand is a direct function of passenger enplanements. Monthly contract parking is driven by employment density and the relative cost of parking versus transit or rideshare alternatives.

Technology shifts — ride-hailing (Uber/Lyft) adoption has reduced short-term airport and event parking; autonomous vehicle scenarios (15–25 year horizon) threaten to dramatically reduce parking demand in urban areas as self-driving vehicles could drop off passengers and park in lower-cost peripheral locations or return home. EV adoption creates demand for charging infrastructure upgrades within garages.

## Supply Dynamics

New parking garage construction is typically ancillary to other development (a garage built as part of a mixed-use project) rather than standalone investment-driven supply. Standalone structured parking development rarely pencils as a primary use in contemporary markets given construction cost, declining long-term demand outlook, and the optionality value of the land for higher-and-better uses. Municipal minimum parking requirements — which historically mandated parking ratios — are being reduced or eliminated in many cities, reducing compelled supply. Surface parking lots remain abundant in many CBDs and suburban markets, maintaining competition for structured garages.

## Key Operating Metrics

**Revenue per Stall (Monthly / Annual):** Total gross revenue divided by total stall count, expressed monthly or annually. The primary normalization metric for cross-asset comparison. Separating transient revenue per stall from contract revenue per stall is important because the two segments have different cost structures and demand profiles.

$$\text{Revenue per Stall (Annual)} = \frac{\text{Total Annual Revenue}}{\text{Total Stalls}}$$

**Transient vs. Contract Mix:** The percentage of gross revenue from transient (hourly/daily) parking vs. monthly contract parking. Higher transient exposure generates higher revenue per stall but is more volatile; high contract exposure provides stable, predictable revenue at lower per-stall rates. The optimal mix depends on local market conditions and demand drivers.

**Occupancy Rate by Segment:** Monthly contract occupancy (contracted stalls / available contract stalls) and peak/average transient utilization rate should be tracked separately, as they reflect different demand dynamics.

**Revenue per Transaction (Transient):** Average revenue per transient parking event. Benchmarks against local market rates and indicates pricing power relative to competition.

**Total Revenue per Square Foot:** Annual gross revenue divided by total gross square footage of the garage. Enables comparison with other real estate uses (retail, office) for highest-and-best-use analysis and adaptive reuse decision-making.

**Operating Cost per Stall:** Total annual operating expenses (labor, utilities, insurance, maintenance) divided by stall count. Key input for NOI modeling and for evaluating management contract alternatives.

**NOI per Stall:** Net operating income divided by stall count. The fundamental value metric for parking assets, analogous to NOI per unit in multifamily.

$$\text{NOI per Stall} = \frac{\text{NOI}}{\text{Total Stalls}}$$

## Common Deal Structures

Parking garages are acquired via [[Core Acquisition]] for stabilized, captive-demand assets (hospital, airport), or [[Value-Add Acquisition]] for undermanaged or transitional urban parking. [[Adaptive Reuse]] is an active and growing strategy, converting obsolete parking garages to office, residential, or mixed-use — though structural spans, floor-to-floor heights, and ramp configurations often limit conversion feasibility. [[Land Banking]] via surface lot acquisition is a distinct strategy where the parking investment thesis is secondary to the land value.

Management structures range from self-management (rare for institutional owners) to third-party management contracts (paying an operator a flat fee plus incentive) to gross leases to parking operators (operator pays fixed rent and retains upside; landlord receives stable income with no operating risk). Management contracts are most common for institutional owners; lease structures are common when the owner lacks parking operational expertise. Financing involves [[Senior Debt]] from banks and [[CMBS]], often at conservative LTV given obsolescence concerns; hospital-affiliated garages may access lower-cost financing through health system relationships.

## Major Risks

**Structural Demand Decline (AV/Remote Work):** The most significant long-term risk is a structural decline in parking demand driven by autonomous vehicles (which could dramatically reduce private vehicle ownership and parking need) and persistent hybrid/remote work (which has already reduced commuter parking volumes). While the AV timeline is uncertain, lenders and investors increasingly discount terminal value for parking garages, particularly CBD commuter-focused assets.

**Competition from TNCs (Uber/Lyft/rideshare):** Transportation network companies have reduced short-term transient parking demand at airports, entertainment venues, and urban destinations. This trend accelerates as ride-hailing becomes more affordable and accessible.

**Operational Risk:** Parking is operationally intensive; poor management leads directly to revenue leakage (unpaid transactions, equipment failure, poor customer service driving demand to competitors). Management contract selection and oversight are key value drivers.

**Municipal Policy Changes:** Cities actively reducing parking minimums, implementing congestion pricing, or converting parking lanes to transit/bike infrastructure indirectly affect parking demand. Some cities are also pursuing right-of-first-refusal or affordability requirements on parking facilities.

**Structural Deterioration:** Parking garage concrete and post-tensioned structures are susceptible to corrosion (chloride attack from road salt and moisture), requiring significant periodic capital investment (sealants, deck coatings, joint replacement, post-tension cable repairs) to maintain structural integrity. Deferred maintenance can accelerate structural deterioration and liability exposure.

## Regulatory Considerations

Building codes govern parking garage structural design (IBC Chapter 4 group S-2 occupancies, specific span and ventilation requirements for open vs. enclosed garages). ADA Title III requires accessible stalls, paths of travel, signage, and ticketing equipment. Local zoning governs permitted uses, parking stall minimums or maximums, height restrictions, and façade requirements (many cities now require active ground-floor uses on parking garage street fronts). Fire codes (NFPA 88A) govern ventilation, fire suppression, and egress. EV charging requirements are increasingly being added to building codes for new and renovated garages.

Revenue operations (pay stations, payment processing) are subject to PCI DSS if credit card payments are accepted. In some states, parking is subject to state and local tax (parking tax), which must be collected from customers and remitted. Environmental considerations include stormwater management (impervious surface runoff) and fuel/oil spill mitigation for garages handling commercial or RV vehicles.

## Notable Markets

**Chicago CBD:** Among the largest urban parking markets in the US, with significant institutional ownership of downtown structures. Office demand reduction has pressured occupancy; event and residential parking demand partially offsets.

**New York City:** Extremely high-value urban parking (some midtown stalls reportedly leased at $1,000+/month); extremely limited supply due to density and transit alternatives. Mostly institutional-scale assets.

**Boston, Washington DC, San Francisco:** Dense urban markets with captive demand from office, medical, and government campuses; high monthly contract rates; constrained new supply.

**Major Airport Markets (LAX, O'Hare, DFW, Hartsfield-Jackson):** Airport parking is a high-volume, institutionally managed product with direct tie to airline capacity.

**Hospital-adjacent markets everywhere:** Medical campus parking is consistently the most resilient submarket and attracts the most conservative institutional capital.
