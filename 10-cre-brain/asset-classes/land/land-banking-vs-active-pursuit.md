# Land Banking vs. Active Pursuit

Land can be held in two fundamentally different modes: passively as a long-duration store of value (land banking), or actively as a platform for value creation through entitlement and infrastructure (active pursuit). These modes have different capital requirements, management intensities, return profiles, and exit timelines. Most sophisticated land programs combine elements of both — holding some positions patiently while actively pursuing approvals on others — but understanding the strategic distinction is essential for portfolio construction and investor communication.

## Land Banking

### Strategy and Thesis

[[Land Banking]] is the acquisition of land in the path of growth — at or beyond the current urban fringe, or in transitional corridors within existing urban areas — and holding it patiently until rising demand and market appreciation push the land value to a target level. The land banker is not primarily in the business of entitling land; they are in the business of correctly identifying where growth will occur and acquiring basis advantage before the market fully prices that growth in.

The thesis depends on three conditions being met over the holding period: (1) the growth vector anticipated at acquisition actually materializes (population growth, employment growth, infrastructure investment reach the area); (2) the holding period required is within the investor's capital duration constraints; and (3) carry costs do not erode returns to unacceptable levels. See [[Land — Key Metrics]] for carry cost analysis.

Land banking is most associated with large-scale greenfield acquisitions in high-growth Sunbelt and Mountain West metros — Phoenix, Las Vegas, Dallas, Houston, and their suburban peripheries — where land prices have historically appreciated in advance of development activity as growth outpaces the planning and entitlement apparatus. Agricultural and transitional land acquisitions at the urban fringe are classic land banking opportunities.

### Capital and Management Requirements

Land banking requires patient, long-duration capital. Hold periods of 5–15+ years are common. The management burden during the hold is relatively low — primarily property tax monitoring, lease management if agricultural or other income uses are in place, boundary and title maintenance, and monitoring for regulatory changes that might affect value. There is no active entitlement work and typically no infrastructure expenditure.

Carry costs must be actively managed. Leasing the land for agricultural use, grazing, billboard placement, cell tower ground leases, or solar lease-to-own agreements can offset a portion of property taxes and help reduce negative carry. However, leases that are difficult to terminate may impede the development process when the time to entitle arrives — lease terms must be structured with adequate termination provisions.

### Return Profile

Land banking returns are realized through appreciation in land value over time, not through current income or development spread. Total return is driven by basis (acquisition price relative to intrinsic value), appreciation velocity, and carry cost efficiency. IRR metrics for pure land banking strategies are often lower than active entitlement strategies when holding periods are long, but can be impressive on an equity multiple basis if basis was acquired well.

The risk is primarily timing: if the anticipated growth vector shifts, delays, or reverses, the land may not appreciate on schedule, and the carry clock continues to run. Unlike active entitlement strategies, land banking does not generate milestone-based value inflection points — appreciation is gradual (and sometimes stagnant for extended periods) rather than punctuated by approval events.

## Active Pursuit

### Strategy and Thesis

The [[Entitlement Play]] is an active value-creation strategy: acquire land (raw or partially improved), invest management effort and capital to obtain discretionary approvals and install infrastructure, and sell or develop the improved asset at a value that reflects the work product. The active pursuer is in the business of converting raw land into buildable product — the highest-value transformation in the land lifecycle.

Value creation steps in an active pursuit include: pre-application meetings with planning staff; community outreach and opposition management; submission and processing of zone change, GPA, or conditional use permit applications; environmental review coordination; tentative and final map processing; infrastructure design and installation; and negotiation of development agreements or public facility financing structures. Each of these steps reduces risk, increases the buyer pool, and justifies a higher land price.

### Capital and Management Requirements

Active pursuit is capital- and management-intensive. Entitlement professional fees (land use attorneys, engineers, environmental consultants, traffic engineers, biologists, community outreach consultants) can range from modest (a simple zone change on a small infill parcel) to several million dollars for a large master-planned community EIR. These costs must be budgeted accurately and included in the [[Residual Land Value Method]] development cost stack.

Management bandwidth is substantial: active pursuit requires dedicated project management — tracking agency timelines, coordinating consultants, managing community relationships, attending hearings, and responding to conditions of approval. For this reason, active pursuit is primarily the domain of experienced developers and entitlement-specialist operators who can deploy this expertise at scale across multiple projects. Capital partners who provide equity funding typically do so through [[Joint Venture Structures]] that compensate the operating partner through a promote on successful entitlement — see [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]].

### Return Profile

Active pursuit generates returns through the entitlement premium — the increment of value created by successful approval. A parcel worth $X as raw land may be worth 1.5X–3X or more as an entitled, shovel-ready site. The [[Equity Multiple]] and [[Internal Rate of Return (IRR)]] are driven by the size of this premium relative to cost and the speed of execution.

Hold periods are shorter than land banking — typically 2–5 years from acquisition through entitlement and sale — but the active management burden is high and the binary risk of entitlement failure must be priced in. Successful active pursuit programs underwrite conservatively, run multiple projects simultaneously to diversify binary risk, and structure option agreements on raw land acquisitions wherever possible to limit downside exposure.

## Choosing the Approach

| Dimension | Land Banking | Active Pursuit |
|-----------|-------------|----------------|
| Strategy | Appreciation in path of growth | Entitlement value creation |
| Hold period | 5–15+ years | 2–5 years |
| Management intensity | Low | High |
| Capital intensity | Moderate (acquisition + carry) | High (acquisition + entitlement costs) |
| Binary risk | Market/timing | Entitlement outcome |
| Required IRR | 15%–22% | 18%–30%+ |
| Exit timing control | Low | Moderate |
| Execution expertise required | Site selection, market analysis | Entitlement, community relations, project management |

In practice, the most effective land programs employ a portfolio approach: banking some land for long-term appreciation while actively pursuing entitlements on sites where the approval path is clearest and the value creation is largest. [[Demographic Analysis]], [[Absorption Rate]] tracking, and [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] all inform the decision of which parcels to actively pursue versus which to hold. The [[Development Feasibility Test]] provides a formal screen for identifying when a parcel is ready for active development.
