# Raw Land vs. Improved Land

The distinction between raw (unentitled) land and improved (entitled or infrastructure-ready) land is the most fundamental categorization in land investing. It defines risk level, required return, buyer universe, financing availability, and underwriting method. Understanding precisely where a parcel sits on this spectrum is the first step in any land analysis.

## Definitions

**Raw land** refers to parcels without meaningful entitlement progress — no zone change, no general plan amendment, no environmental clearances beyond what is inherent in the existing zoning designation, and typically no infrastructure installed to or within the site. Raw land may have some preliminary planning or conceptual studies, but no formal approvals from any governmental body. The development potential is a thesis, not a confirmed right.

**Improved land** — also called entitled, approved, or paper-ready land — has obtained at least one or more significant discretionary approvals that confirm a specific development program is permissible. This may range from a simple zone change (which permits a use but does not approve a specific project) to a fully approved tentative subdivision map, recorded final map, and installed infrastructure (finished lots). The term is used loosely in the market; underwriters should specify exactly what approvals have been obtained and what remains outstanding.

## Risk Profile Comparison

Raw land carries a higher and more binary risk profile than improved land. The key risks are:

- **Entitlement binary risk:** raw land's development program is a projection; it may be denied, substantially modified, or delayed for reasons outside the investor's control. A single agency denial or successful legal challenge can write off the entitlement investment entirely.
- **Timeline uncertainty:** entitlement timelines for raw land are difficult to predict, especially for projects requiring environmental review. Base-case assumptions of 2–3 years frequently extend to 4–6 years on complex projects.
- **Carry accumulation:** every month of hold accretes property taxes, interest, and professional fees into the land basis with no offsetting income. Extended timelines can erode project returns substantially — see [[Land — Key Metrics]] for carry cost definitions.
- **Market timing exposure:** raw land holders are exposed to a full market cycle during the entitlement period. If the end-product market weakens, the residual land value declines before any liquidity event.

Improved (entitled) land has already converted the binary entitlement risk into a defined (if still uncertain) development cost and timeline. The residual risk shifts from "will this be approved?" to "can we execute development on time and budget?" This shift dramatically widens the buyer pool and reduces the required return.

## Pricing and Underwriting

Both categories are underwritten using the [[Residual Land Value Method]], but the assumptions differ:

For **raw land**, the full cost of entitlement — professional fees (land use attorneys, engineers, environmental consultants, biologists, traffic engineers), public outreach, agency fees, and the time value of capital during the process — must be added to the development cost stack before computing RLV. The discount rate applied to raw land is higher (typically 20–30%+ IRR hurdle), reflecting the binary risk and illiquidity. Comparable sales (raw-to-raw comps only) serve as a secondary check. See [[Sensitivity Analysis]] for the range of outcomes.

For **improved land**, entitlement costs have already been incurred and are embedded in the asking price. The underwriting focus shifts to remaining development costs, infrastructure completion, and absorption. Discount rates compress relative to raw land (typically 15–22% IRR target range), and the buyer pool expands to include homebuilders, institutional developers, and private equity funds that cannot underwrite binary entitlement risk. Comparable sales should be other entitled parcels with similar density, product type, and remaining risk.

The table below summarizes the key differences:

| Dimension | Raw / Unentitled Land | Improved / Entitled Land |
|-----------|----------------------|--------------------------|
| Entitlement status | None or early-stage | At least one major approval obtained |
| Primary risk | Binary entitlement failure | Execution, cost, market timing |
| Required IRR (typical) | 20%–30%+ | 12%–22% |
| Buyer universe | Narrow (developers, speculators) | Broader (builders, institutional developers) |
| Financing availability | Limited; 40%–55% LTV recourse | Better; 50%–65% LTV, some non-recourse |
| Comp pool | Raw land comps only | Entitled land comps, finished-lot comps |
| Hold period | 3–10+ years (entitlement) | 1–4 years (infrastructure + construction start) |
| Carry risk | High (long, uncertain hold) | Moderate (defined timeline) |

## Transaction Dynamics

Raw land transactions often involve option agreements rather than outright purchases, limiting the developer's capital at risk to an option fee while the entitlement process runs. Outright raw land purchases are made when a developer has high conviction in the entitlement outcome (established relationships with planning staff, precedent approvals in the area) or is pursuing a [[Land Banking]] strategy that accepts a long hold in exchange for basis advantage.

Improved land transactions are generally outright purchases or structured takedown agreements. Public homebuilders buying entitled residential land frequently negotiate staggered lot takedown schedules — committing to purchase lots in tranches over 12–36 months, matching their capital deployment to their construction pipeline. This structure benefits the seller (certainty of execution) and the buyer (capital efficiency). See [[Entitlement Play]] and [[Land Banking]] for deal-type framing.
