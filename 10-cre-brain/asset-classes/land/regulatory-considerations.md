# Land — Regulatory Considerations

For land, regulatory status is not merely a constraint — it is the primary value driver. The same acreage with different zoning and entitlement status can have a 3x to 10x difference in market value depending on what has been approved and what remains to be approved. Practitioners must assess the full regulatory stack — from general plan designation through subdivision mapping — before underwriting a land acquisition.

---

### Zoning and Entitlement Status

The current zoning designation establishes what uses are permitted by-right and what uses require discretionary approval. Zoning exists within a hierarchy: the general plan (or comprehensive plan) sets land use policy, which zoning ordinances implement, which individual project approvals confirm. A parcel may carry favorable general plan land use designation but unfavorable existing zoning, requiring a [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] to realize the development potential. Conversely, zoning may be appropriate but the general plan may require an amendment before the zone change can be granted — a [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] is typically the highest-risk and longest-duration entitlement step.

Discretionary approvals that commonly apply to land include: [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], [[Variance]], and [[Planned Unit Development (PUD)]]. Each involves public hearings, agency staff review, and elected/appointed body decision — all of which introduce political and timing risk. The [[Public Hearing Process]] note covers the mechanics; [[Appeals Process]] covers challenge pathways. [[Neighborhood Outreach]] is often a prerequisite for major entitlement approvals and can be a deciding factor in contested projects.

A parcel's entitlement status should be characterized precisely in any underwriting memo: is the use permitted by-right; is a CUP required; is a zone change required; has environmental review been initiated or completed? Each distinction has a material impact on risk-adjusted land value.

### Subdivision and Platting

For residential and mixed-use development on parcels that will be divided into multiple lots or units, subdivision approval is a distinct regulatory layer from zoning. The [[Subdivision Approval]] process under state subdivision map acts (California's Subdivision Map Act, Arizona's subdivision statutes, etc.) requires a tentative map review (by planning commission or equivalent) and a final map recordation (by the city/county engineer or equivalent). Until a final map is recorded, individual lots generally cannot be sold separately and construction permits for individual units typically cannot be issued.

Subdivision approval timelines vary widely by jurisdiction and project complexity — simple 10-lot residential subdivisions may take 6–18 months; master-planned community tentative maps covering thousands of acres may take 3–7 years. Conditions of approval attached to tentative maps frequently require specific infrastructure improvements, public facility dedications, fee payments, and environmental mitigation measures that must be completed or bonded before final map recordation. These conditions are part of the development cost basis and must be reflected in the pro forma.

### Environmental Review

Environmental review is often the most time-consuming and litigious regulatory step in major land entitlements. The primary frameworks are:

- **CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act):** Required for discretionary approvals in California. Can require a Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND) or a full Environmental Impact Report (EIR), which can take 18 months to 3+ years and is subject to litigation. See [[Environmental Review Overview]].
- **NEPA (National Environmental Policy Act):** Required when a federal agency is involved (federal permit, federal funding, federal land). Can result in an Environmental Assessment (EA) or Environmental Impact Statement (EIS).
- **State-level analogs:** Many states have their own environmental review requirements (SEPA in Washington, MEPA in Massachusetts, etc.) that overlay or supplement federal requirements.

Common environmental triggers include: wetlands and jurisdictional waters of the United States (Section 404 of the Clean Water Act — U.S. Army Corps of Engineers jurisdiction); listed threatened or endangered species habitat (Endangered Species Act — U.S. Fish & Wildlife Service or NMFS); cultural and archaeological resources; hazardous materials and contamination. Each trigger can require mitigation measures, habitat conservation planning, agency consultation, and permitting steps that add months to years and potentially significant mitigation costs.

Phase I and Phase II Environmental Site Assessments are essential pre-closing due diligence for any land acquisition. A recognized environmental condition (REC) found in Phase I that is not resolved or appropriately priced can create open-ended post-closing liability.

### Water Rights and Availability

Water availability is frequently the binding constraint on land development in the arid western United States and is increasingly a constraint in other regions experiencing drought or infrastructure limitations. In prior appropriation states (much of the West), water rights are property rights that must be obtained, transferred, or proven adequate for the proposed use before development approvals will be granted.

In Arizona, the Assured Water Supply program requires developers in Active Management Areas (including the Phoenix and Tucson metro areas) to demonstrate a 100-year assured water supply before plat approval. This effectively limits large residential development to areas served by utilities with proven long-term water portfolios. In Nevada, water rights must be acquired or assigned for new development in most areas. California's water situation varies by district and region.

In non-prior-appropriation states (East, Midwest), water availability constraints typically manifest as inadequate infrastructure capacity (water main extensions, treatment plant capacity) or new well-drilling limitations, rather than rights-based scarcity. Either way, the status of water service availability must be confirmed with the relevant utility or agency during due diligence before underwriting development potential.

### Access and Utility Easements

Legal access is a prerequisite for land development. A parcel without a recorded easement or dedicated right-of-way for vehicle and pedestrian access to a public road is effectively landlocked — it cannot be developed, financed, or sold to a developer without resolving the access issue. Access due diligence includes: reviewing the chain of title for recorded easements, confirming that access road(s) meet jurisdictional width and improvement standards, and verifying that private access easements are enforceable and benefiting the parcel.

Utility easements — for water mains, sewer lines, electric, gas, and telecommunications — must also be confirmed. The location of existing easements may constrain building placement, density, and grading even if the site otherwise has good infrastructure access. Extension of utilities from the public right-of-way to the site (or across neighboring parcels via new easements) may be required, adding cost and potentially requiring negotiations with adjacent landowners. All off-site infrastructure costs should be itemized in the development cost budget.

### Agricultural Designations and Preservation Programs

Land that carries an agricultural designation — whether through zoning, a general plan land use category, or a state/local preservation program — may face significant regulatory barriers to conversion.

**California's Williamson Act (Land Conservation Act of 1965)** allows landowners to contract with counties to restrict agricultural land from non-agricultural uses for renewable 10-year periods in exchange for reduced property tax assessments. Non-renewal (cancellation) of a Williamson Act contract requires a 9-year cancellation notice period and payment of a cancellation fee, making conversion to development uses a slow and costly process. Development-focused land acquisitions in California must include a Williamson Act status check.

**Oregon's farm and forest zones** and **Washington's agricultural protection zones** provide similar protections at the state level, often with additional local requirements. **Conservation easements** in many states can permanently restrict development potential through voluntary agreements with land trusts — these are permanent encumbrances that run with the land.

In Arizona, the State Trust Land system controls significant acreage in the Phoenix and Tucson metro peripheries. State trust land cannot be privately purchased without going through a public auction process administered by the Arizona State Land Department; development of state trust land requires state authorization and, for large parcels, master planning frameworks such as Draft and Final Environmental Impact Statements at the state level.

Any land acquisition in a state with active agricultural preservation, conservation easement activity, or state trust land proximity requires explicit confirmation that conversion is feasible and the timeline and cost to achieve it are accurately reflected in the underwriting.
