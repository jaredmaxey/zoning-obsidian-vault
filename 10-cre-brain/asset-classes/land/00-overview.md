# Land

## Overview

Land is the foundational asset class in commercial real estate — it is the raw input from which every other property type is created. Unlike income-producing assets, raw and unentitled land generates no current cash flow; its value derives entirely from what can be built on it, when, and at what cost and risk. Institutional investors, regional developers, and merchant builders each approach land differently: some hold speculatively through market cycles ([[Land Banking]]), others actively pursue entitlements to convert raw acreage into a permitted, shovel-ready product ([[Entitlement Play]]), and still others acquire finished lots to begin construction immediately. Understanding where a parcel sits on the entitlement spectrum is the starting point for every land underwriting exercise.

Because land has no in-place income, conventional cap rate or NOI-based valuation does not apply. The dominant underwriting framework is the [[Residual Land Value Method]]: supportable land price equals projected completed project value minus total development costs minus required developer profit. This residual approach forces the analyst to build a full [[Ground-Up Development]] pro forma before arriving at a land bid, making land underwriting among the most assumption-sensitive exercises in CRE. Small changes in exit pricing, construction costs, or absorption timing can swing residual land value by 20–50% or more. The [[Residual Land Value Feasibility]] note extends this into a go/no-go framework.

Sentiment toward land is highly cyclical. In expansion phases, rising end-product values inflate residual land values and attract speculative capital; in contractions, the same leverage works in reverse and illiquid land positions can become deeply impaired. Land lacks the rent floor that cushions income-producing assets, so mark-to-market declines can be severe. The carry cost of holding land — property taxes, debt service on acquisition loans, and opportunity cost — accretes against the land basis daily, creating urgency to either entitle and develop or sell. Investors and developers who understand the interplay of [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]], entitlement timing, and market absorption are best positioned to generate returns in this asset class.

## Subtypes

Land spans a wide spectrum from raw, unentitled acreage to finished, pad-ready lots. See [[Land — Subtypes]] for full profiles. Key categories include: raw/unentitled land (highest risk, highest upside), entitled/improved land (risk partially de-risked through approvals), finished lots (infrastructure in, ready for vertical construction), infill sites (constrained supply, strong locational value), greenfield tracts (lower land cost, longer entitlement and absorption), agricultural/transitional land (conversion play), and land held for development versus land held for immediate resale. See also [[Raw Land vs. Improved Land]] and [[Infill vs. Greenfield]] for comparative analyses, and [[Land Banking vs. Active Pursuit]] for strategic framing.

## Demand Drivers

Land demand is derived demand — it moves directionally with the end-product markets it feeds. Residential land demand tracks homebuilder confidence, single-family and multifamily starts, lot inventory levels, and affordability metrics. Commercial and industrial land demand follows tenant absorption, lease rates, and developer pipeline for the relevant product type. Key leading indicators include building permit activity, lot absorption velocity in active subdivisions, builder land acquisition pace, and migration and job-growth trends in the target submarket. [[Demographic Analysis]] and [[Employment and Jobs Analysis]] are especially important for residential land, while [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] and [[Absorption Rate]] data anchor commercial land underwriting.

Entitlement status is itself a form of demand signal: a parcel that obtains a discretionary approval (zone change, general plan amendment, environmental clearance) in a constrained supply environment immediately captures an entitlement premium reflecting the time and risk that approval process consumed. Markets with high regulatory barriers to entitlement — coastal California, metro Phoenix's water-constrained fringes, Pacific Northwest urban growth boundaries — tend to sustain wider residual land value ranges and more pronounced entitlement premiums than supply-permissive Sunbelt markets.

## Supply Dynamics

Land supply is geographically fixed and jurisdiction-controlled. New developable supply can only be created by converting existing land to a higher use (agricultural to residential, residential to commercial), clearing environmental constraints, or extending infrastructure (roads, water, sewer) to previously remote parcels. This process is slow — entitlement timelines for large master-planned projects can span 3–10 years, and environmental review under CEQA, NEPA, or state-equivalent processes adds cost and uncertainty. Supply barriers are therefore a function of regulatory complexity, infrastructure availability, and physical constraints (topography, flooding, contamination). See [[Land — Regulatory Considerations]] and [[Environmental Review Overview]] for detail.

In practice, the effective supply of shovel-ready, entitled land in many growth markets is much thinner than total acreage statistics suggest. Finished-lot supply can tighten sharply when homebuilder demand surges, creating pricing spikes that outpace the ability of the entitlement pipeline to respond. Conversely, overbuilding of finished lots in prior cycles can depress pricing for years as builders work off excess inventory.

## Underwriting Norms

Land has no operating income, so the standard cap rate table is replaced by residual value and return metrics. See [[Land — Underwriting Norms]] for the full norms note.

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Price per acre (raw, unentitled) | Wide — $5,000–$500,000+/acre | Varies enormously by location, infrastructure, and HBU |
| Price per buildable SF (commercial) | $5–$100+/buildable SF | Urban infill commands significant premium over suburban |
| Price per door / buildable unit (residential) | $20,000–$150,000+/door | Finished lots at high end; raw acreage at low end |
| Finished-lot value as % of new home price | 15%–30% | Higher in constrained markets; lower in commodity Sunbelt |
| Land as % of total development cost | 10%–25% | Varies by product type and market |

| Required return / discount rate | 15%–25%+ IRR | Reflects illiquidity, binary entitlement risk, carry |
| Carry cost (annual, as % of land basis) | 3%–8%+ | Property taxes + financing + opportunity cost |

## Key Operating Metrics

Land has no operating metrics in the conventional sense — there is no rent roll, no NOI, no occupancy rate. The relevant performance metrics are all forward-looking and pro forma driven. The most important is **residual land value (RLV)** — see [[Land — Key Metrics]] and [[Residual Land Value Method]] for definitions and formulas. Supporting metrics include price per acre, price per buildable SF, price per door (for residential), finished-lot ratio, land basis as percentage of total project cost, and entitlement premium over raw-land comps. Carry cost (taxes, interest, opportunity cost accreting against the land basis) is tracked as an annual dollar figure and as a percentage of acquisition basis.

Holding period and entitlement milestones serve as the operational metrics analogous to occupancy or lease-up in income-producing assets: each milestone (general plan amendment, zone change, environmental clearance, final map recordation, utility commitments) reduces risk, de-levers the required return, and increases the parcel's marketability to the next buyer in the value chain.

## Common Deal Structures

Land transactions can be structured as outright purchases, option agreements, or joint ventures. **Option agreements** are extremely common: a developer pays a modest option fee for the right to acquire the parcel within a defined period after achieving entitlement milestones, limiting capital at risk during the long entitlement phase. **Joint ventures** between a capital partner (equity) and an operating partner (entitlement/development expertise) mirror the [[Joint Venture Structures]] used across CRE, but with a greater emphasis on promote structures tied to entitlement milestones rather than income thresholds — see [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]].

Financing for land acquisitions is challenging. Most institutional lenders avoid raw land entirely; specialized land lenders, private credit funds, and seller financing fill the gap. Loan-to-value ratios on raw land loans are typically 40%–60%, with recourse requirements. Construction-to-permanent financing becomes available only after entitlement is obtained — see [[Construction Loan]] and [[Bridge Loan]]. In merchant builder transactions, homebuilders often structure takedown schedules (staggered lot purchases) rather than acquiring an entire tract at once, managing their inventory exposure while giving the developer/seller a defined exit timeline.

## Major Risks

Land carries the most concentrated risk profile in CRE: no income to offset carry, binary entitlement outcomes, sensitivity to market timing, and illiquidity. [[Land — Risks & Considerations]] provides detailed treatment. Key risks include: entitlement failure (political opposition, agency denial, legal challenge); market timing (if end-product values fall during a multi-year hold, residual land value can go negative on a leveraged basis); carry and debt service accumulating against a non-earning asset; physical constraints (contamination, floodplain, topography); and liquidity risk (the buyer pool for raw land is far thinner than for income-producing assets). Mitigants include phased option structures, thorough due diligence, conservative residual land value underwriting, and avoiding excessive leverage.

## Regulatory Considerations

Entitlement status is the single most important value driver for land. A parcel's ability to be developed as its [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] contemplates depends entirely on approvals from local planning departments, governing bodies, regional agencies, and state/federal environmental authorities. See [[Land — Regulatory Considerations]] for detailed treatment covering zoning, subdivision, environmental, water rights, and agricultural designations. Key processes: [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]], [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Environmental Review Overview]], [[Subdivision Approval]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], [[Planned Unit Development (PUD)]].

## Notable Markets

Residential land markets are most active in high-growth metros: Phoenix/Scottsdale, Dallas/Fort Worth, Houston, Austin, Nashville, Southeast Florida, and the Carolinas. Constrained-supply markets — coastal California, Seattle, Denver — generate the highest per-acre and per-door pricing but also the longest entitlement timelines and greatest regulatory risk. Industrial and logistics land is in demand near major distribution hubs (Inland Empire, I-35 corridor, Chicago, New Jersey/I-78). Infill commercial and mixed-use land is highly site-specific, driven by locational scarcity and municipal redevelopment incentives.
