# Infill vs. Greenfield

The choice between infill and greenfield development is one of the fundamental strategic decisions in land investing and development. The two categories have nearly opposite cost structures, entitlement dynamics, risk profiles, and market positioning. Understanding the trade-offs is essential for evaluating any land acquisition.

## Definitions

**Infill land** refers to parcels within or immediately adjacent to existing developed areas — surrounded by urban or suburban infrastructure, with utilities available, road network in place, and market demand generated by proximity to existing demand generators (employment, retail, transit). True infill is scarce by nature: once a market is built out, new infill opportunities arise only through demolition/redevelopment, lot splits, or repurposing of underutilized sites (parking lots, obsolete commercial, former industrial).

**Greenfield land** refers to previously undeveloped land at or beyond the urban fringe, where the surrounding area is not yet built out and infrastructure must be extended to serve the site. Greenfield development is the engine of metropolitan expansion — new master-planned communities, suburban subdivisions, and peripheral commercial development are almost entirely greenfield in character. The primary advantage is land cost; the primary disadvantages are infrastructure cost, entitlement timeline, and absorption risk over long buildout periods.

## Site Characteristics

Infill sites are typically:
- Smaller parcels (fractional acres to low single-digit acres in urban contexts; up to 20–50 acres in suburban infill corridors)
- Already served by water, sewer, power, and road access
- Adjacent to existing neighborhoods or commercial districts — community opposition to density increases is common
- Potentially constrained by legacy contamination (brownfields), irregular lot configurations, or existing improvements requiring demolition
- Subject to higher land prices per acre and per buildable unit, reflecting locational value

Greenfield sites are typically:
- Larger tracts (50 acres to thousands of acres for master-planned communities)
- Requiring full infrastructure installation — roads, water distribution, sewer collection, drainage, power
- Subject to longer entitlement timelines due to scale and complexity of required approvals
- Often involving negotiations with municipalities over public facility financing (development fees, special assessment districts, CFD/Mello-Roos structures)
- Lower land prices per acre, but infrastructure cost per unit or per buildable SF can close much of the gap

## Entitlement Dynamics

Infill entitlement tends to be more **process-intensive per project** but often more **predictable in outcome** than greenfield. Infill projects typically fit within an existing general plan land use framework — the planning and policy infrastructure already contemplates development in this area. The entitlement path may involve a zone change, density bonus application, design review, or conditional use permit, but major policy-level battles (general plan amendment, environmental impact at a program level) are less common for modest-scale infill.

Greenfield entitlement, by contrast, often requires foundational policy-level approvals: [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] for large project areas, [[Environmental Review Overview]] covering biological resources, traffic, water supply, and public facilities, and in some states, regional agency coordination (transportation, air quality, habitat conservation plans). These approvals take years and involve multiple agencies and hearings. The scale of political and legal exposure is larger — organized opposition to a 2,000-unit master planned community is more consequential than opposition to a 50-unit infill project.

## Infrastructure and Cost Structure

The cost structure difference is significant:

**Infill** — high land cost, low incremental infrastructure cost. Infrastructure is already in place; the developer pays connection fees and possibly some frontage improvements, but does not bear the cost of building a water system or road network. Hard costs per unit may be higher due to constrained site conditions (underground parking, tall buildings, demolition), but infrastructure cost per unit is lower.

**Greenfield** — low to moderate land cost, high infrastructure cost. Road construction, water main extensions, trunk sewer lines, dry utilities, drainage systems, parks, schools (through fee payments or land dedication), and fire stations are all costs that ultimately flow through the project pro forma either as direct costs or as impact fees. In large master-planned communities, developers often finance infrastructure through municipal special districts (Mello-Roos, CFDs, MUDs) that spread the cost over future property owners, but the underwriting must account for these obligations.

Both cost structures must be reflected in the [[Residual Land Value Method]] analysis, with infrastructure costs modeled accurately before arriving at a supportable land price.

## Absorption and Demand Risk

Infill demand is supported by the existing urban context — proximity to employment, transit, amenities, and established neighborhoods creates immediate, demonstrable demand from buyers and tenants who value location. Absorption tends to be faster and more certain (though still market-dependent), and the downside case for absorption is better anchored by observable market activity.

Greenfield absorption is a longer-duration bet. A master-planned community may require 10–20 years of buildout, crossing multiple real estate market cycles. [[Absorption Modeling]] for greenfield projects requires demographic projections ([[Demographic Analysis]]), employment growth assumptions ([[Employment and Jobs Analysis]]), and competitive supply analysis ([[Supply Pipeline Analysis]]). The developer must also consider whether competing greenfield projects will dilute absorption — in growth markets, multiple master plans often compete simultaneously for the same buyer pool.

## Return Profile and Strategic Positioning

| Dimension | Infill | Greenfield |
|-----------|--------|------------|
| Land cost per acre | High | Low to moderate |
| Infrastructure cost per unit | Low | High |
| Entitlement complexity | Moderate | High (policy-level) |
| Entitlement timeline | Shorter (1–4 years typical) | Longer (3–10+ years for large projects) |
| Absorption period | Shorter, more certain | Longer, more variable |
| Required IRR | Typically 15%–22% | Often 18%–28%+ (duration and scale) |
| Buyer type | Urban/suburban in-fill developers | Master-plan developers, large homebuilders |
| Competitive moat | Locational scarcity | Scale and phased lot delivery |

The right choice depends on capital duration, risk tolerance, operating capability, and market context. Neither type is categorically superior. Infill offers faster cycles and more defensible demand; greenfield offers basis advantage and scale, at the cost of duration and infrastructure investment.
