# Land — Subtypes

Land parcels exist on a continuous spectrum from completely raw and unentitled to fully improved with all infrastructure installed. Each position on that spectrum has a distinct risk/return profile, buyer universe, and underwriting approach. The distinctions below are practical, not mutually exclusive — a single parcel can span categories (e.g., partially entitled infill) — but the taxonomy is essential for framing a land deal accurately.

---

### Raw / Unentitled Land

**Profile.** Raw land carries no planning approvals beyond existing zoning and, in many cases, the current zoning does not reflect the parcel's [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] potential. There is no grading, no utilities stubbed to the site, no recorded plat or final map, and no environmental clearances beyond whatever the seller discloses. The parcel is acquired on the basis of a developer or investor's thesis about what can be entitled and built — a forward-looking bet on use, density, and timing.

**Deal profile.** Acquisitions are typically all-cash or lightly leveraged (40%–60% LTV max from specialized lenders or sellers). Option structures are especially common here: the buyer controls the land for a period while pursuing entitlement, with minimal capital at risk until milestones are achieved. Pricing is derived from a residual land value analysis anchored to a specific development program — see [[Residual Land Value Method]] and [[Residual Land Value Feasibility]]. Expected holds can span 3–10 years from acquisition to entitlement and sale or construction start.

**Who buys.** Merchant developers, master-planned community developers, private equity opportunity funds, patient family offices, and farmers/landowners rolling proceeds from agricultural sales. Institutional capital generally avoids raw land directly; it may participate through LP positions in opportunity funds.

---

### Entitled / Improved Land

**Profile.** Entitled land has cleared major discretionary hurdles — a general plan amendment, zone change, or conditional use permit — and may also have completed environmental review (CEQA/NEPA) and preliminary grading or infrastructure planning. The entitlement premium is already embedded in pricing, but the parcel typically still requires subdivision mapping, infrastructure installation, and possibly additional agency approvals before construction can begin. Risk is meaningfully lower than raw land but not eliminated.

**Deal profile.** Pricing reflects the risk and capital consumed in the entitlement process, plus a forward estimate of remaining costs to reach shovel-ready status. Returns are compressed relative to raw land but the hold period is shorter and the buyer pool is wider. Institutional and semi-institutional developers are active here, including homebuilders acquiring land for future lot development, multifamily developers, and commercial developers pursuing pad sites. Seller financing and JV structures remain common.

**Who buys.** Public homebuilders (Lennar, D.R. Horton, PulteGroup and peers), regional multifamily developers, commercial and industrial developers, and private equity funds targeting value-add entitlement risk. See [[Entitlement Play]] for deal-type framing.

---

### Finished Lots

**Profile.** Finished lots are pad-ready: the subdivision map has been recorded, streets are in, utilities are stubbed to each lot, and all required public improvements are bonded or complete. The buyer can pull a building permit and begin vertical construction with minimal additional infrastructure work. Finished lots represent the lowest-risk land product from an entitlement and infrastructure standpoint, and pricing reflects that de-risked status.

**Deal profile.** Homebuilders typically acquire finished lots through bulk purchases or staggered takedown agreements, buying lots in tranches matched to their construction and sales velocity. Pricing is analyzed on a per-door or per-lot basis versus the finished-lot value — typically 15%–30% of the projected new home price in most markets, though this ratio compresses in high-cost markets and expands in commodity Sunbelt submarkets. Finished-lot transactions are the most liquid in the land market.

**Who buys.** Almost exclusively homebuilders and small-lot residential developers. Merchant developers may also hold finished lots for retail sale to custom home builders. Institutional capital accesses this segment through build-to-rent (BTR) platforms acquiring finished lots for single-family rental development — see [[Ground-Up Development]].

### Infill Land

**Profile.** Infill sites are located within or immediately adjacent to established urbanized areas, surrounded by existing development and infrastructure. Value derives from locational scarcity — true infill opportunities are rare by definition. Sites may carry legacy contamination, irregular configurations, shared-access constraints, or existing improvements that must be demolished. Entitlement in infill contexts often involves adaptive reuse approvals, variance requests, or density bonus applications rather than greenfield subdivisions.

**Deal profile.** Infill land commands a significant premium over comparable greenfield acreage, reflecting infrastructure availability, proximity to demand generators, and permitting advantages in many jurisdictions that incentivize infill development. Underwriting is highly site-specific, with [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] driving the program assumption. Hold periods are typically shorter than greenfield because entitlement paths are more defined and infrastructure costs are lower. See [[Infill vs. Greenfield]] for a direct comparison.

**Who buys.** Urban infill developers, mixed-use developers, workforce and affordable housing developers (who benefit from density bonus programs), and national homebuilders targeting urban infill subdivisions. Institutional capital is active in high-barrier infill markets where supply constraints provide pricing support for completed product.

---

### Greenfield Land

**Profile.** Greenfield sites are undeveloped land on the periphery of built-out areas, typically with no existing infrastructure and requiring full extension of roads, water, sewer, power, and sometimes schools and fire service. Greenfield development is the engine of suburban expansion in growth markets. The primary advantage over infill is lower land cost per acre and the ability to plan large, cohesive projects; the disadvantages are longer entitlement timelines, infrastructure cost burden, and exposure to absorption risk over multi-year buildout periods.

**Deal profile.** Greenfield transactions are often large in scale (hundreds to thousands of acres), involving master-planned community frameworks, development agreements with municipalities, and complex cost-sharing arrangements for infrastructure and public facilities. Return profiles require long hold underwriting — often 5–15 years from first acquisition to final lot sale — with substantial carry costs and multiple absorption assumptions. Public-private partnership structures are common for large greenfield master plans. See [[Land Banking vs. Active Pursuit]] for strategic framing.

**Who buys.** Master-planned community developers (e.g., regional and national private developers), large homebuilders acquiring land banks for future programs, and opportunity funds willing to accept long durations in exchange for basis advantage.

---

### Agricultural / Transitional Land

**Profile.** Agricultural and transitional land parcels are actively farmed or otherwise in primary-sector use but are located in corridors or at the urban fringe where conversion to residential or commercial development is a credible near-to-medium-term outcome. Value is a blend of agricultural income (often minimal relative to land value) and speculative conversion premium. In states with agricultural preservation programs — California's Williamson Act, Oregon's farm and forest zone protections — the path to conversion is legally constrained and may involve contract cancellation periods of 9–10 years or longer.

**Deal profile.** Acquisitions are typically priced above agricultural income value but below full entitled-land value, reflecting the probability and timing of conversion. The agricultural income stream can offset some carry cost but rarely covers it fully once the speculative premium is embedded in the basis. Due diligence must address water rights (critical in the arid West), soil conditions, existing lease obligations to farming tenants, and the regulatory path to conversion. See [[Land — Regulatory Considerations]].

**Who buys.** Speculative investors, family offices with long investment horizons, master-planned community developers acquiring future land banks, and institutional opportunity funds in select growth corridors. Some farmland REITs and agricultural investment managers hold transitional land as a byproduct of agricultural portfolios.

---

### Land Held for Development

**Profile.** Land held for development is owned by a developer or homebuilder with the intent to develop the site themselves — they are not acquiring to entitle and flip, but to pursue the full development cycle through construction and sale or lease-up. This is the developer's working inventory, and it sits on the balance sheet as a land asset until construction commences. Underwriting is a full development pro forma, not a residual land value flip analysis.

**Deal profile.** The hold period and capital deployment schedule are tied to the development timeline. Developers typically seek to minimize the time land sits without adding value, as carry costs accumulate without offsetting income. [[Construction Loan]] proceeds are drawn once building permits are in hand; the land basis converts into a hard-cost budget. Risk management focuses on permitting timelines, construction cost inflation, and end-product demand — the three largest drivers of development spread. See [[Development Feasibility Test]] and [[Yield on Cost]].

**Who buys.** Developers of all scales. Public homebuilders maintain land banks measured in years of supply (typically 3–6 years of owned and optioned lots). Commercial developers hold permitted sites through pre-leasing or pre-sale phases.

---

### Land Held for Resale

**Profile.** Land held for resale is owned by an investor or developer who intends to sell the parcel — either raw, partly entitled, or fully entitled — rather than develop it directly. This is the merchant land model: buy low on the risk/entitlement spectrum, add value through entitlement or infrastructure, and sell to the next buyer in the value chain (often a builder or developer). Profits are realized through the land markup, not through development returns.

**Deal profile.** Merchant land operators typically target 2–5 year hold periods, generating returns through entitlement value creation rather than construction. The exit buyer (homebuilder, commercial developer) pays a price reflecting remaining development risk and their own required margin. Pricing discipline at acquisition is critical — overpaying for land on an optimistic residual model leaves no margin for error. Option structures, phased acquisitions, and development agreements with municipalities that provide entitlement certainty are common risk-management tools. See [[Land Banking]] and [[Entitlement Play]] for deal-type framing.

**Who buys.** Merchant lot developers, regional private equity land operators, family office land funds, and real estate private equity platforms with dedicated land strategies.
