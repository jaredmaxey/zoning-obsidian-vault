# Land — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Land underwriting is fundamentally different from income-property underwriting. There is no rent roll, no NOI, no cap rate — every valuation methodology must instead start with a forward-looking development pro forma and work backwards to a supportable land price. The [[Residual Land Value Method]] is the primary tool: supportable land value equals projected completed-project value minus total development costs (hard + soft + financing) minus a required developer profit margin. This residual approach compresses all assumptions about future revenue, cost, timing, and risk into a single land-price conclusion, making it highly sensitive to each input.

Because land values are derived from end-product assumptions, small changes in any major driver — exit pricing, construction costs, absorption pace, or entitlement timing — can produce large swings in residual value. A 5% increase in hard costs or a 10% decrease in projected exit pricing can reduce the residual land value by 20–50% or more on a typical project, depending on the profit margin assumption. This sensitivity necessitates rigorous [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]] at every stage of land underwriting.

## Valuation Approach

### Residual Land Value — Primary Method

The [[Residual Land Value Method]] is the foundation of every land underwriting exercise. The mechanics are:

1. Estimate the **projected completed value** of the development (using comparable sales, direct cap, or DCF for income properties; comparable home sales for residential).
2. Estimate **total development costs** — hard costs, soft costs, financing costs, carry, and sales/lease-up costs — using the [[Hard Costs]] and [[Soft Costs]] frameworks.
3. Deduct a **required developer profit** (typically 10%–20%+ of total project cost, reflecting the developer's risk and effort).
4. The remainder is the **maximum supportable land price** — the residual land value.

This approach forces the analyst to commit to a specific development program (use, density, unit mix, building specifications) before underwriting the land. Changing the assumed program can change the RLV dramatically. The [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] framework is used to determine which program to underwrite.

For a [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] land underwriting, the same logic applies over time: model the entitlement timeline, infrastructure investment, construction period, and absorption schedule; discount all cash flows at the required return; the NPV at time zero is the land value. This method is especially important for large, phased projects where cash flows span many years.

### Comparable Sales — Secondary / Corroborating Method

After completing the residual analysis, the analyst checks conclusions against comparable land sales ([[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]]). Land comps must be adjusted for: entitlement status, density/FAR, infrastructure condition, timing, and physical characteristics. Raw land comps should not be compared directly to entitled comps without explicit adjustments. The comp analysis validates whether the residual conclusion is within the market's observable pricing range — if the two methods diverge significantly, the inputs should be revisited.

## Carry & Timing

Carry cost is the most insidious risk in land underwriting. Unlike income properties, where operating cash flow offsets debt service, land generates nothing while property taxes, interest on acquisition loans, insurance, and legal/entitlement costs accrue every month. Even a modest $5M land acquisition with 50% leverage at a 9% interest rate generates approximately $225,000/year in interest carry alone, before taxes or other holding costs. Over a 4-year entitlement process, that accretes roughly $900,000+ in carry into the land basis — compounding the required exit price.

Underwriters must explicitly model the entitlement timeline as a range of scenarios. The base case should assume a realistic (not optimistic) entitlement duration; the downside case should stress the timeline by 12–24 months and examine the impact on required exit pricing. Development agreements, option structures, and phased acquisition schedules are the primary tools for managing carry exposure — see [[Land Banking vs. Active Pursuit]] for strategic discussion.
