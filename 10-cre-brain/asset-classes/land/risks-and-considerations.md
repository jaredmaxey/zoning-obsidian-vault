# Land — Risks & Considerations

Land carries the most concentrated risk profile of any major CRE asset class. The absence of in-place income removes the floor that cushions income-producing assets, leaving land values fully exposed to changes in development economics, entitlement outcomes, and market timing. Practitioners must approach land with a clear-eyed assessment of four primary risk categories before committing capital.

---

### Market

**Description.** Land value is derived from the end-product market — what can be built and at what price. Market timing risk is therefore amplified in land: a correction in home prices, office rents, or industrial demand directly impairs residual land value, often at a magnified rate. A 10–15% decline in projected completed-project value can reduce residual land value by 30–50%+ when development margins are thin. Moreover, the long hold periods inherent in land ownership mean investors are exposed to market cycles they cannot predict — a parcel that makes excellent economic sense in year one may face a cyclical trough at the expected exit in year four or five.

End-product market risk also includes changes in capital markets conditions: rising cap rates compress exit values for commercial development projects, reducing the residual calculation backward through to the land. Similarly, rising construction costs — which have demonstrated significant volatility — compress margins and reduce supportable land prices without any change in end-product demand.

**Mitigants.**
- Underwrite to conservative residual assumptions, not peak pricing.
- Run downside [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]] scenarios explicitly, not just base-case.
- Prefer markets with demonstrated long-term population and employment growth to reduce timing dependency.
- Structure option agreements or phased takedown schedules to avoid committing full capital before market conditions are validated.

---

### Entitlement

**Description.** Entitlement risk is the binary, often political, risk that a required government approval will be denied, substantially modified, delayed, or legally challenged. Even a project with clear legal entitlement rights can face years of delay from environmental review challenges, neighborhood opposition, appeals, or litigation under statutes such as CEQA (California), SEPA (Washington), or NEPA (federal). An entitlement denial is potentially a total loss of entitlement-related investment — professional fees, carrying costs, and any capital spent on improvements.

Entitlement risk includes process risk (the approval path is more complex or slower than anticipated), political risk (elected officials or community opposition block approvals that seemed certain), and legal risk (approved entitlements are challenged in court, creating injunctions or reversals). General plan amendments and zone changes carry greater political risk than ministerial approvals; larger projects attract greater scrutiny. See [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]], [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Public Hearing Process]], and [[Appeals Process]].

**Mitigants.**
- Conduct thorough pre-acquisition entitlement risk assessment: feasibility studies, preliminary community outreach, and meetings with planning staff before closing.
- Use option structures where possible — commit only a refundable or limited option fee until entitlement milestones are achieved.
- Budget realistically for entitlement timelines, including 12–24 month contingency buffers.
- Hire experienced local land use counsel and project managers; political relationships and process knowledge are often decisive.
- Negotiate development agreements with municipalities where feasible — these lock in entitlement terms and timelines.
- Diversify across multiple entitlement-stage projects to avoid portfolio concentration in a single binary outcome.

---

### Carry & Liquidity

**Description.** Land generates no income, yet ownership is not free: property taxes, loan interest, insurance, and entitlement professional fees accrue every month. On a leveraged basis, carry costs can absorb 5–10%+ of the land basis annually. Extended entitlement delays or market downturns can force investors to hold properties far longer than underwritten, compounding the carry burden and elevating the required exit price to break even. If a forced sale occurs during a downturn, bid-ask spreads on raw land can be wide and liquidity is limited — the buyer pool for unentitled land is far thinner than for income-producing assets.

Leverage magnifies carry risk. An acquisition financed with a recourse land loan (typically required by lenders for raw land — see [[Recourse vs. Non-Recourse]] and [[Bad-Boy Carve-Outs]]) creates debt-service obligations regardless of project progress. If an entitlement stumbles and the loan matures, the borrower faces refinancing at potentially worse terms or forced liquidation. Distressed land transactions are common at cycle troughs; see [[Distressed Acquisition]].

**Mitigants.**
- Prefer option structures over outright purchases for entitlement-stage land whenever possible.
- Minimize leverage on raw land; do not underwrite carry-heavy scenarios with optimistic exit assumptions.
- Maintain adequate liquidity reserves at the portfolio level to fund extended holds.
- Structure acquisition loans with interest reserves and extension options to avoid forced-sale scenarios.
- Prefer seller financing where possible — sellers often carry paper at below-market rates, reducing carry burden.
- Model explicit downside carry scenarios: what is the breakeven exit price if the hold extends 18 months beyond base case?

---

### Physical & Environmental

**Description.** Physical and environmental constraints can impair or eliminate a site's development potential regardless of entitlement and market conditions. Contamination (Phase I and Phase II environmental site assessments are essential pre-purchase steps) can require remediation at costs that may exceed land value. Floodplain designation (FEMA flood zones) constrains buildable area and increases development costs. Topographic limitations — steep slopes, rock outcroppings, unstable soils — elevate grading and foundation costs. Wetlands, jurisdictional waters, and listed species habitat trigger regulatory mitigation requirements under federal and state environmental law, adding cost and delay or foreclosing development entirely on affected portions.

Access and utility availability are equally critical. A parcel without legal vehicle access, or that requires significant off-site infrastructure extension (roads, water, sewer, power) to be developable, carries cost and timing risk that must be quantified in the pro forma. In the arid West, water rights and water service availability are often the binding constraint on development — a parcel may be perfectly entitled for residential use but have no available water supply within a reasonable cost range. See [[Land — Regulatory Considerations]] for detailed regulatory treatment.

**Mitigants.**
- Commission Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (and Phase II if Phase I reveals recognized environmental conditions) prior to closing.
- Order a FEMA flood zone determination and review topographic surveys early in due diligence.
- Engage a civil engineer to assess grading, drainage, utilities, and access as part of due diligence.
- Verify water availability with the relevant water utility or irrigation district, particularly in Western states.
- Obtain title insurance with endorsements covering access, utility easements, and any known encumbrances.
- Reserve environmental mitigation costs explicitly in the development pro forma, not in contingency.
- Negotiate seller representations and indemnities for undisclosed environmental conditions; include survival periods beyond closing.
