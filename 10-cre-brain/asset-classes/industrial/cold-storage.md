# Cold Storage

## Overview

Cold storage real estate encompasses buildings designed to maintain product at controlled temperatures below ambient — typically refrigerated (34–50°F for produce, dairy, and beverages), frozen (-10°F to 32°F for frozen food and certain pharmaceuticals), or a combination of both zones within a single facility. End markets served include fresh and frozen food distribution (the largest demand segment), pharmaceutical and biologics cold-chain logistics (a fast-growing segment driven by vaccine and biologic storage requirements), floral distribution, and specialty chemicals.

Cold storage commands the highest replacement cost per SF in the industrial universe, driven by the specialized building envelope (thick insulated panels, thermal-break dock doors, vapor barriers), heavy refrigeration infrastructure (compressors, condensers, evaporators, piping, and controls), robust electrical service, hardened concrete floors for heavy fork-lift traffic, and elevated fire-suppression requirements. This high replacement cost creates a meaningful barrier to entry — the economics of new development are challenging except in strong demand markets or under build-to-suit structures — and supports rent premiums relative to ambient warehouse on a $/SF basis.

The specialized nature of cold storage operations means that most institutionally owned cold storage is operated by specialist companies rather than general industrial landlords. Major operators — Americold Realty Trust (public REIT), Lineage Logistics, US Cold Storage, and Preferred Freezer Services — collectively own and operate a substantial share of institutional-grade cold storage in the US. Investors entering the cold storage market typically either partner with one of these operators or invest in specialized funds with existing cold-chain operating expertise.

## Physical Profile

Cold storage buildings range from small neighborhood frozen-food distribution facilities (10,000–50,000 SF) to large regional distribution centers (500,000+ SF). Key physical characteristics:

- **Clear height:** 30–50+ ft clear in high-bay facilities with automated stacker-retrieval systems; lower in older or multi-temperature facilities (20–30 ft)
- **Insulated building envelope:** Rigid insulated panel systems (typically 4–6 inches thick for refrigerated; 6–8 inches for frozen), with all penetrations thermally sealed to prevent condensation and energy loss
- **Dock doors:** Fewer doors per SF than ambient warehouse (each opening is a significant thermal breach); dock doors are typically insulated and equipped with sealing systems to minimize temperature exchange during loading
- **Floor system:** Thickened concrete slab over a sub-floor heating system (to prevent frozen-ground heaving from sustained sub-freezing operation); heavy-duty surface coating for fork-lift durability
- **Refrigeration system:** Ammonia (NH3) or HFC-based refrigerant systems; large rooftop or ground-level mechanical yards; cooling towers for heat rejection
- **Power:** Significantly higher power demand than ambient warehouse; confirm available utility capacity and cost before underwriting

## Demand Drivers

Food distribution is the primary cold storage demand driver, tracking grocery and food-service distribution volumes, population growth, and the expansion of grocery delivery and fresh/frozen e-commerce (a rapidly growing demand source that requires cold-chain capability within the last-mile network). Pharmaceutical and biologics cold chain is a growing demand segment as mRNA vaccines, cell therapies, and specialty drugs require strict temperature control at each point in the distribution chain.

Supply of institutional-grade cold storage has historically lagged demand — the capital intensity of development, the complexity of operations, and the limited pool of qualified tenants and operators have suppressed speculative development. This structural supply deficit has contributed to rent growth in well-located, modern cold storage and has driven the institutionalization of the asset type.

## Deal Profile

Cold storage investment is typically structured as one of:

**Build-to-Suit with Operator Lease:** Developer/investor builds a custom facility to the operator's specifications and leases it long-term (15–25 years) to a creditworthy cold-chain operator on a NNN or absolute-net basis. This structure resembles a net-leased single-tenant industrial deal with a bond-like cash flow, but requires careful diligence on operator financial strength and the building's functional specificity (highly customized cold storage may have a very limited re-use market if the operator fails). See [[Build-to-Suit]].

**Sale-Leaseback from Owner-Operator:** An owner-operating food company or 3PL monetizes its cold storage real estate through a sale-leaseback transaction, retaining occupancy under a long-term NNN lease. This provides the seller with capital for operations or business investment and delivers the buyer a stabilized, long-lease asset. See [[Sale-Leaseback]].

**Acquisition via Specialist Platform:** Investors may gain cold storage exposure through investment in an operating platform (Americold REIT shares, LP interest in a Lineage fund) rather than direct asset ownership, trading direct-asset upside for operational expertise.

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Replacement cost analysis:** Cold storage per-SF costs vary widely by vintage, temperature profile, and location, but they substantially exceed ambient warehouse. Underwriters should confirm that acquisition pricing reflects a reasonable relationship to [[Replacement Cost]] — paying close to or above replacement cost for functionally sound product may be justified in supply-constrained markets; paying above replacement cost for aging infrastructure with refrigerant compliance issues is not.

**Refrigerant compliance:** EPA regulations on HFCs (AIM Act phase-down) require facilities with legacy high-GWP refrigerants to transition to approved alternatives over a defined timeline. Ammonia systems above threshold volumes trigger OSHA PSM and EPA RMP compliance obligations. These compliance costs and timelines must be modeled as a capital requirement.

**Energy costs:** Refrigeration is energy-intensive. Confirm the utility rate structure applicable to the property and model energy cost as a major operating line item. Some leases pass through energy costs to the tenant; others do not. A triple-net structure that includes energy recovery substantially improves the landlord's NOI stability.

**Operator financial strength:** Unlike ambient warehouse, where the building can re-lease to a broad tenant universe if a tenant vacates, cold storage has a narrow re-use market. Underwrite the operator's financial covenant carefully, stress-test a vacancy scenario, and model realistic downtime and capital requirements if the building must be re-tenanted or re-purposed.
