# Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS)

## Overview

Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS) describes improved or semi-improved land parcels used for the open-air storage of large movable assets: commercial vehicles (semi-trucks, trailers, fleet vehicles), construction equipment and heavy machinery, shipping containers, building materials, salvage materials, automotive inventory (new and used vehicles), and utility or infrastructure equipment. Physical improvements are typically minimal — a paved or compacted-gravel surface, security fencing (chain link with barbed wire or crash-rated barriers for high-security sites), security lighting, and sometimes a small office or guard structure. The defining characteristic of IOS is that the primary product is paved, secured outdoor land — not a building.

Despite (or because of) its apparent simplicity, IOS has attracted substantial institutional capital in recent years. The investment thesis rests on several converging factors: supply constraint driven by municipal opposition and zoning crackdowns, low capital expenditure requirements, and high income yields relative to more complex product types. The result is an asset type transitioning from a fragmented, local-operator-dominated market toward institutionalization, with specialist platforms (Alterra IOS, GreenPoint IOS, and others) aggregating portfolios at scale.

## Physical Profile

IOS sites vary considerably in size, improvement level, and configuration:

- **Site size:** ranges from less than 1 acre (urban infill trailer drops) to 50+ acres (large intermodal or automotive storage yards)
- **Surface:** paved asphalt or concrete is preferred by tenants requiring stable, all-weather access; compacted gravel is acceptable for many uses but may require periodic maintenance; some sites are partially paved with gravel overflow areas
- **Fencing and security:** perimeter fencing (8 ft chain link minimum; additional barbed wire or security camera systems for higher-value stored assets); gated entry with keypad or security guard
- **Lighting:** security lighting adequate for 24-hour operations in carrier and container depot uses
- **Drainage:** grading and drainage for stormwater runoff; impervious surface coverage may be regulated; sites with heavy vehicle traffic or chemical storage may require oil-water separators
- **Building improvements:** small office or dispatch structure (often 200–1,000 SF); occasionally a small maintenance bay; minimal in most cases
- **Utilities:** basic electrical for lighting and office; water and sewer for restroom facilities; not required in all configurations

## Demand Drivers

IOS demand is driven by the logistics economy's need for staging and storage space near transportation infrastructure:

- **Trucking and freight:** Long-haul carriers, regional trucking companies, and last-mile delivery operators need sites to drop trailers, stage loaded and empty containers, and park fleet vehicles. Proximity to interstate interchanges, port terminals, rail yards, and distribution hubs is critical.
- **Construction sector:** Contractors and equipment rental companies need accessible storage for heavy equipment, materials, and vehicles. Demand tracks construction activity.
- **Automotive:** Car dealerships, auto auctions (Manheim, ADESA), rental car companies, and OEM overflow storage drive demand for large, paved, well-lit vehicle storage yards.
- **Container depots:** Shipping container storage and repair (empty repositioning yards, depot operations) is a major IOS use concentrated near ports and intermodal facilities.
- **Utility and infrastructure:** Utilities, telecom companies, and infrastructure contractors need accessible outdoor storage for transformers, cable reels, pipes, and vehicles.

## Deal Profile

IOS investment is predominantly a value-add or opportunistic strategy, given the fragmented ownership base and historically non-institutional nature of the asset type:

**Aggregation / Portfolio Assembly:** The dominant institutional strategy — acquiring individual IOS sites across a metro area or region and assembling them into a professionally managed portfolio. Scale creates operating efficiencies, improves access to institutional financing, and creates exit optionality via portfolio sale. Specialist platforms (Alterra, GreenPoint) are the primary practitioners.

**Value-Add Repositioning:** Acquisition of a below-market-leased or underutilized site with a plan to re-tenant at market rents, improve surface condition and security, and achieve a stabilized yield. The re-leasing market for IOS is often thin outside of major logistics hubs, so local demand analysis is essential.

**Land Banking Adjacent to Industrial Nodes:** Some IOS acquisitions are thesis-driven land banking — acquiring land near port, rail, or intermodal infrastructure with the expectation that demand for outdoor storage (and potentially vertical industrial development) will grow as the logistics node matures. See [[Land Banking]].

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Zoning and use rights:** IOS is the industrial subtype most exposed to zoning and regulatory risk. Many IOS sites operate under non-conforming use status (legal pre-existing use) or conditional use permits that could be challenged or restricted by municipal action. Investors must confirm the legal status of the outdoor storage use, whether any conditions of approval exist, and the risk of code-enforcement action or rezoning that could curtail or eliminate the use. See [[Industrial — Regulatory Considerations]] and [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]].

**Environmental history:** Industrial land with extended outdoor storage or vehicle maintenance use may have soil contamination from petroleum products, hydraulic fluids, or other vehicle-related releases. Phase I ESA is mandatory; Phase II may be required if RECs are identified.

**Improvement-to-land ratio and residual value:** IOS has a very low improvement-to-land ratio — in some cases the land itself represents 85–100% of the property value. This creates a favorable residual value floor (land has alternative uses) but also means the buyer is effectively underwriting land value and use-rights continuity, not building depreciation. Model a [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] to confirm whether the IOS use is the optimal long-term use or whether the land has higher-value development potential.

**Financing:** IOS financing has historically been more challenging than conventional industrial — lenders are less familiar with the asset type, minimal improvements create limited collateral value, and LTV constraints tend to be tighter. As the asset type institutionalizes and specialist lenders develop IOS programs, conditions are improving, but underwriters should budget for higher equity requirements and more conservative debt terms than on conventional industrial buildings.

**Rent structure and lease terms:** IOS leases are typically shorter than ambient industrial leases (1–5 years is common), providing more frequent mark-to-market opportunities but also more leasing-cost turnover. Some sites are licensed on a monthly or rolling-term basis, which maximizes flexibility but creates income uncertainty. Target stabilized occupancy and a lease-expiration schedule that avoids simultaneous vacancy across multiple positions.
