# Bulk Warehouse

## Overview

Bulk warehouse — also called regional distribution, big-box industrial, or mega-distribution — refers to large-format industrial buildings typically ranging from 200,000 to 1,000,000+ SF. These facilities sit at the core of national and regional supply chains, serving as the primary nodes for receiving inbound freight, consolidating and sorting product, and dispatching outbound shipments to regional distribution points or end destinations. Their scale requires access to major transportation infrastructure: interstate highway interchange proximity, Class I rail access, or proximity to major port terminals or intermodal yards.

Bulk warehouse is the segment of industrial real estate most closely associated with institutional ownership. REITs (Prologis, EastGroup Properties, STAG Industrial), open-end diversified core funds, pension fund advisors, and foreign sovereign wealth vehicles are the dominant long-term holders. Transaction velocity in major markets is high, price discovery is relatively efficient, and cap rates are closely tracked against comparable sales in comparable submarkets. Lease terms are long (typically 7–15 years), structures are NNN or absolute-net, and annual rent bumps (2–4% per year, or CPI-linked) are standard.

## Physical Profile

Modern bulk warehouse specifications have escalated substantially over the past two decades, driven by tenant requirements for higher throughput and automation compatibility. As of the mid-2020s, new Class A bulk distribution targets:

- **Clear height:** 36–40 ft clear (some purpose-built automation facilities spec 50+ ft)
- **Column spacing:** 52×52 ft or 56×50 ft bay grids to maximize racking efficiency
- **Dock doors:** approximately 1 per 8,000–12,000 SF of building area (varies by use)
- **Truck court depth:** 185–210 ft, measured dock face to opposite boundary or trailer parking
- **Trailer parking:** 1 spotted-trailer stall per 1,000–2,000 SF is a common target for high-throughput distribution
- **Office component:** 2–5% of total SF, typically a single-story or mezzanine office module
- **Sprinkler system:** ESFR throughout
- **Parking:** lower auto ratio than light industrial (0.5–1.0 stall per 1,000 SF)

Older bulk product (built pre-2000) commonly features 18–28 ft clear, narrower column spacing, and inadequate truck courts by modern standards. These functional shortcomings create a two-tier market: modern Class A commands the tightest cap rates and highest rents; Class B/C product trades at discounts that reflect the cost and feasibility of upgrading specifications or re-leasing at below-market rents.

## Deal Profile

Bulk warehouse investment most commonly takes one of several forms:

**Stabilized Core Acquisition:** Purchase of a single-tenant or multi-tenant building with in-place long-term NNN leases, targeting a core or core-plus return profile. Underwriting focuses on lease term remaining, rent versus market, tenant credit, and physical specifications versus future re-leasing competitiveness. See [[Core Acquisition]].

**Value-Add Acquisition:** Acquisition of a functionally dated building or a recently vacated asset at a discount to replacement cost, with a plan to re-lease at market rents (possibly after capital improvements). The thesis requires a realistic assessment of re-leasing demand depth, TI requirements, and downtime. See [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

**Ground-Up Development (Speculative or BTS):** Developer acquires land, obtains entitlements, and constructs a new building — either speculatively (targeting a tenant at or after completion) or as a [[Build-to-Suit]] for a pre-committed tenant. The primary underwriting metric is [[Yield on Cost]] relative to market cap rates (the [[Development Spread]]). A positive spread justifies development risk; a compressed or negative spread makes spec development untenable.

**Portfolio Acquisition:** Institutional investors frequently acquire portfolios of bulk warehouse assets across multiple markets, trading a blended cap rate in exchange for diversification. Portfolio buyers accept some weaker assets to access a better-quality aggregate.

## Ownership Universe

- **Public REITs:** Prologis, EastGroup Properties, STAG Industrial, Rexford Industrial (infill/SoCal focus)
- **Open-end core funds:** Major pension advisors (Clarion, TA Realty, JP Morgan) hold bulk logistics in their diversified core portfolios
- **Closed-end value-add funds:** Blackstone BREIT, Brookfield, Ares, Crow Holdings industrial platforms
- **Sovereign wealth funds:** GIC (Singapore), ADIA (Abu Dhabi), and others active in direct and JV acquisition of logistics portfolios
- **Developer-owners:** IDI Logistics, Seefried, Sunbelt Industrial, and numerous regional developers who build and sell or hold

## Key Underwriting Considerations

Single-tenant rollover risk is the central underwriting challenge in bulk warehouse. A 500,000 SF building generating $3M+ in annual NOI drops to zero income if the tenant vacates and the market re-leasing period extends 12–24 months. Underwriters must stress-test the scenario of full vacancy at lease expiration, model realistic downtime and TI requirements for the replacement tenant, and confirm that the remaining lease term and in-place debt service allow sufficient headroom.

Physical specifications must be evaluated against current submarket standards and the prospective re-leasing tenant universe. A 28 ft clear building in a market where 36 ft clear is the modern standard will re-lease to a narrower tenant set, likely at a discount to Class A rents. The cost to raise clear height (if structurally feasible at all) is typically prohibitive, meaning functional shortcomings in clear height are largely permanent and should be permanently priced into residual-value assumptions.

Supply pipeline modeling is especially important in growth markets. [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] should identify all planned and under-construction comparable product in the submarket, compare it to historical net absorption rates, and stress-test a scenario where new supply delays re-leasing of a vacant asset or compresses rents at renewal.
