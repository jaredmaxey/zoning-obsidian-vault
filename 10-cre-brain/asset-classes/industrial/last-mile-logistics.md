# Last-Mile Logistics

## Overview

Last-mile logistics facilities are small-to-mid-size industrial buildings (typically 20,000–200,000 SF) positioned within or immediately adjacent to dense urban population centers, serving the final segment of the delivery chain from distribution hub to end consumer. The term "last mile" refers to the delivery leg from a local sortation or distribution point to the recipient's door — the most time-sensitive and expensive segment of the logistics chain on a per-unit basis. Proximity to population density is the defining locational requirement; physical building specifications are secondary.

The structural driver of last-mile demand is the accelerating consumer expectation for same-day and next-day delivery, which requires fulfillment infrastructure embedded within dense urban areas rather than sited at suburban or exurban logistics parks. As this expectation has become standard — driven by Amazon, grocery delivery platforms, and third-party parcel carriers — the value of well-located urban infill industrial has re-rated dramatically. Supply constraint is severe: infill urban land is expensive, entitlement is slow and politically contentious, and many potential sites have environmental complications from prior industrial uses. These barriers create a durable rent premium relative to suburban bulk warehouse on a $/SF basis.

## Physical Profile

Last-mile facilities vary widely in specification, reflecting the heterogeneity of urban infill stock. Unlike bulk warehouse, which is mostly new-construction Class A product, the last-mile universe includes converted older industrial buildings, urban flex properties, former auto dealerships, and purpose-built urban logistics facilities.

Common characteristics:
- **Building size:** 20,000–200,000 SF; occasionally up to 300,000 SF in dense metros with multi-story options
- **Clear height:** 18–32 ft clear in older stock; newer urban builds may achieve 32–36 ft where land allows adequate truck court
- **Loading:** mixed dock-high and grade-level; constraints on truck court depth are common in infill sites
- **Parking:** often limited due to urban land constraints; higher auto ratio per SF than bulk (more employees per SF relative to automation)
- **Multi-story formats:** emerging in the densest markets (New York, Los Angeles) where land costs justify stacked configurations with internal ramps or freight elevators; still a small share of total stock
- **Office component:** variable; some last-mile facilities double as urban distribution hubs with modest office/dispatch functions

Infill last-mile buildings with constrained truck courts and below-modern clear heights may still command premium rents if the location is irreplaceable. Location substitutes for specifications in the last-mile thesis.

## Demand Drivers

E-commerce penetration and delivery speed expectations are the primary demand drivers. Key leading indicators include same-day and next-day delivery share of total e-commerce orders, population density trends in the metro, and the number and scale of existing last-mile operators seeking expansion. Parcel carriers (UPS, FedEx, USPS), Amazon Logistics, grocery delivery platforms (Instacart, DoorDash), and urban delivery startups (cargo bikes, micro-fulfillment) are all active last-mile tenants.

Grocery delivery and pharmaceutical cold-chain distribution add a cold-storage dimension to some last-mile assets, creating demand for hybrid ambient/refrigerated urban facilities. This niche commands the highest rents in the last-mile universe and the highest replacement costs.

## Deal Profile

Last-mile investment is predominantly a value-add or core-plus strategy given the scarcity of new-construction product and the need to renovate or reposition older infill stock. Common deal types include:

**Value-Add Infill Repositioning:** Acquisition of an older urban industrial building with below-market leases or near-term rollover, with a plan to renovate the loading and circulation infrastructure, improve office components, and re-lease to a last-mile tenant at current market rents. The thesis is driven by location — the building may be functionally dated but the land is irreplaceable. See [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

**Adaptive Reuse:** Conversion of a non-industrial building (auto dealer service bay, urban retail warehouse, cold-storage facility) into last-mile logistics use. Entitlement and construction risk require careful modeling. See [[Adaptive Reuse]].

**Portfolio Assembly:** Aggregating multiple small infill industrial assets in a metro area to create a branded last-mile network. This strategy is pursued by specialized operators (Rexford, Link Logistics) and private equity platforms that can achieve scale and brand value across a fragmented ownership base.

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Location quality** is the paramount underwriting variable. Proximity to dense residential population, freeway or arterial access for van and small-truck delivery, and the absence of competitive alternative sites in a defined geographic radius are more important than building specifications. A [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] should confirm that the industrial use is sustainable against competing uses (residential conversion, mixed-use redevelopment) that may offer higher land value in some markets.

**Re-leasing risk** is moderate compared to bulk single-tenant assets — urban last-mile locations with genuine scarcity tend to attract multiple competing tenants, reducing vacancy risk. However, shorter lease terms (3–7 years) mean more frequent rollover events, each requiring TI and leasing commission reserves.

**Environmental diligence** is critical: urban infill sites commonly have prior industrial use (auto repair, dry cleaning, gasoline storage) that may have left soil or groundwater contamination. Phase I and Phase II ESA diligence are mandatory before acquisition. Remediation costs can substantially erode acquisition returns and should be reflected in pricing.

**Entitlement and operational restrictions** may be more complex than suburban industrial: truck-traffic routing, operating-hours restrictions, and neighbor opposition are common in urban settings. Confirm the property's entitlement record and any conditional use limitations before underwriting.
