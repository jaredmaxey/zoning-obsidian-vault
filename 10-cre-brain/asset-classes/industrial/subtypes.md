# Industrial — Subtypes

Industrial real estate encompasses multiple distinct product types, each with its own physical profile, tenant base, underwriting logic, and ownership universe. Understanding subtype differences is essential for comparing assets, setting rent and vacancy assumptions, and evaluating capital expenditure requirements. The seven primary subtypes are profiled below.

### Bulk/Big-Box Warehouse

Bulk warehouse — sometimes called big-box or regional distribution — refers to large-format facilities typically ranging from 200,000 to 1,000,000+ SF. These buildings are sited near major interstate interchanges, rail yards, or port facilities to minimize transportation costs for tenants moving goods at regional or national scale. Physical specifications are demanding: modern bulk product targets 36–40 ft clear height, dock doors at a ratio of roughly one per 8,000–12,000 SF of floor area, deep truck courts (185–210 ft is a common standard), abundant trailer parking, and wide column spacing (50–54 ft bay depths are typical). Construction is predominantly tilt-up concrete.

Tenants are typically large 3PLs, e-commerce fulfillment operators, national retailers, and food and consumer goods distributors. Lease terms tend to be 7–15 years, often NNN or absolute-net, with annual rent bumps of 2–4%. Single-tenant structures are common, though large parks may house multiple tenants in adjacent or shared facilities. Ownership skews heavily institutional — REITs (Prologis, Duke, EastGroup), pension fund advisors, open-end core funds, and sovereign wealth vehicles are the predominant buyers. Deal sizes are large ($50M–$1B+), transaction velocity is high in core markets, and cap rates vary by market and vintage.

### Last-Mile/Infill Logistics

Last-mile logistics properties are smaller-format (50,000–200,000 SF) facilities located within or immediately adjacent to dense urban population centers. The defining characteristic is proximity — these buildings exist to shorten the final delivery leg to the end consumer. Because land in urban infill locations is scarce and entitlement is slow, last-mile properties often trade at premium rents and compressed cap rates relative to bulk product on a $/SF basis, even though the absolute building size is smaller.

Physical specs are often a step behind bulk (24–30 ft clear is common for older infill stock; newer infill product may reach 32–36 ft where land permits), and truck-court depths may be constrained by adjacent uses. Tenants include parcel carriers, grocery delivery operators, same-day e-commerce services, and urban distributors. Lease terms are often shorter (3–7 years) than bulk, and multi-tenant formats are more common. Investors value supply constraint over physical specs, and many value-add strategies involve converting older urban industrial into functionally competitive last-mile space. See [[Last-Mile Logistics]] for a deeper profile.

### Light Industrial

Light industrial encompasses multi-tenant, smaller-bay properties (typically 5,000–50,000 SF per unit) that serve a diverse range of local tenants: contractors, trade businesses, small distributors, light assemblers, service providers, and neighborhood-scale manufacturers. Buildings are typically 18–28 ft clear, grade-level or dock-high loading depending on vintage and bay size, and often feature a small front-office component per unit. Parking ratios are higher relative to bulk product to accommodate a larger employee base per SF.

Light industrial is attractive to investors seeking income diversification (many tenants, no single rollover wipes out cash flow), lower barriers to entry relative to bulk (smaller deal sizes, broader lending markets), and value-add potential through lease-up, re-tenanting, and minor capital improvements. Rents are quoted NNN on a $/SF/year basis, though recovery structures on older product may be modified gross. Tenants are often small businesses with limited covenant strength, so rent-roll depth and tenant diversification are key underwriting considerations. See [[Light Industrial]] for a deeper profile.

### Flex/R&D

Flex industrial straddles the line between office and warehouse/production space. A typical flex building contains a mix of office (ranging from 10% to 50%+ of SF, depending on submarket) and open warehouse or R&D floor space, often with grade-level roll-up doors in the rear. Flex parks are common in suburban business parks and technology corridors, and tenants include technology firms, biotech/life sciences companies at early stages, defense contractors, media production companies, and service businesses requiring a combination of presentable office and functional back-of-house space.

Flex is sometimes considered a hybrid between office and industrial from an underwriting perspective — vacancy is more sensitive to office market conditions than pure-warehouse product, and TI allowances can be substantial for tenants requiring specialized build-outs. Rents may be structured NNN, modified gross, or gross depending on market convention. Obsolescence risk is meaningful: as life sciences and technology users grow and require purpose-built lab or clean-room space, they tend to graduate out of flex. Deal sizes are typically moderate, and ownership includes local/regional operators alongside smaller institutional vehicles. See [[Flex Industrial]] for a deeper profile.

### Cold Storage

Cold storage (also called refrigerated warehousing or temperature-controlled logistics) refers to facilities maintaining product at controlled temperatures — refrigerated (34–50°F), frozen (-10°F to 32°F), or a combination. End markets include fresh and frozen food distribution, pharmaceutical storage (including cold-chain biologics and vaccines), floral, and specialty chemicals. Buildings are highly specialized: thick insulated wall and roof panels, ammonia or Freon refrigeration systems, dedicated dock seals, concrete or epoxy floors engineered for fork traffic, and robust power infrastructure.

Cold storage commands the highest replacement cost per SF in the industrial universe, and the operational complexity means ownership skews toward specialist operators (Americold, Lineage, US Cold Storage) or build-to-suit structures for large food companies. Investors entering cold storage must underwrite refrigerant compliance costs (EPA regulations on HFCs), energy costs (refrigeration is energy-intensive), and operator expertise. Rents and cap rates vary widely by vintage, location, and temperature specification. See [[Cold Storage]] for a deeper profile.

### Manufacturing

Manufacturing facilities range from light assembly plants to heavy industrial production operations, with physical specifications driven by the tenant's process requirements. Key variables include floor load capacity (measured in pounds per SF), clear height, crane rails and hook height, power supply (amperage, voltage, three-phase availability), gas supply, ventilation and exhaust systems, and rail access. Modern manufacturing tenants — particularly in automotive, EV battery, semiconductor, aerospace, and consumer goods — have highly specific requirements that may make general-purpose industrial product unsuitable without substantial modification.

From an investment perspective, manufacturing facilities carry higher residual risk than bulk logistics — if the single tenant vacates, the re-leasing pool is often thin without expensive re-tooling. Investors mitigate this through long-term [[Build-to-Suit]] leases with credit tenants, [[Sale-Leaseback]] structures that provide immediate income from an established occupant, or value-add acquisition of functionally flexible older manufacturing buildings that can serve multiple light-manufacturing or distribution uses. Incentive programs (CHIPS Act, state enterprise zones, IRA credits) have increased the viability of new manufacturing development in select markets. See [[Manufacturing]] for a deeper profile.

### Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS)

IOS refers to improved or semi-improved land parcels used for outdoor storage of large assets: commercial vehicles, construction equipment, shipping containers, building materials, automotive inventory, and fleet vehicles. Properties typically consist of a secured, paved or compacted-gravel yard with minimal building improvements — a small office structure, security lighting, and perimeter fencing are often the extent of permanent improvements. Despite (or because of) low improvement ratios, IOS has attracted significant institutional attention as a supply-constrained, low-capex asset type with strong cash yields.

The investment thesis centers on land scarcity and zoning barriers: municipalities have tightened industrial zoning codes to restrict outdoor storage uses near residential areas, reducing the available supply of compliant sites in desirable locations. IOS tenants (trucking companies, construction firms, car rental depots, container operators) often sign shorter-term leases (1–5 years), creating mark-to-market rent opportunities. Because improvements are minimal, CapEx requirements are low, and operating expenses (beyond property tax, insurance, and basic maintenance) are limited. Key risks include rezoning or code-enforcement pressure, environmental liability from tenant activities, and the challenge of institutionalizing a historically fragmented ownership base. See [[Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS)]] for a deeper profile.
