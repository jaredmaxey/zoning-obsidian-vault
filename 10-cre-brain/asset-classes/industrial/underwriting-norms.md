# Industrial — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Industrial underwriting is distinguished by its reliance on net lease structures, long-term contractual cash flows, and a relatively lean operating-expense profile. Because tenants typically absorb most or all property-level costs under NNN leases, the landlord's focus is on underwriting lease term, rent level relative to market, rollover risk, and physical functionality of the asset. The analysis shifts toward a bond-like cash-flow model for single-tenant long-lease product, while multi-tenant light industrial and flex properties require a more granular rent-roll and lease-expiration analysis closer to office/retail underwriting conventions.

Key inputs to stress-test include: market rent versus in-place rent (over-/under-leased position), remaining lease term and rollover schedule, tenant credit quality, physical specifications versus current market standards (clear height, dock doors, truck court), and local supply pipeline relative to projected demand. A building that is 5 ft below market clear height may underperform on re-leasing even in a strong market. See [[Industrial — Key Metrics]] for the physical metrics that drive re-leasing competitiveness.

Before finalizing assumptions, analysts should anchor to [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] and [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] from the relevant submarket. Industrial cap rates and rents are highly submarket-specific — the same 200,000 SF building will price very differently in the Inland Empire versus a secondary Midwest market. See [[Assumption Hierarchy: Actual vs. Pro Forma]] for the precedence framework when actual lease data, trailing financials, and market estimates conflict.

## Lease Structures

**Triple-Net (NNN):** The dominant lease structure for industrial. The tenant pays base rent plus real estate taxes, property insurance, and maintenance/operating expenses. Under a pure NNN structure the landlord's only recurring obligation is structural components (roof, foundation, building shell) and major capital expenditures if so negotiated. This structure creates a highly predictable net income stream and simplifies underwriting, as operating expenses pass through to the tenant. NNN leases typically include annual fixed rent bumps (2–4%) or CPI-indexed escalations with caps and floors.

**Absolute-Net (Bondable Net):** A more extreme form of NNN in which the tenant is responsible for all costs including structural repairs and replacements. Common in build-to-suit single-tenant assets with investment-grade credit tenants. The "bondable" characterization reflects the landlord's near-zero ongoing obligation, creating a cash flow stream that can be valued similarly to a long-term bond. Lenders and core investors prize these structures for their predictability.

**Modified Gross / Industrial Gross:** More common in multi-tenant light industrial and flex parks, particularly older product. The landlord may pay certain operating expenses (often property taxes and insurance as a base-year amount, with increases passed through) while the tenant pays utilities and internal maintenance. Underwriters should carefully model which expenses are actually recoverable versus held by the landlord under each lease.

**Rent Bumps and Escalations:** Fixed annual bumps (e.g., 2.5% per year) are the most common escalation structure in industrial leases. CPI-linked escalations are used, often with a floor (e.g., 2%) and cap (e.g., 4%) to bound exposure. Some leases feature periodic fixed-step increases (e.g., 10% every 5 years) rather than annual bumps. Model each lease's escalation structure explicitly when building a rent roll; don't apply a single blended growth rate across a heterogeneous portfolio.

**Weighted Average Lease Term (WALT):** Industrial assets are often evaluated partly on their WALT — a longer WALT (7+ years remaining) supports tighter cap rates and more favorable financing; a shorter WALT (3 years or less) introduces rollover risk that must be priced in via cap-rate expansion, re-leasing downtime assumptions, and TI/LC reserves in the underwriting model.

## OpEx and Recovery Norms

Under a true NNN structure, the landlord's property-level operating expenses are limited to reserves for structural components and items not covered by the tenant lease. In underwriting, analysts should confirm what the actual lease documents say rather than assuming pure NNN; many leases labeled "NNN" contain carve-outs (e.g., landlord pays roof repairs above a certain cost, or structural capital expenditures).

**Recoverable operating expenses** commonly include: property taxes, property insurance, property management fees (for multi-tenant parks), common-area maintenance (CAM) costs, landscaping, parking lot maintenance, and utilities for common areas. In multi-tenant parks, a CAM reconciliation process allocates shared expenses pro-rata to each tenant's leased area.

**Non-recoverable landlord expenses** to model include: structural capital expenditures (roof replacement, HVAC replacement at lease expiration), leasing commissions, TI allowances at rollover, and management costs in excess of what the lease allows for recovery. The [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] note explains how to build these reserve items into the cash flow model.

**Operating expense ratios** for NNN single-tenant industrial are very low from the landlord's perspective (often 5–15% of EGI once recoveries are netted), making NOI margins high relative to other asset classes. Multi-tenant light industrial and flex run higher effective expense ratios (15–30%+) due to greater management intensity, higher TI turnover costs, and more frequent vacancy.
