# Flex Industrial

## Overview

Flex industrial — sometimes called flex space, flex/R&D, or business park space — occupies the product spectrum between pure industrial warehouse and conventional office. A typical flex building contains a mix of office space (ranging from 10% to 50%+ of total SF, depending on tenant and building vintage) and open-floor-plan warehouse, production, or R&D space, often accessible via grade-level roll-up doors at the rear or side. Flex buildings are commonly found in suburban business parks and technology corridors, developed during the 1980s–2000s to house the back-office and operational functions of suburban employers.

The flex category encompasses a diverse tenant base: technology firms, defense contractors and government services companies, biotech and life sciences tenants at early or growth stages, media and content production, light assembly, showroom-and-warehouse operations, and general service businesses that require a combination of client-presentable office space and functional back-of-house. This diversity makes flex a complex underwriting exercise — the functional value of the office component tracks office market conditions, while the warehouse/production component tracks industrial market conditions, and the optimal mix for re-leasing may differ from the existing build-out.

## Physical Profile

Flex buildings are typically single-story or occasionally two-story, with unit sizes ranging from 2,500 to 20,000+ SF. Key physical characteristics:

- **Clear height (warehouse portion):** 12–24 ft clear in older product; newer flex may spec 24–28 ft
- **Office component:** 20–50% of unit SF in most flex formats; front-facing with professional finish
- **Loading:** grade-level roll-up doors in the rear; dock-high loading is less common than in light industrial
- **Parking:** higher ratios than pure warehouse (3.0–5.0 per 1,000 SF total) to accommodate office density
- **HVAC:** climate control throughout, including warehouse/R&D portion in many modern buildings
- **Unit sizes:** highly variable; demisable walls allow reconfiguration for different tenant sizes

Older flex buildings (1980s–1990s vintage) often have deferred maintenance, dated office finishes, and inadequate HVAC for modern life-sciences or data-intensive tenants. Repositioning these buildings to attract technology or R&D tenants may require substantial TI.

## Demand Drivers

Flex demand is a function of suburban employment trends — specifically, the health of small-to-mid-sized businesses in technology, professional services, defense/government contracting, and light manufacturing. Suburban business parks were developed to serve exactly this market, and the segment's fortunes correlate with suburban office absorption and small-business formation.

Life sciences and biotech tenants represent a growth demand source for flex: early-stage companies often need a combination of office and wet-lab or production space that conventional pure-office or pure-industrial buildings cannot accommodate. Proximity to research universities and medical centers, available power infrastructure, and vented exhaust capability are key site requirements for life-sciences flex tenants. Owners successfully repositioning flex toward life sciences can achieve rent premiums and tenant quality improvements.

Defense and government contracting is a stable demand driver in markets with a military or federal agency presence (Northern Virginia, San Diego, Huntsville, Colorado Springs). Government-tenant leases may have different renewal patterns than private-sector leases — often following federal budget cycles — but credit quality is high.

## Deal Profile

Flex is most commonly a value-add or core-plus acquisition target, given that new-construction flex development has been limited relative to pure industrial product. Investors pursuing flex must decide whether their thesis is:

1. **Industrial re-rating:** Repositioning flex parks where warehouse/distribution demand has grown to command market-rate rents that exceed the blended office-industrial going-in cap rate. In strong infill industrial markets, flex parks with good loading infrastructure may re-lease primarily to light industrial or last-mile tenants who value the location more than the office component.

2. **Office stabilization:** Maintaining the flex format and re-leasing at market rents to professional service and technology tenants. Success depends heavily on the health of the suburban office market, which has been challenged in many markets post-pandemic.

3. **Life sciences conversion:** Targeted repositioning of flex buildings in submarkets with life sciences demand. Requires significant TI investment ($50–150/SF or more for wet-lab conversion) and deep submarket knowledge. See [[Life Sciences]] for a related asset-class overview.

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Hybrid market exposure:** Flex underwriting must track both office and industrial market conditions. Vacancy benchmarks, rent comps, and TI norms differ between the office and warehouse components, and the appropriate blended assumptions depend on the building's functional mix and the tenant universe being targeted.

**Obsolescence risk:** Flex buildings age faster functionally than pure industrial, because office finishes, HVAC, and telecommunications infrastructure require periodic reinvestment. A flex building with 1990s finishes competing against newly renovated suburban office or modern light industrial will underperform on leasing velocity and rent. Model realistic capital costs for unit refresh at rollover.

**Tenant diversity and rollover:** Flex parks with many small tenants (2,500–5,000 SF units) have meaningful leasing churn — small businesses have higher failure and relocation rates than institutional-grade industrial tenants. Build vacancy and leasing-cost reserves accordingly.

**[[Highest and Best Use Analysis]]:** In markets where industrial demand has grown strongly, the flex park's land value for pure industrial redevelopment may approach or exceed the going-concern value of the existing improvements. A careful highest-and-best-use analysis should evaluate whether repositioning or redevelopment creates more value than stabilizing the existing flex use.
