# Light Industrial

## Overview

Light industrial refers to multi-tenant or smaller-format industrial properties where individual units typically range from 5,000 to 50,000 SF, serving a diverse base of local business tenants. These properties house contractors (plumbing, electrical, HVAC, landscaping), small distributors, light assemblers, regional food producers, e-commerce fulfillment operations, media and production studios, automotive service, and a wide array of service-business back-of-house operations. Because unit sizes are small and tenants are numerous, light industrial offers an income-diversification profile unavailable in single-tenant bulk warehouse — no single tenant represents a catastrophic rollover event.

Light industrial is often accessible to a broader investor universe than bulk logistics: deal sizes are typically smaller (ranging from a few million dollars for a single small park to $50–100M+ for a sizable multi-park portfolio), financing is available from a wider pool of lenders, and operational management is relatively straightforward. This democratized ownership base means local and regional operators compete directly with institutional capital, and value-add returns are achievable for investors who can improve occupancy, re-lease at market rents, or modernize common-area and loading infrastructure.

## Physical Profile

Light industrial buildings are typically 18–28 ft clear height, with grade-level roll-up doors at the rear or side of each unit (dock-high loading is less universal than in bulk product, though newer multi-tenant parks may include a mix of dock-high and grade positions). Unit front facades often feature a small office and showroom component (500–2,000 SF per unit), with the remainder dedicated to warehouse or production floor space. Column-free clear spans within each bay are important to tenant operational flexibility.

Buildings are often constructed as tilt-up concrete or masonry with metal roofing. Parking ratios are higher than bulk warehouse (2.0–4.0 per 1,000 SF is common) to accommodate the higher employee-per-SF density of light industrial tenants. Common-area improvements are modest — surface parking lots, landscaping buffers, and exterior lighting — keeping CapEx requirements manageable relative to more complex product types.

Multi-tenant parks range from a single building subdivided into multiple bays to large master-planned parks with dozens of buildings and hundreds of units. The economics of park scale are attractive: shared infrastructure, a broader leasing pool, and the ability to move tenants within the park at renewal reduce vacancy risk and improve net effective rents.

## Demand Drivers

Light industrial demand is closely correlated with local economic activity — construction starts (which drive contractor demand for storage space), small-business formation, local retail and food service supply chains, and the general health of the regional employment base. Population growth markets that generate sustained residential construction activity (Phoenix, Dallas, Nashville, Salt Lake City) tend to have deep and durable demand for contractor-and-trade light industrial.

E-commerce growth has added a new tenant category to light industrial: small-scale fulfillment operators (direct-to-consumer brands, Etsy-style manufacturing, regional specialty distributors) that require 5,000–20,000 SF of combined office and warehouse space. This demand source has pushed vacancy lower and rents higher in many light industrial submarkets since 2018, attracting institutional attention to a product type historically dominated by local operators.

## Deal Profile

Light industrial investment most commonly takes one of two forms:

**Core-Plus Stabilized Acquisition:** Purchase of a high-occupancy, well-maintained multi-tenant park with a mix of in-place and rolling leases, targeting a return profile slightly above core. Underwriting focuses on lease-expiration schedule, in-place versus market rent, tenant credit quality, deferred maintenance, and local competitive supply. See [[Core-Plus Acquisition]].

**Value-Add Repositioning:** Acquisition of a partially vacant or below-market-leased park, with a plan to renovate units, improve common areas, re-tenant at market rents, and exit at a stabilized cap rate once the business plan is executed. The value-add delta is the spread between the going-in yield (on below-market NOI) and the stabilized cap rate applied to a higher NOI. See [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Lease expiration laddering:** A concentration of lease expirations in a single year can create a lumpy cash-flow event if re-leasing demand softens. Model each lease's expiration date, renewal probability, and market rent assumption individually rather than applying a blended vacancy rate. [[Rent Roll Analysis]] is the core analytical tool.

**Tenant credit and diversification:** Light industrial tenants are often small businesses with limited financial covenant strength. Review available financial information, assess business longevity and industry trends, and evaluate the diversification of the tenant base across industries. Avoid portfolios where a single tenant represents more than 20–25% of revenue.

**TI and leasing cost reserves:** Light industrial has lower TI costs than office or flex but still requires capital at rollover (paint, dock leveler servicing, unit improvements for new tenants). Build TI ($3–10/SF for new leases) and leasing commission reserves into the underwriting model.

**Deferred maintenance:** Older light industrial parks often have deferred maintenance on roofs, parking lots, dock equipment, and HVAC units. A thorough property condition assessment during due diligence is essential. [[CapEx Planning]] models this capital requirement over the hold period.

**Operating expenses:** Multi-tenant light industrial is more operationally intensive than single-tenant NNN product. [[Property Management Fundamentals]] and [[Operating Expense Ratio]] analysis should confirm that expense recoveries under existing leases are consistent with actual costs, and that any above-market or below-market recovery structures are modeled accurately in the pro forma.
