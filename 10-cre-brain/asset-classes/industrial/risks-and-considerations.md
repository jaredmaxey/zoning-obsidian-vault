# Industrial — Risks & Considerations

Industrial real estate benefits from strong structural demand tailwinds and lean operating profiles, but it is not without meaningful risk. Risks are amplified by concentration (single-tenant assets), subtype complexity (cold storage, manufacturing), and the speed at which speculative supply can materialize in growth markets. A rigorous risk framework should examine market-level dynamics, operational execution, capital structure and financing conditions, and the physical asset's durability and functional relevance.

### Market

**Demand Cyclicality:** While structural e-commerce demand provides a long-term floor, industrial absorption is not immune to economic cycles. Manufacturing output, trade volumes, and retail sales — the primary drivers of logistics demand — contract during recessions, driving up vacancy and softening rent growth. Tenants may seek to sublease excess space during downturns, adding shadow supply to the market even when no new construction occurs. Underwriting models should apply [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]] to test vacancy and rent-growth assumptions across multiple scenarios.

**Supply Waves in Growth Markets:** Fast entitlement timelines and abundant land in markets like Phoenix, Dallas, Las Vegas, and Indianapolis allow speculative warehouse supply to materialize quickly. When demand softens or misses projections, supply-heavy markets can absorb poorly. Investors underwriting ground-up development or heavy value-add in supply-prone markets must rigorously model the [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] and test assumptions against delayed absorption scenarios. The spread between [[Yield on Cost]] and market cap rates can narrow rapidly if new supply compresses rents.

**E-Commerce Penetration Plateauing:** The extraordinary demand pull from rapid e-commerce growth may moderate as penetration rates stabilize in mature product categories. Additionally, advances in robotic fulfillment and automation allow tenants to process greater volumes per SF over time, potentially reducing their incremental space needs per unit of revenue growth. Long-term demand forecasts should be held to a conservative baseline rather than extrapolating recent-cycle growth rates.

**Submarket Selection Risk:** Industrial values and rent trends are highly submarket-specific. A portfolio of secondary-market assets with limited transportation infrastructure, thin tenant demand, and fragmented ownership is far more vulnerable to a downturn than infill coastal assets. Mitigant: anchor assumptions to [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] and [[Demand Driver Analysis]] from a defined submarket, not national trends.

### Operational

**Single-Tenant Rollover Risk:** Single-tenant industrial assets (a common format in bulk warehouse and build-to-suit manufacturing) concentrate all income risk in one lease event. If the tenant vacates at expiration, the property produces zero income while the owner carries debt service, property taxes, and carrying costs. Re-leasing a large industrial building can take 6–24+ months in softer markets, and the replacement tenant may require significant TI and concessions. Mitigant: underwrite weighted average lease term ([[Rent Roll Analysis]]), model realistic downtime at rollover, reserve for TI/LC, and ensure sufficient DSCR headroom at the financing level.

**Functional Obsolescence:** Buildings with clear heights below current market standards (e.g., 18–24 ft clear in a market where modern tenants require 36 ft+), inadequate dock-door ratios, shallow truck courts, or insufficient power capacity may be unable to attract or retain competitive tenants without substantial capital investment. As tenant specifications rise with e-commerce fulfillment and automation requirements, the gap between functional and obsolete product widens. Mitigant: model a discount to market rent for functionally inferior assets, budget realistic capital expenditures for improvements, and evaluate whether the cost to cure exceeds the building's residual land value (see [[Replacement Cost]] analysis).

**Cold Storage Operational Complexity:** Cold storage assets require specialized operators and carry unique risks: refrigerant system failures can damage or destroy tenant inventory; ammonia systems carry explosion and toxic-release risk; energy costs are high and variable; and maintenance of insulated envelopes, dock seals, and refrigeration equipment requires specialized expertise. Investors without cold-storage operating experience should partner with a specialist operator before underwriting cold-chain assets.

**Tenant Credit Concentration:** Small multi-tenant light industrial and flex properties often house tenants with limited financial covenant strength — sole proprietors, small contractors, and local businesses. A cluster of lease expirations or business failures in a small park can cause significant income disruption. Mitigant: spread lease expirations across the calendar, monitor tenant credit signals during asset management, and build collection reserves into underwriting.

### Capital & Financing

**Cap Rate Expansion Risk:** Industrial cap rates compressed dramatically from 2015 through 2022, reaching historically tight levels in core markets. As interest rates rose beginning in 2022, many industrial investors faced mark-to-market losses on recently acquired assets. Cap rate expansion between acquisition and disposition directly reduces residual values and IRR — underwriters should model exit caps with a realistic spread over going-in cap (see [[Exit Cap Rate]] and underwriting norms in [[Industrial — Underwriting Norms]]) and stress-test scenarios where exit caps are 50–150 bps wider than the base case.

**Debt Market Conditions:** Large single-tenant industrial assets and ground-up development projects are sensitive to CMBS and life-company lending conditions. When credit spreads widen or lenders tighten LTV covenants, refinancing risk materializes — particularly for assets acquired with short-term bridge financing that must be recapitalized at stabilization. See [[Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)]] and [[Loan-to-Value (LTV)]] for underwriting the debt stack against stressed NOI and value scenarios.

**Construction Cost Volatility:** Ground-up industrial development is exposed to hard-cost fluctuations (steel, concrete, labor) that affect feasibility. A pro forma underwritten with a target yield on cost may not pencil if construction costs rise 15–20% from initial budget. Mitigant: use GMP or fixed-price contracts where possible, build adequate contingency, and confirm that the development spread over market cap remains adequate before committing to land.

**IOS Financing Challenges:** Industrial Outdoor Storage assets have historically been difficult to finance conventionally — lenders are unfamiliar with the asset type, minimal improvements create low collateral value relative to land, and institutional lending programs for IOS are still developing. As the asset type institutionalizes, lending conditions are improving, but underwriters should expect higher equity requirements and more conservative LTV covenants than for conventional industrial buildings.

### Structural & Physical

**Deferred Maintenance and Roof Risk:** Industrial buildings have large roof surface areas. A failed or near-end-of-life roof is a significant capital liability — replacement costs for a 500,000 SF building can run $3–8M or more, depending on system type. Under NNN leases, tenants are typically responsible for routine maintenance but not full structural replacement, making this a landlord expense. Underwriters should confirm roof age and condition during due diligence, reserve accordingly, and scrutinize lease language on structural repair obligations.

**Environmental Contamination:** Industrial sites often have prior industrial uses that may have caused soil or groundwater contamination. Prior tenants may have stored chemicals, petroleum products, solvents, or other hazardous materials that released into the ground. A Phase I Environmental Site Assessment is mandatory for acquisitions; a Phase II may be required where recognized environmental conditions (RECs) are identified. Contamination can be expensive to remediate and can result in regulatory liability, delayed closings, or title insurance complications. See [[Industrial — Regulatory Considerations]] for the Phase I/II framework.

**Seismic and Natural Hazard Exposure:** Large-footprint tilt-up concrete buildings in seismically active markets (Southern California, Pacific Northwest) must be evaluated against current seismic standards. Older tilt-up construction built before modern seismic codes may require expensive retrofits. Similarly, industrial assets in flood plains, hurricane corridors, or wildfire-risk zones require careful insurance underwriting and may face increasing coverage costs.

**Fire and Life Safety:** Warehouses storing commodity goods, plastics, aerosols, flammable liquids, or lithium-ion batteries have elevated fire risk and may require specialized sprinkler systems (ESFR — Early Suppression Fast Response — or in-rack suppression), fire-wall separations, and excess hose-valve arrangements. Ensuring the building's fire-suppression system is adequate for the tenant's actual commodity class is a critical lease-compliance and insurance issue. Changes in tenant commodity mix during a lease term can create compliance gaps.
