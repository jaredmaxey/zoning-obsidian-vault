# Industrial

## Overview

Industrial real estate encompasses all income-producing properties designed for the storage, distribution, manufacturing, or light-assembly of goods. The sector spans a wide spectrum — from multi-million-square-foot bulk distribution centers serving national retailers to sub-50,000 SF urban infill units catering to last-mile delivery and light manufacturing tenants. Institutionally, industrial has re-rated from a secondary asset class to a core holding over the past decade, driven by the structural tailwinds of e-commerce growth, supply-chain reconfiguration, and nearshoring of manufacturing closer to end consumers.

Industrial properties typically operate under net lease structures (NNN or absolute net), providing landlords with predictable income and limited operating-expense exposure. Leases tend to be longer-term (5–15 years for bulk; 3–7 years for light industrial and flex), and major tenants include third-party logistics providers (3PLs), e-commerce fulfillment operators, food-and-beverage distributors, building-materials companies, and automotive parts suppliers. The sector's operating simplicity, durable cash flows, and favorable supply-demand dynamics in core logistics corridors have attracted significant institutional and foreign capital.

Current institutional sentiment is broadly positive but increasingly bifurcated by subtype and geography. Bulk logistics in established coastal and inland-empire markets remain in high demand but face scrutiny around escalating land costs and community opposition to large-format development. Last-mile infill product commands premium rents and tight cap rates where supply is genuinely constrained. Cold storage and manufacturing attract specialized buyers willing to underwrite complexity premiums. Investors are closely watching nearshoring trends (particularly US-Mexico border logistics), power-infrastructure availability for advanced manufacturing and data-adjacent industrial, and the pace at which e-commerce penetration continues to drive incremental square-footage demand.

## Subtypes

Industrial encompasses several distinct subtypes, each with its own physical profile, tenant base, and investment characteristics. See [[Industrial — Subtypes]] for full profiles. Key subtypes include:

- **[[Bulk Warehouse]]** — Large-format (200,000–1,000,000+ SF) distribution and fulfillment facilities sited near interstates and port infrastructure.
- **[[Last-Mile Logistics]]** — Infill locations (50,000–200,000 SF) in or near dense urban cores optimized for rapid last-mile delivery.
- **[[Light Industrial]]** — Multi-tenant, smaller-bay (5,000–50,000 SF) properties serving local service, light manufacturing, and distribution tenants.
- **[[Flex Industrial]]** — Hybrid product with a mix of office and warehouse/R&D space, often in suburban business parks.
- **[[Cold Storage]]** — Temperature-controlled facilities for food, pharmaceutical, and perishables; high replacement cost and specialized operators.
- **[[Manufacturing]]** — Purpose-built or adaptable facilities for production operations; heavy power and specialized infrastructure.
- **[[Industrial Outdoor Storage (IOS)]]** — Improved or semi-improved land parcels used for storage of vehicles, equipment, containers, and building materials.

## Demand Drivers

Industrial demand is principally driven by goods movement volumes — themselves a function of population growth, consumer spending, and trade flows. The structural shift toward e-commerce has been the single most powerful demand driver of the past decade; estimates vary, but e-commerce requires roughly two to three times the warehouse square footage per dollar of sales compared to traditional brick-and-mortar retail fulfillment. Leading demand indicators to watch include retail sales data, import container volumes at major ports, PMI/manufacturing output indices, trucking tonnage, and e-commerce penetration rates.

Supply-chain resiliency strategies adopted post-pandemic — including inventory buffering, nearshoring, and friend-shoring — have added structural demand above prior-cycle baselines. Nearshoring of manufacturing activity to Mexico and the US Sun Belt is driving demand for cross-border logistics, assembly, and light manufacturing space, particularly along the I-35 and US-Mexico border corridors. Population migration to Sun Belt metros (Phoenix, Dallas-Fort Worth, Atlanta, Nashville) has extended the footprint of viable last-mile delivery zones, opening formerly secondary markets to institutional-quality logistics investment.

Cold storage demand is driven by grocery delivery penetration, pharmaceutical distribution, and food-safety regulations requiring temperature control at more points in the supply chain. Manufacturing demand tracks capital investment cycles, reshoring incentive programs (CHIPS Act, IRA), and sector-specific growth in electric vehicles, battery production, and semiconductor fabrication.

## Supply Dynamics

Industrial development has historically been faster and less entitlement-intensive than other asset classes, though this varies considerably by geography. In growth markets with available land (inland Empire, Phoenix, Dallas, Chicago O'Hare), bulk warehouse development cycles are relatively short (18–30 months from land to delivery), and speculative supply can accumulate quickly during up-cycles. In infill urban markets, entitlement timelines, environmental remediation requirements, land scarcity, and community opposition create meaningful barriers to entry, supporting rent growth and tighter cap rates for well-located last-mile product.

Supply pipelines should be evaluated relative to net absorption — a market with strong absolute deliveries but equally robust demand absorption can sustain healthy fundamentals, while a secondary market with modest deliveries but weaker demand can tip into oversupply. Key supply barriers to assess include land availability and cost, local entitlement timelines, truck-traffic and noise opposition from adjacent uses, infrastructure capacity (power, water for cold storage), and environmental constraints (prior industrial uses may require Phase I/II studies and remediation).

Developers and investors also track construction cost cycles carefully, as hard-cost volatility directly affects the feasibility of speculative development and the Yield-on-Cost spread over market cap rates. See [[Development Spread]] and [[Yield-on-Cost vs. Market Cap Spread]] for underwriting frameworks.

## Underwriting Norms

Industrial properties are typically underwritten on a NNN or absolute-net lease basis, where tenants pay base rent plus all or most operating expenses. Rents are quoted as $/SF/year (NNN), and annual rent bumps of 2–4% are common in longer leases. See [[Industrial — Underwriting Norms]] for detailed guidance, lease-structure notes, and OpEx/recovery conventions.

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Going-in cap rate | 4.5% – 7.0% | Varies widely: coastal infill tighter, secondary/tertiary wider; varies by market and vintage |
| Stabilized vacancy | 3% – 8% | Bulk/logistics markets tighter in cycle peaks; flex/light industrial wider |
| NNN recovery ratio | 85% – 100% | Higher for newer single-tenant product; older multi-tenant product may not recover all OpEx |
| Rent growth (annual) | 2% – 6% | Varies by market, lease vintage, and supply-demand balance |
| TI allowance (new lease) | $0 – $15/SF | Bulk/big-box often minimal; flex/light industrial higher per SF |
| Exit cap spread to going-in | +25 bps – +75 bps | Depends on hold period, lease term remaining, and market direction |

*Ranges current as of 2026-05-24. Verify against current market data before underwriting.*

## Key Operating Metrics

Industrial properties are evaluated on a set of physical and financial metrics unique to the class. See [[Industrial — Key Metrics]] for full definitions and formulas.

- **Clear height** — The usable interior ceiling height (in feet) from finished floor to the lowest obstruction; modern bulk warehouses typically specify 36–40 ft clear; older product may be 18–28 ft.
- **Dock-door ratio** — Number of dock-high loading doors per square foot of warehouse space; a critical throughput metric for logistics tenants.
- **Truck court depth** — The distance between the dock face and the opposite boundary (trailer parking, building, or fence); determines whether tractor-trailers can maneuver safely.
- **Trailer/auto parking** — Total stalls on-site, separated by trailer (spotted trailers awaiting loading) and automobile (employee/visitor) parking.
- **$/SF NNN rent** — Annualized base rent per square foot on a triple-net basis; the primary income metric.
- **Cubic utilization** — Ratio of usable cubic storage volume to total building volume; emphasizes clear height and floor-plate efficiency.
- **Power capacity** — Critical for cold storage (refrigeration loads), advanced manufacturing, and EV/battery operations.

## Common Deal Structures

Single-tenant industrial is frequently structured as triple-net (NNN) or absolute-net leases, with the tenant responsible for taxes, insurance, and maintenance (and in absolute-net, structural repairs). Institutional buyers prefer longer lease terms (7–15+ years) on single-tenant assets to minimize rollover risk and support financing. Multi-tenant light industrial and flex parks are more operationally intensive but provide income diversification across a larger tenant roster.

Common deal types include [[Build-to-Suit]] for credit tenants requiring custom specifications, [[Sale-Leaseback]] transactions where an owner-occupier monetizes real estate while retaining occupancy, and [[Value-Add Acquisition]] of older product requiring capital improvements to bring rents and physical specifications to market. JV structures pairing a developer/operator with institutional equity are standard for ground-up development, with promotes and preferred returns structured per [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]]. Financing typically utilizes [[CMBS]], [[Life Company Debt]], or [[Bank Debt]] for stabilized assets, and [[Construction Loan]] plus [[Bridge Loan]] for development and lease-up.

## Major Risks

Industrial's primary risks are examined in full in [[Industrial — Risks & Considerations]]. Key headline risks include:

- **Functional obsolescence** — Older product with low clear height, inadequate dock doors, or insufficient trailer parking may lose competitiveness as tenant specifications rise.
- **Single-tenant rollover** — A single vacancy in a large single-tenant asset can eliminate all income; rollover timing and replacement-tenant depth require careful underwriting.
- **Supply waves** — Fast entitlement timelines in growth markets allow rapid speculative supply response; lease-up assumptions must be stress-tested against supply-pipeline scenarios.
- **E-commerce structural shifts** — Tenants adjusting footprint based on automation (robotic fulfillment) may require fewer SF per unit of throughput over time.
- **Cap rate expansion** — Interest rate volatility directly affects industrial cap rates given the sector's prior aggressive compression.

## Regulatory Considerations

Industrial development and ownership face a distinct set of regulatory considerations addressed in [[Industrial — Regulatory Considerations]], including industrial zoning and use conflicts, truck-traffic mitigation requirements, environmental contamination risk (Phase I/II), cold-storage refrigerant compliance, fire and hazmat codes for tenant uses, and municipal crackdowns on Industrial Outdoor Storage in residential-adjacent areas.

## Notable Markets

Major established industrial markets in the US include the Inland Empire (Southern California), Chicago/I-55 corridor, Dallas-Fort Worth, New Jersey/I-287 corridor, Atlanta, and the Pennsylvania I-78/81 logistics corridor. Growth markets gaining institutional traction include Phoenix, Las Vegas, Nashville, Memphis, Indianapolis, and the I-35 corridor from Laredo to Kansas City. Infill last-mile markets command their own rent-premium logic in New York/New Jersey, South Florida, Seattle, and the San Francisco Bay Area, where land scarcity is severe.
