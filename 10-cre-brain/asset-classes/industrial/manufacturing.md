# Manufacturing

## Overview

Manufacturing facilities are industrial buildings designed or adapted to support production operations — the transformation of raw materials, components, or sub-assemblies into finished goods. Unlike distribution warehouses focused on goods movement and storage, manufacturing facilities must accommodate the specific machinery, process flows, utility requirements, and worker density of a production operation. This makes them among the most tenant-specific, and therefore most complex, industrial asset types to own and underwrite.

Manufacturing real estate spans a vast range of industries and scales: automotive and EV assembly plants requiring millions of SF with rail access and overhead crane infrastructure; semiconductor and electronics fabrication facilities with ultra-clean HVAC and vibration-isolation systems; food and beverage production plants with specialized drainage, sanitation, and refrigeration; aerospace and defense manufacturing requiring large clear spans and precision environmental control; and light assembly operations that could operate effectively in a standard multi-tenant light industrial building with modest modifications. This spectrum means that "manufacturing real estate" is not a homogeneous asset class — physical requirements, tenant profiles, and investment characteristics differ dramatically across sectors.

Institutional investment in manufacturing real estate has historically been more limited than in logistics, due to the asset-specificity risk: a building purpose-built for semiconductor fabrication is nearly impossible to re-let to an alternative user without a complete gut renovation. However, several trends are driving renewed investor interest: domestic reshoring of strategic manufacturing (CHIPS Act, IRA manufacturing tax credits), EV and battery production investment in the Sun Belt, and defense spending on advanced manufacturing facilities. These drivers create opportunities for long-term [[Build-to-Suit]] and [[Sale-Leaseback]] transactions with creditworthy tenants.

## Physical Profile

Manufacturing facility specifications vary enormously by industry sector and process requirements. Key variables include:

- **Clear height:** Highly variable — from 16 ft for light assembly to 50+ ft for aerospace or heavy industrial operations requiring crane access
- **Floor load capacity:** Measured in pounds per SF; standard warehouse slabs support 5,000–7,500 psf; heavy manufacturing may require 10,000–30,000+ psf; crane rails require independent structural footings
- **Crane infrastructure:** Overhead bridge cranes (measured by ton capacity) are required for heavy manufacturing, turbine assembly, and shipbuilding; rails must be embedded in the structural steel, not retrofitted
- **Power:** Three-phase power at high amperage and voltage (480V or higher for heavy industrial); gas supply for furnaces, dryers, and CNG vehicle fueling; backup generator capacity for critical processes
- **HVAC and ventilation:** Process fume extraction, explosion-proof ratings for solvent environments, clean-room environments for semiconductor and pharmaceutical manufacturing
- **Rail access:** Class I or short-line rail spur required for heavy manufacturing operations with large inbound raw-material or outbound finished-goods volumes
- **Drainage:** Industrial floor drains with appropriate wastewater pretreatment for food/beverage production, metal finishing, and chemical manufacturing

## Demand Drivers

Manufacturing real estate demand tracks capital investment cycles in specific industrial sectors. Key current demand drivers as of 2026 include:

- **Semiconductor and advanced electronics:** CHIPS Act incentives are driving significant greenfield and brownfield semiconductor fab investment in the US; these facilities have extremely specialized requirements (Class 1–100 clean rooms, ultra-pure water, vibration isolation, massive power loads) and are almost universally build-to-suit by the occupying company
- **EV and battery manufacturing:** Domestic battery cell, module, and pack production investment has driven large-format manufacturing demand in the Sun Belt (Georgia, Tennessee, Kentucky, Arizona); these plants are large (1M–3M+ SF), highly customized, and typically developed as long-term BTS arrangements
- **Aerospace and defense:** Stable government-driven demand in markets with established defense manufacturing bases (Southern California, Texas, Washington State, Connecticut)
- **Food and beverage:** Ongoing consolidation and modernization of food production drives periodic large-plant investments; often structured as SLB from existing owner-occupiers
- **Nearshoring to US-Mexico border:** Light assembly and sub-assembly operations migrating from Asia or deeper Mexico are creating demand for cross-border industrial and manufacturing space in markets along I-35 and the border corridor

## Deal Profile

**Build-to-Suit:** The dominant structure for new manufacturing real estate. A developer or real estate partner builds a facility customized to the tenant's process requirements, with a long-term lease (typically 15–25 years) providing the income stream that supports the development cost. The investor is essentially buying a long-duration net-leased bond backed by the tenant's credit covenant. The key risk is residual value — what is the facility worth at lease expiration if the tenant does not renew and re-purpose requires major capital expenditure? See [[Build-to-Suit]].

**Sale-Leaseback:** An owner-occupying manufacturer monetizes its real estate to free capital for operations, equipment, or growth, selling to an investor and leasing back under NNN terms. The buyer acquires an in-place income stream from a known occupant with an established operating history at the site. Due diligence must evaluate environmental history (manufacturing sites are among the highest-contamination-risk industrial properties), lease structure, and the tenant's financial strength. See [[Sale-Leaseback]].

**Value-Add Repositioning of Vacated Manufacturing:** Acquisitions of vacated or underutilized manufacturing plants at a discount to replacement cost, with a plan to re-tenant for an alternative manufacturing or heavy industrial use, or to redevelop the site for a less-specialized use (conversion to light industrial, distribution, or flex). This strategy requires careful diligence on environmental condition, structural integrity, and the cost to modernize utilities and systems.

## Key Underwriting Considerations

**Asset-specificity and residual value:** The single greatest risk in manufacturing real estate investment is tenant-specific customization that cannot be recouped if the tenant vacates. Model the residual value at lease expiration explicitly under three scenarios: renewal, re-lease to an alternative manufacturing tenant, and conversion to a less-specialized use. Discount the purchase price from scenarios that require expensive retrofit to achieve re-leasing.

**Environmental diligence:** Manufacturing sites are among the highest-risk categories for environmental contamination. Mandatory Phase I ESA and Phase II where RECs are identified. Confirm the status of any existing regulatory agreements, consent orders, or remediation in progress before closing.

**Utility and infrastructure capacity:** Confirm that available power, gas, water, and wastewater capacity at the site are sufficient not only for the current tenant's operations but for the most likely alternative use scenarios. Utility upgrade costs can be substantial and long-lead.

**Incentive dependency:** Some manufacturing investments are underwritten assuming continuation of state or federal incentive programs (enterprise zone credits, CHIPS grants, IRA manufacturing tax credits). Model the base-case return without incentives to ensure the investment is viable on its own merits, and treat incentives as upside.
