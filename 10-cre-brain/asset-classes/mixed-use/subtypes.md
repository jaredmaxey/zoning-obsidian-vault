# Mixed-Use — Subtypes

Mixed-use projects are categorized by how uses are physically organized — stacked vertically within a single structure or distributed horizontally across a multi-building campus — and by the programmatic relationship between uses. The five principal subtypes below reflect the dominant development and investment formats encountered in institutional practice. Physical format governs construction type, financing structure, regulatory pathway, and operational complexity; matching subtype to market conditions and capital structure is a primary feasibility determinant.

### Vertical Mixed-Use

Vertical mixed-use stacks two or more distinct uses in a single building, most commonly with activated ground-floor retail or lobby uses and residential or office occupants on upper floors. The format is the logical product of high land costs and infill density requirements — stacking uses on the same footprint maximizes FAR utilization and captures value from multiple income streams. Projects typically involve ground-floor retail (1–2 stories) beneath residential floors (3–12+ stories), with structured parking integrated into a podium or basement. Construction type is typically Type III-A, Type I, or Type V podium depending on height; Type V wood-frame over a concrete podium (the "five-over-one" or "four-plus-one") is the dominant mid-rise form in most U.S. markets.

**Deal profile:** New ground-up development in infill urban or TOD locations; total project cost $20M–$200M+ depending on scale; ground-floor retail represents 5–20% of rentable area. Pro forma requires component-level underwriting — residential by the multifamily methodology, retail by NNN/modified-gross lease analysis — with blended cap rate and yield-on-cost targets that reflect the combined risk. Stabilization periods are longer than single-use residential because ground-floor retail lease-up typically lags residential.

**Typical owners:** Urban developer/operators with mixed-use track records; institutional core-plus and value-add funds; municipal or nonprofit developers in affordable/workforce TOD contexts; LIHTC developers incorporating ground-floor activation requirements.

### Horizontal / Town-Center

Horizontal mixed-use distributes uses across multiple buildings within a walkable, interconnected master plan. Retail streets or a central plaza anchor the ground plane; residential buildings (typically multifamily or for-sale condominium) ring the retail core; office pads, hotel, or civic uses may be integrated. The format allows phased development — individual buildings can be financed and delivered independently — and reduces the operational complexity of managing multiple uses in a single structure. It is the dominant form for large-scale infill redevelopment (legacy mall conversion, master-planned suburban town centers, mixed-use transit villages).

**Deal profile:** Larger land assemblages (5–50+ acres); master-developer entity often controls land and ground-leases or sells pads to component builders; total capitalization can reach $100M–$1B+ across phases. Individual phase deals are sized comparably to standalone residential or retail projects. Phasing risk is significant — if later retail or residential phases underperform, the placemaking premise of the project is undermined and early-phase asset values can suffer.

**Typical owners:** Large-scale private developers; REITs with mixed-use platforms (Federal Realty, Brookfield Properties); institutional opportunity funds; public-private partnerships (municipality provides land or infrastructure; private developer executes building program).

### Retail-Residential Podium

See [[Retail-Residential Podium]] for a full standalone note. The podium subtype is the most prevalent institutional mixed-use form in mid-rise and high-rise urban markets. A concrete podium of 1–5 stories contains ground-floor retail, structured parking, and building amenity space; a wood-frame or concrete residential tower or wrap sits on top. The podium allows residential construction to "float" above grade, improving unit views and separation from street-level activity while incorporating structured parking within the building envelope.

**Deal profile:** Predominantly ground-up development; construction cost varies by market and height but podium concrete adds meaningful cost versus pure stick-frame. Retail component is typically 5,000–30,000 SF of ground-floor inline or end-cap space. Financing typically via a single [[Construction Loan]] with residential and retail treated as separate components for underwriting; takeout financing may split along component lines.

**Typical owners:** Multifamily developers and operators with mixed-use experience; institutional multifamily funds; public developers in inclusionary or TOD contexts.

### Live-Work

Live-work units blend a permitted residential dwelling with an allowable commercial or studio component in the same unit, typically ground-floor or mezzanine. They originated in adaptive reuse of loft industrial buildings in urban creative districts (SoHo model), where artists and craftspeople needed production space attached to living quarters. Contemporary live-work development has broadened to include home-based professional services, artisanal production, and creative studio uses; zoning definitions and permitted commercial activities vary significantly by jurisdiction. See [[Live-Work Units]] for full treatment.

**Deal profile:** Smaller-scale projects (10–80 units) in artisan or creative-industry districts; often adaptive reuse of historic industrial buildings. Rents reflect both residential and commercial utility — typically modest premium over comparable residential in the immediate trade area. Ownership is often small private operators or nonprofit housing developers; institutional capital is less common except in market-rate adaptive reuse transactions.

**Typical owners:** Small private developers; creative-industry community development organizations; adaptive reuse specialists; individual condo buyers in converted loft buildings.

### Mixed-Use TOD

Transit-oriented development (TOD) mixed-use organizes residential, retail, and often office uses around a transit node — light rail, commuter rail, BRT station, or ferry terminal. TOD is less a physical form than a location and regulatory designation: projects are typically within a quarter-mile to half-mile walk of the transit stop, comply with TOD overlay zoning (often featuring relaxed parking minimums, bonus density, and affordable housing set-asides), and may involve public-private partnerships with the transit agency or municipality. The programmatic mix is similar to vertical mixed-use or podium formats, but the TOD context adds regulatory dimensions, financing sources (transit agency land contributions, tax-increment financing, federal transit grants), and demand drivers tied to ridership.

**Deal profile:** Development or adaptive reuse adjacent to rail or BRT; may involve ground lease of transit agency-owned land; typical inclusion of affordable housing component (10–20% of units); financing often layered with public sources (TIF, HUD, LIHTC). Underwriting must account for transit-ridership-driven retail demand and the unique parking demand profile (reduced auto-dependency). See [[Planned Unit Development (PUD)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], and [[Mixed-Use — Regulatory Considerations]] for regulatory pathway.

**Typical owners:** Public-private joint ventures; affordable housing developers with TOD experience; transit agency development subsidiaries; institutional funds with ESG mandates; major urban developers (Trammel Crow, Related, Greystar) with TOD track records.
