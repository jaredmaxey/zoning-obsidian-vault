# Climate-Controlled vs. Non-Climate

The single most consequential product decision in self-storage development and underwriting is the mix of climate-controlled (CC) versus non-climate (NC) units. The choice has significant implications for construction cost, street rate potential, operating expense, customer demand profile, competitive positioning, and exit valuation. For acquisitions, the existing product mix is a fixed starting point, but conversion of NC to CC is a common value-add strategy that warrants careful analysis.

## Demand and Customer Profile

Climate-controlled units serve customers whose stored goods are sensitive to temperature and humidity: electronics, documents, artwork, wine, clothing, furniture with wood or leather components, and pharmaceutical or food products. The CC demand base skews toward urban and densely populated suburban markets where residents lack alternative climate-controlled storage space (no garage, no basement) and where the goods stored tend to be of higher value. In Sunbelt markets, extreme summer heat makes CC functionally necessary for a wide range of customer goods even where the customer might otherwise accept a drive-up unit.

Non-climate units serve customers whose goods are relatively insensitive to temperature variation: landscaping and contractor equipment, seasonal gear, vehicles, boxes of non-sensitive items, and business inventory that is not heat-sensitive. NC demand is more geographically distributed and more robust in northern markets where extreme heat is not a year-round concern. NC units also serve larger-size customers (20×30, 10×40) who need the oversized dimensions and drive-up access that NC drive-up formats provide.

The practical demand implication for underwriting: CC-heavy facilities in markets with constrained indoor residential storage space (urban, Sunbelt) tend to achieve higher physical occupancy at higher rent per square foot. NC-heavy facilities in markets with ample competition or lower density may achieve similar physical occupancy but at materially lower rent per square foot.

## Construction Economics

Non-climate, single-story drive-up is the least expensive self-storage product to build. The basic structural system is a simple steel building with concrete slab; roll-up doors, minimal electrical, basic security cameras, and a simple gate system round out the capital requirements. Construction cost varies by location, labor market, and site conditions, but single-story NC is meaningfully cheaper per NRSF than any other storage format.

Climate-controlled units require: (1) an insulated building envelope — typically a conditioned interior with insulated walls and ceilings; (2) HVAC systems sized for the building's climate load; and (3) in multi-story configurations, elevator shafts and elevator equipment. These additions can increase construction cost per NRSF by a significant margin relative to single-story non-climate product, with multi-story urban CC at the high end. The incremental capital cost of CC must be justified by the incremental rent premium and occupancy benefit; yield-on-cost analysis comparing CC vs. NC should be central to any development feasibility evaluation. See [[Yield on Cost]].

Operating costs are also higher for CC facilities. HVAC energy consumption is the dominant variable — a well-insulated CC facility in a moderate climate may carry manageable utility costs, while a poorly insulated facility in Phoenix or Houston can see energy costs that are multiples of a comparable NC facility. Building mechanical maintenance (HVAC systems, filters, condensate drainage) adds to the operating expense relative to NC. These operating cost differentials must be modeled explicitly in the pro forma rather than assumed away.

## Rent Premium and NOI Impact

Climate-controlled units command a rent premium over comparable non-climate units at the same facility. The size of this premium varies by market, unit size, and competitive environment, but is generally meaningful — CC units in supply-constrained urban markets can command premiums of 20%–50% or more per square foot over comparable NC product. In more competitive or rural markets, the premium may be narrower.

The NOI implication is that a CC-heavy facility mix, assuming the higher construction and operating costs are offset by higher rents, can generate higher NOI per NRSF than a comparable NC facility. This is why institutional investors and REITs have generally preferred CC-heavy or mixed facilities in their acquisition targets, and why CC-heavy assets consistently trade at tighter cap rates than NC-dominant properties.

## Conversion: NC to CC

Converting existing non-climate drive-up units to climate-controlled interior units is a common value-add strategy for aging suburban single-story facilities. The conversion typically involves enclosing an existing drive-up building bay, adding insulation and HVAC, constructing interior corridors, and subdividing large bays into smaller interior units. The key economic drivers:

- **Incremental capital cost** — Varies widely based on building condition, layout, and local construction costs. Converting a drive-up bay to interior CC space is not trivial; structural limitations may prevent conversion of some buildings.
- **Incremental rent uplift** — CC rents per square foot significantly exceed NC rents; the conversion generates new revenue on what may have been below-market or vacant NC space.
- **Yield on cost** — The test for conversion viability is whether the incremental NOI generated by converted CC units, capitalized at market, exceeds the conversion capital cost by a sufficient margin to justify the risk and time. Sensitivity to both the CC rent premium and the conversion capital cost is important.
- **Competitive demand** — Conversion is most valuable where local demand for CC product is demonstrably unmet — existing CC units in the market should be at or near full occupancy at street rates. Converting to CC in a market already oversupplied with CC adds cost without corresponding revenue.

For underwriting conversion scenarios, use [[Yield on Cost]] and [[Yield-on-Cost vs. Market Cap Spread]] frameworks to stress-test the investment thesis.

## Summary Comparison

| Dimension | Climate-Controlled | Non-Climate / Drive-Up |
|-----------|-------------------|----------------------|
| Construction cost per NRSF | Higher | Lower |
| Utility / operating cost | Higher | Lower |
| Rent per SF | Higher | Lower |
| Typical cap rate | Lower (tighter) | Higher (wider) |
| Customer goods sensitivity | Higher-value, heat-sensitive | Equipment, seasonal, bulky |
| Demand driver | Urban density, Sunbelt heat, residential storage deficit | Geographic convenience, size availability |
| Barrier to new supply | Moderate–high (CC adds cost) | Lower in suburban markets |

*Figures vary by market and vintage.*
