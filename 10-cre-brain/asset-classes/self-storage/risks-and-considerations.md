# Self-Storage — Risks & Considerations

Self-storage is frequently cited as a recession-resilient asset class, and that characterization has validity — but it obscures a set of real risks that have caught investors off guard in oversupplied markets, operational transitions, and rate cycles. This note catalogs the primary risks across market, operational, capital/financing, and structural/physical dimensions.

---

### Market

**Oversupply and low barriers to entry.** The defining market risk in self-storage is oversupply driven by the asset class's relatively low barriers to new construction, particularly for single-story non-climate suburban product. Land is inexpensive in suburban and exurban markets; entitlement is often by-right or straightforward; construction timelines are short. These characteristics attract developers during periods of strong occupancy and rate growth, producing supply waves that consistently overshoot demand in competitive corridors. The 2016–2019 period saw significant new supply in many Sunbelt metros that depressed street rates and extended lease-up timelines for newly opened facilities. Mitigant: rigorous [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] that inventories planned, permitted, and under-construction competitive supply within the primary trade area (typically a 1–3 mile drive radius); underwriting occupancy and rate assumptions that stress-test against a supply scenario.

**Recession-resilience nuance.** Self-storage demand is often described as counter-cyclical, and the "four D's" (death, divorce, dislocation, downsizing) do provide demand stability during downturns. However, this characterization has limits. Extended economic distress reduces household formation, suppresses residential mobility (a primary demand trigger), and limits customers' ability to absorb ECRI rate increases. Delinquency rises during deep recessions. Business customer demand (contractors, small businesses) correlates more directly with economic conditions than consumer demand. Mitigant: model occupancy and rate scenarios that reflect a moderate recession impact, including a 5–10% physical occupancy decline and a pause or reversal of ECRI programs.

**Trade area saturation.** Self-storage is a hyper-local business. A new 80,000 NRSF competitor opening within 1–2 miles of an existing facility can create direct occupancy competition for years. Unlike large-format retail where brand and destination appeal matter nationally, storage customers overwhelmingly select the closest convenient option. Mitigant: detailed competitive mapping of existing and planned supply, analysis of demand density relative to existing supply, and a clear articulation of the thesis for why the subject facility maintains a competitive position.

### Operational

**Revenue management dependence.** Value-add and lease-up underwriting in self-storage is heavily dependent on the assumption that sophisticated revenue management — particularly ECRI programs — will be implemented effectively. Under-managed facilities acquired from mom-and-pop operators often show large gaps between in-place and market rates, which represents genuine upside, but converting that upside requires disciplined execution. If ECRI programs are implemented too aggressively (large increases, short intervals), move-out rates spike and occupancy erodes; if too conservatively, the pro forma revenue trajectory is missed. Mitigant: use an experienced third-party operator or REIT platform from day one; model move-out sensitivity to ECRI assumptions and stress-test the revenue curve.

**Digital marketing and customer acquisition costs.** Self-storage customer acquisition has shifted almost entirely to digital channels — Google Ads, search engine optimization, and national listing platforms (SpareFoot, StorageCafe). Facilities without a strong digital presence or national brand affiliation are at a structural disadvantage in converting new move-ins, particularly against REIT-affiliated competitors with national marketing budgets. A mom-and-pop facility relying on drive-by traffic and word of mouth will consistently underperform a comparable institutionally managed facility in the same market. Mitigant: budget appropriately for digital marketing activation as part of the value-add business plan; consider third-party management with platform marketing capabilities.

**Management transition risk.** Acquiring an independent facility and transitioning to a new management platform carries short-term occupancy risk. Tenants may not receive rate increase notices correctly, online leasing systems require re-integration, and the operator's ECRI database must be rebuilt from scratch. These risks are manageable but should be priced into underwriting through a conservative near-term revenue assumption during the management transition period.

### Capital & Financing

**Lease-up risk for ground-up and value-add deals.** New construction and heavily repositioned facilities typically require 18–36 months to reach stabilized occupancy. During this window, revenue is below debt service, requiring equity capital reserves or a bridge facility that carries the deficit. If competitive supply enters the market during the lease-up period, stabilization can be delayed significantly. Deals with thin equity reserves or insufficient lease-up financing to withstand extended absorption timelines are at high risk of distress. Mitigant: conservatively underwrite lease-up timing (use the 75th percentile, not median, of comparable lease-up comps); size equity reserves to cover operating deficits through a 36-month stabilization scenario. See [[Ground-Up Development]] and [[Bridge Loan]].

**Bridge loan refinancing risk.** Value-add and development deals commonly use bridge financing that must be refinanced into permanent debt upon stabilization. If market conditions shift — rising cap rates, widening credit spreads, tightening lender underwriting standards — the stabilized facility may not appraise at the value needed to support permanent debt at the expected proceeds, requiring additional equity or a discounted exit. See [[Loan-to-Value (LTV)]], [[Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)]], and [[Stress Testing]].

**Cap rate expansion risk.** Self-storage cap rates compressed significantly in the 2010s and early 2020s as institutional capital flowed into the sector. At tighter cap rates, the same NOI supports higher valuations, but cap rate expansion of even 50–100 basis points on a stabilized asset can produce meaningful value impairment. Mitigant: underwrite exit cap rates at a prudent spread above going-in; avoid pro formas that depend on cap rate compression for return achievement.

### Structural & Physical

**Deferred maintenance and conversion costs.** Mom-and-pop facilities often carry deferred roof, paving, gate, and roll-up door maintenance that is not visible in the income statement but must be addressed post-acquisition. Climate conversion of existing non-climate units requires HVAC installation, insulation, and potentially structural modifications; costs vary widely depending on existing building conditions. Mitigant: full physical due diligence including roof, paving, electrical, and HVAC assessment; budget clearly for deferred maintenance and conversion capital in the business plan.

**Environmental and site conditions.** Storage facilities on former industrial sites may carry environmental contamination risk; ground-up development on infill sites may require environmental remediation. Surface drainage issues on outdoor facilities can create liability from flooding damage to stored goods. Mitigant: Phase I (and Phase II if warranted) environmental assessment; review stored goods limitation policies and tenant insurance requirements.

**Security and liability.** Customer goods damage, theft, and unauthorized access claims are the primary liability exposure. Facilities without adequate camera coverage, individually alarmed units, and proper access control systems face higher claim rates and potential litigation. Tenant insurance or protection plan enrollment mitigates facility liability but requires proper structuring (see [[Self-Storage — Regulatory Considerations]]). Mitigant: invest in security infrastructure as part of the capital program; implement tenant protection plan enrollment at move-in.
