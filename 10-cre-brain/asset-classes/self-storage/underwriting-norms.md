# Self-Storage — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Self-storage underwriting shares the structural logic of other income-property asset classes — estimating stabilized [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] and applying a market cap rate — but differs in several important ways. Revenue is highly granular (hundreds of month-to-month leases rather than a few anchor leases), pricing is dynamic (street rates change weekly or daily on managed platforms), and NOI can be moved significantly by management quality. The [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] is a critical starting point for acquisitions, but in-place NOI may substantially understate or overstate stabilized potential depending on operator sophistication, tenure of existing tenants, and current market conditions.

The key underwriting disciplines for self-storage are: (1) accurately separating physical occupancy from economic occupancy; (2) modeling [[Effective Gross Income (EGI)]] with appropriate concession and delinquency assumptions; (3) stress-testing ECRI assumptions that drive same-store growth; and (4) for development deals, carefully modeling lease-up timing relative to competitive supply entries. See [[Pro Forma Construction Method]], [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]], and [[Direct Capitalization]] for the broader methodology context.

## Revenue Management

### Street Rate vs. In-Place Rate

Street rates are the advertised rates for vacant units at a given point in time — effectively the market rate for new customer acquisition. In-place rates are the actual rents charged to existing tenants, which may be above or below current street rates depending on how aggressively the operator has implemented rate increases. A facility with long-tenured customers and an inactive ECRI program will often show a large spread between street and in-place rates, representing unrealized revenue upside. Conversely, a recently managed facility may have pushed in-place rates well above street — a sign of potential churn risk if customers comparison-shop.

For underwriting purposes, the spread between street and in-place rates informs both the short-term revenue upside and the risk of elevated move-outs as ECRI programs push existing tenants toward and beyond their willingness-to-pay thresholds. Buyers should model both the ECRI upside and the corresponding increase in churn and re-leasing friction when projecting same-store NOI growth. See [[Self-Storage — Key Metrics]] for rate-per-occupied-SF and RevPAF definitions.

### ECRI (Existing-Customer Rate Increase) Programs

ECRI — existing-customer rate increase — is the single most powerful lever in self-storage revenue management. Unlike most commercial leases with fixed escalation schedules, month-to-month storage leases can be adjusted with 30 days' notice (subject to state law). National REITs and sophisticated third-party managers use algorithmic ECRI programs to systematically increase rents on tenants based on tenure, unit type, occupancy, and local competitive conditions — typically in increments of 5%–15% every 3–9 months.

Underwriting ECRI correctly requires: (1) understanding current in-place versus street rate differentials by unit type; (2) modeling move-out sensitivity to rate increases (historically, most tenants do not move out over modest increases due to the friction and cost of self-moving); and (3) normalizing for any recent aggressive ECRI implementation that may have already extracted near-term upside. Deals with low in-place rates relative to street and an unsophisticated current operator represent the highest-conviction ECRI upside.

## OpEx Norms

Self-storage has the lowest operating expense ratio of any major commercial real estate class, typically ranging from 30%–45% of EGI (excluding management fee), versus 40%–55% for multifamily or office. Key expense line items:

- **Payroll / on-site staffing** — Varies significantly based on facility automation. Unmanned, kiosk-operated facilities have minimal staffing costs; larger managed facilities with extended hours carry higher payroll. Ranges widely — typically 15%–25% of total OpEx.
- **Marketing and advertising** — Digital marketing (Google Ads, SEO, listing platforms) has become the primary customer acquisition channel. Budget typically 5%–12% of EGI depending on facility occupancy and competitive environment.
- **Utilities** — Climate-controlled facilities carry materially higher utility costs (HVAC). Non-climate drive-up is predominantly lighting and security; climate-controlled can run $0.75–$2.00+ per NRSF annually depending on climate zone and building efficiency.
- **Property taxes** — Market-dependent. In high-tax jurisdictions, real estate taxes can be 20%–35% of total OpEx.
- **Insurance** — Property and liability insurance; typically 1%–3% of EGI.
- **Repairs and maintenance** — Lower than most property types given simple building systems; includes gate maintenance, roll-up door repairs, paving, and HVAC. Typically 3%–7% of EGI.
- **Management fee** — Third-party management fees of 5%–8% of EGI are standard for institutional deals using national or regional operators.

When underwriting an acquisition, normalize expenses using [[Expense Normalization]] methodology — owner-operated facilities often omit management fees and understate marketing costs, inflating apparent NOI.
