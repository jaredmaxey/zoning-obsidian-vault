# Self-Storage — Subtypes

Self-storage is not a monolithic product type. Facility design, unit mix, customer profile, construction economics, and competitive dynamics vary substantially across subtypes. Understanding the distinctions is essential for accurate underwriting, supply analysis, and investment thesis construction.

---

### Climate-Controlled

**Profile.** Climate-controlled (CC) units are enclosed within a conditioned building envelope, maintaining temperature (typically 55°F–85°F) and, in better-built facilities, humidity within specified ranges. Units are accessed via interior corridors with elevator service in multi-story configurations. The physical product ranges from purpose-built multi-story urban facilities to retrofitted single-story buildings with HVAC added. CC units are typically 5×5 to 10×30 feet; the standard institutional offering skews toward smaller sizes (5×5, 5×10, 10×10) where CC commands the largest premium over non-climate.

**Deal Profile.** Climate-controlled facilities trade at lower cap rates than comparable non-climate product, reflecting higher demand density, stronger in-place rate growth potential via ECRI programs, and greater barrier to supply in urban markets. Value-add plays often involve converting underutilized or partially-climate portions of an existing facility. Stabilized CC-heavy facilities in supply-constrained markets are core and core-plus targets; lease-up CC deals in competitive suburban markets carry meaningful oversupply risk.

**Ownership.** National REITs (Public Storage, Extra Space Storage/Life Storage, CubeSmart) dominate CC-heavy urban and suburban portfolios. Institutional private equity platforms hold significant CC inventory; independent operators remain active in secondary and tertiary markets but are increasingly at a competitive disadvantage versus REIT revenue management and digital marketing scale.

---

### Non-Climate / Drive-Up

**Profile.** The traditional self-storage format: single-story, exterior-access units arranged in rows, with roll-up doors opening directly to a paved drive aisle. No HVAC beyond what ambient conditions provide. Unit sizes range from 5×10 to 10×40 or larger; large drive-up units serve contractors, landscapers, and small businesses with bulky inventory or equipment. Construction cost per NRSF is substantially lower than climate-controlled product, making this the dominant development format in suburban and rural markets.

**Deal Profile.** Drive-up-heavy facilities typically carry higher cap rates than CC-dominant peers, reflecting lower rent per square foot and greater competitive exposure from easy-to-build new supply. Value-add opportunities include adding CC units to existing drive-up facilities, improving management and marketing, and raising below-market rents via ECRI programs on long-tenured customer cohorts. Many mom-and-pop acquisition targets are drive-up dominant — operational uplift from professional management can be meaningful. See [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

**Ownership.** Highly fragmented. Independent operators control a large share of the non-climate drive-up supply base, particularly in markets outside top-50 metros. REITs hold portfolios that include drive-up product but have generally been more active acquirers of mixed or CC-heavy facilities. Regional and national private platforms actively aggregate drive-up assets at scale for operational efficiency.

### Multi-Story Urban

**Profile.** Elevator-served, climate-controlled facilities in infill urban or dense suburban locations, typically 3–8 stories with parking below grade or integrated into the building. Interior corridors, key-fob or keypad-controlled access, sophisticated security systems (cameras, individual unit alarms), and digital kiosk or unmanned checkout. Construction cost per NRSF is significantly higher than suburban single-story product — structural, elevator, and HVAC costs are substantial — but land constraints and high demand density in urban markets support premium street rates and strong physical occupancy.

**Deal Profile.** Urban multi-story deals typically require ground-up development or [[Adaptive Reuse]] of an existing building (former big-box retail, warehouse, or office). Entitlement is more complex, often requiring a [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] and extended public hearing timelines. The capital intensity and longer entitlement timeline create meaningful barriers to entry that support long-term rate and occupancy performance once stabilized. Lease-up can take 24–36 months; cash-on-cash returns during this period are minimal. Exit to REIT or large private buyer is a viable disposition path in many markets. See [[Urban vs. Suburban Facility Design]].

**Ownership.** A mix of national REITs (who have been active acquirers of stabilized urban assets), institutional development sponsors, and local developers with entitlement expertise. True mom-and-pop ownership is rare in urban multi-story due to capital requirements.

---

### Single-Story Suburban

**Profile.** The legacy suburban storage format: one-story facilities on suburban or exurban sites, often a mix of drive-up exterior units and climate-controlled interior corridors. Land-intensive; typical site coverage is 35%–50% with parking and drive aisles consuming the remainder. Many existing single-story suburban facilities were built in the 1980s–2000s and represent value-add opportunities through climate-conversion, technology upgrades, facade improvement, and revenue management implementation.

**Deal Profile.** Single-story suburban is the most common acquisition target for value-add private equity sponsors. Cap rates are wider than urban CC product, reflecting competitive exposure, lower barriers to new supply, and often below-market management. Upside comes from operational improvement (revenue management, online leasing, ECRI programs) and physical upgrades (climate conversion of interior units, improved signage and curb appeal). See [[Climate-Controlled vs. Non-Climate]] for conversion economics.

**Ownership.** The most fragmented subtype by ownership — independent operators own a large share of suburban single-story supply. REITs are net sellers of lower-quality suburban assets and net buyers in higher-barrier suburban markets. Mid-market private platforms are active acquirers.

### Portable / Mobile Storage

**Profile.** Portable storage providers (e.g., PODS, 1-800-PACK-RAT) deliver steel or wood-framed containers to customers at their home or business, which are then transported to a storage yard or kept on-site. The real estate footprint is a storage yard rather than a traditional self-storage facility. Unit sizes are typically 8×8 to 8×16 feet. The model is more logistics-intensive and less scalable per square foot than fixed-facility storage, but captures demand from customers who value on-site pickup/delivery convenience (moving, remodeling).

**Deal Profile.** Portable storage is primarily an operating business with a real estate component (yard lease or ownership). The investment thesis is more analogous to a fleet-based logistics business than a traditional self-storage real estate play. Cap rate benchmarks from fixed-facility storage do not directly translate. Institutional investment in the space has been limited to operator-level platform acquisitions rather than individual yard transactions.

**Ownership.** National franchise systems (PODS, operated by 1-800-Flowers/PODS Enterprises) and regional independents. Not a significant target for traditional CRE institutional capital.

### RV, Boat & Vehicle Storage

**Profile.** Facilities dedicated to storing recreational vehicles, boats, campers, trailers, and other oversized vehicles. Three formats: outdoor parking (lowest cost, highest risk of weathering), covered/carport structures (mid-range), and fully enclosed drive-in units (highest cost, highest rate). Unit sizes are large — 12×40 to 15×60 feet for RV/boat — requiring significant land area. Demand is driven by population density (insufficient driveway space), HOA restrictions on at-home storage, and growth in RV/boat ownership.

**Deal Profile.** RV and boat storage trades at wider cap rates than climate-controlled self-storage but has attracted growing institutional interest as a niche sub-sector. Demand is less recession-tested than traditional storage and correlates to consumer discretionary spending on recreational assets. Supply constraints in Sunbelt and mountain resort markets support strong fundamentals. Development is straightforward for outdoor and covered formats; enclosed drive-in product requires more substantial construction investment. Value-add opportunities include converting outdoor lots to covered or enclosed formats and adding tenant amenities (dump stations, wash bays).

**Ownership.** Predominantly independent operators and regional platforms. National self-storage REITs have limited RV/boat exposure as a percentage of total portfolios. Private equity interest has grown as the niche matures.
