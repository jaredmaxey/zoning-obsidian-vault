# Limited-Service Hotels

## Overview

Limited-service hotels — typically classified as economy and budget-tier properties — represent the most accessible price point in the lodging market and the segment with the highest barriers to exit for individual owner-operators. These hotels offer a minimal amenity set: no restaurant, no meeting space, no pool (or a basic pool only), and a simple room product focused on cleanliness and value. The operational footprint is lean by design: a front desk operating limited hours, minimal housekeeping, and no F&B staff. This simplicity produces the lowest labor-to-revenue ratio of any hotel segment and the lowest absolute break-even occupancy — many limited-service hotels can cover all operating costs and modest debt service at 40%–55% occupancy.

The limited-service segment serves a distinct and persistent demand base: price-sensitive leisure travelers, long-haul truckers, construction and project workers, government per-diem travelers, and families on road trips. This demand is not luxury-aspirational — it is utility-driven — and it is relatively resilient in downturns because the traveler is already at the lowest viable price point. The risk, conversely, is that ADR growth is constrained by competition from both adjacent branded competitors and informal lodging alternatives.

## Demand Profile

Limited-service demand is driven by price elasticity rather than brand loyalty, though economy flags do benefit from their brands' loyalty programs at the margin. The primary demand driver is affordability relative to alternatives: the limited-service hotel competes against camping, staying with family, and even the upper end of its own segment (budget-tier Wyndham flags competing with mid-tier Choice flags). Highway interchange locations, proximity to distribution centers and manufacturing facilities, and positioning along leisure drive corridors are common demand anchors.

Market selection is particularly important in limited-service underwriting: a limited-service hotel in a market with a diversified and stable demand base (regional medical center, military installation, industrial employment) will outperform an equivalent asset in a market whose demand base is a single employer or a seasonal attraction.

## Typical Metrics and Underwriting

Limited-service underwriting emphasizes simplicity and downside resilience over revenue maximization:

- **Going-in cap rates**: Typically 7.5%–10.5% for stabilized assets, reflecting the wider buyer pool discount and higher perceived risk relative to select-service.
- **Stabilized occupancy**: 55%–72% in most markets; lower is acceptable if ADR and cost structure support debt service at minimum 1.25× DSCR.
- **ADR**: Lowest of any branded segment; significant market-to-market variation. Underwriting ADR growth at modest CPI-like rates (2%–3%) is conservative and appropriate.
- **GOP margin**: 35%–50%; the lean cost structure partially offsets the lower absolute revenue base.
- **FF&E reserve**: 4%–5% of total revenue; older economy product may require above-reserve capex for brand standard compliance at renewal.

Distressed acquisition opportunities are more common in limited-service than other segments: deferred maintenance, weak franchisee operators, and over-leveraged family owners create a steady pipeline of workout and note acquisition situations. See [[Distressed Acquisition]].

## Ownership and Flags

Ownership of limited-service hotels is dominated by individual franchisee families, regional owner-operators, and small institutional platforms rather than large REITs. The deal sizes — often $3M–$15M per property — are below the threshold of most institutional direct investment programs, making aggregation strategies (portfolio roll-up followed by institutional exit) a common private equity approach.

Key economy and budget flags include:
- **Choice Hotels**: Comfort Inn, Sleep Inn, Econo Lodge, Rodeway Inn, Suburban Extended Stay
- **Wyndham Hotels & Resorts**: Super 8, Days Inn, Travelodge, Howard Johnson, La Quinta (lower end)
- **IHG**: Holiday Inn Express (lower-cost markets; some overlap with upper midscale)
- **G6 Hospitality**: Motel 6, Studio 6
- **Extended Stay America**: Positioned between limited-service and extended-stay

Many limited-service hotels in secondary and tertiary markets operate as true independents without flag affiliation, relying entirely on OTA distribution and local reputation. See [[Brand vs. Independent]] for the trade-off analysis relevant to this segment.
