# Hospitality — Risks & Considerations

Hotel investments carry a risk profile that is qualitatively different from leased commercial real estate. The absence of long-term leases means every risk that is normally contractually mitigated in office, retail, or industrial — tenant default, early vacancy, rent decline — is permanent and daily in hospitality. Understanding this risk profile is not an argument against hotel investment; it is a prerequisite for sizing returns and structuring investments appropriately.

---

### Market

**RevPAR Cyclicality and Volatility.** Hospitality is the most economically sensitive commercial real estate sector. RevPAR can decline 20–40% during a recession (the 2008–2009 cycle and the 2020 COVID-19 shock are the most instructive historical cases) as corporate travel budgets are cut, group bookings are cancelled, and leisure demand contracts. Unlike a lease that protects the owner for 3–10 years, hotel revenue resets daily — a demand shock is immediately visible in weekly STR reports. Assets with high leverage and near-term loan maturities are particularly exposed to a RevPAR downturn. See [[Stress Testing]] and [[Sensitivity Analysis]].

*Mitigants:* Conservative LTV at origination (50%–60%), adequate DSCR cushion above 1.30×, diversified demand mix (business transient + leisure + group), and a debt structure with sufficient term to bridge a downturn without forced refinancing. Extended-stay and drive-to leisure assets demonstrate lower RevPAR volatility.

**Supply Pipeline Risk.** New supply additions — even one or two large hotels in a competitive submarket — can compress RevPAR index scores and absolute RevPAR for existing hotels. Unlike multifamily, where new units lease up gradually, a new hotel opens at full inventory and immediately competes for every demand segment. A tight underwriting hold period thesis can be disrupted by a new 250-key Marriott Courtyard that wasn't in the pipeline when the deal was acquired. See [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]].

*Mitigants:* Rigorous supply pipeline monitoring (STR pipeline reports, city permitting databases), preference for markets with high barriers to new construction (land-constrained urban cores, resort destinations with limited developable land), and RevPAR index underwriting that accounts for supply stress scenarios.

**Demand Driver Concentration.** Hotels in single-employer markets, university towns, or specialized destination markets (ski, beach) are highly dependent on the continued vitality of that demand driver. Corporate relocation or bankruptcy of a major employer can devastate occupancy in a market built around that company's travel patterns. Seasonality in leisure markets concentrates risk in off-peak periods when occupancy can fall below operating break-even. See [[Demand Driver Analysis]].

*Mitigants:* Diversified demand base underwriting, stress testing off-season occupancy at or near break-even, and avoidance of single-employer dependency without material return premium.

### Operational

**Operator Dependence and Management Quality.** Hotel performance is far more management-sensitive than other real estate asset classes. A skilled general manager and revenue management team can outperform an average comp set by 10–20 RevPAR index points; a poor operator can lose those points equally fast. Unlike a net lease structure where management quality is irrelevant to the owner, hotel owners are exposed to every management decision — staffing levels, revenue management pricing, sales effort, maintenance standards, and guest service delivery. Management team turnover is a recurring operational risk.

*Mitigants:* Thorough operator due diligence (references, portfolio track record, comp set performance), negotiation of performance tests (RevPAR index and GOP thresholds) with termination rights, alignment through incentive management fee structures, and active asset management engagement.

**Brand PIP Capital Calls.** A property improvement plan (PIP) — required at flag change, at sale, or at franchise renewal — can impose $5,000–$60,000+ per key in mandatory capital improvements within an 18–36 month window. PIPs that were deferred by a seller surface during due diligence and can materially erode projected returns if not priced into the acquisition. Mid-hold PIPs at franchise renewal can impose unbudgeted capital needs that were not modeled in the original pro forma. See [[FF&E]] and [[Reserves for Replacement]].

*Mitigants:* Obtain brand PIP letter prior to offer finalization, budget PIP in acquisition cost basis, negotiate PIP escrow or seller credit, and confirm remaining franchise agreement term to identify mid-hold renewal risk.

**OTA Channel Dependency and Distribution Costs.** Online travel agencies (OTAs — Expedia, Booking.com) charge commission rates of 15%–25% per booking. A hotel with high OTA dependency has a structurally higher cost of revenue than a well-branded hotel that captures direct bookings through the brand's loyalty app and website. Independent and boutique hotels are particularly exposed. Shifts in OTA commission structures or ranking algorithm changes can meaningfully affect revenue even when demand is stable.

*Mitigants:* Brand affiliation for loyalty program access (see [[Brand vs. Independent]]), active direct-booking program, and monitoring of channel mix as part of asset management KPIs.

### Capital & Financing

**Hotel Lending Market Volatility.** Hotel lending markets are materially more volatile than other CRE debt markets. In recessions, many lenders either exit hotel lending entirely or tighten LTV and DSCR requirements substantially, creating refinancing risk for owners whose loan maturities fall in a downturn. CMBS execution for hotels can disappear quickly (as it did in 2020), forcing borrowers to refinance into more expensive bridge debt or seek extensions. Life company and bank lenders have historically been more consistent but also more selective on market and asset quality. See [[CMBS]], [[Bridge Loan]], and [[Bank Debt]].

*Mitigants:* Conservative initial LTV (50%–60%), match loan term to hold period, build extension options into bridge financing, maintain adequate liquidity reserves, and avoid near-term maturities in a rising rate environment.

**Capital Intensity and Ongoing CapEx.** Hotels are among the most capital-intensive real estate assets to maintain. Beyond the FF&E reserve (4%–6% of revenue annually), structural systems (HVAC, elevator, roofing, parking structures), pool and spa infrastructure, technology (property management systems, in-room tech, point-of-sale), and exterior improvements require periodic capital injections beyond the operating reserve. Full-service and resort assets with aging infrastructure can require $20,000–$50,000+ per key in capital improvement cycles that are not fully funded by the FF&E reserve.

*Mitigants:* Detailed property condition report (PCR) at acquisition, adequate PCR-derived capex reserve in the hold-period model, and conservative NOI projection that accounts for renovation disruption (occupancy and ADR dislocation during construction).

**Interest Rate Sensitivity.** Hotel cap rates historically follow interest rate movements, though the relationship is less rigid than other CRE classes because of the operational complexity premium. Rising rates increase debt service costs (particularly for floating-rate bridge loans common in hotel value-add), compress levered returns, and expand cap rates on exit, compressing terminal value. See [[Going-In Cap Rate]], [[Exit Cap Rate]], and [[Spread to Treasuries]].

*Mitigants:* Interest rate caps on floating-rate debt, fixed-rate term financing where available, and underwriting exit cap at a spread to the entry cap sufficient to absorb rate movement.

### Structural & Physical

**Physical Asset Obsolescence.** Hotel product ages quickly. A hotel opened 15 years ago without an intervening renovation competes against current-generation product with modern in-room technology, fitness facilities, and social spaces — and loses. Guest expectations are increasingly driven by social media visibility and design standards that reset continuously. A dated room product can cause RevPAR index deterioration of 5–15 points even in a stable market, as guests self-select toward newer product. This physical obsolescence risk is structural in hospitality and must be budgeted for in every hold period.

*Mitigants:* Renovation cycle modeling in the pro forma, adequate FF&E reserve funding, and acquisition pricing that reflects the capex needed to maintain competitive product positioning.

**Seasonality and Revenue Concentration.** Resort and leisure assets can concentrate 50–60% of annual revenue in a 90–120 day window. A single weather event, a natural disaster, or public health restriction during peak season can eliminate a disproportionate share of annual NOI. Seasonal assets require higher cash reserves to fund off-season operations and debt service, and their NOI volatility makes lender underwriting more conservative.

*Mitigants:* Adequate seasonal cash flow reserves, debt service reserve accounts, conservative peak-season NOI assumptions that apply weather/disruption haircuts, and demand diversification (weddings, corporate retreats) to extend the revenue season.

**ADA and Life-Safety Compliance.** Older hotel assets may carry deferred ADA upgrades (accessible rooms, common areas, pools) and life-safety compliance requirements (fire suppression, egress, elevator certification) that surface in due diligence as capital obligations. In jurisdictions with active enforcement, non-compliance can result in fines, operational restrictions, or closure orders. See [[Hospitality — Regulatory Considerations]].

*Mitigants:* Property condition report with ADA audit as part of due diligence, budget for compliance capital in the acquisition model, and confirm current life-safety permits and inspection records.
