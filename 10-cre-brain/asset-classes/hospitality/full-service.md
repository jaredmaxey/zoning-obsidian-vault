# Full-Service Hotels

## Overview

Full-service hotels are the most operationally complex segment of the commercial hospitality sector. A true full-service hotel offers a comprehensive guest experience: multiple food and beverage outlets (at minimum a restaurant and bar; often room service), significant meeting and event space (ballrooms, breakout rooms, pre-function areas), a full fitness center, concierge services, and in many cases additional amenities such as a pool, spa, or business center. The rooms component — while still the largest revenue contributor — is supplemented by meaningful ancillary revenue from F&B, meeting room rental, audio-visual services, parking, and other departments.

The full-service model exists to capture all three major demand segments simultaneously: business transient travelers who need F&B and meeting access, group bookings (conventions, weddings, conferences) that require event infrastructure, and leisure guests who value the amenity experience. This multi-segment approach is what distinguishes full-service from select-service, but it also drives significantly higher operating complexity and cost. Labor costs in full-service typically run 35%–45% of total revenue, compared to 25%–32% for select-service, driven by the F&B and banquet staffing requirements.

Institutionally, full-service hotels represent the largest share of total hotel industry real estate value but a smaller share of transaction volume compared to select-service, due to their size and complexity. They are concentrated in major metropolitan markets, urban cores, and airport corridors, where the demand volume and corporate account concentration justify the amenity investment.

## Demand Profile

Full-service hotel demand is the most complex and diversified of any hotel segment. Business transient demand — individual corporate travelers booking with or without negotiated corporate rate agreements — is typically the largest demand segment during weekdays. Group demand — conventions, conferences, corporate meetings, social events (weddings, galas) — provides forward revenue visibility (the group "book" can extend 12–36 months) and drives F&B and meeting room revenue capture. Leisure demand — families, couples, and individual leisure travelers — fills weekends and shoulder periods.

The group segment is particularly important for full-service underwriting. A hotel with a strong group book not only generates rooms revenue but captures significant ancillary revenue per delegate (F&B per group attendee, AV charges, meeting room rental, sleeping room pickups). Properties with competitive meeting space, in-house catering, and a skilled group sales team can generate $30–$60 TRevPAR premium over their rooms-only RevPAR, substantially boosting NOI margins relative to a raw RevPAR comparison.

## Typical Metrics and Underwriting

Full-service hotel underwriting requires a full departmental pro forma rather than a simplified rooms-only model. Key considerations:

- **RevPAR** is typically higher than select-service in the same market, reflecting the premium positioning, but the NOI margin on that revenue is lower due to F&B cost drag.
- **F&B margin**: Full-service F&B departments typically operate at 20%–35% departmental profit margins — meaning 65%–80% of F&B revenue is consumed by food cost and labor. Many hotels operate F&B at or near breakeven, treating it as an amenity to drive rooms revenue rather than a profit center.
- **Meeting space revenue**: Meeting room rental, AV, and catering charges can add meaningfully to TRevPAR for well-positioned properties with strong group sales.
- **Cap rates** for full-service assets vary widely: stabilized gateway-market full-service assets may trade at going-in caps of 6.0%–7.5%; secondary-market full-service assets may trade at 7.5%–9.5%.
- **LTV**: Lender underwriting for full-service hotels is conservative — typically 55%–65% LTV — reflecting the revenue complexity and higher debt coverage requirements.

Common value-add theses include: F&B concept refresh to improve departmental capture and guest satisfaction scores; meeting space renovation to improve group rate and capture rate; guestroom renovation to move ADR and RevPAR index; and brand upgrade (e.g., converting an independent full-service to an Autograph Collection or Curio flag) to improve distribution and direct booking share.

## Ownership and Flags

Institutional ownership of full-service hotels is concentrated among hotel-focused REITs (Host Hotels & Resorts is the largest public hotel REIT by asset value and is primarily full-service), large private equity sponsors, and sovereign wealth funds with multi-decade hold horizons for trophy assets. Single-asset family office ownership is also present, particularly for iconic independent properties.

Common full-service flags across the major systems include:
- **Marriott International**: Marriott Hotels, Renaissance, Sheraton, Westin, Le Méridien
- **Hilton Hotels & Resorts**: Hilton, DoubleTree, Embassy Suites
- **Hyatt**: Hyatt Regency, Grand Hyatt
- **IHG**: InterContinental, Crowne Plaza, Kimpton
- **Soft brands / independents**: Autograph Collection (Marriott), Curio (Hilton), Tapestry (Hilton), unaffiliated independents

Many urban full-service hotels in gateway markets operate as true independents or under soft-brand collections, preserving unique identity while accessing partial distribution benefits. See [[Brand vs. Independent]].
