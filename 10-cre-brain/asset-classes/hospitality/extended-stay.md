# Extended-Stay Hotels

## Overview

Extended-stay hotels are designed from the ground up to serve guests staying five or more nights — typically weekly or monthly — rather than the transient one- or two-night traveler. The product is suite-based: every room includes an in-room kitchen or kitchenette (full-size refrigerator, microwave or stovetop, dishwasher in upscale tiers), more living space than a standard hotel room, and a residential-feeling environment. The amenity footprint is intentionally stripped of full-service complexity — no restaurant, limited or no meeting space — but includes laundry facilities, a fitness center, and often a social/gathering area. The operational model is efficient: housekeeping frequency is weekly rather than daily, front desk staffing can be reduced relative to transient hotels, and the guest self-manages more of their in-room needs.

The defining characteristic of extended-stay hotels is demand resilience. Weekly and monthly guests — corporate relocations, project-based workers, healthcare travelers, insurance-displaced households, government contractors — are far less likely to check out based on a single bad day in the economy than a transient leisure traveler. Extended-stay occupancy in the 2008–2009 recession declined meaningfully but significantly less than the broader hotel market; in the 2020 COVID-19 shock, extended-stay occupied the least-disrupted position in the hotel landscape as transient and group travel collapsed. This resilience makes extended-stay the most defensive hotel product type for investors who value occupancy stability over peak RevPAR potential.

## Demand Profile

Extended-stay demand is driven by workforce mobility, project-based employment, and life transition events:

- **Corporate relocation and project workers**: Companies relocating employees or deploying project teams (construction, IT, consulting, healthcare travel nursing) generate predictable, often contracted weekly demand. Corporate accounts are a critical revenue driver for extended-stay hotels near employment nodes.
- **Healthcare and travel nursing**: The growth of travel nursing and locum physician staffing has created a durable and growing demand segment for extended-stay hotels near major medical centers.
- **Insurance/disaster displacement**: Households displaced by fire, flood, or storm are commonly placed in extended-stay hotels under insurance company master accounts; this segment provides occupancy resilience even in economic downturns.
- **Government and military**: Government per-diem travelers, training programs, and military temporary duty assignments provide predictable extended-stay demand in appropriate markets.
- **Leisure extended-stay**: Snowbirds, temporary workers, and "workation" travelers increasingly use extended-stay product for 2–8 week stays.

Demand is most durable in markets with major employment concentration in healthcare, energy, technology, logistics, or government — sectors that maintain workforce activity through economic cycles.

## Typical Metrics and Underwriting

Extended-stay underwriting focuses on length-of-stay mix, corporate account relationships, and weekly/monthly rate structures:

- **Going-in cap rates**: Typically 50–100 bps tighter than comparable select-service in the same market, reflecting superior occupancy resilience; ranges commonly 5.5%–8.0% for stabilized assets.
- **Stabilized occupancy**: 75%–88% in demand-deep markets; extended-stay can maintain 70%+ occupancy in downturns where transient hotels drop to 50%–60%.
- **Weekly vs. nightly rate mix**: Extended-stay hotels offer lower per-night rates for longer stays (weekly rates are typically 10%–25% below rack nightly rates). Underwriting must model ADR as a blend of the actual length-of-stay distribution — a hotel with 60% of room nights from weekly/monthly guests will show lower ADR than a comparable transient hotel but compensate with higher occupancy and lower per-room cost of sales.
- **GOP margin**: 40%–55%; comparable to select-service given the lean operational model.
- **FF&E reserve**: 4%–5% of total revenue; in-room kitchens and appliances add some additional maintenance burden.
- **LTV**: 55%–65%; CMBS is a common execution venue.

## Ownership and Flags

Extended-stay has attracted significant institutional ownership given its defensive characteristics. Apple Hospitality REIT is the largest institutional owner of extended-stay keys (primarily Homewood Suites and Residence Inn). Chatham Lodging, Carey Watermark, and various private equity platforms are also active. Development of new extended-stay has been strong in Sun Belt markets where corporate demand growth has outpaced existing supply.

Key flags by tier:
- **Upscale extended-stay**: Marriott Residence Inn, Marriott Element; Hilton Homewood Suites; Hyatt House; Staybridge Suites (IHG)
- **Upper-midscale extended-stay**: Marriott TownePlace Suites; Hilton Home2 Suites; Candlewood Suites (IHG)
- **Economy extended-stay**: Extended Stay America (Blackstone/Starwood JV then public), WoodSpring Suites (Choice Hotels), Suburban Extended Stay

The extended-stay segment has seen significant new supply activity, particularly in the upper-midscale tier (Home2 Suites, TownePlace Suites), driven by construction cost efficiency (smaller keys, no restaurant infrastructure) and strong RevPAR performance.
