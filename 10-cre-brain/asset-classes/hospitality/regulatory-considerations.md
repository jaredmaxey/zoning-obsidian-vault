# Hospitality — Regulatory Considerations

Hotel assets operate within overlapping regulatory environments: federal civil rights and accessibility law, state and local licensing, local tax policy, brand contractual requirements, and labor law. Unlike most commercial real estate where regulatory complexity is primarily at acquisition and entitlement, hotels face an ongoing regulatory compliance burden throughout the operating period. The regulatory landscape also interacts with competitive dynamics in ways specific to hospitality — notably the short-term rental regulatory environment and local transient occupancy tax policy.

---

### Franchise and Brand Agreements — Quasi-Regulatory Character

Franchise agreements are not government regulation, but they function as a private regulatory system of comparable practical consequence. A franchisor (Marriott, Hilton, IHG, Hyatt, etc.) sets brand standards governing room product specifications, technology systems, operating procedures, staffing minimums, sustainability requirements, and physical plant maintenance. Compliance with brand standards is monitored through periodic property quality assurance (PQA) audits — unannounced inspections that score the property against brand standards and can result in warnings, remediation requirements, or in extreme cases, franchise termination.

Brand agreements also include **area protection clauses** — provisions that restrict the franchisor from opening a same-brand or competitive-tier hotel within a defined geographic radius. The strength of area protection varies significantly by flag and agreement vintage; newer agreements tend to have weaker area protection as brands have expanded their portfolio. Area protection is a critical due diligence item when acquiring a hotel in a market where the brand has active development pipeline. Weak or expired area protection can result in a new same-flag hotel opening within a mile of the acquired asset.

**PIP obligations** (Property Improvement Plans) at acquisition and flag renewal represent the quasi-regulatory capital burden discussed in [[Hospitality — Risks & Considerations]]. Buyers should treat PIP compliance as a regulatory requirement — non-compliance risks franchise termination, which would eliminate brand distribution and potentially trigger loan default under a CMBS or institutional lender covenant.

### Transient Occupancy Tax (TOT)

Transient occupancy taxes — also called hotel taxes, bed taxes, or lodging taxes — are assessed by cities, counties, and sometimes special tourism districts on hotel room revenue. Rates vary from approximately 5% to 20%+ of room revenue depending on jurisdiction, and multiple layers (city + county + state + tourism district) can stack. TOT is paid by the guest but collected and remitted by the hotel operator; collection non-compliance creates legal liability for the owner.

TOT rates matter competitively: a hotel in a jurisdiction with a 15% combined TOT rate faces a structural competitiveness disadvantage relative to drive-to competitors across a state line with a 7% rate, affecting total price to the traveler. In underwriting, TOT should not be modeled as a hotel revenue item (it is a pass-through), but the competitive implications of local TOT policy on demand capture deserve market-analysis consideration. Changes in TOT rates — which are politically common as municipalities seek hotel revenues for general fund or tourism promotion budgets — should be monitored as a regulatory risk during the hold period.

### Liquor Licensing

Hotels offering F&B operations with alcohol service — restaurants, bars, in-room minibars, banquet alcohol — require state and local liquor licenses. Liquor licensing is a state-regulated matter with significant jurisdictional variation. Key considerations:

- **License types** vary by state: on-premise consumption licenses, hotel-specific licenses, catering/banquet licenses, and retail beer-and-wine versus full liquor licenses carry different cost, availability, and renewal requirements.
- **License availability** can be capacity-constrained in some states (e.g., quota-based systems) — a valuable license may be acquired as part of a hotel transaction and should be specifically identified in the purchase agreement.
- **License transfer** at acquisition may require regulatory approval, can take 30–120+ days, and in some jurisdictions requires a public notice period or hearing. A delay in license transfer can force the hotel to suspend alcohol sales post-closing, impacting F&B revenue.
- **Violations** (serving minors, over-service liability) can result in suspension or revocation of the license — a severe operational risk for any full-service or resort hotel dependent on F&B revenue.

### ADA — Americans with Disabilities Act

The ADA imposes federal accessibility requirements on places of public accommodation, which explicitly includes hotels. Key requirements affecting hotel operations and capital planning:

- **Accessible guest rooms**: A percentage of all guest rooms (generally 2%–4% depending on total room count) must be ADA-compliant, with additional specific requirements for roll-in showers, hearing-accessible rooms, and mobility-accessible rooms.
- **Common areas**: Lobbies, restaurants, meeting rooms, fitness centers, pools, and parking must comply with accessible design standards (door widths, ramp grades, accessible parking ratios, pool lifts).
- **Pool lifts**: The 2012 ADA standards added pool lift requirements that many older hotels have still not fully addressed — a common finding in property condition reports.
- **Ongoing obligation**: The ADA is an ongoing obligation, not a one-time compliance event. Renovations that "touch" a non-compliant area trigger a "path of travel" requirement to upgrade the accessible route to that area.

ADA non-compliance creates both regulatory liability (DOJ enforcement, private lawsuits) and reputational risk. Buyers should obtain an ADA audit as part of due diligence and budget compliance capital into the acquisition model.

### Health and Safety Regulations

Hotels are subject to comprehensive health and safety inspections by multiple agencies, which vary by state and locality. Key regulatory categories:

- **Food service inspections**: County or municipal health departments inspect hotel restaurants, kitchens, and food-handling areas on a regular (often annual) basis. Inspection results may be publicly posted; a failing grade can damage reputation and trigger operational restrictions.
- **Fire safety**: Fire marshals inspect life-safety systems (sprinklers, alarms, egress signage, emergency lighting, exit doors) on a regular basis. Non-compliant systems require remediation and can trigger operational restrictions or closure.
- **Pool and spa inspections**: Health departments inspect pool water quality, chemical storage, and safety equipment. Closures for non-compliance during peak season can materially impact resort revenue.
- **Elevator inspection**: States require regular elevator inspections and certifications; non-compliant elevators must be taken out of service.
- **Building permits and occupancy**: Renovations, additions, or conversion of spaces require permits; unpermitted work discovered post-acquisition creates retroactive compliance obligations.

### Labor and Union Considerations

Hotels are among the most labor-intensive commercial real estate investments, with labor typically representing 30%–45% of total revenue in full-service assets. Labor regulatory considerations include:

- **Minimum wage and tip credit**: Hotel labor is subject to federal and state minimum wage laws, with significant jurisdictional variation. Cities such as New York, San Francisco, and Chicago have hotel-specific minimum wages in some cases. Tip credit rules affect cost for F&B workers.
- **Union agreements**: Urban full-service hotels in major gateway markets (New York, Chicago, Los Angeles, Boston, Seattle, Honolulu) are frequently unionized under UNITE HERE or other hospitality unions. Union Collective Bargaining Agreements (CBAs) impose specific wage scales, benefit requirements, scheduling rules, and grievance procedures that materially affect operating costs and management flexibility. A buyer assuming a union hotel should review the CBA as a priority due diligence item.
- **Scheduling laws**: Several jurisdictions have enacted "fair workweek" or predictive scheduling laws that require advance notice of schedules and additional pay for last-minute schedule changes — these add labor cost and operational constraints.
- **Worker retention ordinances**: Some cities have hotel worker retention ordinances requiring that a new hotel owner retain existing employees for a transition period (typically 90 days) following a change in ownership or management contract.

### Short-Term Rental Competition and Regulation

The growth of short-term rental (STR) platforms — Airbnb, Vrbo, and others — has created a new competitive dynamic in hospitality markets. In leisure-oriented markets (beach, mountain, and urban destination cities), STR supply can represent a significant percentage of effective lodging supply and directly competes with hotels for leisure demand. The degree to which STR compresses hotel RevPAR depends on the regulatory environment in each jurisdiction:

- **Restrictive jurisdictions** (New York City, San Francisco, Paris) heavily limit STR via permit requirements, primary residency restrictions, or per-night caps — reducing STR competitive impact on hotels.
- **Permissive jurisdictions** allow widespread STR with minimal licensing requirements, creating meaningful competitive supply that is not tracked in traditional hotel supply pipeline data.
- **Regulation trajectory**: STR regulation continues to evolve rapidly as municipalities respond to housing affordability concerns, hotel industry lobbying, and neighborhood opposition. An underwriting assumption about STR supply levels in a leisure market should account for regulatory change risk in both directions.

In underwriting leisure and resort hotels, buyers should assess the existing STR inventory via AirDNA or similar platforms, monitor the local regulatory environment, and sensitize RevPAR assumptions to scenarios of both growing and contracting STR supply.

### Entitlement and Development Approvals

New hotel development or significant hotel conversions typically require local land use approvals. Common entitlement considerations:

- **Zoning and conditional use permits**: Hotels are not always permitted by right in commercial zones; a [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]] may be required, particularly for new full-service hotels with significant F&B and event programming.
- **Height, density, and design review**: Urban hotel projects are often subject to design review, view corridor restrictions, and height limits that constrain product size and configuration.
- **Adaptive reuse**: Converting non-hotel buildings to hotel use may require [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]] or [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] in addition to building permit approvals for the change of occupancy. See [[Adaptive Reuse]].
- **Environmental review**: Large hotel projects in states with CEQA (California) or SEPA (Washington) equivalents require environmental review that can extend timelines and impose mitigation conditions. See [[Environmental Review Overview]].
