# Hospitality — Key Metrics

Hotel performance is measured through a set of industry-specific operating metrics distinct from other commercial real estate asset classes. These metrics are standardized across the industry via the STR (CoStar's hospitality benchmarking platform) and the Uniform System of Accounts for the Lodging Industry (USALI), enabling market-level competitive benchmarking that is unusually transparent relative to other CRE sectors. Every metric below should be read against competitive set benchmarks — absolute levels matter less than penetration relative to comp set peers.

---

### ADR — Average Daily Rate

**Definition.** The average revenue earned per occupied room during a specified period. ADR measures the pricing power of the hotel, reflecting the blended rate across all room types, booking channels, and rate plans (corporate, leisure, group, and transient).

**Use.** ADR is the primary rate management metric. Growth in ADR above inflation signals improving pricing power, a competitive position above comp set peers, or a successful rate optimization program. In underwriting, ADR growth is typically assumed at 2%–4% annually in steady-state, with above-trend growth modeled during a repositioning or post-renovation period. See [[Hospitality — Underwriting Norms]].

**Formula.**

Plain: ADR = Rooms Revenue / Number of Rooms Sold

LaTeX: $\text{ADR} = \frac{\text{Rooms Revenue}}{\text{Rooms Sold}}$

---

### Occupancy

**Definition.** The percentage of available rooms that are occupied (sold) during a specified period.

**Use.** Occupancy reflects demand capture efficiency. A hotel at 80% occupancy has sold 80 out of every 100 available rooms on average over the period. Occupancy is sensitive to competitive supply additions, seasonal patterns, and macroeconomic conditions. In underwriting, stabilized occupancy assumptions should be benchmarked against the competitive set and trended against the Smith Travel Research (STR) market reports for the submarket. Extended-stay and select-service properties often target 75%–85% stabilized occupancy; resort properties may have peak occupancies exceeding 90% in season and sub-50% in off-season.

**Formula.**

Plain: Occupancy = Rooms Sold / Rooms Available

LaTeX: $\text{Occupancy} = \frac{\text{Rooms Sold}}{\text{Rooms Available}}$

---

### RevPAR — Revenue per Available Room

**Definition.** The single most important top-line metric in hospitality, combining both occupancy and ADR into one measure of revenue generation efficiency per available room. RevPAR is the standard yardstick for market comparisons, year-over-year performance tracking, and competitive set benchmarking.

**Use.** RevPAR growth is the primary driver of NOI growth in stabilized hotels. A 1% increase in RevPAR translates directly to a 1% increase in rooms revenue, and because a large portion of hotel costs are fixed (labor minimums, utilities, insurance, property taxes), the incremental NOI from a $1 RevPAR gain can be $0.50–$0.80 depending on the hotel's cost structure. This operating leverage makes RevPAR growth projections the most consequential assumption in a hotel pro forma. See [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]].

**Formula.**

Plain: RevPAR = ADR × Occupancy
  OR
RevPAR = Rooms Revenue / Rooms Available

LaTeX: $\text{RevPAR} = \text{ADR} \times \text{Occupancy}$

$\text{RevPAR} = \frac{\text{Rooms Revenue}}{\text{Rooms Available}}$

---

### TRevPAR — Total Revenue per Available Room

**Definition.** An extension of RevPAR that includes all revenue generated by the hotel — not just rooms — divided by available rooms. TRevPAR captures F&B revenue, parking, spa, golf, meeting room rental, resort fees, and any other ancillary streams.

**Use.** TRevPAR is the more relevant top-line metric for full-service and resort hotels where non-rooms revenue can represent 30%–50% of total revenue. For select-service and limited-service hotels with minimal ancillary revenue, TRevPAR is close to RevPAR. In underwriting resort assets, TRevPAR growth assumptions require modeling each revenue department separately — spa demand, F&B capture rates, golf rounds — rather than a single RevPAR growth rate.

**Formula.**

Plain: TRevPAR = Total Revenue / Rooms Available

LaTeX: $\text{TRevPAR} = \frac{\text{Total Revenue}}{\text{Rooms Available}}$

---

### GOP — Gross Operating Profit and GOPPAR

**Definition.** GOP is revenue less all operating expenses excluding fixed charges (management fee, property taxes, insurance, FF&E reserve, debt service). GOPPAR (GOP per Available Room) expresses that profit on a per-room basis for cross-property comparison.

**Use.** GOP and GOPPAR measure the operational efficiency of the hotel management team. They are the standard basis for calculating operator incentive management fees and for benchmarking against peer properties. A select-service hotel generating $50 GOPPAR vs. a comp-set average of $45 GOPPAR demonstrates superior management efficiency. GOP margin — GOP as a percentage of total revenue — is the key efficiency ratio: select-service hotels should achieve 40%–55% GOP margin; full-service hotels typically run 25%–40% due to the F&B drag.

**Formula.**

Plain: GOP = Total Revenue − Departmental Expenses − Undistributed Operating Expenses
GOPPAR = GOP / Rooms Available

LaTeX: $\text{GOP} = \text{Total Revenue} - \text{Departmental Expenses} - \text{Undistributed Expenses}$

$\text{GOPPAR} = \frac{\text{GOP}}{\text{Rooms Available}}$

---

### NOI and Flow-Through

**Definition.** Net Operating Income in hospitality equals GOP less fixed charges: management fee, property taxes, insurance, FF&E reserve, and non-operating expenses. Flow-through (also called "flow" or "incremental margin") measures what percentage of incremental revenue growth falls to NOI.

**Use.** Because hotel fixed costs are substantial (insurance, property taxes, minimum labor), incremental revenue growth flows to NOI at a higher-than-proportional rate — a characteristic called operating leverage. A typical select-service hotel might show 50%–70% flow-through on RevPAR gains: for every additional $1.00 of rooms revenue, $0.50–$0.70 reaches NOI. This lever works in reverse in downturns, making downside sensitivity analysis essential. NOI is the basis for [[Going-In Cap Rate]] and [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] valuation. See [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]].

**Formula.**

Plain: NOI = GOP − Management Fee − Property Taxes − Insurance − FF&E Reserve

LaTeX: $\text{NOI} = \text{GOP} - \text{Management Fee} - \text{Taxes} - \text{Insurance} - \text{FF\&E Reserve}$

Flow-Through: $\text{Flow-Through} = \frac{\Delta \text{NOI}}{\Delta \text{Revenue}}$

---

### RevPAR Index / Penetration — STR Benchmarking

**Definition.** The RevPAR Index (or Market Penetration Index) measures a hotel's RevPAR performance relative to a defined competitive set, expressed as a percentage. An index of 100 means the hotel is capturing exactly its fair share of competitive set RevPAR. An index above 100 indicates outperformance; below 100 indicates underperformance.

**Use.** The RevPAR index is the most important competitive benchmarking tool in hospitality. It reveals whether a hotel's performance changes are driven by market-wide forces (RevPAR index stable while absolute RevPAR falls = market softness, not underperformance) or by hotel-specific factors (RevPAR index falling while market holds = competitive share loss). In underwriting a value-add hotel, a buyer may model a RevPAR index improvement from 90 to 105 as a result of renovation, brand upgrade, or management change — this is a core component of the return thesis. STR (now part of CoStar) provides monthly market reports with index data. See [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]].

**Formula.**

Plain: RevPAR Index = (Hotel RevPAR / Comp Set RevPAR) × 100

LaTeX: $\text{RevPAR Index} = \frac{\text{Hotel RevPAR}}{\text{Comp Set RevPAR}} \times 100$

### FF&E Reserve

**Definition.** A mandatory funded reserve — typically 4%–6% of total revenue — set aside annually to fund the replacement of furniture, fixtures, equipment, soft goods, technology, and operating equipment that depreciates over time. The FF&E reserve is not a discretionary capital improvement fund; it is a contractually required component of most franchise and management agreements.

**Use.** In hospitality underwriting, the FF&E reserve is treated as an operating expense line item (deducted before NOI), not as below-the-line capex. This convention is specific to hotels and reflects the recurring, mandatory nature of the expenditure. Failing to fully fund the FF&E reserve is a common seller behavior that inflates trailing NOI; buyers must normalize for underfunded reserves as part of [[Expense Normalization]]. The reserve funds ongoing soft-goods refreshes (linens, carpets, case goods) and larger periodic replacements (elevators, HVAC systems, POS systems), and is distinct from the one-time PIP capital required at brand transition. See [[Reserves for Replacement]] and [[FF&E]].

**Formula.**

Plain: FF&E Reserve = Total Revenue × Reserve Percentage (typically 4%–6%)

LaTeX: $\text{FF\&E Reserve} = \text{Total Revenue} \times r$ where $r \in [0.04, 0.06]$
