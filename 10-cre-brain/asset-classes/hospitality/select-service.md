# Select-Service Hotels

## Overview

Select-service hotels are the institutional core of the hospitality sector — the product type most favored by institutional investors, the most liquid transaction market, and the most predictable from a cash flow standpoint. These hotels, typically 90–200 keys, occupy the upper-midscale and upscale segments of the brand hierarchy. They offer a limited but well-defined amenity set: a complimentary breakfast (or a food-and-beverage pantry), a fitness center, often a pool, and in some cases a small bar or grab-and-go market. What they deliberately omit is the full restaurant, room service, ballroom, and large meeting infrastructure that defines full-service — and in doing so, they achieve dramatically better GOP margins.

The select-service operating model is deliberately simple. The absence of full F&B operations eliminates the single largest source of cost complexity and labor intensity in hotel operations. With a lean staffing model, a well-run select-service hotel can achieve GOP margins of 40%–55%, meaning nearly half of every revenue dollar reaches the operational profit line before fixed charges. This flow-through efficiency means RevPAR gains translate rapidly to NOI growth, and RevPAR declines — while painful — are less likely to push the asset below debt service coverage thresholds than in a full-service hotel with higher fixed costs.

Strong brand loyalty programs are the backbone of select-service demand: Marriott Bonvoy, Hilton Honors, IHG One Rewards, and World of Hyatt each drive a substantial share of occupancy through member bookings, reducing dependence on OTA channels and their 15%–25% commission costs. This brand-driven distribution advantage is why select-service buyers almost universally require brand affiliation and why unbranded select-service assets trade at material discounts.

## Demand Profile

Select-service demand skews toward business transient travelers — road warriors, project workers, consultants, and sales representatives who value reliable room quality, loyalty point accumulation, and proximity to employment nodes over F&B or meeting amenities. Breakfast inclusion (where offered) eliminates a friction point for this guest, and the consistent brand experience reduces the risk of a bad hotel night. Weekend occupancy is supplemented by leisure guests who price-shop against full-service alternatives and choose value-conscious travel.

Competitive set benchmarking via STR is particularly important in select-service because the product type is commoditized enough that RevPAR index performance directly measures the competitiveness of the specific hotel versus its comp set — brand flag, RevPAR index positioning, and proximity to demand generators are the primary differentiators.

## Typical Metrics and Underwriting

Select-service is the most underwritten subtype in institutional hospitality, with broadly accepted norms:

- **Going-in cap rates** commonly range from 6.0%–8.5% for stabilized select-service in primary and secondary markets, varying by market tier, brand flag, physical vintage, and competitive supply dynamics.
- **Stabilized occupancy**: 70%–82% for well-positioned select-service in demand-deep markets; lower in oversupplied corridors.
- **GOP margin**: 40%–55% for efficiently operated select-service; assets at the lower end of this range typically have sub-optimal brand flags, labor agreements, or market position issues worth investigating.
- **FF&E reserve**: 4%–5% of total revenue; brand minimums are typically 4%, but lenders often underwrite 5% for conservative modeling.
- **LTV**: 55%–65%; CMBS is a common execution venue for stabilized select-service assets.

Value-add theses are straightforward: renovation-driven ADR uplift (room product upgrade), RevPAR index improvement through revenue management and direct booking focus, flag upgrade (e.g., converting a Comfort Inn to a Holiday Inn Express or an IHG flag to a Courtyard), or operational efficiency improvement in a mismanaged asset. ## Ownership and Flags

Select-service is the most democratized segment of institutional hospitality ownership. Buyers range from large hotel REITs (Apple Hospitality REIT, Chatham Lodging, Summit Hotel Properties, RLJ Lodging Trust) to private equity platforms (Carey Watermark, Ares, Davidson Kempner), regional syndicators, family operators, and high-net-worth individual investors. Portfolio transactions — buying 5–20 select-service assets simultaneously — are common because individual deal sizes are manageable and portfolio scale drives operator efficiency.

The dominant flags are:
- **Marriott**: Courtyard by Marriott, Fairfield Inn & Suites, SpringHill Suites, AC Hotels (upper select)
- **Hilton**: Hilton Garden Inn, Hampton Inn, Hampton Inn & Suites, Tru by Hilton
- **IHG**: Holiday Inn Express, Holiday Inn Express & Suites, avid Hotels
- **Hyatt**: Hyatt Place, Hyatt House (dual select/extended-stay positioning)
- **Wyndham**: La Quinta Inn & Suites (upper-midscale bridge to select)

Flag selection matters materially for a given market: brand loyalty concentration (corporate accounts in a given city may heavily favor Marriott or Hilton), brand penetration in the competitive set, and the brand's historic capital support (system-wide renovation programs, technology investment) all affect RevPAR performance.
