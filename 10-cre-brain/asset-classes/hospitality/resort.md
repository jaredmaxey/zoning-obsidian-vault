# Resort Hotels

## Overview

Resort hotels differ from all other hotel subtypes in a fundamental way: the destination itself — the beach, the mountain, the desert landscape, the golf courses, the spa — is the primary reason guests book, not the brand flag or the price point. The hotel is the access mechanism to the destination experience, which gives resort properties a degree of pricing power and differentiation that transient-focused hotels can never fully replicate. This destination character also means resort performance is driven by the quality of the location and the strength of the amenity programming rather than purely by RevPAR management and brand loyalty.

Resort hotels are typically the largest and most capital-intensive assets in the hotel universe, with significant infrastructure beyond the room product: multiple pool complexes, a full-service spa, one or more food and beverage outlets (often three to five separate concepts for larger resorts), meeting and event space (resort weddings and corporate retreats are major revenue drivers), golf courses, beach clubs, ski-in/ski-out access, or other activity infrastructure. This amenity depth generates the highest TRevPAR of any hotel subtype — often $300–$700+ per available room daily in top-tier destination markets — but also the highest operating cost per key.

Revenue composition is the most diversified in hospitality: for a full-resort property, rooms may represent only 50%–65% of total revenue, with F&B (25%–35%), spa (5%–10%), golf, retail, and recreation making up the remainder. This means RevPAR understates the revenue opportunity, and TRevPAR is the correct benchmarking metric. Underwriting a resort requires a full departmental model for each revenue stream, each with its own seasonality, labor intensity, and margin profile.

## Demand Profile

Resort demand is almost entirely leisure-driven, though many full-service resorts have developed significant group/meetings segments (corporate retreats, association conferences, incentive travel programs) to extend the revenue season and reduce seasonality risk. Key demand drivers include:

- **Geographic accessibility**: Drive-to leisure resorts (within 2–5 hours of a major metro) tend to be more occupancy-resilient because the demand base is broader and less dependent on airline capacity. Fly-to destination resorts have higher RevPAR potential but greater exposure to airline pricing, capacity constraints, and international travel competition.
- **Seasonality**: Beach resorts peak in summer; ski resorts peak in winter; desert/spa resorts often peak in spring and fall. Understanding the seasonal revenue distribution is essential — a Florida Gulf Coast resort may generate 55% of annual revenue in December through April. Off-season operating cost management and demand diversification (weddings, corporate groups) are critical to full-year NOI.
- **Experiential positioning**: In an era of social media-driven travel decisions, resorts compete on Instagram-worthy moments and unique experiences as much as on amenity inventory. Pools, F&B concepts, and recreational programming designed for social sharing are increasingly a competitive requirement.
- **Branded residence adjacency**: Many luxury and upper-upscale resort developments incorporate branded residential sales (condos, fractional, whole ownership) that generate upfront capital proceeds and ongoing resort fee revenue. This component requires separate underwriting and feasibility analysis.

## Typical Metrics and Underwriting

Resort underwriting is the most complex in hospitality, requiring modeling of each ancillary revenue department independently:

- **Going-in cap rates**: Range widely — 6.0%–10.0%+ — depending on market tier, brand, seasonality, and whether embedded development rights or residential components affect value. Trophy coastal and mountain resorts may trade on a per-key basis ($500,000–$2M+ per key) when cap rate methodology understates embedded value.
- **TRevPAR**: The primary revenue benchmark; in top-tier destination resorts, TRevPAR of $400–$700+ per available room per day is achievable in peak season.
- **Seasonality modeling**: A monthly revenue model (rather than annualized averages) is necessary for resort underwriting because the peak-to-trough occupancy ratio can be 3:1 or higher. Cash flow modeling must show the hotel can fund off-season operations and debt service from peak-season surplus.
- **F&B and ancillary margins**: Each revenue department should be modeled separately; resort F&B margins are typically better than urban full-service F&B because captive guests (no outside dining alternatives on a private beach) have lower price sensitivity.
- **Exit valuation**: Resort exits are often priced on a per-key basis for trophy properties, with the buyer and seller negotiating embedded value premiums for location, brand, and development rights. This makes exit cap rate derivation more art than science.

## Ownership and Flags

Resort ownership is the most concentrated among large-capital institutional investors because deal sizes and operational complexity limit smaller buyers. Blackstone, KSL Capital Partners, Starwood Capital Group, Ares Management, and Sycamore Entertainment Group are among the largest resort acquirers. Sovereign wealth funds have been significant buyers of trophy coastal and island resorts as long-duration inflation-hedging assets.

Key resort flags:
- **Ultra-luxury**: Four Seasons (independent), Aman (independent), Rosewood, Banyan Tree
- **Luxury (chain)**: Marriott (Ritz-Carlton, St. Regis, W Escape, Edition), Hilton (Waldorf Astoria, LXR, Conrad), Hyatt (Park Hyatt, Alila, Andaz, Miraval), IHG (Six Senses, InterContinental)
- **Upper-upscale resort**: Marriott (Westin Resort, Renaissance Resort, JW Marriott Resort), Hilton (Signia, Curio Resort), Hyatt (Hyatt Regency Resort, Grand Hyatt Resort)
- **Independents and soft brands**: A large portion of destination resort inventory operates independently or under soft-brand collections (Autograph, Tapestry, SLH, Design Hotels) to preserve property identity.

Resort development activity is constrained by the scarcity of available beachfront, waterfront, and mountain land, making acquisitions the primary entry strategy for most institutional buyers. Ground-up resort development — when undertaken — is typically 5–10 year projects with significant entitlement, infrastructure, and pre-opening risk.
