# Hospitality — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Underwriting a hotel differs fundamentally from underwriting any other commercial real estate asset class because revenue is not contractual. There are no leases, no guaranteed rent rolls, and no tenant credit to backstop cash flows. Instead, every room is re-priced daily against a competitive set, and NOI is a direct function of operational execution. This means the underwriter must simultaneously be a real estate analyst and a hospitality operations analyst — assessing market RevPAR trends, competitive positioning, management quality, brand contributions, and expense efficiency before arriving at a stabilized NOI figure.

A hotel pro forma begins with rooms revenue (ADR × occupancy × available room nights) and then builds ancillary revenue streams (F&B, parking, spa, meeting room rental, etc.) for full-service and resort assets. Expenses are broken down departmentally — rooms department, F&B department, administrative and general, sales and marketing, property operations and maintenance, utilities — and then below-the-line items (management fee, franchise fee, FF&E reserve, property taxes, insurance) are deducted to arrive at NOI. This multi-department structure means hospitality pro formas are substantially more complex than a simple income/expense sheet. See [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] and [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]].

The most common underwriting approach is a discounted cash flow over a 5–10 year hold, supplemented by a terminal value derived from an exit cap applied to Year 1 stabilized NOI at exit. Direct capitalization is used primarily for quick sanity checks or for simple, stabilized limited-service assets. See [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] and [[Direct Capitalization]].

## Operating Model

### Management and Franchise Agreements

The typical hotel operates under two separate agreements that a buyer must understand and either assume or renegotiate at acquisition. The **franchise agreement** grants the owner the right to use the brand's flag, reservation system, loyalty program, and brand standards for a specified term (typically 10–20 years). The franchisee (owner) pays a royalty fee — commonly 4%–7% of rooms revenue — plus a reservation fee, loyalty program assessment, and other system fees. The franchise agreement is a key value driver: a strong flag materially increases reservation channel contribution and loyalty-program-driven occupancy. See [[Brand vs. Independent]].

The **management agreement** engages a third-party hotel operator to run day-to-day operations in exchange for a base management fee (typically 2%–4% of total revenue) plus an incentive management fee (typically 10%–20% of GOP above a threshold). Management agreements are typically long-term (10–20 years), often with renewal options, and may include performance tests that allow the owner to terminate the manager if RevPAR or GOP thresholds are not met. The negotiation of management agreement terms — particularly termination rights, performance tests, and key money — is one of the most important levers in a hotel acquisition or recapitalization. Many select-service properties self-manage under a franchise, eliminating the management fee layer.

For underwriting purposes, both fees are treated as operating expenses below GOP, reducing NOI. Because these fees are revenue-based (not NOI-based), they do not decrease when profitability drops — making them fixed-cost-like in downturns.

### Brand Standards and Property Improvement Plans (PIPs)

Every franchise agreement requires the owner to maintain the hotel to brand standards covering room product, exterior, common areas, technology systems, and operating procedures. When a hotel is acquired, the brand will conduct an inspection and issue a **Property Improvement Plan (PIP)** — a capital improvement mandate the buyer must complete within a specified timeframe (typically 18–36 months) to retain the flag. PIP costs vary enormously: select-service PIPs in the range of $5,000–$20,000 per key are common for a well-maintained asset; a full gut renovation of a full-service hotel can exceed $50,000–$80,000 per key.

Underwriting must account for PIP costs as part of the acquisition cost basis or as a near-term capital outflow in the first years of the hold. A PIP that is not funded in the acquisition underwriting is a common source of surprise losses. Best practice is to obtain a brand PIP letter as part of due diligence before finalizing pricing. The PIP obligation is also a negotiating lever: sellers may provide a price concession or an escrow to cover PIP costs. See [[FF&E]] and [[Reserves for Replacement]].

### FF&E Reserve

The FF&E reserve — funded at 4%–6% of total revenue — is a contractual requirement under most franchise and management agreements. Unlike capex for structural improvements, FF&E covers furniture, fixtures, technology, soft goods, and operating equipment with useful lives of 5–10 years. In hotel underwriting, the FF&E reserve is treated as an operating expense (it reduces NOI) because it represents a recurring and mandatory capital outflow, not an optional capex. Hotels that defer FF&E funding degrade the guest experience over time, ultimately eroding ADR and occupancy relative to competitive set.

## Department and Expense Norms

Hotel operating statements follow the Uniform System of Accounts for the Lodging Industry (USALI), which is the hospitality industry's equivalent of GAAP for hotel financials. The departmental structure is standardized, enabling apples-to-apples benchmarking across properties. Key departmental norms (as a percentage of department revenue, varies by market and vintage):

| Department | Typical Labor + Direct Cost % of Dept Revenue | Notes |
|------------|-----------------------------------------------|-------|
| Rooms department | 25%–40% | Includes housekeeping, front desk, reservations |
| F&B department (if applicable) | 65%–80% | High labor and food cost; often a loss leader in full-service |
| Administrative & General | 8%–12% of total revenue | Corporate overhead, legal, accounting |
| Sales & Marketing | 4%–8% of total revenue | Advertising, OTA commissions, sales team |
| Property Operations & Maintenance | 4%–7% of total revenue | Engineering, preventive maintenance |
| Utilities | 3%–6% of total revenue | Climate-dependent; resort and full-service higher |

**Expense normalization** for hotel acquisitions (per [[Expense Normalization]]) must address: management fee normalization (actual vs. arm's-length market rate), FF&E reserve adequacy, PIPs deferred by seller, franchise fee changes on flag transfer, seasonality in trailing twelve-month (T-12) financials, and any non-recurring or owner-specific expense items. The T-12 and T-3 periods (see [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]]) are critical for understanding stabilized run-rate performance versus seasonal or cyclical aberrations.

**Sensitivity analysis** (see [[Sensitivity Analysis]]) on hotel underwriting should test at minimum: ADR ±$10–$20, occupancy ±5–10 points, franchise fee changes, management fee changes, and exit cap rate ±50–100 bps. Given the operating leverage inherent in fixed hotel costs, a 5-point occupancy decline can translate to a 15–25% NOI decline in a select-service hotel. Stress testing against a recession RevPAR scenario (see [[Stress Testing]]) — typically modeled as a 15–25% RevPAR decline over 12–18 months — is standard for institutional-quality underwriting.
