# Hospitality — Subtypes

This note profiles the six primary hospitality subtypes. Each subtype has a distinct demand profile, capital requirement, operating model, and position in the institutional ownership spectrum. See [[Brand vs. Independent]] for the cross-cutting brand alignment decision that applies across all subtypes.

---

### Full-Service Hotels

**Profile.** Full-service hotels are typically 200–500+ keys and offer a comprehensive amenity set: one or more food and beverage outlets (restaurant, bar, room service), significant meeting and event space, a full fitness center, and often a pool and concierge. These assets compete for business transient, group, and leisure demand simultaneously. ADR is highest in this segment, but operating complexity and cost structure are also highest: labor intensity (F&B staffing, banquets, front desk, housekeeping), departmental management, and brand standards all compound. GOP margins are typically in the 25–40% range, significantly below select-service, because of the revenue leakage through F&B and amenity operations.

**Deal Profile.** Full-service assets trade at a wide range of cap rates, tighter in gateway markets and wider in secondary and tertiary markets. Transactions are most common in the $30M–$300M+ range. Value-add theses are prevalent: a buyer targeting a brand conversion, F&B concept refresh, or meeting space renovation can significantly re-rate ADR and group capture. Underwriting requires deep analysis of the group booking pace (forward book) and a detailed departmental pro forma beyond just rooms revenue. See [[Value-Add Acquisition]] and [[Opportunistic Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** Institutional owners include REITs (e.g., Host Hotels, Pebblebrook), private equity (Blackstone, Starwood Capital), sovereign wealth funds, and family offices. Common flags include Marriott (Marriott, Renaissance, Autograph), Hilton (Hilton, DoubleTree, Curio), and Hyatt. Many urban full-service properties operate as independents or under soft-brand collections (Autograph, Tapestry, SLH) to retain identity while accessing distribution.

---

### Select-Service Hotels

**Profile.** Select-service is the institutional sweet spot of the hospitality sector, representing the highest volume of institutional transaction activity by deal count. These hotels — typically 90–200 keys — offer a limited F&B program (free breakfast, grab-and-go, or a single limited bar), no meeting space or minimal meeting rooms, and a standard amenity set (fitness center, pool optional). The operating model is intentionally lean: departmental complexity is low, labor-to-revenue ratios are favorable, and GOP margins commonly reach 40–55%, generating strong flow-through from RevPAR gains to NOI. ADR is moderate — below full-service in the same market — but occupancy is often higher because of loyalty program demand and price-sensitive business transient capture.

**Deal Profile.** Select-service deals are the most liquid segment of the hotel market. Assets trade in the $10M–$80M range most commonly, with portfolios transacting above $500M. Going-in caps are typically tighter than full-service of equivalent market tier, reflecting higher margin predictability. Brand affiliation is nearly universal: buyers almost always require a Marriott, Hilton, IHG, or Hyatt flag as a condition of acquisition. Common theses include renovation-driven ADR uplift, flag upgrades, and RevPAR index penetration improvement. See [[Core-Plus Acquisition]] and [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** The broadest ownership universe in hospitality: institutional REITs (Apple Hospitality, Chatham Lodging, Summit Hotel Properties), private equity, high-net-worth syndicators, and family operators. Dominant flags include Marriott Courtyard, Fairfield, and Residence Inn; Hilton Garden Inn, Hampton Inn, and Homewood Suites; IHG Holiday Inn Express; and Hyatt Place and Hyatt House.

### Limited-Service Hotels

**Profile.** Limited-service hotels — economy and budget properties — offer minimal amenities: no restaurant, no meeting space, and a basic room product. These assets typically operate with the smallest staff footprint per key of any segment and can achieve positive NOI at relatively low occupancy levels (40–55%) due to lean cost structures. ADR is the lowest of any segment in a given market, targeting price-sensitive leisure travelers, extended road warriors, and transient workers. Brand loyalty programs are a meaningful demand driver even at this level. The room product is modest by design, and capital requirements on the rooms side are lower than upper-midscale, but brand PIPs can still require material investment at sale or flag renewal.

**Deal Profile.** Limited-service assets frequently trade below $10M per property and are common in secondary, tertiary, and highway-interchange markets. Cap rates are wider, reflecting higher brand/market risk and thinner institutional buyer pools. Portfolio aggregation is a common strategy: assembling 10–30 economy-flag properties enables institutional-scale exits to REITs or private equity. Distressed and value-add plays are more prevalent here, particularly for assets with deferred maintenance or underperforming flags. See [[Distressed Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** Ownership is dominated by individual operators, franchisee families, and small regional investors rather than institutional REITs. Economy flags include Marriott's Fairfield (upper end), Choice Hotels (Comfort Inn, Sleep Inn, Econo Lodge), Wyndham (Super 8, Days Inn, La Quinta), and IHG (Holiday Inn Express, Avid). Some properties operate as independents in markets too small to support a flag.

### Extended-Stay Hotels

**Profile.** Extended-stay hotels are designed for guests staying 5+ nights, often weekly or monthly, typically targeting project workers, corporate relocations, government contractors, and insurance/disaster displacement. The product is suite-based with in-room kitchens or kitchenettes, and the amenity set is intentionally residential: laundry facilities, a small fitness center, and sometimes a limited grocery offering. Extended-stay exhibits more occupancy stability than transient hotels: weekly rates are lower on a per-night basis than nightly rates, but demand is "stickier" — guests don't check out after one bad day in the economy. During recessions, extended-stay occupancy declines are consistently smaller than other segments, making it the most defensive hotel product type.

**Deal Profile.** Extended-stay assets commonly trade at going-in caps 50–100 bps tighter than comparable select-service in the same market, reflecting superior occupancy resilience. Assets range from 80 to 150+ keys. Common flags include Marriott (Residence Inn, TownePlace Suites, Element), Hilton (Homewood Suites, Home2 Suites), and Hyatt (Hyatt House). Underwriting emphasizes weekly and monthly rate mix, length-of-stay distribution, and the stability of the local demand base (corporate accounts, construction projects, healthcare institutions). See [[Core-Plus Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** Institutional ownership is significant, including Apple Hospitality REIT (largest owner of Homewood and Residence Inn), Chatham Lodging, and private equity. Extended-stay is also a favored target of syndicators given the cash-flow predictability. Development of new extended-stay (particularly Home2 Suites and TownePlace Suites) has been active in supply-constrained Sun Belt markets.

### Resort Hotels

**Profile.** Resort hotels are destination-driven assets where the property itself — its location, amenities, and experience — is the primary demand driver rather than brand flag or corporate demand. Key amenity categories include pool complexes, spa, golf, beach or ski access, multiple F&B concepts, and recreational programming. Revenue composition is the broadest of any hotel segment: rooms revenue may represent only 50–70% of total revenue, with F&B, spa, retail, golf, and ancillary generating the remainder, making TRevPAR the more relevant top-line metric. ADR is typically the highest of any subtype in a given destination market, but occupancy seasonality is also the most pronounced: a peak season representing 30–40% of annual room nights may generate 50–60% of annual revenue.

**Deal Profile.** Resort transactions are among the largest and most complex in hospitality, often ranging from $50M to $1B+. Cap rates vary widely by location, brand, and RevPAR profile; trophy resort trades are sometimes priced on a per-key basis rather than cap rate, especially when embedded land value or development rights are significant. The most common institutional theses are: (1) ADR repositioning through capital investment and brand upgrade; (2) amenity expansion (adding spa, F&B concept, or branded residences); and (3) mixed-use entitlement plays where hotel use enables adjacent residential or retail development. Financing for resorts is typically more bespoke — [[Bridge Loan]] or life company debt — as CMBS lending for high-ADR resort assets is less common. See [[Opportunistic Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** Institutional ownership includes Blackstone, KSL Capital, Starwood Capital, and a range of family offices and sovereign wealth funds with long-duration capital comfortable with seasonality. Common brands include Marriott (Ritz-Carlton, St. Regis, W, Westin), Hilton (Waldorf Astoria, Conrad, LXR), Hyatt (Park Hyatt, Alila, Miraval), and Four Seasons and Rosewood (independent luxury). Soft brands and unaffiliated independents are also common in resort destinations.

### Boutique & Lifestyle Hotels

**Profile.** Boutique and lifestyle hotels compete on design, authenticity, and experiential hospitality rather than flag loyalty points and system-wide distribution. They are typically 50–200 keys, deeply embedded in their local context (adaptive reuse of historic buildings is common), and command a significant ADR premium over branded competition in the same market when positioned correctly. The trade-off is distribution: without a major loyalty program, boutique hotels depend on direct bookings, OTA (online travel agency) channels, press coverage, and word-of-mouth — all of which require skilled and consistent marketing investment. Operating margins are often lower than select-service comparables because labor and programming costs are higher as a percentage of revenue.

**Deal Profile.** Boutique hotel acquisitions are more idiosyncratic than branded hotel deals, requiring an operator-first underwriting approach: the performance potential is highly dependent on the caliber and vision of the management team. Many institutional buyers acquire boutique assets with the intent to affiliate with a soft-brand collection (Marriott Autograph, Hilton Curio, Hyatt Unbound Collection) to gain distribution without sacrificing identity. Adaptive reuse development is a common entry strategy, often paired with historic tax credits. See [[Adaptive Reuse]] and [[Value-Add Acquisition]].

**Owners and Flags.** Ownership is fragmented: independent operators (Commune Hotels, Proper Hospitality, Graduate Hotels), private equity, family offices, and high-net-worth individuals with passion for design. Soft brand collections from the major flags (Autograph Collection, Tapestry Collection, SLH, Design Hotels) increasingly capture boutique assets that want distribution support while retaining independence. See [[Brand vs. Independent]] for a full comparison of the brand affiliation decision.
