# Retail

## Overview

Retail real estate encompasses a broad spectrum of commercial properties purpose-built to house the exchange of goods and services between tenants and their consumer base. The asset class ranges from single-tenant freestanding buildings occupied by national quick-service restaurant or pharmacy chains to multi-million-square-foot regional malls anchored by department stores. Within institutional portfolios, retail has historically served as a yield-oriented, income-stable allocation — particularly for necessity-based formats such as [[Grocery-Anchored Retail]] and [[Single-Tenant Net Lease (STNL)]] — while discretionary and experiential formats carry higher return requirements commensurate with greater obsolescence risk.

The decade following 2015 fundamentally restructured investor sentiment toward retail. E-commerce penetration accelerated the winnowing of physical retail into two survivable poles: convenience and necessity (grocery, pharmacy, discount, QSR, service-oriented), and experiential or destination-driven uses that online channels cannot replicate (food and beverage, fitness, entertainment, medical/dental). Formats caught between these poles — mid-tier department stores, apparel-heavy enclosed malls, lifestyle centers without critical mass — experienced severe occupancy and rent erosion. As of mid-2020s, the surviving retail landscape is characterized by leaner GLA per capita, stronger necessity-based tenancy, shorter lease terms relative to the prior decade's norms, and an ongoing repositioning of failed retail boxes into mixed-use, last-mile logistics, or multifamily uses. Cap rate spreads between commodity strip and high-barrier grocery-anchored product have widened materially, reflecting bifurcated investor conviction.

Institutional capital continues to seek well-located, grocery- or service-anchored open-air centers in infill markets, single-tenant NNN product with investment-grade credit and long lease term, and select value-add opportunities in power centers where big-box vacancies can be redivided or repurposed. Secondary and tertiary market enclosed malls are broadly shunned by institutional players absent substantial distress pricing. The retail thesis in 2026 is fundamentally locational and tenant-credit-driven: the right format in the right trade area with the right anchor wins; generic product in over-retailed suburban corridors does not.

## Subtypes

See [[Retail — Subtypes]] for full profiles. In brief:

- **[[Power Center]]** — Large open-air center (250,000–600,000 SF) anchored by three or more category-dominant big-box tenants (home improvement, sporting goods, electronics, discount). High traffic generators; vulnerable to anchor vacancy.
- **[[Grocery-Anchored Retail]]** — Neighborhood or community center (75,000–200,000 SF) with a supermarket anchor driving weekly necessity trips. Most defensible institutional retail format.
- **[[Strip Center]]** — Smaller open-air inline or neighborhood center (10,000–100,000 SF), often unanchored or service-anchored (nail salon, insurance, fast food). Highest cap rates; most management-intensive.
- **[[Lifestyle Center]]** — Upscale open-air center (300,000–800,000 SF) with soft-goods retail, dining, and entertainment; suburban high-income trade areas. Discretionary exposure.
- **[[Mall Formats]]** — Enclosed regional mall (400,000–1.2M SF) or super-regional mall anchored by department stores; also includes outlet and open-air town-center variants. Highly bifurcated by quality tier.
- **[[Single-Tenant Net Lease (STNL)]]** — Freestanding single-occupant property (1,500–20,000 SF) with a long-term (10–25 year) NNN or absolute-net lease to a brand-name tenant. Closest to a bond proxy in real estate.
- **[[Urban Street Retail]]** — Ground-floor or podium retail in dense urban infill locations. Highly location-specific; driven by pedestrian counts, daytime population, and residential density above.

## Demand Drivers

Retail demand is ultimately a function of consumer spending power, population density, and trade area competitive dynamics. The primary demand driver is household income and spending velocity within the trade area — retailers underwrite locations based on the intersection of co-tenancy, access, visibility, and the projected customer pool. For necessity-based retail, population density and proximity to residential concentration matter most; for destination and discretionary formats, daytime employment population, regional draw, and drive-time accessibility are equally important.

Leading indicators include retail sales trends by category (ICSC, CoStar, US Census monthly retail trade), consumer confidence indices, unemployment rates, and e-commerce penetration by merchandise category. Service-use retail (medical, dental, personal services, QSR) has outperformed goods retail over the past decade because those uses are inherently online-resistant. Retailers in the following categories have demonstrated resilience: grocery, pharmacy, off-price/dollar, home improvement, fitness, and food-and-beverage with experiential differentiation. Categories still under structural pressure include apparel, electronics, books/music/video, and mid-tier department store formats.

Demographic tailwinds that support new retail supply or repositioning include suburban household formation in Sun Belt and Mountain West markets, affluent suburban trade areas with above-average household income, and neighborhoods underserved by existing grocery or pharmacy anchors. [[Trade Area Analysis (Retail)]] and [[Traffic Counts and Visibility]] are the primary market-analysis inputs for retail underwriting; [[Demographic Analysis]] and [[Psychographic Analysis]] layer in consumer profile validation.

## Supply Dynamics

New retail supply has been constrained since the 2010s as retailers rationalized their store counts and lenders tightened construction underwriting for speculative retail. The most active new supply categories are freestanding QSR/fast-casual (driven by corporate and franchise expansion), grocery-anchored community centers in underserved suburban growth corridors, and STNL build-to-suit development pre-leased to national credit tenants. Speculative multi-tenant retail development without an anchor commitment is rare in current market conditions.

Development timelines for retail vary by format: a freestanding STNL can be developed, permitted, and delivered in 12–18 months; a community center with grocery anchor typically requires 24–36 months; a power center or lifestyle center, 36–60 months when including entitlement. Barriers to entry in supply-constrained infill markets are meaningful — available land with adequate size, access, visibility, and traffic counts is scarce in established urban and inner-suburban markets. In greenfield suburban growth corridors, barriers are lower but require anchoring tenants to commit before construction financing is available.

Adaptive reuse and redevelopment of obsolete retail — converting dead malls to mixed-use, multifamily, or medical campuses, sub-dividing large big-box vacancies for multi-tenant use — represents a growing share of "new" retail supply activity, and intersects heavily with [[Adaptive Reuse]] deal structures and [[Entitlement Play]] strategies.

## Key Operating Metrics

See [[Retail — Key Metrics]] for full definitions and formulas. The core metrics for retail are:

- **Sales per SF** — tenant sales volume normalized to GLA; the primary measure of tenant health and percentage rent trigger.
- **Occupancy Cost Ratio (OCR)** — total occupancy cost (base rent + NNN) as a percentage of tenant sales; the "health ratio" indicating whether a tenant can sustain its rent obligation. Typically sustainable at 10–15% for soft goods, 5–8% for grocery anchors.
- **NNN Recovery Ratio** — share of common area maintenance, insurance, and real estate taxes recovered from tenants; impacts effective NOI.
- **Co-tenancy Thresholds** — lease clauses allowing a tenant to reduce rent or terminate if anchor occupancy or overall center occupancy falls below a threshold.
- **Traffic/Footfall Counts** — vehicle counts at access points and pedestrian flow for mixed-use; correlated with sales and used in site underwriting.

## Common Deal Structures

Retail is predominantly owned fee-simple with NNN or modified-gross leases to tenants. Institutional ownership structures range from open-end core funds (favor grocery-anchored and STNL with investment-grade credit) to closed-end value-add funds (power centers with vacancy upside, repositioning plays) to private REITs and 1031 exchange buyers (STNL, small strip). [[Sale-Leaseback]] structures are common for corporate owner-operators (QSR franchisees, regional chains) seeking to monetize occupied real estate. [[Build-to-Suit]] development with a national tenant pre-commitment is the standard structure for new STNL and anchor-driven community centers.

Financing for stabilized retail typically uses [[CMBS]], [[Life Company Debt]], or [[Bank Debt]]; grocery-anchored in strong markets can access [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] through some programs. Construction lending relies on [[Construction Loan]] facilities with recourse during the build period, converting to permanent [[Senior Debt]] at stabilization. [[Mezzanine Debt]] and [[Preferred Equity]] are used in value-add and development capitalizations where leverage or return requirements exceed senior debt parameters. JV structures — see [[Joint Venture Structures]] and [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] — are standard for institutional equity capitalizations.

## Major Risks

See [[Retail — Risks & Considerations]] for full treatment. Primary risks include:

- **E-commerce displacement** — ongoing secular shift in goods-retail to online channels, particularly affecting apparel, electronics, and mid-tier department stores.
- **Anchor and credit-tenant vacancy** — loss of an anchor tenant can trigger co-tenancy rent reductions or terminations among inline tenants, creating a cascade of occupancy loss and NOI impairment.
- **Lease structure obsolescence** — long-term leases with below-market rents and landlord-unfavorable co-tenancy or kickout provisions impair NOI and asset value.
- **Consumer spending cyclicality** — discretionary retail formats are highly sensitive to recession; necessity formats are more resilient.
- **Physical and functional obsolescence** — suburban power centers and enclosed malls face repositioning costs that may exceed residual retail value.

## Regulatory Considerations

See [[Retail — Regulatory Considerations]] for full detail. Key regulatory dimensions for retail include ADA compliance (accessible parking, path of travel, restrooms), local zoning/use permits governing retail by category, signage ordinances, parking minimum requirements by use type, drive-thru restrictions, liquor licensing, and exclusive-use covenants in CC&Rs that limit competing uses in multi-tenant centers.

## Notable Markets

Grocery-anchored retail is concentrated in high-barrier infill markets (Boston, New York metro, San Francisco Bay Area, Washington D.C., Los Angeles) where land scarcity limits new supply and population density supports anchor sales. STNL product is distributed nationally, with concentrations in Sun Belt growth markets (Phoenix, Dallas-Fort Worth, Atlanta, Tampa, Charlotte) where corporate tenant expansion tracks household growth. Power centers remain strongest in suburban Sun Belt markets with large household counts and strong auto-orientation. Enclosed mall quality is bifurcated by geography — top-performing centers in affluent suburban and tourist markets; structural distress concentrated in secondary and tertiary markets with population loss or income decline.
