# Single-Tenant Net Lease (STNL)

## Overview

Single-tenant net lease (STNL) properties are freestanding or inline single-occupant commercial buildings — typically 1,500 to 20,000 SF — leased to a single national or regional brand under a long-term NNN or absolute-net lease structure. The tenant is responsible for the property's operating expenses — real estate taxes, property insurance, and maintenance (NNN) — and in the case of absolute-net leases, also roof and structure. The landlord's obligation is essentially passive: collect a net rent check and manage lease administration, while the tenant operates the building as if it were their own property.

Common STNL tenant categories include quick-service restaurants (McDonald's, Chick-fil-A, Taco Bell, Wendy's, Starbucks, Chipotle), pharmacy (Walgreens, CVS), convenience retail (7-Eleven, Casey's, Circle K), dollar/off-price (Dollar General, Dollar Tree, Family Dollar), auto parts (AutoZone, O'Reilly Auto Parts, Advance Auto Parts), medical/health (urgent care, dialysis, dental), and bank branches (though bank branch expansion has slowed significantly). Lease terms at inception typically run 10 to 25 years with periodic rent increases — commonly 10% every 5 years, or 1.5%–2.0% annually for newer-vintage leases. After the initial term, tenants typically hold multiple renewal option periods (3–5 options of 5 years each), giving them operating flexibility.

STNL is the closest real estate product to a bond — the income is contractual, the management is passive, and the yield is priced relative to the tenant's credit quality and the term remaining on the lease. This bond-proxy character makes STNL broadly attractive to a diverse buyer pool: 1031 exchange investors seeking passive income, institutional core funds seeking low-volatility yield, private equity aggregators, and public non-traded REITs (Realty Income, NNN REIT, STORE Capital, Agree Realty) that build large diversified STNL portfolios.

## Pricing Framework

STNL is priced on four primary variables: (1) tenant credit quality (investment-grade vs. sub-investment-grade; corporate guarantee vs. franchisee guarantee); (2) remaining lease term (longer term = lower cap rate, reflecting lower near-term rollover risk); (3) rent escalation structure (annual bumps vs. 5-year steps; higher escalations command premium pricing); and (4) location/real estate quality (strong location with alternative-use optionality commands a premium over a weak location).

The cap rate for investment-grade STNL with long term (15+ years remaining) and good location ranges from 4.5% to 5.5% in primary markets, widening to 5.5%–6.5% in secondary markets. Sub-investment-grade credits (franchisees, regional operators) with the same term trade at 5.5%–7.5% depending on operator strength, concept performance, and location. As remaining lease term shortens below 10 years, cap rates widen materially — a property with 5 or fewer years remaining on its lease is priced as a "speculative" property where the buyer is essentially pricing in their re-tenanting cost and timeline.

The STNL market's relationship to interest rates is more direct than other retail formats. Because the income stream is fixed or near-fixed, cap rates are explicitly compared to [[Spread to Treasuries]] — when the 10-year Treasury rises, buyers demand higher yields, and STNL prices compress. This sensitivity was particularly visible in 2022–2023 when rapid rate increases forced cap rate expansion of 75–150 bps across the STNL market.

## Investment Structures

The primary acquisition structures for STNL are straightforward fee-simple acquisitions from developers, owner-operators, or portfolio sellers. [[Sale-Leaseback]] is a significant origination source: corporate operators (QSR chains, convenience store chains, regional retailers) monetize owned real estate by selling to an STNL investor and entering into a long-term lease — the operator unlocks capital while retaining operating control of the location. [[Build-to-Suit]] development is another primary origination: a developer builds to a tenant's specifications, with the tenant pre-committed to a 15–20 year NNN lease, and the stabilized property is either held or sold to an STNL investor at a development spread.

Financing STNL properties benefits from the clean income stream and long lease term: [[Life Company Debt]], [[CMBS]], and [[Senior Debt]] from major banks are all available. Non-recourse, fixed-rate, long-term (10–15 year) financing is achievable at 55%–70% LTV for investment-grade product with long lease term. The [[Debt Yield]] test is the binding constraint for many CMBS lenders; for STNL, debt yields of 7%–10% are typically required depending on credit and term.

[[Direct Capitalization]] is the primary valuation method for STNL; [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] is used when modeling a hold period that extends beyond the initial lease term and requires underwriting of renewal probability, re-leasing economics, and exit value.

## Key Risks

**Lease Expiration and Re-Tenanting Risk.** The primary risk in STNL investing is that the tenant does not renew at expiration, leaving the owner with a purpose-built building designed for one specific operator. A former QSR pad — with a drive-thru lane, small footprint, and specific utilities — has limited alternative uses and may require significant capital to re-tenant or redevelop. The real estate's residual value absent the lease is often a fraction of the going-concern NNN value.

**Tenant Credit Deterioration.** An STNL investment rated on investment-grade credit becomes a sub-investment-grade investment if the tenant's credit profile deteriorates. Corporate bankruptcies (a major risk in the casual dining, apparel, and electronics categories) can convert a stable income stream into a vacancy. The location's standalone real estate value is the floor — properties in strong locations with alternative use optionality hold value better in credit stress scenarios.

**Rent Escalation Lagging Inflation.** Many older STNL leases have flat rent or step-bump structures that do not keep pace with inflation. Over a 20-year lease, flat rent becomes significantly below-market in nominal terms; when the tenant renews at a rent reset to market, they may accept below-market renewal rent rather than relocate, or they may use the leverage of non-renewal to extract further below-market terms.

**Franchisee vs. Corporate Guarantee.** Many STNL properties appear to be leased to a national brand (McDonald's, Taco Bell) but are in fact leased to a franchisee entity — a small LLC that is the local franchisee operator. Franchisee credit is substantially weaker than corporate credit, and the difference is material in underwriting. Confirm the lease guarantor and assess whether corporate or franchisee guarantee is in place.

## Operational Notes

STNL is the least management-intensive real estate investment available. With a single NNN tenant handling all operations, the landlord's responsibilities are limited to: monitoring tenant rent payments; reviewing and approving any lease assignment or subletting requests; maintaining corporate guarantee validity; and managing lease administration as term approaches. For portfolio investors (aggregators, REITs), the operational efficiency at scale is a significant advantage — a single property manager can administer hundreds of STNL properties.

The exit market for STNL is the broadest in retail: the combination of 1031 exchange buyers (motivated by tax deferral), institutional core funds, private equity aggregators, and foreign capital creates deep and liquid demand for high-quality STNL product. This buyer depth is a meaningful exit risk mitigant compared to other retail formats. Cap rate transparency in STNL is also high — the NNN market has robust data from CoStar, LoopNet, and dedicated STNL brokers, making pricing discovery reliable.
