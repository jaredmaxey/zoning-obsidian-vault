# Retail — Risks & Considerations

Retail real estate carries a risk profile that is both complex and highly format-dependent. The secular disruption from e-commerce, a structural decline in department store anchors, and the bifurcation of the market between necessity-driven and discretionary formats have fundamentally shifted how institutional investors assess risk. The framework below organizes risks across four dimensions — market, operational, capital and financing, and structural and physical — with mitigants for each.

### Market

**E-Commerce Displacement.** The shift of goods consumption from physical retail to online channels is the most significant secular risk facing the retail asset class. Categories most exposed include apparel, electronics, books, sporting goods, and home goods — historically the core tenancy of enclosed malls and power centers. Categories with online-resistance include grocery and fresh food, personal services (hair, nail, dental, medical), QSR and food-and-beverage, fitness, and experiential entertainment. Underwriters must assess the e-commerce exposure of each tenant category in a center's mix and assign higher vacancy risk to goods-retail-heavy properties.

*Mitigant:* Favor formats with necessity-based or online-resistant tenancy (grocery-anchored, service-dominated strip, STNL in essential categories). Stress-test portfolios for continued goods-retail closures in [[Sensitivity Analysis]]. Assess trade area demographics for resilience — affluent markets with high food-and-beverage spending support experiential formats better.

**Trade Area Saturation and Over-Retailing.** Many suburban U.S. markets were overbuilt for retail through the 1990s and 2000s; per-capita retail SF in the U.S. remains above most international peer markets. In over-retailed markets, occupancy recovery after anchor closures is slow because existing tenants have little incentive to relocate or expand. Competitive new supply — even necessity-based — can impair existing center performance when trade area retail density is already high.

*Mitigant:* Conduct [[Trade Area Analysis (Retail)]] before acquisition to assess supply-to-demand ratios, competitive inventory, and lease expiration concentration. Avoid acquiring in over-retailed corridors unless at a significant discount to replacement cost.

**Consumer Spending Cyclicality.** Discretionary retail is highly cyclical — during economic downturns, consumer spending on apparel, home furnishings, and dining falls rapidly, creating vacancy risk across lifestyle centers and higher-tier malls. Necessity retail (grocery, pharmacy, dollar) is far more resilient. Recessions also pressure small-business tenants in strip centers disproportionately, as local independent retailers have limited capital reserves and credit access.

*Mitigant:* Underwrite with recession-scenario vacancy assumptions for discretionary tenant mix. Institutional buyers typically apply a 10%–20% NOI haircut in a downside scenario for discretionary retail. Weight portfolio allocation toward necessity formats.

### Operational

**Anchor Vacancy and Co-Tenancy Cascades.** The loss of an anchor tenant — particularly in a power center or enclosed mall — is the highest-impact single event in retail operations. Beyond the direct revenue loss from the anchor space, anchor departure triggers co-tenancy rent reductions and termination rights in inline tenant leases. In a severe case, one anchor closure can cascade into a center-wide occupancy decline as inline tenants exercise co-tenancy provisions, creating a feedback loop of declining occupancy and further co-tenancy triggers. This mechanism has been observed repeatedly in department-store-anchored mall closures since 2015.

*Mitigant:* Inventory co-tenancy provisions in every lease at acquisition; model the NOI floor under a co-tenancy cascade. Prioritize acquiring centers where co-tenancy provisions are limited or where the anchor has strong sales metrics and a long remaining lease term. For malls, focus on centers where non-department-store uses (entertainment, food hall, fitness) reduce dependence on a single anchor.

**Tenant Credit Deterioration.** Retailer financial health is dynamic; a national tenant that was investment-grade at lease inception may face significant credit stress at renewal. Bankruptcy and store closure risk is concentrated in apparel, mid-tier department stores, and discretionary goods categories. When a tenant closes, the landlord faces the cost of re-tenanting (TI, leasing commissions, and a rent-free period) plus potential co-tenancy triggers from the closure itself.

*Mitigant:* Monitor tenant-level financial filings and store closure announcements. Diversify tenant mix to limit single-tenant concentration. Proactively approach at-risk tenants prior to lease expiration to extend term and re-set rent if necessary to retain occupancy.

**Lease Rollover and Below-Market Leases.** Retail leases tend to be longer-term (5–15 years for inline, 15–25 years for anchors) than other asset classes, which creates dual risk: below-market rents are locked in for extended periods, and concentrated lease expirations create "rollover cliffs" where a large share of GLA turns over simultaneously. In an underwriting context, below-market leases with long remaining terms create mark-to-market risk — when the lease expires, the tenant may not renew at market if their business has deteriorated, or they may extract significant concessions.

*Mitigant:* In acquisition underwriting, schedule all lease expirations and identify the concentration of rollover in years 3–7 of the hold period. Model realistic re-leasing spreads and TI costs for each expiring tenant category. For value-add theses built on below-market leases rolling to market, confirm that current market rents are achievable based on [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] in the trade area.

### Capital & Financing

**CMBS and Financing Market Exposure.** A substantial portion of retail real estate is financed through [[CMBS]], which is more sensitive to asset-class sentiment than relationship-based bank or life company financing. During periods of retail distress (2009–2010, 2020–2021), CMBS lending for retail contracted sharply, and delinquency rates on retail loans rose significantly. Properties encumbered by maturing CMBS debt in a distressed credit environment face refinancing risk — particularly if NOI has declined.

*Mitigant:* Match loan maturity to hold period. Avoid overleveraging in formats with structural headwinds. Maintain adequate interest reserves and DSCR covenant cushion. Model a downside refinancing scenario assuming a 50–100 bps wider cap rate and 25%–35% lower NOI at maturity.

**High Capital Expenditure Requirements.** Retail assets — particularly older malls, power centers, and enclosed formats — require substantial periodic capital investment: parking lot resurfacing, roof replacement, facade renovation, HVAC systems, and tenant improvement packages for backfill leasing. For value-add and distressed acquisitions, the cost to re-tenant a large vacant anchor box or reposition a wing of a mall can run $50–$150 PSF or more. Undercapitalized ownership and deferred maintenance compound over time and impair the ability to attract and retain quality tenants.

*Mitigant:* Commission a property condition assessment prior to acquisition. Establish sufficient capital reserves in the underwriting. For value-add acquisitions, project realistic TI, LC, and capital costs for the repositioning thesis — and stress-test the cost side as well as the revenue side. See [[Reserves for Replacement]] and [[CapEx Planning]].

**Interest Rate Sensitivity.** Retail cap rates are sensitive to the interest rate environment, particularly for STNL and other bond-proxy formats. When the 10-year Treasury rate rises, required yields on long-term NNN product rise in tandem, compressing prices. Short-term rate increases also raise floating-rate debt service, pressuring cash-on-cash returns for leveraged buyers. Retail assets with declining NOI and rising debt costs can generate negative leverage if cap rates do not expand to offset income deterioration.

*Mitigant:* Underwrite to a fixed-rate permanent loan where possible. Use [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]] to model return scenarios across a range of exit cap rates (widening 50–150 bps from going-in). Consider shortened hold periods for assets with meaningful interest rate exposure.

### Structural & Physical

**Format Obsolescence.** Enclosed malls, older power centers, and generic strip centers face functional obsolescence as the formats that drove their original leasing are no longer in expansion. Department stores — the anchor of the regional mall model for 60 years — have been contracting since the early 2000s. The physical structure of a traditional enclosed mall (large enclosed boxes, long interior corridors, limited exterior storefronts, surface parking moats) is poorly suited to modern retail, food-and-beverage, entertainment, or mixed-use conversion without extensive capital investment.

*Mitigant:* Assess whether the physical asset can be repositioned for an alternative highest-and-best use — see [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] and [[Adaptive Reuse]]. In many cases, obsolete retail boxes are worth more as residential conversion, last-mile industrial, medical campus, or mixed-use infill development than as continued retail. The feasibility of conversion depends heavily on land value, site configuration, municipal entitlement support, and the cost of demolition or structural conversion.

**Deferred Maintenance and Environmental.** Older retail properties often carry significant deferred maintenance: aging HVAC systems, deteriorated roofing, cracked pavement, poor lighting, and asbestos or other environmental contaminants in pre-1980s buildings. These items are difficult to identify from financial statements alone and require physical due diligence. Environmental conditions at former dry-cleaning tenants, auto parts stores, or gas station outparcels can carry significant remediation liability.

*Mitigant:* Commission Phase I environmental assessments on all acquisitions; Phase II for any indicated conditions or high-risk tenants. Factor physical condition costs into the acquisition basis and proforma. Negotiate seller credits or escrows for identified deferred maintenance at closing.

**Parking and Access.** Retail real estate performance is highly dependent on adequate parking ratios, clear ingress and egress, and visibility from arterials. Older centers may have inadequate parking ratios relative to current leasing mix (particularly as food-and-beverage, which generates higher parking demand than soft goods, displaces apparel tenancy). Restricted or awkward access points that impair traffic flow are costly to remediate. Municipalities sometimes recapture excess retail parking for road improvements or other public use, reducing parking supply.

*Mitigant:* Confirm current and projected parking ratios against municipal minimums and market norms for the tenant mix. Assess ingress/egress quality in site visit and traffic study. Review any easements or cross-access agreements that constrain parking or access. See [[Retail — Regulatory Considerations]] for parking regulation context.
