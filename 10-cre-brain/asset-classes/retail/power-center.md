# Power Center

## Overview

A power center is a large open-air shopping center, typically 250,000 to 600,000 square feet of GLA, anchored by three or more "big box" category-dominant retailers. The defining characteristic is a line-up of large-format tenants — each occupying 20,000 to 120,000 SF — whose individual category dominance drives destination traffic without requiring an enclosed mall structure. Common anchor categories include home improvement (Home Depot, Lowe's), off-price apparel and housewares (T.J. Maxx, Marshalls, Burlington, HomeGoods), wholesale club (Costco, Sam's Club), sporting goods (Dick's Sporting Goods, Bass Pro Shops), electronics (Best Buy), and pet supplies (PetSmart, Petco). Inline tenants — typically 1,200 to 10,000 SF — occupy strips connecting anchors or line smaller buildings on the periphery; pad sites (outparcels) are leased to QSR, bank, or auto services tenants who benefit from center traffic.

Power centers emerged as the dominant suburban retail format in the 1980s and 1990s as discount and category-killer retailers displaced full-line department stores. Unlike enclosed malls, power centers have minimal common area, low operating cost structures, and direct drive-to-door access for each anchor. For investors, the format offers high visibility, strong co-tenancy from multiple national credit tenants, and generally higher cap rates than grocery-anchored product, reflecting the volatility of big-box tenant expansion cycles and the cost of backfilling large-format vacancies.

## Investment Thesis and Deal Profile

The power center investment thesis is fundamentally a function of anchor lease duration, tenant credit quality, and the remaining useful life of the anchoring format. When anchors are in-place on long leases (10+ years remaining) with high-credit tenants, power centers are relatively stable income producers. The risk profile shifts materially as anchor leases approach expiration or as individual anchor categories face structural challenges — the implosion of the electronics (Circuit City, CompUSA), sporting goods (Sports Authority), and office supplies (Staples, Office Depot) categories removed significant power center tenancy in the 2010s, leaving substantial vacant anchor boxes in many centers.

Going-in cap rates for power centers range from 6.5% to 8.5% for well-occupied product in primary and secondary markets, widening to 9.0%+ for centers with anchor vacancy or near-term lease rollovers. Value-add opportunities are common: vacant anchor boxes offer the potential for redivision into multi-tenant space, conversion to entertainment (bowling, fitness, trampoline), or medical/healthcare use (urgent care, dialysis, outpatient surgery). These conversion plays require meaningful capital ($20–$60 PSF or more for redivision or conversion) and depend on finding a replacement tenant willing to pay rents that justify the investment.

Acquisitions typically use [[Core-Plus Acquisition]] or [[Value-Add Acquisition]] frameworks; financing is predominantly [[CMBS]] or [[Senior Debt]] from regional banks. Cap rate sensitivity to anchor lease term and rollover is high — exit underwriting should model the impact of anchor renewal or non-renewal on NOI and the cost-to-re-tenant scenario.

## Demand Drivers

Power centers are driven by consumers' preference for one-stop shopping across discretionary categories at value price points. The combined traffic of multiple category-dominant anchors creates a destination that attracts shoppers from a broad trade area — typically 5–15 miles depending on density. The format thrives in auto-oriented suburban markets with sufficient household density (typically 100,000+ households within a 5-mile radius), high traffic arterials or freeway adjacency, and a limited number of competing power centers.

Tenant demand from big-box retailers is cyclical: it tracks corporate expansion plans, same-store sales performance, and format evolution. Off-price and discount categories have been among the strongest performers in retail since 2010, with T.J. Maxx Companies, Burlington, and Dollar/off-price formats continuing to expand. Wholesale clubs (Costco, Sam's Club) remain among the most traffic-generative power center anchors. Categories in contraction include mid-tier apparel (Gap, J.Crew), electronics, and traditional sporting goods. Tracking anchor category health is an ongoing asset management priority.

## Underwriting Considerations

Key underwriting inputs for power centers include: anchor lease terms and renewal option pricing; anchor co-tenancy provisions in inline leases; recovery structure (anchor recovery is often below-proportional, shifting a larger share of CAM to inline tenants or absorbing into NOI); parking ratio and its adequacy for current and prospective tenant mix; and the physical condition of large anchor boxes (deferred maintenance, asbestos in older vintage buildings, roof conditions).

Vacancy underwriting for inline space ranges from 5% to 12% depending on center quality and location; for centers with anchor vacancy, total economic vacancy can be substantially higher. TI and LC for inline re-leasing ranges from $20 to $60 PSF depending on condition and category. Large anchor box vacancy — typically defined as spaces over 20,000 SF — may carry a 12–24+ month re-leasing timeline and $30–$80 PSF in TI even for a straightforward backfill.

Sensitivity to exit cap rate expansion is a key risk: power centers trade on thin cap rate spreads relative to other retail formats, and even modest anchor credit deterioration or lease expiration can widen cap rates by 75–150 bps at disposition.

## Operational Notes

Power centers are typically managed by third-party property managers with national retail expertise, or directly by institutional owners with in-house retail platforms. Asset management priorities include: proactive anchor relationship management to identify renewal risk 3–5 years in advance; coordination of signage rights and pylons for tenants; parking lot maintenance and lighting as a shopper experience driver; and outparcel management for QSR and bank pads. Many power centers have declarant-controlled sign criteria agreements and reciprocal easement agreements that require coordination with outparcel owners.

Anchor tenant lease negotiations — particularly at renewal — are among the most significant value creation or preservation events in a power center's hold period. Anchors typically have leverage at renewal: moving an anchor tenant requires the anchor to absorb relocation costs and the landlord to absorb re-leasing costs plus potential co-tenancy NOI impairment. Institutional investors prioritize renewal certainty over maximizing rent at the margin in many cases.
