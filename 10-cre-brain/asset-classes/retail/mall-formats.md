# Mall Formats

## Overview

Mall formats represent the most capital-intensive and structurally complex category in retail real estate. The traditional enclosed regional mall — 400,000 to 1,200,000 SF of GLA, anchored by two to four department stores with an enclosed climate-controlled concourse of inline specialty retail — was the dominant retail format in the United States for the four decades following 1960. At peak, there were approximately 1,100–1,500 enclosed malls in operation nationally. The sector has undergone dramatic contraction since 2010, driven by the dual forces of department store anchor failure and e-commerce displacement of soft-goods retail — the two categories most exposed to online substitution.

Mall formats today encompass four distinct subtypes: (1) **enclosed regional and super-regional malls** (the traditional format); (2) **outlet centers** (off-price retail in open-air or enclosed configurations, operated by specialized REITs including Simon Premium Outlets and Tanger); (3) **open-air town center / mixed-use hybrids** (high-quality lifestyle-mall hybrids with anchors, inline retail, F&B, and mixed-use components); and (4) **adaptive reuse conversions** (former enclosed malls repositioned for residential, medical, entertainment, logistics, or mixed-use). Each subtype carries a distinct risk profile and investor base.

The institutional mall sector is sharply bifurcated. Class A and "luxury" malls — held primarily by Simon Property Group, Brookfield Properties, Taubman Centers, and Macerich — generate strong sales per SF (often $500–$1,000+), maintain high occupancy, and attract luxury and premium brand tenancy. These properties trade at institutional cap rates below 7% and in some cases below 6%. Class B/C malls face occupancy decline, anchor closures, and re-financing difficulty; many have become "zombie" malls with partially-dark anchor space, declining footfall, and negative leverage in the current rate environment. Institutional capital is largely absent from the Class B/C mall market; distressed buyers, municipalities, and opportunistic real estate developers are the operative buyers.

## Class A / Trophy Enclosed Malls

Trophy enclosed malls — typically the single dominant regional mall in a high-barrier market with above-average household income — continue to generate strong operating metrics and attract institutional capital. Key performance characteristics: sales per SF of $500–$1,000+; occupancy 90%–96%+; anchor tenancy by premium/luxury department stores (Nordstrom, Macy's Backstage, JCPenney in better markets); luxury and premium brand inline tenancy (Apple, Coach, Lululemon, Tesla showroom, Food Hall); and strong shopper dwell time driven by F&B, entertainment, and event programming.

Investment for Class A malls is largely held by a small number of specialist REITs who built and own these properties over decades. Acquisition opportunities are rare; when they occur, pricing is reflective of the assets' income durability and replacement cost impossibility. The thesis for holding Class A malls is the scarcity of comparable competitive product in the trade area, the high barriers to building a competing mall (entitlement, capital, department store anchoring), and the re-leasing optionality of the physical structure for higher-and-better uses if needed.

## Class B / C Enclosed Malls and Distressed Product

The Class B/C mall market represents the structural distress story of U.S. retail. These properties typically have: one or more dark or closed department store anchors; occupancy of 70%–85% (or lower); declining sales per SF; limited interest from national credit tenants seeking new locations; and financing that may be in default or approaching maturity on a devalued asset. The capital structure is typically under water — CMBS loans from 2006–2008 or 2012–2014 vintages at values far above current supportable levels.

The investment thesis for distressed malls is an [[Adaptive Reuse]] or [[Distressed Acquisition]] framework: acquire at a substantial discount to peak value, model an alternative highest-and-best use (see [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]]), and execute a repositioning over a 5–10 year timeline. Common repositioning scenarios include: conversion of anchor boxes to fulfillment/last-mile logistics; conversion to residential (apartments, senior housing) with retention of some retail; medical campus or hospital expansion; indoor entertainment (e-sports, Family Entertainment Center, trampoline park); and full demolition and redevelopment. The feasibility of conversion depends on physical structure, municipal support for rezoning, local demand for the proposed use, and the net cost of conversion versus ground-up development.

Financing distressed malls is extremely challenging: traditional lenders will not extend new construction or permanent loans on Class C enclosed malls. Opportunistic investors rely on bridge equity or hard-money bridge loans, often at high LTC and with significant equity at risk. The exit market is thin.

## Outlet Centers

Outlet centers are a distinct and more resilient mall format. Simon Premium Outlets (Woodbury Common, Las Vegas Premium Outlets, etc.) and Tanger Outlets operate networks of open-air off-price centers where brands sell prior-season inventory, overruns, and outlet-exclusive merchandise at discounts to full retail. Outlet center tenancy is dominated by fashion and lifestyle brands (Gap Outlet, Ralph Lauren, Coach, Kate Spade, Restoration Hardware), and increasingly includes food-and-beverage and entertainment tenants.

Outlet centers have outperformed traditional enclosed malls through the 2015–2025 period because: (1) the off-price value proposition is resilient across economic cycles; (2) destination-drive character means trade areas are regional (1–3 hours) rather than local; and (3) the format is controlled by specialized operators with the scale to maintain leasing relationships and portfolio performance. Outlet center cap rates are tighter than general mall product — typically 5.5%–7.5% for high-quality product — reflecting better performance and a more institutional buyer base.

## Open-Air Town Center / Mixed-Use Hybrids

Open-air town centers blur the line between lifestyle centers and traditional malls: they may have enclosed anchor stores, large dining and entertainment components, residential towers, hotel, and office uses integrated into a walkable district format. Examples include Easton Town Center (Columbus), The Grove (Los Angeles), and Bellevue Square (Seattle). These centers combine the traffic diversity of mixed-use with the curation of a mall, creating more resilient income through use diversity.

The investment thesis for town centers is similar to lifestyle centers — placemaking differentiation, trade area income depth, and F&B/entertainment density — but the mixed-use component adds complexity and diversification. Underwriting requires integrating retail, residential, hotel, and office cash flows; ownership structures are often complex (condominium ownership of individual pads, ground leases, JV structures). See [[Mixed-Use]] for additional treatment of mixed-use investment dynamics.

## Underwriting and Valuation Notes

Mall underwriting — particularly for non-trophy product — requires exceptional care. Key inputs: sales per SF by tenant and anchor (often available from market data providers, mall operators' public filings, or tenant disclosure requirements); department store lease terms and co-tenancy provisions; total occupancy cost ratio by tenant; physical condition of the center (deferred maintenance, HVAC systems, roofing, parking structure); and financing maturity profile.

For distressed mall acquisitions, the residual land value and alternative-use feasibility drive the investment case more than any in-place income analysis — see [[Residual Land Value Method]] and [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]]. Stress-testing should model zero-retail NOI scenarios and alternative-use conversion costs to establish a floor value for the land and structure.
