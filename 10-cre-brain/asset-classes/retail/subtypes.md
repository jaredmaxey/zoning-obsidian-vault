# Retail — Subtypes

Retail real estate is not a monolithic asset class. Investor demand, underwriting assumptions, risk profile, and management intensity differ substantially across formats. The seven primary subtypes below represent distinct product categories with different tenant mixes, lease structures, capital requirements, and performance drivers. Understanding these distinctions is foundational to retail underwriting and portfolio construction.

### Power Center

**Profile.** A power center is a large open-air shopping center typically ranging from 250,000 to 600,000 square feet, anchored by three or more category-dominant "big box" retailers occupying 20,000 to 100,000+ SF each. Common anchors include home improvement (Home Depot, Lowe's), sporting goods (Dick's Sporting Goods), off-price apparel (T.J. Maxx, Burlington), wholesale clubs (Costco, Sam's Club), electronics (Best Buy), and pet supplies (PetSmart, Petco). Inline tenants — typically 1,200–10,000 SF — cluster in pad sites or anchor-flanking strips.

**Deal Profile.** Power centers are priced on in-place NOI with a meaningful eye toward anchor lease expirations and rollover risk. Big-box anchor rents are typically well below replacement-cost-justified rents, creating upside on renewal or backfill but vacancy risk if the anchor dark. Inline occupancy cost is moderate. Typical going-in caps range from 6.5% to 8.5% for well-occupied product, widening materially for centers with anchor vacancy or near-term lease rollover. Value-add plays center on vacant anchor box redivision or concept repositioning. See [[Power Center]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** Open-end core-plus funds, private REITs, family offices, and institutional value-add funds. REITs like Inland, Kite Realty, and PREIT have historically been active acquirers.

### Grocery-Anchored Retail

**Profile.** A grocery-anchored center is a neighborhood or community center, typically 75,000–200,000 SF, anchored by a supermarket that generates high-frequency necessity-trip traffic. The grocery anchor — occupying 40,000–65,000 SF — drives consumer flow that supports inline tenants in adjacent categories: pharmacy, dry cleaner, nail salon, urgent care, QSR, insurance, and similar convenience-service uses. Centers are typically single-story, surface-parked, and located on major arterials in established suburban or infill trade areas.

**Deal Profile.** Widely regarded as the most defensible institutional retail format. Grocery anchor lease terms are typically 15–25 years with renewal options; anchor rents are below market but provide income stability. Investment thesis centers on the durability of necessity-trip traffic, inline tenant renewal velocity, and the scarcity of re-anchorable grocery boxes in supply-constrained infill markets. Going-in caps range broadly from 5.5% to 7.5% depending on market, grocer credit, and inline occupancy. See [[Grocery-Anchored Retail]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** Institutional core and core-plus funds, open-end REITs (Regency Centers, Weingarten/Kimco, Whitestone), private equity platforms, and 1031 exchange buyers.

### Neighborhood / Strip Center

**Profile.** Strip centers are smaller open-air multi-tenant centers — typically 10,000 to 100,000 SF — that may be unanchored or anchored by a service or convenience tenant (dollar store, nail salon, QSR). "Neighborhood centers" in the ICSC taxonomy anchor with a drugstore or convenience grocery; "strip centers" often have no dominant anchor. Tenant mix is heavily service-oriented: personal care, food, insurance, medical, and local retailers. Centers are auto-oriented, single-story, and surface-parked. Asset quality ranges from Class A (well-located, national-tenant lineup, excellent ingress/egress) to Class C (deferred maintenance, independent tenant mix, secondary location).

**Deal Profile.** Highest cap rate retail format, reflecting management intensity, tenant rollover frequency, and credit profile of small/local tenants. Going-in caps range from 6.5% to 9.0%+ for commodity strip. Local and regional buyers dominate; institutional capital focuses on anchored neighborhood centers in strong infill markets. See [[Strip Center]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** Private investors, family offices, 1031 exchange buyers, and local/regional owner-operators. Institutional capital is selectively active in well-anchored neighborhood centers in top-tier markets.

### Lifestyle Center

**Profile.** A lifestyle center is an upscale open-air retail destination typically ranging from 300,000 to 800,000 SF, positioned as an experiential alternative to the enclosed mall. Lifestyle centers are characterized by national soft-goods retailers (Gap, Williams-Sonoma, J.Crew, Anthropologie), restaurant and food-and-beverage anchors, entertainment tenants, and a curated tenant mix targeting affluent suburban households. They often incorporate outdoor pedestrian streets, placemaking amenities (water features, outdoor seating), and residential or hotel components in larger developments. Examples include Kierland Commons (Scottsdale), Easton Town Center (Columbus), and Santana Row (San Jose).

**Deal Profile.** Discretionary retail exposure creates more cyclicality than necessity-based formats. Performance is highly correlated to trade area household income and the center's ability to maintain experiential differentiation and tenant quality. Cap rates range from 5.5% to 8.0% depending on occupancy, anchor quality, and market. Lease terms have shortened since 2015; percentage rent and co-tenancy provisions are more prevalent. See [[Lifestyle Center]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** Institutional REITs (Federal Realty, Macerich, Simon in some cases), open-end core funds for top-tier product, value-add funds pursuing repositioning.

### Mall Formats

**Profile.** The enclosed regional or super-regional mall is the most capital-intensive and bifurcated retail format. Traditional regional malls (400,000–1,200,000 SF) are anchored by department stores (Macy's, Nordstrom, JCPenney, Sears) with an enclosed climate-controlled concourse of inline specialty retail. Super-regionals exceed 800,000 SF and draw from trade areas of 15–25+ miles. The market is sharply divided: "Class A" and "luxury" malls (Simon, Brookfield, Taubman portfolio) command below-7% cap rates and strong sales per SF; "Class B/C" and distressed malls trade at 8%–12%+ or may not be financeable at all. Outlet centers (Simon Premium Outlets, Tanger) represent an adjacent format with distinct tenancy. Open-air "town center" or "lifestyle mall" hybrids blur format lines. See [[Mall Formats]] for full detail.

**Deal Profile.** Institutional capital is largely bifurcated: trophy/Class A malls are held by the largest REITs (Simon, Brookfield); Class B/C product is distressed or in hands of workout lenders and opportunistic buyers. Redevelopment and adaptive reuse are the primary investment thesis for lower-tier malls.

**Typical Owners.** Public REITs for Class A/trophy; CMBS special servicers, debt funds, and opportunistic buyers for distressed; municipal entities for some adaptive reuse conversions.

### Single-Tenant Net Lease (STNL)

**Profile.** STNL properties are freestanding single-occupant buildings — typically 1,500 to 20,000 SF — leased to a single national or regional brand under a long-term NNN or absolute-net lease. The tenant is responsible for property taxes, insurance, and maintenance; the landlord collects a net check. Common tenant categories include QSR (McDonald's, Chick-fil-A, Starbucks, Taco Bell), pharmacy (Walgreens, CVS), convenience (7-Eleven), dollar (Dollar General, Dollar Tree), auto parts (AutoZone, O'Reilly), and medical (urgent care, dialysis). Lease terms range from 10 to 25 years with periodic rent bumps (typically 1.5%–10% every 5 years or annually).

**Deal Profile.** STNL is the closest real estate product to a bond proxy — income is contractual, management is passive, and pricing reflects the credit of the tenant, the length of the remaining lease term, and the location's perceived re-tenanting optionality. Going-in caps range from 4.5% for long-term absolute-net IG QSR to 7.0%+ for shorter-term or sub-investment-grade credits. Risk concentrations include tenant credit deterioration, lease expiration and renewal risk, and a structural building that may have limited alternative uses. See [[Single-Tenant Net Lease (STNL)]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** 1031 exchange buyers (broadest buyer pool), private investors, open-end funds, and institutional core funds for investment-grade product. Public non-traded REITs (Realty Income, NNN REIT, STORE Capital) are major aggregators.

### Urban Street Retail

**Profile.** Urban street retail occupies ground-floor or podium space in dense urban environments — high streets, mixed-use buildings, transit corridors, and residential tower bases. Tenants range from luxury boutiques and flagship stores on trophy high-street locations (Fifth Avenue, Michigan Avenue, Rodeo Drive, Union Square) to local F&B and service uses on secondary urban corridors. Space sizes vary widely (500–20,000 SF), with irregular configurations common in historic buildings. Rents are highly location-specific and correlated to pedestrian counts, daytime population density, and residential above. Leases are typically shorter (5–10 years) and often include percentage rent components.

**Deal Profile.** Urban street retail is priced on location scarcity and the durability of pedestrian traffic — factors that are harder to model than suburban metrics. Cap rates range from 4.0% to 7.5% and are highly market-specific; trophy locations in gateway cities may trade at sub-5% caps. The asset class was disproportionately impacted by the COVID-19 pandemic (2020–2021) due to office and tourism traffic disruption; recovery has been uneven. Risk factors include mixed-use building ownership complexity, landlord-unfavorable leases in soft markets, and regulatory exposure (rent regulation in some jurisdictions). See [[Urban Street Retail]] for full detail.

**Typical Owners.** Institutional core funds for gateway-city trophy locations; private owners, family offices, and foreign capital for secondary urban retail; mixed-use developers for podium retail components.
