# Retail — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Underwriting retail real estate requires a format-specific framework that accounts for the diversity of lease structures, tenant credit profiles, recovery regimes, and obsolescence risks across subtypes. Unlike multifamily (where unit-level rent is the primary lever) or industrial (where NNN rents on long leases simplify projection), retail cash flow modeling integrates percentage rent, tenant-by-tenant lease-by-lease rent roll analysis, variable recovery ratios, and co-tenancy contingencies that can materially alter NOI under stress scenarios. The starting point is always a thorough [[Rent Roll Analysis]] and [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] of actuals before building any [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] projection.

The most common underwriting methods for retail acquisitions are [[Direct Capitalization]] (for stabilized product with in-place NOI) and [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] (for any asset with meaningful rollover, vacancy, or repositioning upside over the hold period). For value-add retail, DCF analysis over a 5–10 year hold period is the standard; sensitivity on exit cap rate and lease-up velocity drives the return range. For STNL and other bond-proxy product, direct capitalization on in-place NOI against market cap rates is the dominant pricing methodology.

Market conditions, subtype quality, tenant credit, and geographic context all affect underwriting inputs materially. The ranges below reflect mainstream institutional assumptions for stabilized product in primary and secondary U.S. markets as of 2026-05-24; they are not a substitute for current market data.

## Lease Structures

### NNN (Triple Net)

The dominant lease structure across institutional retail. Under a true NNN lease, the tenant pays base rent plus its pro-rata share of real estate taxes, property insurance, and common area maintenance (CAM). The landlord's economic exposure is limited to landlord-controlled capital expenditures (roof, structure, parking lot in some leases) and vacancy. NNN leases simplify landlord income projection but shift credit exposure to the tenant — the quality of the NOI stream is only as good as the tenant's covenant.

Key NNN underwriting considerations include: (1) the definition of CAM (controllable vs. uncontrollable expenses, management fee cap, audit rights, exclusions); (2) the definition of "gross-up" provisions allowing the landlord to recover expenses as if the center were fully occupied; (3) the landlord's responsibility for capital items (roof, HVAC, structural) often retained even in NNN leases; and (4) the recovery cap or floor embedded in anchor leases that limits the anchor's CAM exposure. Anchor tenants typically negotiate below-proportional CAM recovery, which means inline tenant recovery ratios must be higher to close the gap.

### Percentage Rent

Percentage rent provisions require the tenant to pay an additional rent amount equal to a percentage of gross sales above a "natural breakpoint" — typically calculated as base rent divided by the percentage rate. For example, a tenant paying $50,000/year in base rent at a 5% rate has a natural breakpoint of $1,000,000; if sales exceed $1,000,000, the tenant owes 5% of the excess. Percentage rent rates vary by retail category: grocery anchors typically 0.5%–1.5%; soft-goods inline tenants 4%–6%; QSR 6%–8%; specialty tenants 4%–7%.

Percentage rent adds NOI upside when tenant sales are strong but creates analytical complexity: it requires underwriters to model tenant sales per SF and occupancy cost trajectory. For institutional underwriting, percentage rent is typically treated as a conservative upside assumption rather than base-case NOI unless the tenant's historical sales support a recurring overage.

### Co-Tenancy Clauses

Co-tenancy provisions are among the most significant structural risks in retail leasing and must be identified and stress-tested in underwriting. These clauses allow an inline tenant to reduce its rent — typically to a percentage-of-sales-only basis — or to terminate its lease if a specified condition occurs: most commonly (1) the anchor tenant vacates or closes, (2) center occupancy falls below a stated threshold (e.g., 75%), or (3) a named co-tenant leaves the center.

In underwriting, co-tenancy cascades are modeled by identifying all tenants with anchor co-tenancy triggers, estimating the probability of anchor vacancy, and modeling the NOI impact if 15%–40% of inline tenants shift to percentage-of-sales rent. This analysis often reveals that the effective floor NOI is substantially below the current stabilized NOI — a risk that is difficult to underwrite in a traditional cap rate framework and is better captured in a [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] stress scenario.

### Gross Leases and Modified-Gross Leases

Less common in institutional multi-tenant retail but prevalent in certain urban street retail, older strip centers, and some secondary markets. Under a gross lease, the landlord receives a flat rent and bears all operating expenses. Modified-gross leases typically have the landlord pay base-year expenses and tenants pay increases above the base year. These structures increase landlord operating risk and complicate NOI comparability across a portfolio. When underwriting properties with gross or modified-gross leases, normalize to NNN-equivalent before comparing cap rates to market.

## OpEx and Recovery Norms

**CAM expenses** for open-air retail centers typically range from $3 to $8 PSF of GLA depending on market, age, and amenity level. Major components: parking lot maintenance and repair, landscaping, lighting, security, property management fees, and administrative costs. Controllable CAM expense growth is typically underwritten at 2%–4% annually; uncontrollable items (taxes, insurance) are passed through as incurred.

**Real estate taxes** vary widely by jurisdiction and assessment methodology. In underwriting, use the actual current tax bill for existing properties and budget for reassessment at acquisition price for projections. Tax appeals are common for retail properties and should be factored into underwriting for value-add acquisitions.

**Property insurance** has increased substantially post-2020 in many markets due to catastrophic loss events; underwrite current market pricing for the subject market rather than rolling forward historical costs. Coastal and catastrophe-exposed markets warrant additional sensitivity on insurance expense.

**Management fees** for retail are typically 3%–5% of effective gross income (see [[Effective Gross Income (EGI)]]); higher for smaller or more complex centers. Most institutional underwriting uses 3%–4% for institutional-grade multi-tenant retail and 4%–5% for smaller or less stable assets.

**Reserves** for retail capital expenditures vary by format and age. Typical institutional underwriting applies $0.15–$0.50 PSF annually for ongoing reserves; major capital items (roof replacement, parking lot resurfacing, HVAC replacement) are scheduled separately based on condition assessments.
