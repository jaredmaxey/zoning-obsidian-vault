# Ground-Up Development — Regulatory Considerations

## Overview

Ground-up development involves more regulatory interaction than any other CRE activity. From the earliest land acquisition through the final occupancy permit, the developer navigates multiple layers of government oversight — land use entitlement, environmental review, building code compliance, utility agreements, and ongoing inspections. Each layer imposes time, cost, and approval uncertainty. Regulatory competence is a core competitive advantage for developers: those who understand the local process, build relationships with planning staff, and proactively address community concerns can complete entitlement in months; those who do not can spend years and still fail. Regulatory cost is increasingly material — in high-cost jurisdictions, impact fees, exactions, and inclusionary housing requirements can represent 10–20% of total project cost.

---

## Entitlement Dependency

All ground-up development depends on receiving discretionary land use approvals before construction can begin. The entitlement process varies dramatically by jurisdiction, project type, and political context, but typically involves some combination of the following approvals. See [[Entitlement Risk Phases]] for a complete phase-by-phase risk breakdown.

**General Plan Amendment (GPA).** Required when the project's proposed land use is inconsistent with the jurisdiction's adopted General Plan (or Comprehensive Plan). GPAs are legislative actions decided by the city council or board of supervisors, involve public hearings, and carry significant political risk. They can take 12–24+ months in complex cases. See [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]].

**Zone Change / Rezoning.** Required when the site's base zoning does not permit the intended use or density. Like GPAs, rezonings are legislative and politically sensitive. They are often paired with development agreements that lock in the entitlement in exchange for community benefits or infrastructure contributions. See [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]].

**Conditional Use Permit (CUP).** A quasi-judicial approval for uses that are permitted in the zone only with conditions. CUPs attach specific operational and design conditions to the project and are frequently the vehicle by which community concerns are addressed. They can be appealed. See [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]].

**Variance.** Relief from a specific development standard (setback, height, parking) that the project cannot meet due to site constraints. Variances require a findings-based showing of hardship and are granted more narrowly than CUPs. See [[Variance]].

**Site Plan Review.** Administrative or quasi-judicial review of the project's site design for compliance with standards. Common for larger projects even when zoning is already in place. See [[Site Plan Review]].

**Planned Unit Development (PUD).** A site-specific zoning approval that establishes custom development standards for a project, typically in exchange for design quality, public amenities, or affordable units. Provides flexibility but requires negotiation and approval. See [[Planned Unit Development (PUD)]].

**Subdivision Approval.** Required when land is being divided into parcels (for fee-simple sale, lot-line adjustment, or tract development). See [[Subdivision Approval]].

The [[Public Hearing Process]] is the vehicle through which most discretionary approvals are decided. Neighbor opposition, expressed through the [[Neighborhood Outreach]] process, is one of the most common sources of project modification or denial. Unfavorable decisions can be challenged through the [[Appeals Process]].

## Building Codes and Permits

After entitlement is secured, the project must obtain a building permit from the jurisdiction's building department before construction begins. Building permits confirm compliance with the adopted building code (typically the International Building Code with local amendments), fire code, plumbing, mechanical, and electrical codes. Plan check timelines vary from weeks (expedited jurisdictions) to 6–18 months (high-volume permitting departments or highly complex projects). Permit fees are typically tied to construction valuation and can range from modest to significant depending on jurisdiction.

Throughout construction, the building department conducts inspections at defined milestones (foundation, framing, rough MEP, etc.). A failed inspection can stop work and require remediation. At project completion, a [[Certificate of Occupancy (CO)]] is issued confirming the building is code-compliant and may be legally occupied. Without a CO, tenants cannot legally take occupancy and the developer cannot commence rent collection or trigger permanent financing triggers. Managing the CO process — including punchlist and final completion items — is a critical closeout milestone; see also [[Punchlist and Final Completion]].

## Impact Fees and Exactions

Impact fees are charges levied by local governments on new development to fund infrastructure capacity required to serve the development — transportation, parks, schools, libraries, water, sewer, and fire/police. Impact fees are set by ordinance and vary enormously by jurisdiction. In high-growth Sun Belt jurisdictions with active infrastructure programs, transportation impact fees alone may exceed $5,000–$15,000 per residential unit; in California and high-cost metros, total impact fees (transportation, schools, parks, utilities) of $30,000–$75,000+ per unit are not uncommon.

Exactions are non-fee contributions — land dedications for rights-of-way, public plazas, or affordable housing units — extracted as conditions of approval. Inclusionary housing requirements mandate that a percentage (commonly 10%–20%) of units in multifamily projects be rented or sold at below-market (affordable) rates, effectively cross-subsidizing affordable units from market-rate revenue. Impact fees and exactions must be included in the [[Pre-Development Budget]] and [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] from the earliest feasibility analysis, as they directly affect the residual land value ([[Residual Land Value Method]]) and development spread.

## Prevailing Wage and Labor Requirements

Projects that receive public subsidies (tax-exempt bonds, HOME funds, CDBG, Low-Income Housing Tax Credits), are located on public land, or are constructed under public contract are frequently subject to prevailing wage requirements — mandating that construction workers be paid the locally prevailing union wage rate as determined by the relevant labor authority. In California, New York, and several other states, prevailing wage requirements apply broadly to publicly assisted projects and can increase labor costs by 20%–40% relative to market wages.

Beyond prevailing wage, some jurisdictions require construction workers to meet local hiring requirements (a percentage of workers from local zip codes) or to use union labor as a condition of project approval or public funding. These requirements add cost and procurement complexity. Projects pursuing public financing layers — particularly those structured as P3s — must model labor cost requirements accurately; underestimating labor costs is a common source of budget overruns in affordable and public-sector developments.

## Environmental Review

The [[Environmental Review Overview]] governs the process by which a lead agency (city, county, or state) assesses the potential environmental impacts of a proposed development and determines whether mitigation measures are required. In states with robust environmental statutes — most notably California's CEQA (California Environmental Quality Act) — environmental review can be one of the most consequential and time-consuming regulatory hurdles a project faces.

Environmental review may be triggered by any entitlement application and can range from a brief categorical exemption (for small infill projects) to a full Environmental Impact Report (EIR) taking 1–3 years and costing $500,000–$2,000,000+ for complex projects. Issues commonly studied include traffic, air quality, noise, biological resources, cultural/historic resources, hazardous materials, and greenhouse gas emissions. Mitigation measures imposed through the environmental process — traffic signal upgrades, biological resource surveys, cultural resource monitoring — become binding conditions of approval and add cost and schedule.

Phase I and Phase II Environmental Site Assessments (ESAs) are routinely required by lenders and prudent practice before land acquisition. Phase I identifies recognized environmental conditions (RECs) from historical uses; Phase II involves physical sampling to confirm or rule out contamination. Identified contamination must be disclosed, remediated, or priced into the land acquisition and can add significant cost and timeline to a project.

## Utility and Infrastructure Agreements

Large development projects often require formal agreements with utility providers — water, sewer, electric, gas, and telecommunications — to secure capacity for the project and fund infrastructure extensions or upgrades. Utility agency agreements specify the timing, cost, and conditions of service extension. In growth-constrained markets, utility capacity may be insufficient to serve new development without major infrastructure investment, creating an additional feasibility variable that must be resolved before permitting.

Capacity reservation fees, connection fees, and the cost of off-site infrastructure (extending mains, upgrading pump stations) are part of the project's infrastructure budget and can represent $2,000–$20,000+ per unit in capital cost. The timing of utility agreements — securing commitments before land acquisition or as a condition of construction permit issuance — affects the project schedule and the pre-development capital at risk. See [[Pre-Development Budget]] for how these costs are budgeted and tracked.
