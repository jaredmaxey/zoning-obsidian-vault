# Ground-Up Development — Subtypes

## Overview

Ground-up development is not a monolithic strategy — it encompasses distinct approaches that differ fundamentally by exit strategy, timing of tenant commitment, risk tolerance, and the type of investor or developer best suited to execute. Understanding which subtype a deal represents is essential for applying the correct return benchmarks, capital structures, and risk frameworks. These subtypes can also overlay each other: a pre-leased industrial project that the developer intends to hold is simultaneously "pre-leased" and "build-to-core."

---

### Merchant Build-to-Sell

Merchant development is the purest form of ground-up development as a business rather than an investment: the developer builds and sells the completed, stabilized (or near-stabilized) asset to a long-term investor. The developer's return is measured as profit on cost — typically targeted at 15–25% of total development cost, or an IRR of 15–25%+ depending on duration and leverage. Capital recycling is the defining characteristic; merchant builders are volume-oriented operators who return equity to LPs and redeploy it rather than building a hold portfolio.

Merchant builders are most active in multifamily, industrial, and for-sale residential condominiums. Institutional LP capital supporting merchant builders typically targets opportunistic or high-value-add returns. Risk is concentrated in construction and lease-up: the merchant builder must deliver at or below budget and achieve sufficient occupancy to support a sale at or above the underwritten exit cap rate. Exit timing is negotiated with the capital partner and typically occurs 6–24 months post-certificate of occupancy. The developer's GP promote is generally crystallized at sale; see [[GP Co-Invest and Promote Crystallization]] and [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]].

---

### Build-to-Core / Hold

Build-to-core development uses the development process as a means of acquiring a stabilized core or core-plus asset at below-market cost. Rather than selling at stabilization, the sponsor retains the asset, refinances out the [[Construction Loan]] into permanent debt ([[Senior Debt]], [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]], [[Life Company Debt]], or [[CMBS]]), and holds for long-term income and appreciation. The return thesis is that the developer's all-in cost is 10–20% below the stabilized value that an acquirer would pay, creating immediate embedded equity in the hold portfolio.

This approach is pursued most commonly by large institutional developers, pension fund advisors, and family offices with patient capital. It requires long-term alignment between the developer and equity partner, as IRR metrics are less favorable over multi-decade holds compared to shorter merchant-build cycles. Cash-on-cash return and stabilized yield on cost are the primary return metrics. The risk profile is similar to merchant build through the construction and lease-up phases, but hold-period exit risk shifts from near-term market timing to long-cycle capital market assumptions.

---

### Build-to-Suit (BTS)

Build-to-suit is development of a purpose-built asset for a single pre-committed tenant who has signed a lease (or purchase agreement) before groundbreaking. By eliminating speculative lease-up risk, BTS projects command tighter developer spreads — often 50–100 bps less development spread than comparable spec product — but offer significantly de-risked execution. Lenders favor BTS projects and will often advance higher LTC ratios or offer tighter spreads on [[Construction Loan]] facilities.

BTS is dominant in industrial (distribution centers, manufacturing), office (corporate campus, government), and retail (single-tenant net lease). The credit quality of the anchor tenant drives both the project's financeability and the exit cap rate; investment-grade tenants effectively securitize the income stream. Developers pursue BTS to utilize their entitlement and construction capabilities while minimizing speculative risk; institutional developers frequently sign BTS agreements as part of tenant-relationship strategies. See also [[Build-to-Suit]] (deal-type note) for additional mechanics.

---

### Spec Development

Speculative development breaks ground without pre-committed tenants, betting on demand forecasts and market timing. Spec projects require the deepest conviction in demand absorption and the strongest local market knowledge. The risk-adjusted return premium over pre-leased development compensates for the possibility that the building delivers into a soft or oversupplied market — the scenario where lease-up stalls, carry costs mount, and equity returns erode.

Spec development is most common in industrial (bulk warehouse and last-mile logistics) and multifamily, where individual tenants are small relative to building size, demand is broad, and absorption can often be completed within 12–18 months of delivery. Spec office is far rarer and viewed as high-risk in most markets given longer lease-up timelines and the severity of oversupply consequences. Lenders scrutinize spec projects heavily; most construction lenders require evidence of strong submarket fundamentals, developer track record, and pre-leasing milestones as loan-advance conditions. Sensitivity analysis on lease-up timing is critical; see [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]].

---

### Pre-Leased / Pre-Sold Development

Pre-leased development occupies the middle ground between spec and BTS: the developer secures meaningful tenant commitments — typically 30–60%+ of the project by square footage or units — before beginning construction, then completes the remainder on a spec basis. Pre-sales (condominiums, for-sale housing) are the analogous structure in residential contexts. Pre-leasing de-risks lease-up, satisfies lender pre-leasing requirements common in construction lending agreements, and validates the rent assumptions used in the project pro forma.

Institutional capital increasingly requires pre-leasing thresholds as a condition to closing [[Joint Venture Structures|JV equity]] or funding [[Construction Loan|construction loans]]. The developer manages a tension between moving quickly to start construction and waiting for sufficient pre-leasing — delay increases cost carry but insufficient pre-leasing may make the deal un-financeable. Pre-leasing economics typically involve below-market rents relative to the project's stabilized target, as early tenants extract concessions for commitment risk; the developer must underwrite these discounts carefully against the benefit of reduced speculative exposure.

---

### Public-Private Partnership (P3)

Public-private partnerships involve a formal collaborative structure between a private developer and a government, quasi-public, or nonprofit entity to develop projects that serve a public purpose — affordable housing, civic infrastructure, transit-oriented development, university housing, or municipal facilities. In exchange for advancing public goals (affordability, density, community benefit), the developer typically receives public land at below-market cost, tax increment financing, infrastructure funding, density bonuses, or expedited entitlement.

P3 structures vary enormously: ground leases on public land, air rights transfers, tax-exempt bond financing (via [[Mezzanine Debt]] equivalents), Low-Income Housing Tax Credit (LIHTC) equity, and various government loan programs are common tools. Returns are generally below market on a pure IRR basis but are supported by the public subsidy layer, making projects feasible where private-market rents alone would not support development costs. Execution complexity is significantly higher — government procurement processes, prevailing wage requirements, community benefits agreements, and political risk add time and cost that must be priced into the pro forma. Institutional affordable housing developers, mission-driven organizations, and municipalities with strong development offices are the primary players.
