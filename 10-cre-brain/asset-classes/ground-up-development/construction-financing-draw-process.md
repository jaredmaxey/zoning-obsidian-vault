# Construction Financing Draw Process

## Overview

The construction draw process is the mechanism through which a developer draws down the proceeds of a [[Construction Loan]] to fund ongoing construction costs. Unlike permanent loans that are fully funded at closing, construction loans are disbursed in increments — called draws or advances — as work is completed. Each draw represents a reimbursement (or advance) for costs incurred since the prior draw, verified by an independent inspection and supported by contractor invoices, lien waivers, and title confirmation. The draw process is one of the most practically consequential parts of construction finance: delays in draw approval or disbursement can stall subcontractor payments, create lien exposure, and damage relationships with the general contractor. Managing the draw process efficiently is a core GP operational competency.

---

## Construction Loan Structure Recap

Before understanding the draw process, it is useful to recall the loan's basic architecture. The [[Construction Loan]] is a revolving-to-term facility — the lender commits to lend up to a maximum amount (the "loan amount"), but the outstanding balance builds over time as draws are made rather than being disbursed at closing. Key structural features that affect the draw process:

- **Loan budget:** An approved line-item budget allocating the loan proceeds across hard costs, soft costs, interest reserve, and contingency. All draws must reconcile to this budget; budget reallocations require lender approval.
- **LTC covenant:** Maximum outstanding loan balance may not exceed the loan-to-cost ratio ([[Loan-to-Cost (LTC)]]) applied to total costs incurred to date.
- **Interest reserve:** An allocated line item within the loan budget that accrues interest charges monthly without requiring cash equity contributions; sized to cover the expected interest through construction completion.
- **Retainage:** A percentage (typically 5%–10%) withheld from each hard-cost draw as a quality-assurance reserve, released after substantial completion or [[Certificate of Occupancy (CO)]].
- **Equity funded first:** Most lenders require that developer equity be contributed before any loan proceeds are advanced, or on a pro-rata basis; draw mechanics enforce this sequencing.

## Monthly Draw Cycle

### Step 1: Draw Request Preparation

The general contractor (GC) submits a monthly Application for Payment — typically using AIA Form G702/G703 or equivalent — to the developer. This document itemizes:
- Work completed during the period, broken out by cost code or specification section.
- Materials stored on-site but not yet incorporated (subject to lender approval and insurance confirmation).
- Total earned to date and retainage withheld to date.
- Net amount requested for the period.

The developer reviews the GC's application against the approved [[Construction Schedule and Critical Path]] and their own records of observed progress. The developer also prepares a parallel draw request for soft costs (architecture fees, engineering invoices, permit fees, insurance premiums, developer fee accruals, interest reserve draws) incurred during the period. All supporting invoices, bills, and receipts are attached.

### Step 2: Lender's Inspector / Construction Monitor

The lender engages an independent third-party construction manager or inspector — sometimes called the Owner's or Lender's Construction Monitor (LCM) — who physically visits the site each month and prepares an inspection report assessing:
- Percentage of completion for each major trade, compared to the contractor's claimed completion.
- Conformance with approved plans and specifications.
- Material compliance with the project schedule; identification of any delays or schedule slippage.
- Adequacy of the remaining loan budget to complete the project (a "cost to complete" analysis).
- Any observed defects, disputes between GC and subcontractors, or quality concerns.

The LCM's approval recommendation is required before the lender will advance the draw. If the LCM approves a lower percentage of completion than the GC claimed, the draw amount is reduced accordingly.

### Step 3: Title Company Bring-Down

Before funding each draw, the title company performs a "bring-down" of the title insurance policy — a date-down endorsement confirming that no new liens, encumbrances, or mechanic's liens have been recorded against the property since the prior draw. If any liens appear, they must be resolved (typically by the developer bonding over the lien or paying it off) before the draw is funded. The lender's position as the first-lien lender on the project must be maintained at all times.

### Step 4: Lien Waivers

As a condition of draw funding, the lender and title company require:
- **Conditional lien waivers** from the general contractor and all major subcontractors waiving their lien rights through the current draw amount, conditional on receipt of payment.
- **Unconditional lien waivers** from the prior period — confirming that the prior draw was received and those waiver conditions have been satisfied.

Lien waivers are the developer's and lender's primary protection against mechanics' liens that would subordinate the lender's security interest. Failure to obtain lien waivers — particularly from subcontractors who are not paid by the GC despite the developer funding the draw — is a common source of lien exposure on troubled projects.

### Step 5: Lender Review and Approval

The lender's construction loan administrator reviews the complete draw package — draw request, LCM report, title bring-down, lien waivers, and supporting invoices — for completeness and compliance with the loan agreement. If approved, the lender issues a disbursement directive to the title/escrow company.

Draw review timelines vary by lender: institutional construction lenders (banks, debt funds) with dedicated construction lending teams typically process draws in 5–10 business days; smaller or less-experienced lenders may take 2–3 weeks. Slow draw processing is one of the most common complaints from developers about construction lenders, and GC payment obligations are typically contractually due within 30–45 days of payment application, creating potential disputes when lender review delays occur.

### Step 6: Disbursement

The title/escrow company disburses funds from the construction loan account — either by wire to the developer's operating account (for soft costs disbursed by the developer) or directly to the GC via a two-party check (GC plus developer, or through a joint check arrangement for direct sub payments). Direct disbursement to the GC is more common in institutional construction lending as it reduces the developer's commingling risk and protects against developer cash diversion (a [[Bad-Boy Carve-Outs]] trigger).

## Retainage Release

Throughout construction, typically 5%–10% of each hard-cost draw is withheld as retainage — held in a separate trust account and released only upon:
- Substantial completion of the project (as certified by the architect and confirmed by the LCM).
- Receipt of the [[Certificate of Occupancy (CO)]].
- Satisfactory completion of the project punchlist.
- Expiration of the applicable lien period with no unresolved claims.

Retainage is a quality-assurance mechanism incentivizing the GC and subcontractors to complete punchlist items and close out the project rather than moving on to new work. In many states, statutory prompt-pay laws govern retainage release timing and rates; violation exposes the developer to interest and penalties.

## Common Draw Process Complications

**Budget line-item reallocations.** When costs in one line item (e.g., structural steel) run over budget and another line item (e.g., landscaping) has savings, the developer must request a formal budget reallocation from the lender before drawing from the higher-cost line. Most lenders restrict reallocations out of contingency without pre-approval. Unauthorized reallocations are loan agreement violations.

**Stored materials.** Materials purchased and stored off-site (e.g., major equipment or pre-fabricated components) are not automatically eligible for draw funding; lenders typically require independent storage confirmation, insurance, and an assignment of the material to the lender.

**Disputed pay applications.** Disputes between the developer and GC over claimed work completion percentages can delay or reduce draws, potentially triggering contractual late-payment provisions and stalling subcontractor payments.

**Construction loan maturity.** If the project is delayed and the construction loan is approaching maturity without reaching substantial completion, the developer must negotiate extension options — typically 1–2 extensions of six months each, with extension fees of 0.25%–0.50% of the loan amount per extension. Failure to complete the project before maturity without a negotiated extension constitutes a loan default.
