# Ground-Up Development — Risks & Considerations

## Overview

Ground-up development is the CRE activity that carries the most simultaneous risk vectors. Unlike stabilized acquisitions, where risk is primarily forward-looking lease-up or market deterioration, development stacks entitlement, construction, capital, and market risks across a multi-year timeline before any income is generated. Each category of risk is manageable individually, but the compound probability of one or more adverse outcomes occurring over a 3–5 year project cycle is significant. Experienced developers mitigate risk through phasing, contractual structures, pre-commitment strategies, and disciplined contingency planning — but they cannot eliminate it. Understanding these risks in detail is foundational to both development underwriting and investor due diligence.

---

### Market

**Demand deterioration at delivery.** The most fundamental development risk is that market conditions at the time the project delivers differ materially from conditions at the time the developer committed to the project. Rents may have declined due to new supply, a demand shock, or a macro recession; vacancy may have increased; target tenant profiles may have shifted. Development timelines of 18–36+ months mean that demand forecasts are inherently speculative.

**Mitigants:** Pre-leasing commitments reduce demand risk; absorption rate analysis ([[Absorption Rate]]) and supply pipeline monitoring ([[Supply Pipeline Analysis]]) can identify deteriorating conditions early enough to pause or pivot; phased development allows the sponsor to hold or defer later phases if phase-one performance is soft. [[Stress Testing]] across lease-up timing and rent scenarios should be standard.

**Timing and cycle risk.** Breaking ground at peak-of-cycle conditions — high land prices, elevated construction costs, tight spreads — creates exposure to delivering into a downturn. The [[Real Estate Cycle and Phases]] is a useful framework; developers who commit to multi-year projects near the top of the cycle frequently find that exit cap rates have expanded by delivery, eroding spread.

**Mitigants:** Underwrite with conservative exit cap assumptions (at or above current market caps, with expansion scenarios); use untrended rent yields as the primary feasibility test; avoid assuming both rent growth and cap rate compression simultaneously.

---

### Entitlement & Approvals

**Entitlement denial or delay.** Development projects require government approvals before construction can begin. A [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]], or [[Environmental Review Overview|environmental review]] can be denied, appealed by neighbors or activists, or delayed for years in high-barrier markets. Pre-development capital invested in design, studies, and carrying costs can be lost entirely if entitlement fails. See [[Entitlement Risk Phases]] for a phase-by-phase breakdown of where entitlement risk concentrates.

**Mitigants:** Perform entitlement risk assessment before committing land capital; structure land acquisitions with contingencies tied to entitlement milestones; engage community stakeholders early; retain experienced land use counsel; underwrite entitlement failure scenarios as a real possibility in [[Development Feasibility Test|feasibility analysis]].

**Scope reduction through conditions.** Even when approvals are granted, conditions of approval — height reductions, setback requirements, affordable housing exactions, infrastructure contributions — may materially alter the project's economics from the original underwriting assumptions. Impact fees and exactions in high-cost jurisdictions can add $10,000–$50,000+ per unit to the cost basis.

**Mitigants:** Negotiate conditions actively; model downside scenarios for maximum possible conditions during land pricing; include entitlement cost contingency in the [[Pre-Development Budget]].

---

### Construction & Cost

**Cost overruns.** Hard cost inflation, material price escalation (steel, concrete, lumber, mechanical/electrical), labor shortages, and scope changes are among the most common drivers of budget overruns. The difference between a GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price) contract and a cost-plus contract determines how much overrun risk the developer absorbs versus the general contractor. See [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] for the contractual risk allocation framework.

**Mitigants:** Contract structure (GMP contracts transfer overrun risk to the GC within the guaranteed maximum); hard cost contingency of 5%–10% ([[Contingency: Hard vs. Soft]]); early procurement of long-lead items; [[Value Engineering]] to identify cost reduction without scope elimination; [[Payment and Performance Bonds]] on large projects to backstop GC performance.

**Construction schedule delays.** Permitting delays, subcontractor failures, material delivery delays, weather events, and labor availability can push delivery timelines beyond the construction loan maturity. Every month of delay adds interest carry costs and defers revenue; in rising-rate environments, the marginal cost of delay is particularly high.

**Mitigants:** [[Construction Schedule and Critical Path]] analysis to identify and protect schedule-critical activities; [[Builder's Risk Insurance]] for physical damage events; construction loan maturities with extension options (typically 1–2 six-month extensions); conservative interest reserve sizing.

**Subcontractor and GC failure.** A subcontractor insolvency or general contractor default mid-project can be catastrophic — creating liens, stopping progress, and requiring expensive replacement contractors. [[Payment and Performance Bonds]] provide recourse but resolution is slow and imperfect.

**Mitigants:** Pre-qualify GC and key subcontractors financially; require payment and performance bonds on major scopes; maintain a short list of replacement contractors; monitor GC financial health throughout construction.

---

### Capital & Financing

**Construction loan maturity and refinancing risk.** Construction loans typically mature 18–36 months after close. If the project is delayed or if lease-up has not progressed sufficiently to qualify for permanent financing, the developer faces a maturity crisis. Lenders may extend only with punitive fees or may exercise remedies if the project is in default.

**Mitigants:** Size the construction loan with adequate extension options; maintain an interest reserve sufficient to cover worst-case delays; underwrite permanent financing qualifying criteria (DSCR, occupancy thresholds) and confirm they are achievable before break-ground; have a [[Bridge Loan]] fallback modeled.

**Rising interest rates.** Development is exposed to floating interest rates during construction. In a rising-rate environment, carry costs increase on existing draws, and the permanent financing exit is priced at higher cap rates, compressing development spread and residual value simultaneously.

**Mitigants:** Interest rate cap agreements on the construction loan limit upside exposure; some sponsors swap into fixed rate synthetically; underwrite at stressed interest rates; size the interest reserve conservatively.

**Equity partner disagreement or default.** JV disputes, LP capital calls during construction delays, or LP default on equity commitments can freeze projects at critical moments. The GP-LP relationship in development is particularly high-stakes given the long duration and capital intensity.

**Mitigants:** Well-drafted JV agreements ([[Joint Venture Structures]]) with clear default provisions, dilution mechanics, and decision-making rights; GP liquidity reserves; financing structures that limit LP capital call exposure through waterfall mechanics. See [[Recourse vs. Non-Recourse]] and [[Bad-Boy Carve-Outs]] for debt recourse considerations.

### Lease-Up & Exit

**Extended lease-up timeline.** Even after construction is complete, a project may take significantly longer to achieve stabilized occupancy than underwritten. Office leases require tenant improvement buildout periods of 3–9 months after signing; large multifamily projects may take 12–18 months to reach stabilized occupancy; retail anchor lease-up may be limited by co-tenancy requirements. Each month of extended lease-up adds carry costs and delays the exit or refinance event.

**Mitigants:** Pre-leasing before CO; letter-of-intent pipeline monitoring; realistic absorption rate assumptions calibrated to competitive supply; build [[Bridge Loan]] assumptions into the capital structure to bridge construction loan maturity to stabilization.

**Exit cap rate expansion.** If market cap rates expand between underwriting and stabilization — due to rising interest rates, credit market tightening, or demand deterioration — the stabilized value of the asset declines, compressing profit margin and potentially destroying development spread. Exit cap rate expansion is the most financially consequential single variable in development underwriting.

**Mitigants:** Underwrite the exit cap rate at or above current market levels, not below; run [[Sensitivity Analysis]] testing 50–100 bps of cap rate expansion; include hold scenarios that allow the developer to defer exit if cap rates are punitive; build-to-core strategy avoids short-term exit dependency.

**Tenant credit risk post-stabilization.** For BTS projects and projects with credit-dependent rent structures, tenant credit deterioration or bankruptcy post-stabilization can impair the asset's income stream and exit value. This risk is concentrated in single-tenant structures.

**Mitigants:** Diversify tenant roster where possible; underwrite tenant credit at lease signing and monitor; structure lease guarantees and security deposits appropriately; [[Payment and Performance Bonds]] may not apply post-construction, so lease guaranty structures take on added importance.
