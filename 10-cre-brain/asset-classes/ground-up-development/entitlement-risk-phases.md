# Entitlement Risk Phases

## Overview

Entitlement is the process of obtaining the governmental approvals necessary to build a development project. It is both the highest-risk and the least-financeable phase of the development cycle — capital is deployed, time passes, and outcomes are subject to discretionary government decisions that no amount of technical analysis can guarantee. Developers who understand where risk concentrates at each phase can structure land acquisitions, budget pre-development capital, and design their entitlement strategies to minimize capital exposure at each decision gate. This note maps the five primary entitlement phases, the capital at risk in each, the key risk drivers, and the mitigants available.

The entitlement process varies significantly by jurisdiction — high-barrier coastal markets involve lengthy CEQA/NEPA review, competitive opposition, and multi-year timelines, while business-friendly Sun Belt markets may permit administrative approval of straightforward projects in 3–6 months. Any specific project's entitlement path must be mapped to the local regulatory framework before committing land acquisition capital.

---

## Phase 1: Pre-Application and Site Control

**Description:** Before any formal application is submitted, the developer conducts initial due diligence — confirming zoning, researching the General Plan designation, assessing the approvals required, and identifying community and political stakeholders. This phase often occurs simultaneously with negotiating site control through an option agreement, purchase and sale agreement with feasibility period, or letter of intent.

**Capital at risk:** Minimal — primarily soft costs for preliminary due diligence (title report, land use counsel consultation, zoning analysis, preliminary site plan studies). Typically $25,000–$150,000 depending on project complexity.

**Key risks:**
- Discovering that required approvals are more onerous or politically difficult than initially assessed.
- Competing buyers acquiring the site before entitlement is confirmed.
- General Plan or zoning inconsistencies that make the project infeasible without legislative action.

**Mitigants:**
- Structure land acquisition with a due diligence contingency period (60–180 days) before going hard on the purchase price.
- Use an option agreement that allows the developer to walk away if entitlement proves infeasible; option payments are the capped capital at risk.
- Retain experienced local land use counsel early to assess risk before capital is committed.
- Study the jurisdiction's recent entitlement track record for similar projects.

---

## Phase 2: Pre-Application and Pre-Development

**Description:** After committing to pursue entitlement, the developer funds the pre-development program — assembling the design and consulting team, completing technical studies required for the application (traffic, environmental, geotechnical, cultural resources), preparing application materials, and conducting [[Neighborhood Outreach]] to identify and preemptively address community concerns. Many jurisdictions offer formal or informal pre-application meetings with planning staff.

**Capital at risk:** Moderate — architect, civil engineer, traffic engineer, environmental consultant, and legal fees; application fees; community engagement costs. Typically $200,000–$1,000,000+ for complex projects. This capital is entirely at risk if the application is denied or withdrawn.

**Key risks:**
- Technical study results (environmental contamination, biological resources, traffic levels of service) that create requirements for costly mitigation measures.
- Planning staff opposition or unfavorable findings that foreclose approval pathways.
- Community opposition organized during the outreach process.
- Application completeness reviews that require additional studies and delay the hearing timeline.

**Mitigants:**
- Phase studies to identify fatal flaws before committing to the full pre-development budget.
- Proactive community engagement to surface opposition early and allow for project design modifications.
- Negotiate application fees and study requirements with planning staff in pre-application meetings.
- Track aggregate pre-development spending against the underwritten feasibility assumptions in the [[Pre-Development Budget]].

---

## Phase 3: Application Processing and Public Review

**Description:** The formal application is submitted and enters the jurisdiction's review pipeline. Planning staff reviews the application for completeness, circulates it to referral agencies (fire, public works, utilities, schools), prepares a staff report, and schedules the project for a [[Public Hearing Process|public hearing]] before the Planning Commission or City Council. [[Environmental Review Overview|Environmental review]] — ranging from a categorical exemption to a full Environmental Impact Report — occurs in parallel. This phase is the longest and least predictable in high-barrier markets.

**Capital at risk:** Significant accumulation — the developer continues to carry land acquisition costs (interest on purchase price or option payments), pre-development consultant fees for additional studies and responses to agency comments, and the developer's own overhead. Cumulative pre-development investment of $500,000–$3,000,000+ is common for large projects in complex jurisdictions.

**Key risks:**
- [[Environmental Review Overview|Environmental review]] generating significant mitigation measures that alter project scope or cost.
- Planning staff recommending denial or conditions that change project economics materially.
- Organized opposition at the public hearing triggering project redesign, additional hearings, or denial.
- EIR litigation (in CEQA jurisdictions) threatening to invalidate the approval.
- Extended review timelines pushing delivery beyond the original underwriting window.

**Mitigants:**
- Respond to agency and staff comments promptly and completely.
- Retain community advocates and municipal affairs consultants for politically sensitive projects.
- Design flexibility into the project to accommodate conditions of approval within economic tolerances.
- Budget for EIR litigation defense in high-CEQA-risk jurisdictions.
- Maintain communication with the equity partner about timeline and cost changes as they occur.

---

## Phase 4: Discretionary Approval and Condition Negotiation

**Description:** The decision-making body (Planning Commission, Design Review Board, City Council) holds the public hearing, hears testimony, deliberates, and votes to approve, approve with conditions, or deny the project. If approved, conditions of approval are negotiated and finalized. Development agreements — binding contracts between the developer and the jurisdiction — may be executed at this stage, locking in entitlement certainty in exchange for community benefits, infrastructure contributions, or affordable housing.

**Capital at risk:** Maximum pre-development capital has been expended; at this point, the economics of abandonment are heavily weighted toward proceeding. However, conditions of approval may substantially alter project economics.

**Key risks:**
- Denial: all pre-development capital is lost; timeline to re-application is typically 12+ months in many jurisdictions.
- Onerous conditions of approval — height reductions, parking requirements, affordable housing set-asides, design changes, infrastructure exactions — that alter the project's cost basis or rental economics.
- [[Appeals Process|Appeal]] by opponents delaying implementation for 3–12 months post-approval.
- Development agreement terms that constrain project flexibility over a long entitlement horizon.

**Mitigants:**
- Engage directly with council/commission members during the process (where permitted) to understand concerns.
- Prepare cost-of-conditions analysis so that material conditions can be negotiated based on economic impact.
- Have a project redesign ready as a fallback if standard conditions are projected to be imposed.
- Budget an appeal-related timeline buffer and additional carry costs.
- Negotiate development agreement terms carefully — particularly vesting provisions that protect the project from subsequent code changes.

---

## Phase 5: Post-Approval and Permit Issuance

**Description:** After discretionary approval is granted and any appeal period has passed (or appeals are resolved), the developer obtains construction permits. This involves submitting construction drawings for plan check review, paying permit fees and impact fees, satisfying any pre-construction conditions of approval (biological surveys, archaeological monitoring agreements, utility capacity agreements), and receiving the building permit that authorizes construction to commence.

**Capital at risk:** Impact fees and permit fees are paid at this stage and can represent $5,000–$75,000+ per unit in high-cost jurisdictions. Final design and engineering fees are being incurred. The developer is financially committed; construction mobilization is imminent.

**Key risks:**
- Plan check corrections requiring redesign that delays permit issuance and adds cost.
- Impact fees higher than budgeted due to changes in fee schedules between entitlement and permit.
- Utility capacity agreements not executed, delaying service connection and construction.
- Pre-construction condition requirements (environmental mitigation, archaeological monitoring) creating schedule delays.

**Mitigants:**
- Submit construction documents for concurrent plan check during the late stages of entitlement review (at-risk but time-saving).
- Confirm current impact fee schedules and lock in fee amounts where the jurisdiction's process allows.
- Execute utility agreements as early as possible in the entitlement process.
- Assign a dedicated permit expediter for high-volume or complex permit departments.
- Build permit issuance risk into the construction loan schedule and interest reserve sizing.
