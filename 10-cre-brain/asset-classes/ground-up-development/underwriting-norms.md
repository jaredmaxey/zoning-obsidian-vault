# Ground-Up Development — Underwriting Norms

## Overview

Development underwriting is fundamentally different from stabilized-asset underwriting because the asset being valued does not yet exist and may not stabilize for years. The underwriter must simultaneously forecast construction costs (on a Sources & Uses basis), project the future income the completed asset will generate, test whether that future income supports the required return at the projected total cost, and stress-test the assumptions that are most sensitive — rent growth, construction costs, timeline, and exit cap rate. The governing framework is [[Pro Forma Construction Method]], supplemented by [[Residual Land Value Method]] for land pricing and [[Development Feasibility Test]] for the go/no-go decision.

## Typical Ranges

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Target yield-on-cost (untrended) | 5.5%–8.0% | Multifamily and industrial often 5.5%–6.5%; office and mixed-use typically 6.5%–8.0%; varies by market and vintage |
| Development spread (YOC minus exit cap) | 100–250 bps | Minimum threshold varies by sponsor; compressed markets may accept 75–100 bps; higher-risk projects require 200+ bps |
| Profit margin / return on cost (merchant build) | 15%–25% | Profit as % of total cost; lower in core gateway markets, higher in secondary/spec development |
| Loan-to-cost (LTC) | 55%–70% | Senior construction debt; BTS and pre-leased deals may reach 65%–70%; spec land-heavy deals often limited to 55%–60% |
| Construction loan interest rate (over SOFR) | 250–450 bps | Varies significantly by market cycle, lender appetite, and project risk; all-in rate 7%–11%+ in current environment |
| Hard cost contingency | 5%–10% | 5% for GMP contracts with established GC; 7.5%–10% for cost-plus or early-stage programs |
| Soft cost contingency | 3%–5% | Covers design, permitting, and carry-cost overruns |
| Lease-up period (post-CO to stabilization) | 6–24 months | Industrial/BTS: 0–6 months; multifamily: 6–12 months; office and mixed-use: 12–24+ months |
| Stabilized vacancy assumption | 3%–10% | Asset-class and market dependent; align with long-term market vacancy, not lease-up peak |
| Interest reserve / carry | Sized to maturity | Typically 12–30 months of interest at projected draw schedule; sized to cover worst-case construction duration |

## Trended vs. Untrended Yield on Cost

One of the most consequential underwriting choices in development is whether to test feasibility using **untrended** (today's rents) or **trended** (projected at-delivery rents) assumptions for stabilized NOI.

**Untrended yield on cost** uses today's market rents and expenses without forward adjustment. It is the more conservative test and is preferred by many institutional lenders and equity partners because it reflects current market conditions and avoids layering speculative rent growth on top of already-speculative construction and lease-up assumptions. If a project pencils on untrended rents, any real rent growth at delivery is upside rather than a required assumption.

**Trended yield on cost** projects rents forward to the expected stabilization date — typically 2–4 years from initial underwriting — using a market-supported rent growth rate (commonly 2%–4% annually, varying by sector and market). Trended analysis is appropriate when the developer is underwriting in a proven demand environment with visible rent trajectory and is trying to assess the realistic return at actual stabilization, not just today's snapshot. Many sponsors underwrite both: the untrended yield must clear a minimum hurdle (e.g., 5.75%) and the trended yield must clear the full return target (e.g., 6.25%+) to support a proceed decision.

The danger of trended-only underwriting is that rent projections become a residual plug to justify deals that don't pencil today. Disciplined underwriters treat trended rent assumptions as one of the most stress-tested variables in [[Sensitivity Analysis]], typically running downside cases with 0% rent growth or modest rent declines at delivery.

## Sources & Uses Logic

Every development pro forma begins with a Sources & Uses statement that accounts for every dollar of project cost and how it will be funded. This structure is the foundation of [[Capital Stack Overview|capital stack]] sizing and [[Construction Loan]] negotiations.

**Uses (total project cost)** includes:
- Land acquisition cost (or residual land value if land already owned)
- Hard costs (vertical construction): see [[Hard Costs]]
- Soft costs (design, engineering, fees, permits, legal, insurance, developer fees): see [[Soft Costs]]
- Financing costs (loan fees, interest carry/reserve during construction)
- [[Contingency: Hard vs. Soft|Contingency]] (hard and soft, sized separately)
- Pre-development costs (incurred prior to construction close, often already spent by the time of deal close)
- Lease-up costs (tenant improvement allowances, leasing commissions, concessions)
- Developer fee (varies by JV structure; often 2%–5% of hard costs or total project cost)

**Sources** includes:
- Senior construction debt ([[Construction Loan]]): typically 55%–70% of total cost
- [[Mezzanine Debt]] or [[Preferred Equity]] (if applicable): typically bridges LTC to 75%–85%
- LP equity (majority of the remaining cost)
- GP equity (co-invest, typically 5%–20% of total equity)
- Any public subsidy, grants, or tax credit equity (in P3/affordable structures)

Sources must equal Uses exactly at financial close. Lenders require that uses are fully funded (no funding gaps) and that equity is contributed ahead of or pari passu with debt draws. The Sources & Uses statement ties directly to the [[Loan-to-Cost (LTC)]] and leverage metrics used in construction loan sizing.

## Return Metrics

Development returns are typically evaluated on three complementary measures:

**Yield on Cost** is the primary feasibility metric — stabilized NOI divided by total development cost. It must exceed the prevailing exit cap rate by a sufficient margin (the [[Development Spread]]) to compensate for development risk. Many institutional developers require a minimum 125–200 bps spread before proceeding. See [[Yield on Cost]] and [[Yield-on-Cost vs. Market Cap Spread]].

**Return on Cost / Profit Margin** is used by merchant builders and tracks total profit as a percentage of total cost. A 20% profit margin on a $50M project implies $10M in profit at sale. This is simple and intuitive but does not account for time or leverage; it must be read alongside IRR.

**Levered IRR and Equity Multiple** capture the time value of equity capital, accounting for the J-curve of negative cash flow during construction. Target levered IRRs for development typically range from 15%–25%+ depending on risk profile and subtype; equity multiples of 1.8x–2.5x over 3–5 years are common institutional targets. See [[Internal Rate of Return (IRR)]] and [[Equity Multiple]].
