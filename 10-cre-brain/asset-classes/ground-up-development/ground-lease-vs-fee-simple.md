# Ground Lease vs. Fee Simple

## Overview

When a developer pursues a ground-up project, the land underlying the development can be owned in one of two ways: **fee simple** (outright ownership of the land and improvements) or through a **ground lease** (the developer owns only the improvements; the land is leased from a landowner under a long-term ground lease). Each structure has distinct financial, legal, and operational implications that materially affect project feasibility, financing, and ultimate asset value. Understanding these differences is essential when evaluating development opportunities, underwriting project returns, and structuring deals with institutional landowners, public entities, or long-term landholding families.

---

## Fee Simple Ownership

In a fee simple structure, the developer acquires full ownership of the land parcel and constructs improvements on it. The land acquisition cost is paid upfront (or over an extended seller-carry period) and is included in the total project cost for LTC ([[Loan-to-Cost (LTC)]]) and feasibility purposes ([[Residual Land Value Method]]). At project completion, the developer owns both land and improvements, and the fully integrated property can be sold, refinanced, or held without the encumbrance of a ground lease.

**Advantages of fee simple:**
- Simpler ownership structure with no ongoing rent obligation to a ground landlord.
- Broader lender universe; most conventional lenders, CMBS, and agency debt programs prefer or require fee simple collateral.
- No reversion risk — the developer retains ownership of the land indefinitely.
- Higher exit value relative to leasehold, because buyers pay for unencumbered fee simple real estate at tighter cap rates.
- Flexibility to sell, refinance, recapitalize, or redevelop without ground landlord consent.

**Disadvantages of fee simple:**
- Requires full land acquisition capital upfront, increasing peak equity and reducing development returns (particularly in high-land-cost markets).
- Developer absorbs all land value volatility — both upside and downside.

---

## Ground Lease Structure

A ground lease is a long-term lease of the underlying land from the landowner (lessor) to the developer/tenant (lessee), typically with a term of 50–99 years. The developer builds improvements on the leased land and operates them for the lease term, paying periodic ground rent to the landowner. At lease expiration, the improvements typically revert to the landowner (reversion), though many modern ground leases negotiate extended terms, renewal options, or developer repurchase rights.

**Typical mechanics:**
- **Ground rent**: Typically set as a percentage of land value (often 5%–7% of appraised land value per year) or as a flat annual payment with periodic escalation (CPI, fixed step, or reappraisal). Rent structures vary enormously by negotiation and jurisdiction.
- **Rent escalation**: Most ground leases include periodic rent resets — either CPI-linked increases, fixed percentage bumps, or market reappraisal every 10–25 years. Reappraisal clauses introduce rent uncertainty and are a significant risk factor for the lessee.
- **Leasehold improvements**: The developer owns the improvements for the lease term. Improvements are financed as leasehold collateral; lenders take a security interest in the leasehold estate and improvements, not the land.
- **Subletting and assignment**: Ground leases typically permit subleasing and assignment to creditworthy parties; restrictions vary.
- **Reversion**: At lease expiration, improvements revert to the landowner unless the lease provides otherwise. This creates terminal value risk for long-dated leasehold investors.

**Advantages of ground lease:**
- Reduces upfront land cost, lowering peak equity and improving development returns — particularly in high-land-cost markets.
- Landowner may accept below-market ground rent in exchange for development quality, community benefit, or public purpose (common in P3 and public land transactions).
- Allows developers to control premium land in markets where fee simple sales are unavailable (e.g., university land, public land, family trust land that cannot be sold).
- Developer captures NOI from improvements without deploying full land acquisition capital.

**Disadvantages of ground lease:**
- Ground rent is a senior obligation — it must be paid before debt service and equity distributions, similar to a preferred equity layer in the capital stack.
- Leasehold financing is more complex and expensive than fee simple financing; not all lenders accept leasehold collateral, and those that do typically apply additional underwriting restrictions.
- Reversion risk reduces terminal value, particularly for leaseholds with fewer than 30–40 years remaining; leaseholds with less than 30 years remaining are often considered unleaseable and difficult to finance.
- Rent reappraisal clauses create NOI uncertainty that can impair stabilized value and debt coverage.
- Assignment, sale, and recapitalization require ground landlord consent in many leases, reducing liquidity.

## Leasehold Financing

Financing a development on ground-leased land requires a lender willing to take a leasehold mortgage — a security interest in the lessee's interest in the lease and the improvements, rather than the fee simple land. Leasehold lenders require the ground lease to contain specific protective provisions:

- **Non-disturbance agreement (NDA) / SNDA**: The ground landlord must agree not to disturb the lender's leasehold interest in the event of a lessee default; lenders require the right to cure defaults and continue the lease.
- **Lease term exceeding loan term**: The ground lease must extend materially beyond the loan maturity — typically by at least 10–20 years — to provide adequate collateral duration.
- **Notice and cure provisions**: Lender must receive notice of any lessee default and have the right to cure before the lessor can terminate.
- **New lease requirement**: Lender may require the landlord to enter a new lease directly with the lender (or lender's designee) in the event of lessee default, preserving the collateral even if the original lease is terminated.

Because leasehold mortgages carry higher legal complexity and structural risk than fee simple mortgages, leasehold lenders charge wider spreads, impose lower LTC limits, and have shorter loan terms. Agency debt programs (Fannie/Freddie) will finance leasehold multifamily projects but with additional requirements (minimum remaining lease term, fee title insurance for the ground lease). CMBS and life company lenders are typically more selective about leasehold collateral.

## Underwriting Differences

The developer's pro forma must reflect ground rent as an operating expense — typically treated as a line item below NOI in development underwriting but above net cash flow available for debt service. Ground rent increases the operating cost structure of the project and reduces the NOI available to support debt and equity returns. The stabilized value of a ground-leased asset is lower than an equivalent fee simple asset, all else equal, because buyers discount the leasehold estate for reversion risk, rent escalation uncertainty, and reduced financing flexibility. This discount — commonly expressed as a wider exit cap rate applied to leasehold NOI — must be factored into the development spread calculation when using a leasehold structure.

Residual land value analysis ([[Residual Land Value Method]]) applied to a ground lease context solves for the supportable ground rent (or land value) rather than a purchase price. The developer tests whether the project's residual cash flow, after covering construction costs and required equity returns, can support the ground landlord's required return on land value.

---

## When Each Structure Is Preferred

**Fee simple is preferred** when: land is available for purchase at a supportable residual value; the developer intends to hold the stabilized asset long-term; the exit strategy relies on liquid institutional buyers (who strongly prefer fee simple); or the project is sized to support full land acquisition within the capital structure.

**Ground lease is preferred or required** when: the land is not available for sale (public land, university, family trust); reducing upfront land equity significantly improves projected returns and is achievable with leasehold financing; the developer has negotiated favorable ground rent terms (below-market rent as part of a community benefit or P3 transaction); or the project is in a high-land-cost market where fee simple acquisition makes the development infeasible.
