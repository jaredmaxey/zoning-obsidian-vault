# Ground-Up Development (Asset Class)

## Overview

Ground-up development is the process of creating new real estate from bare or entitled land through design, financing, construction, lease-up, and ultimate stabilization or sale. Unlike acquisitions, which purchase existing income streams, development creates value by delivering a completed asset at a total cost — land plus hard and soft construction costs — that is meaningfully below the stabilized value that asset would trade for on the open market. That spread, commonly measured as [[Yield on Cost]] minus the prevailing [[Exit Cap Rate]] (the [[Development Spread]]), is the fundamental return mechanism and the primary lens through which feasibility is tested.

Development is the highest-risk, highest-return quadrant of the CRE risk spectrum. The investor takes on entitlement risk, construction cost and schedule risk, lease-up risk, and financing risk simultaneously, often over a multi-year timeline before any income is produced. Capital is illiquid during construction; cost overruns, permitting delays, or demand shifts can erode returns or destroy equity entirely. In exchange, successful projects can generate unlevered yields-on-cost that are 100–250+ basis points above market cap rates, and levered IRRs well above what stabilized acquisitions can offer at comparable underwriting periods. Institutional allocations to development typically sit within value-add or opportunistic mandates, and are executed via ground-up JVs, development operating companies, or separate accounts with developer expertise.

The current environment (as of 2026) reflects materially elevated construction costs relative to pre-2022 levels, tighter [[Construction Loan]] availability, and compressed development spreads in many major markets — making project selection and cost control more important than at any point in the prior cycle. Higher land basis, elevated interest carry, and softened rent growth in some sectors have reduced the number of deals that pencil at institutional return thresholds. However, constrained new supply pipelines in industrial, multifamily workforce housing, and select-service hospitality continue to support strong exit cap environments for projects that do break ground. See the deal-type note [[Ground-Up Development]] for deal-level mechanics and [[Development Feasibility Test]] for the go/no-go framework.

## Subtypes

Development approaches vary by exit strategy, risk profile, and capital source. See [[Ground-Up Development — Subtypes]] for full profiles.

- **Merchant Build-to-Sell** — Developer builds and sells upon stabilization or completion; optimizes for return on cost over a 3–5 year timeline rather than long-term hold yield.
- **Build-to-Core / Hold** — Developer constructs with the intent to hold as a core or core-plus asset post-stabilization; optimizes for stabilized NOI and long-term appreciation.
- **Build-to-Suit (BTS)** — Purpose-built for a specific pre-committed tenant; eliminates lease-up risk at the cost of negotiated rents; common in industrial and office.
- **Spec Development** — Breaks ground without pre-committed tenants; requires conviction in demand and timing; higher risk, higher potential return.
- **Pre-Leased / Pre-Sold Development** — Achieves meaningful pre-commitment before groundbreaking; blends BTS economics with merchant-build execution.
- **Public-Private Partnership (P3)** — Joint venture with a government or quasi-public entity; common in affordable housing, civic-adjacent mixed-use, and infrastructure-heavy projects; unique capital and regulatory structures.

## Demand Drivers

Ground-up development demand is derivative: the developer's thesis rests entirely on the strength of end-user demand for the completed product. For multifamily, population growth, household formation, and affordability pressure relative to for-sale housing drive absorption. For industrial, e-commerce penetration, supply chain reconfiguration, and nearshoring create bulk warehouse and last-mile demand. For office, employment growth in knowledge industries, corporate real estate strategy, and hybrid work adoption rates drive leasing demand. For retail, trade area demographics, co-tenancy, and shifting consumer spending patterns determine project viability. Development is therefore a downstream bet on the same demand drivers tracked in [[Demand Driver Analysis]], with the added sensitivity that the product will not deliver for 18–36 months, requiring the developer to forecast demand forward.

Leading indicators for development viability include rent growth trends ([[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]]), vacancy compression below equilibrium, declining supply pipeline from permitting data, employment and population projections ([[Demographic Analysis]], [[Employment and Jobs Analysis]]), and cap rate compression signaling investor appetite for the stabilized product. Developers rely heavily on [[Absorption Rate]] analysis and [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] to time starts and size projects.

## Supply Dynamics

New supply in any market is the aggregated output of individual development decisions. Supply tends to overshoot demand at the peak of the real estate cycle because ground-breaking decisions are made during favorable periods, but construction timelines of 12–36 months mean delivery coincides with softer demand. This dynamic is well-documented in the [[Real Estate Cycle and Phases]] framework. Barriers to new supply — entitled land scarcity, construction cost inflation, permitting timelines, and financing availability — are the structural conditions that make development spreads positive in the first place.

Entitlement timelines are a particularly consequential supply barrier. In high-barrier markets (coastal California, Pacific Northwest, parts of the Northeast), entitlement alone can take 2–5+ years and consume significant pre-development capital before a shovel enters the ground. See [[Entitlement Risk Phases]] and the entitlement registry notes ([[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]], [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], [[Environmental Review Overview]]) for the process detail. In low-barrier markets (Sun Belt metros, Texas, parts of the Mountain West), faster entitlement means supply responds more quickly to demand signals and cycles are correspondingly more volatile.

## Underwriting Norms

See [[Ground-Up Development — Underwriting Norms]] for full detail, including the sources and uses logic and trended vs. untrended yield discussion.

| Metric | Typical Range | Notes |
|--------|---------------|-------|
| Target yield-on-cost (untrended) | 5.5%–8.0% | Varies widely by asset type, market, and capital cost; must exceed exit cap by target spread |
| Development spread (YOC minus exit cap) | 100–250 bps | Minimum spread at which deal "pencils"; compressed in hot markets |
| Profit margin / return on cost | 15%–25% | Total profit as % of total cost; varies by subtype and hold strategy |
| Loan-to-cost (LTC) | 55%–70% | Senior construction debt; lower for ground-up land-heavy deals |
| Construction loan spread (over SOFR) | 250–450 bps | Varies by market cycle, lender type, and project risk |
| Hard cost contingency | 5%–10% | Typically 5% for GMP contracts; 10% for early-stage cost-plus |
| Soft cost contingency | 3%–5% | Design, permitting, and carry cost buffer |
| Lease-up period post-CO | 6–24 months | Industrial/BTS tightest; Class A office and large multifamily longest |

*Ranges current as of 2026-05-24. Verify against current market data before underwriting.*

## Key Operating Metrics

Development underwriting uses a distinct set of metrics not applicable to stabilized acquisitions. See [[Ground-Up Development — Key Metrics]] for full definitions and formulas.

- **[[Yield on Cost]]** — Stabilized NOI divided by total development cost; the central feasibility metric.
- **Development Spread** — Yield on cost minus exit cap rate; the return premium for taking development risk; see [[Yield-on-Cost vs. Market Cap Spread]] and [[Development Spread]].
- **Profit Margin / Return on Cost** — Total profit (value minus cost) divided by total cost; common merchant-build return metric.
- **Peak Equity** — Maximum equity drawn during construction; determines equity multiple denominator.
- **[[Loan-to-Cost (LTC)]]** — Construction loan balance divided by total project cost; the primary leverage metric during construction.
- **Cost per SF / per Unit** — Total development cost divided by rentable area or unit count; basis benchmarking against comparable deliveries.
- **Hard:Soft Cost Ratio** — Proportion of budget allocated to hard construction costs versus soft costs; typically 65–75% hard, 25–35% soft varies by project type.

## Common Deal Structures

Ground-up development is almost universally executed through a joint venture between an operating developer (GP/sponsor) who contributes expertise, entitlement, and deal sourcing, and a capital partner (LP) who contributes the majority of equity. The [[Joint Venture Structures]] framework applies: LP capital typically earns a [[Preferred Return Mechanics|preferred return]] of 6–10% before profit sharing, with developer promote structures (carried interest) of 20–35% above the preferred return hurdle. See [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] and [[GP Co-Invest and Promote Crystallization]].

The [[Construction Loan]] is the primary debt instrument — typically a floating-rate, interest-only facility sized at 55–70% of total project cost ([[Loan-to-Cost (LTC)]]). At stabilization, projects are either sold (merchant build), refinanced into permanent [[Senior Debt]], [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] (for multifamily), [[Life Company Debt]], or [[CMBS]], or held and recapitalized. [[Bridge Loan]] structures may bridge the gap between CO and full stabilization when construction financing matures before lease-up is complete. [[Mezzanine Debt]] or [[Preferred Equity]] layers above senior construction debt are common in complex or higher-leverage capital stacks. See [[Capital Stack Overview]].

## Major Risks

See [[Ground-Up Development — Risks & Considerations]] for full treatment organized by risk category.

- **Entitlement and approval risk** — Projects can be delayed or denied at any stage of the public process; timeline and cost uncertainty is highest in the pre-development phase.
- **Construction cost and schedule risk** — Material price escalation, labor shortages, supply chain disruptions, and subcontractor failures can blow out budgets and push delivery timelines.
- **Capital and financing risk** — Construction lending markets can contract sharply; rising interest rates inflate carry costs on floating-rate debt and reduce going-in feasibility.
- **Lease-up and demand risk** — Market conditions at delivery may differ materially from underwriting assumptions; oversupply or demand softness can extend lease-up timelines significantly.
- **Cost of capital** — Development's long J-curve (negative cash flow through construction) means IRR is highly sensitive to hold-period length and exit cap rate assumptions.

## Regulatory Considerations

See [[Ground-Up Development — Regulatory Considerations]] for full treatment. Development is the most regulation-intensive CRE activity: entitlement processes ([[Conditional Use Permit (CUP)]], [[Zone Change (Rezoning)]], [[Environmental Review Overview]]) must be completed before construction; building permits and inspections govern construction; impact fees and exactions can add $5,000–$50,000+ per unit or per 1,000 SF in high-cost jurisdictions; and utility capacity agreements, prevailing wage requirements, and inclusionary housing mandates layer additional cost and timeline risk.

## Notable Markets

Development activity concentrates in markets where demand growth outpaces existing supply, entitlement timelines are manageable, and construction costs are competitive relative to achievable rents. Sun Belt metros — Phoenix, Dallas-Fort Worth, Austin, Charlotte, Nashville, Atlanta — have historically led in ground-up multifamily and industrial development due to population growth, business-friendly entitlement, and lower construction cost bases. Coastal gateway markets (New York, Los Angeles, San Francisco, Seattle, Boston) command the highest absolute rents but also the highest land, labor, and entitlement costs, compressing development margins and limiting the projects that pencil. Secondary and tertiary markets offer occasionally attractive land pricing but carry greater lease-up risk and thinner exit liquidity.
