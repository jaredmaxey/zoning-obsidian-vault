# Pre-Development Budget

## Overview

The pre-development budget encompasses all capital deployed from the moment a developer commits to pursuing a project — typically at site control — through the day construction begins. This capital is entirely at risk before any debt is advanced and before any income is generated. It is the most speculative capital in the development cycle: if the project is denied entitlement, is deemed infeasible during due diligence, or is cancelled before construction start, the pre-development budget is largely unrecoverable. Managing the pre-development budget with discipline — phasing expenditures to match confidence level, identifying fatal flaws early, and maintaining cost-basis transparency with the equity partner — is a core competency of experienced developers.

Pre-development costs roll into the "soft costs" component of the total development budget and are a required input for the [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] and the [[Residual Land Value Method]]. Underestimating pre-development costs — particularly in high-barrier jurisdictions with lengthy environmental review — is a common source of budget variance on first-time or out-of-market projects.

## Components of the Pre-Development Budget

### Land Acquisition and Carry

- **Land purchase price** (or option payments through entitlement): the single largest pre-development cost component.
- **Closing costs** at land acquisition: title insurance, escrow, transfer taxes, recording fees.
- **Land carry costs**: property taxes, insurance, and interest on land acquisition debt (if leveraged) accruing during entitlement.
- **Option payments**: if the developer uses an option agreement to control the site, option payments are capitalized and credited to the purchase price at exercise. Unexercised options are sunk costs.

### Architecture and Design

- **Schematic design (SD)**: early conceptual design to test massing, program, and feasibility assumptions. Typically 5%–10% of total architecture fee.
- **Design development (DD)**: further development of the approved design concept to 30%–60% construction document level.
- **Construction documents (CDs)**: full design package required for building permit submission. The majority of the architecture fee is earned at this stage.
- **Structural, civil, and MEP engineering** fees, typically contracted separately from the architect.
- **Interior design** (for hospitality, multifamily amenity-heavy projects): additional design fee.

### Technical Studies and Environmental

- **Phase I Environmental Site Assessment (ESA)**: standard lender and prudent-practice requirement; $3,000–$8,000 typically.
- **Phase II ESA** (if warranted by Phase I findings): physical sampling for contamination; $10,000–$100,000+ depending on scope.
- **Geotechnical investigation**: soil borings and subsurface analysis to inform foundation design; $15,000–$75,000 typically.
- **Traffic study / traffic impact analysis (TIA)**: required by most jurisdictions for projects above a certain trip-generation threshold.
- **Environmental Impact Report (EIR)** or environmental documentation (in CEQA/NEPA jurisdictions): $150,000–$2,000,000+ for full EIRs on complex projects. See [[Environmental Review Overview]].
- **Biological, cultural, and historical resources studies**: mandated by environmental review; cost varies by site conditions.
- **Hazardous materials survey**: required by lenders; $5,000–$15,000 typically.

### Entitlement and Legal Fees

- **Land use counsel**: entitlement attorney fees throughout the discretionary approval process.
- **Application fees**: jurisdiction-specific; range from nominal to $50,000+ for complex discretionary applications.
- **Community outreach and facilitator**: engagement consultant, community meetings, noticing costs.
- **Municipal affairs consultant / lobbying**: for politically sensitive projects.
- **Development agreement negotiation**: additional legal work to negotiate vesting and community benefit terms.
- **Appeal defense costs** (if the approval is appealed by opponents).

### Permitting and Agency Fees

- **Plan check fees**: paid at building permit submission; typically a percentage of estimated construction value.
- **Building permit fee**: paid at permit issuance; triggers construction mobilization authorization.
- **Impact fees**: transportation, parks, schools, utilities — paid at permit or as a condition of approval; highly variable by jurisdiction and can be the largest single pre-construction cost item. See [[Ground-Up Development — Regulatory Considerations]].
- **Utility connection fees**: capacity reservation and connection fees paid to water, sewer, electric, and gas providers.

### Developer Overhead and Miscellaneous

- **Developer fee**: commonly 2%–5% of hard costs or total project cost, earned over the development period; a portion may be accrued during pre-development.
- **Project management overhead**: developer staff time allocated to the project.
- **Lender and broker fees**: origination fees, broker fee for land acquisition or financing placement; typically incurred at construction loan close but negotiated earlier.
- **Title and survey**: updated ALTA survey of the site required for construction financing.
- **Pre-development contingency**: typically 10%–15% of the pre-development soft cost budget (excluding land) to cover unforeseen study requirements, scope additions, and timeline extensions.

## Budget Management Principles

**Phase-gate spending.** The most important discipline in pre-development is not committing full-program spending upfront. Sequence expenditures to confirm feasibility at each stage before funding the next. Commission the Phase I before the geotechnical; complete the preliminary entitlement risk assessment before ordering the full EIR; get schematic design massing before paying for full design development. Each gate should trigger a go/no-go review against the [[Development Feasibility Test]] criteria.

**Transparency with capital partners.** Pre-development spending in JV structures is typically funded by the GP or through a pre-development equity facility. LPs expect monthly reporting on pre-development budget versus actual, with clear documentation of entitlement milestone progress. Any material budget variance — additional studies required, application fee increases, unexpected remediation — should be communicated immediately with an updated feasibility analysis.

**At-risk vs. fully committed capital.** Some pre-development costs are "at-risk" (spent and unrecoverable regardless of project outcome) while others are contingent or cancellable. Option payments fall somewhere in between — not fully committed until exercised. Developers track the at-risk capital threshold carefully: the point at which the economic case for proceeding (even a marginal project) outweighs the sunk cost recovery rationale for abandonment.

**Integration with construction loan.** Pre-development costs that are properly documented and approved by the lender can be included in the [[Construction Loan]] budget as historical soft costs, allowing equity to be partially recouped at construction financing close. Lenders require receipts, contracts, and appraisal support for all historical soft costs included in the loan basis. Undocumented or non-arm's-length pre-development costs are commonly excluded from lender-approved budgets.
