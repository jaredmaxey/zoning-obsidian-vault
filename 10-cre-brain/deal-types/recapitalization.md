# Recapitalization

## Profile

A recapitalization (recap) is a transaction in which the capital structure of an existing property ownership — debt, equity, or both layers — is meaningfully restructured while the asset itself remains under the same management or control. Recaps occur for several reasons: existing equity investors need liquidity before a full asset sale is desired or optimal; the existing debt is maturing or has become misaligned with the current business plan; a new equity investor wants to enter the partnership at a current valuation that allows the original sponsor to crystallize profit without selling; or the sponsor wants to fund a new capital improvement program by bringing in a partner at a reset basis. Unlike an outright sale, the sponsor typically retains control and continues to manage the asset — the recap is a capital structure event, not a change-of-control event.

The most common form is an equity recap: a new institutional equity partner purchases a percentage of the ownership at the current appraised or negotiated value, providing a cash distribution to the existing investors who are selling all or part of their stake. The sponsor (GP) retains its ownership and operational control. This structure is particularly common after a successful value-add business plan has been largely executed: the asset is stabilized or near-stabilized, the existing fund's investment period is ending, and LP investors want to realize gains while the sponsor sees continued upside from holding. The recap allows both objectives to be satisfied simultaneously.

A debt recap — replacing existing debt with new debt at different terms — is another common form, particularly when interest rates fall (allowing refinance at lower cost), when an existing bridge loan matures (forcing a transition to permanent financing), or when an existing loan no longer matches the property's improved performance (an asset that has been value-added now supports higher proceeds on a permanent basis). The [[Refinance Decision Framework]] governs when and how a debt recap is executed.

## Typical Capital Stack

The recap transaction effectively re-sets the [[Capital Stack Overview]] at current property value. In an equity recap, new equity comes in at the appraised value (supported by [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] and [[Direct Capitalization]] of current [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]]), and the existing equity holders are cashed out proportionally. The existing debt may be assumed by the new capital structure (if assumption is permitted and the terms are favorable), refinanced simultaneously, or structured around (if the debt is fixed-rate and advantageously priced). New equity structures typically mirror the format of a standard joint venture or fund structure: [[Preferred Return Mechanics]] for the incoming LP, a GP promote upon exceeding hurdle returns, and [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] that reflect the expected hold period and return profile post-recap.

Where the recap involves new debt, the new loan is typically sized to the current property performance: LTV between 55–70% of the current appraised value (varies by asset class, occupancy, and market conditions, current as of 2026-05-24), with [[Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)]] minimums that reflect the stabilized NOI. If the asset is fully stabilized with strong tenancy, a [[Permanent Loan]] from a life company or [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] (for multifamily) provides the best long-term economics. [[Mezzanine Debt]] or [[Preferred Equity]] may be added as a second lien above the senior loan to provide additional equity proceeds to distributing investors, though this reduces the stabilized cash-on-cash yield for ongoing equity holders.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Recapitalization underwriting must establish the current property value with precision, since that value determines both the cash distribution to exiting investors and the entry price for incoming investors. [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] and [[Rent Roll Analysis]] are used to document the current NOI, and [[Direct Capitalization]] (applying the [[Going-In Cap Rate]] or current market cap rate to the [[Stabilized NOI]]) establishes the property value. [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] provide the cap rate evidence for the valuation.

The incoming equity investor performs underwriting similar to a core-plus or value-add acquisition: a [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] model projecting NOI growth over the expected hold period, an [[Exit Cap Rate]] assumption for the eventual disposition, and sensitivity around lease renewal probabilities and market rent trajectory. The [[GP Co-Invest and Promote Crystallization]] provisions are particularly important in recap structures: how is the original GP's accrued but unpaid promote from the pre-recap period treated? Is it paid out at the recap, carried forward, or reset? These terms can significantly affect the GP's economic incentive in the post-recap period. The [[Hold vs. Sell Analysis]] must justify the decision to recap rather than sell outright — a rigorous comparison of recap NAV to outright sale proceeds is necessary to confirm the recap is the value-maximizing path for exiting investors.

## Risk Factors

The primary risk in a recapitalization is valuation disagreement: the existing investors (or their fund managers) and the incoming investor may have materially different views on current value, current business plan risk, and future return prospects, making the transaction difficult to price. Gaps in valuation expectation are the primary reason recaps fail to close. Market conditions at the time of the recap materially affect both the achievable debt proceeds and the equity pricing — a recap attempted in a rising-rate environment may yield debt proceeds insufficient to meet investor distribution expectations, forcing a choice between a below-expectation distribution, a sale, or waiting.

Structural risk in the new partnership is the other material concern: the incoming equity investor is buying into an existing asset with an existing sponsor, and the new JV documents must carefully govern all future capital needs (additional capex, loan modifications, refinancing decisions) and the sponsor's obligations and fee structure going forward. If the business plan has further execution risk — a remaining renovation program, upcoming lease rollover, or a refinancing event within the hold period — the new equity investor must underwrite those risks as if acquiring the asset fresh. [[Preferred Return Mechanics]] must be carefully structured to ensure the incoming LP's required return is achievable given the reset entry basis.

## Exit Strategies

A recapitalization is itself often a partial exit — the exiting equity investors have achieved liquidity without a full asset sale. The subsequent exit for the remaining partnership (incoming LP and continuing GP) follows the same logic as any stabilized asset: outright sale to a new buyer when market conditions and the fund life warrant, a subsequent refinancing if the asset can support higher debt proceeds, or a second recapitalization in a future cycle. Because the asset has been re-set to a current market basis at the recap, the exit from the post-recap partnership is typically a straightforward sale to a core or core-plus buyer.

In some structures, the recap is a precursor to a longer-term hold: an open-end core or core-plus fund buys into the asset at stabilization, and the closed-end value-add fund exits. This is a deliberate handoff of the asset from a higher-return-seeking closed-end vehicle to a lower-return, longer-duration open-end vehicle, and represents an efficient market function in the institutional real estate ecosystem.

## Example Markets

Recapitalizations are not market-specific but cycle-specific: they peak when closed-end funds are approaching the end of their investment periods or fund lives while markets support fair-to-strong valuations. Major recapitalization activity occurred in 2012–2015 as value-add funds from the 2009–2011 vintage sought to deliver returns to LPs on stabilized portfolios. Industrial and multifamily asset recaps were common in 2018–2022 as rapid cap rate compression and NOI growth produced significant unrealized gains in those sectors. Recaps of stabilized multifamily assets in Sun Belt markets (Dallas, Phoenix, Atlanta) are perennially active given the deep institutional buyer market for those assets.
