# Land Banking

## Profile

Land banking is the acquisition and patient holding of raw, minimally improved, or under-entitled land in a market where the investor believes future population growth, infrastructure investment, or economic development will drive up land values — either by expanding the supply of developable area near the parcel or by increasing the intensity of use the parcel can support. Unlike an [[Entitlement Play]] (which focuses on pursuing active approvals to de-risk a specific development program), land banking is often more passive: the investor acquires the land in anticipation of value-enhancing events that may not be fully in the investor's control — a highway extension, a major employer announcement, a general plan update, or a regional housing shortage that pushes development pressure outward into previously agricultural or exurban land.

The business plan is straightforward but high-risk: buy land cheap enough (in absolute dollars per acre and relative to the eventual land's [[Residual Land Value Method]] supported by future development) that the time value of money and carrying costs are absorbed by the appreciation event when it arrives. Because raw land produces no income during the hold, the entire return is back-loaded, making IRR extremely sensitive to the length of the hold period. Equity multiples of 2×–5×+ are theoretically achievable over a 5–10 year hold, but the annual IRR on a 10-year hold for a 3× multiple is roughly 12–15% — which needs to be evaluated against the substantial liquidity risk and execution uncertainty (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24).

Sponsors range from local landowners and family operators who have held generational agricultural land through high-growth cycles, to institutional land funds that programmatically acquire large parcels in identified growth corridors, to homebuilder optionors who control future lot supply. The most sophisticated practitioners combine land banking with active entitlement prosecution — essentially fusing land banking with the [[Entitlement Play]] strategy — to accelerate value creation and reduce the time horizon.

## Typical Capital Stack

Land banking is among the most equity-intensive deal types in CRE because lenders are deeply reluctant to finance raw, unentitled land. Senior debt — where available at all — is limited: land loans from community banks or private credit lenders typically top out at 40–50% LTV of raw land value (varies widely by lender and market, current as of 2026-05-24), carry short terms (1–3 years), and are often recourse to the borrower. Many land banking deals are done with all cash or minimal leverage because the carrying cost of an expensive land loan on a zero-income asset is destructive to returns.

The equity structure is most commonly simple: a single investor or partnership (family office, private fund, or high-net-worth group) owns the land outright or through a basic LLC with a proportional promote structure. Institutional capital is less common in pure land banking than in land development, though programmatic land banking funds exist. [[Senior Debt]] may be used for land that is nearly entitled or that carries a forward purchase commitment from a homebuilder or developer, which provides enough certainty of cash flow to support some leverage. Creative structures include option agreements (controlling land with minimal upfront capital) and ground leases (monetizing the land holding while a developer builds on top).

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Land banking underwriting is more qualitative and scenario-driven than most other deal types because the primary value driver — timing and magnitude of the appreciation event — is difficult to model with precision. The [[Residual Land Value Method]] is the foundational tool: it asks "if the land were to be developed today to its [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]], what would a developer pay for it after deducting development costs, risk premiums, and profit?" The current purchase price should be a significant discount to that residual value, reflecting the time value of money and the uncertainty of the entitlement and development pathway.

The primary underwriting inputs are market-level: [[Demographic Analysis]] (population growth projections for the metro and submarket), [[Employment and Jobs Analysis]] (is a major employer or employment center drawing residents into this growth corridor?), [[Demand Driver Analysis]] (what is pulling development toward this parcel?), and [[Supply Pipeline Analysis]] (how much competing entitled land already exists in the market, and how does this parcel fit into the sequencing?). [[Absorption Modeling]] for the target use type (residential lots, industrial acres, retail pads) calibrates the pace at which the market will consume the land once it is developed. The IRR model is typically a binary scenario: a base case with the expected appreciation timeline and an extended hold case that tests the return if the appreciation event is delayed by 2–5 years.

## Risk Factors

The most fundamental risk in land banking is that the anticipated growth or entitlement event never materializes, or materializes much later or in a different form than expected. Infrastructure plans change, employers announce and then cancel expansions, general plan updates stall in political processes, and economic cycles reverse growth trajectories. A land holding that was acquired at the frontier of expansion in 2006 could remain agriculturally zoned for a decade if a recession and its aftermath pause development pressure. Carrying costs (property taxes, minimal maintenance, debt service on any land loan) compound continuously against a zero-income asset, eroding the effective return with each passing year.

Liquidity risk is extreme: raw land is one of the least liquid asset types in CRE. If the investor needs to sell before the appreciation event, the exit buyer pool is narrow (other land bankers, local developers, adjacent landowners), bid-ask spreads are wide, and distress pricing can be severe. Entitlement risk — even for a passive land banker — is present because the value of the land is partially conditional on the viability of the ultimate development program, and changes in zoning codes, infrastructure fees, environmental regulations, or affordable housing requirements can reduce land value even without affirmative entitlement action required. Environmental liability risk is present on certain land types (former industrial uses, agricultural with chemical history, riparian areas), and cleanup obligations can exceed the purchase price.

## Exit Strategies

The most common land banking exit is a sale to a merchant developer or homebuilder who will actively pursue entitlements and construction. This exit is typically negotiated when the land banking investor has demonstrated that the location is viable (i.e., competing development has reached nearby parcels, infrastructure has been extended, or a general plan amendment has been adopted for the area) and the buyer values the optionality of proceeding with development. Pricing is based on negotiated residual land value given current entitlement status and a risk premium for remaining entitlement work.

For investors who pursue entitlements actively, the optimal exit is sale of the now-entitled land to a developer or homebuilder at the post-entitlement price — which typically represents the most significant price appreciation event in the land's history. [[Entitlement Play]] logic fully applies at this stage. Some land bankers structure their exit as a ground lease — retaining ownership and leasing to a developer who builds and operates a project on top — which provides ongoing income and preserves the potential for eventual land sale at a higher value. Joint ventures with developers where the land banker contributes the land at an agreed value while the developer contributes capital and expertise is another path that captures development upside without requiring the land banker to undertake construction risk.

## Example Markets

Land banking is most active in high-growth Sun Belt metros where the expansion of urban development into formerly rural or agricultural land has created long-running appreciation cycles: Phoenix metro (East Valley, West Valley, Queen Creek), Las Vegas periphery, Austin–San Antonio corridor, Dallas-Fort Worth exurbs, and the Florida Gulf Coast. Intermountain West markets (Boise, Salt Lake City, Colorado Springs) have seen significant land banking activity driven by in-migration. In faster-growing coastal markets, land banking is more challenging because available raw land is scarcer and carries higher initial pricing, compressing the prospective appreciation multiple.
