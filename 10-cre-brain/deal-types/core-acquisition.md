# Core Acquisition

## Profile

A core acquisition targets fully stabilized, institutional-quality commercial real estate in primary or major secondary markets. The business plan is straightforward: buy a well-leased, well-located asset, collect predictable income, and preserve capital over a long hold. Return objectives are typically weighted toward current yield (cash-on-cash) rather than appreciation, and the equity story centers on reliable distributions backed by strong tenancy and durable demand drivers. Sponsors are most commonly open-end core funds, pension fund advisors, insurance company accounts, and sovereign wealth vehicles — investors whose primary mandate is capital preservation and inflation-hedged income rather than outsized equity gain.

On the risk/return spectrum, core sits at the conservative end. Occupancy at acquisition is generally above 90–95%, weighted average lease term (WALT) is long (often 5+ years remaining), and tenants tend to be credit-rated or have demonstrably strong operating histories. Buildings are typically Class A or high-quality Class B, well-maintained, and requiring only routine capital expenditures rather than repositioning. The [[Cap Rate]] spread over risk-free rates (see [[Spread to Treasuries]]) is thin because the market prices in the low execution risk and high liquidity.

The underwriting assumption is that the going-in yield is close to the stabilized long-run yield. The value proposition relies on holding through multiple cycles, benefiting from gradual rent growth, and eventually selling to another institutional buyer at a similar or tighter cap rate. Location quality, replacement cost, and tenant credit dominate the investment thesis.

## Typical Capital Stack

Core deals are among the most conservatively capitalized in the market. Senior debt is the primary leverage tool, typically permanent fixed-rate financing from life companies, agency lenders (for multifamily), or CMBS conduits. Loan-to-value ratios generally range from 40–60% (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24), lower than value-add or opportunistic deals because the priority is debt service stability and low refinance risk over the full hold. Interest rates are fixed or hedged, and amortization schedules are often long (25–30 years) or interest-only for a portion of the term.

Equity structures are typically simple: a single institutional investor, an open-end commingled fund, or a straightforward joint venture where promote (carried interest) is modest or absent, since returns are expected from income rather than value creation. [[Preferred Return Mechanics]] are less complex than in value-add structures; fee structures emphasize management fees over performance fees. Mezzanine debt and preferred equity are uncommon and generally viewed as inconsistent with the core mandate, though some managers will layer a modest amount of mezz to enhance yield at the margin.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Core underwriting begins with the [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] of actual operating history — a rigorous review of trailing NOI against the [[Rent Roll Analysis]] to confirm that in-place income is sustainable and not artificially inflated by one-time items or expiring free-rent periods. The [[Going-In Cap Rate]] is stress-tested against the [[Stabilized NOI]] derived from normalized operations, and the [[Exit Cap Rate]] assumption is critical: core deals are held long enough that small cap rate expansion at exit meaningfully erodes total returns. Conservative underwriters assume the exit cap rate equals or slightly exceeds the going-in cap rate, pricing in some cap rate mean reversion over a decade-plus hold.

The primary return driver is the [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] growth rate — typically underwritten at 2–3% per annum in line with CPI or market rent escalation, with conservative vacancy assumptions. [[Discount Rate]] selection is calibrated against the [[Weighted Average Cost of Capital (WACC)]] and benchmarked to institutional hurdles for similar risk profiles. The go/no-go metric is most often the going-in yield relative to the fund's required distribution rate, supplemented by an unlevered IRR from [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] analysis that confirms the holding period return meets the mandate. [[Sensitivity Analysis]] focuses on lease renewal probability, market rent trajectory, and cap rate at exit.

## Risk Factors

The dominant risk in core acquisitions is not execution but duration and repricing. Long hold periods expose the asset to interest rate cycles: refinancing at higher rates compresses cash-on-cash returns, and cap rate expansion during a rate-driven repricing event can erode NAV even if the asset performs operationally. The core strategy is most punishing when rates rise sharply because the portfolio's long-duration income streams reprice downward in the same way bonds do.

Lease rollover risk is more nuanced than in value-add: because WALT is long at acquisition, the major lease events may be 5–8 years out, which can be a false comfort if the market deteriorates in the interim. Tenant credit risk — the probability that a key anchor lease terminates or the tenant goes bankrupt — can turn a core asset into a workout quickly. Single-tenant net-lease assets are particularly sensitive to this. Geographic concentration in a single metro creates macro-exposure that diversified core funds mitigate by spreading across markets. Liquidity risk is lower than in opportunistic deals, as core assets trade frequently and attract broad buyer pools, but in a severe dislocation (e.g., 2008–2009) even core assets can face wide bid-ask spreads.

## Exit Strategies

The most common exit for a core acquisition is an outright sale to another institutional buyer — a competing open-end fund, a sovereign wealth vehicle, or a publicly traded REIT. Because the asset remains stabilized through the hold, it remains eligible for the same buyer pool at exit as at acquisition. Pricing at exit is driven almost entirely by the [[Exit Cap Rate]] applied to the [[Stabilized NOI]] prevailing at that time; modest NOI growth over the hold meaningfully compounds the sale price.

Refinancing to return equity is less common as a standalone strategy in core funds (which typically use relatively modest leverage), but in a falling-rate environment a refinance can lock in lower debt costs and boost yield. A core owner may also choose an indefinite hold — open-end funds technically have no fixed life — and simply continue collecting distributions until a fund redemption event or a compelling market-peak sale opportunity arises. [[Hold vs. Sell Analysis]] is ongoing and sensitive to cap rate regime.

## Example Markets

Core acquisitions concentrate in gateway and major secondary markets where institutional capital is deepest and liquidity is most reliable. Multifamily core deals are common in coastal metros (New York, Los Angeles, Seattle, Boston, Washington DC) as well as high-growth Sun Belt cities (Austin, Denver, Nashville) where population and employment growth underpin rent resilience. Office core (increasingly selective post-2020) focuses on trophy Class A towers in top CBDs and well-located suburban campuses leased to investment-grade tenants. Industrial core — logistics, last-mile distribution — has attracted heavy institutional interest in Inland Empire, Chicago O'Hare corridor, Dallas-Fort Worth, and New Jersey/Pennsylvania infill submarkets. Net-lease retail and grocery-anchored centers in dense, high-barrier suburban locations also qualify as core when credit tenancy is strong.
