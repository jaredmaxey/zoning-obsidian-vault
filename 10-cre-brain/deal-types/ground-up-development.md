# Ground-Up Development

## Profile

Ground-up development is the creation of new commercial real estate from a bare, cleared, or minimally improved site. The developer acquires land (or controls it under an option), secures entitlements, designs the project, obtains construction financing, oversees the build, and ultimately delivers a fully constructed building that generates rental income or can be sold. Unlike acquisition strategies, there is no existing income stream to underwrite at the outset — the entire value creation occurs in the future, contingent on successful execution across entitlement, design, construction, and lease-up phases that can collectively span 2–5 years or more. This makes ground-up development the purest expression of entrepreneurial real estate risk.

The return objective is to create an asset worth substantially more than the total cost of production. The fundamental test is the [[Development Spread]]: the gap between the stabilized [[Yield on Cost]] (stabilized NOI divided by total all-in cost) and the market [[Cap Rate]] at which the completed asset would trade. A development that costs $100M to build and generates $6M in stabilized NOI (6.0% yield on cost) against a 5.0% market cap rate creates $20M in "development profit" — the present value uplift of the 100 bps spread. When spreads are thin (below 100–150 bps), the development risk premium is insufficient and the deal does not pencil for most rational developers. Targeted equity returns typically range from 15–25%+ on a levered IRR basis and 1.8×–3.0× equity multiples (ranges vary widely by asset type, market, and cycle position, current as of 2026-05-24).

Sponsors include institutional developers, regional and local development companies, and joint ventures between merchant developers (who originate and manage the project) and institutional equity partners (who provide capital). The developer is compensated through development fees, overhead reimbursements, and a promote tied to IRR thresholds, reflecting the asymmetric value they contribute in origination, entitlement navigation, and execution management.

## Typical Capital Stack

Ground-up development financing is structured in two phases. During construction, a [[Construction Loan]] provides the primary debt — typically 55–65% LTC of total project cost (varies by asset class, lender, and market conditions, current as of 2026-05-24). Construction lenders underwrite to the as-stabilized value and the developer's track record, advancing draws against certified completed work as defined by the construction schedule. [[Hard Costs]], [[Soft Costs]], land, contingency reserves, and financing costs are all included in the LTC calculation.

Equity, mezzanine, and preferred equity fill the gap between the construction loan and total project cost. Typical equity contributions range from 25–40% of total cost, structured as a joint venture between the developer (GP, contributing 5–20% of equity) and an institutional or high-net-worth LP. [[Mezzanine Debt]] or [[Preferred Equity]] may close the capital gap further, though more complex stacks increase execution risk during any period of project stress. The developer's [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] is back-end loaded: development deals are generally unpromising for the GP during construction (cash is flowing out, not in), with the return crystallizing upon completion, lease-up, sale, or refinance.

Upon stabilization, the construction loan is replaced with permanent financing — a [[Permanent Loan]] from a life company, [[Agency Debt (Fannie/Freddie)]] for multifamily, or [[CMBS]] — which locks in long-term debt at a lower rate and releases the developer/equity investors from the floating-rate construction exposure.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Development underwriting begins with the [[Development Feasibility Test]]: can the project be built for a total cost that supports the required return spread over the market cap rate? The [[Residual Land Value Method]] works backward from stabilized NOI and market cap rate to determine the maximum residual value supportable for the land — if the actual land cost exceeds this residual, the project is uneconomic. [[Residual Land Value Feasibility]] turns this into a go/no-go tool.

The [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] models the full development timeline: predevelopment (entitlement, design, permitting), construction (hard cost draw schedule per the [[Construction Schedule and Critical Path]]), lease-up ([[Absorption Modeling]] calibrated against [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] and market [[Vacancy and Collection Loss]] assumptions), and stabilized operations. [[Soft Costs]] (architecture, engineering, permits, legal, financing costs, developer fee) and [[Hard Costs]] (labor, materials, equipment) are separately tracked, with [[Contingency: Hard vs. Soft]] reserves for each. The [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] model must span the full timeline from land closing to eventual sale or refinance. [[Sensitivity Analysis]] and [[Stress Testing]] are critical: construction cost overruns of 10–20%, lease-up timeline extensions of 6–12 months, and cap rate expansion of 50–100 bps at exit each need to be modeled against the equity return.

## Risk Factors

Ground-up development concentrates almost every risk category in CRE into a single deal. Entitlement risk is first: projects requiring rezoning, variances, environmental review, or neighborhood approvals face timeline uncertainty that can postpone construction starts by 12–36 months and consume significant predevelopment capital. Even entitled projects face site plan review and permitting delays that add soft costs and compress returns. Developers can mitigate entitlement risk by acquiring land under option (limiting capital at risk) and investing in [[Neighborhood Outreach]] and community engagement before formal applications.

Construction cost overruns are endemic: market conditions during the build (labor market tightness, material price inflation) can push hard costs 10–20%+ above the underwritten budget, which erodes the development spread if rents do not keep pace. The choice of [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] contract type and the quality of the contractor directly determine how much cost risk is retained by the developer. Schedule slippage extends the financing period, compounds soft cost accrual, and may trigger construction loan maturity risk. Lease-up risk upon completion — if market rents have softened or competing supply has been delivered simultaneously — can materially extend the period to stabilized NOI. Finally, interest rate risk is acute: floating-rate construction debt means that rising rates during an 18–36 month construction period significantly raise total project cost and compress equity returns.

## Exit Strategies

The most common development exit is a forward sale or a sale upon certificate of occupancy or stabilization. In many asset classes and markets, a developer will pre-negotiate a forward purchase agreement with an institutional buyer before or during construction, locking in the exit price (typically a spread over the market cap rate at time of agreement) and eliminating lease-up risk from the developer's perspective. This is particularly common in industrial and build-to-suit development.

Where a forward sale is not arranged, the developer holds through lease-up and sells the stabilized asset to a core or core-plus buyer once [[Stabilized NOI]] is achievable. A construction-to-permanent refinance (converting the construction loan to long-term permanent debt) is a parallel path that allows the developer to hold the asset for longer-term yield, recapture equity through a cash-out refinance, or distribute equity to investors and hold with lower leverage. [[Build-to-Suit]] developments often transfer to the tenant or a sale-leaseback buyer at completion.

## Example Markets

Ground-up multifamily is active across high-growth Sun Belt markets (Phoenix, Austin, Dallas, Nashville, Charlotte) and supply-constrained coastal metros where demand consistently exceeds existing stock. Industrial development — speculative and build-to-suit distribution facilities — remains concentrated near major logistics nodes: Inland Empire, Dallas-Fort Worth, Chicago O'Hare, Lehigh Valley, and Atlanta. Office ground-up has contracted sharply post-2020 and is now largely confined to pre-leased build-to-suit projects or mixed-use environments where office is bundled with residential and retail. Medical office buildings (MOB), life science facilities, and data centers represent active ground-up categories driven by specialized demand.
