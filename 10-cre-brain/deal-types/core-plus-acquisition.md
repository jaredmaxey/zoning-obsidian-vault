# Core-Plus Acquisition

## Profile

A core-plus acquisition occupies the band between purely passive income investing and active repositioning. The target asset is a well-located, institutional-quality property that is largely stabilized but carries a modest imperfection: a lease rollover cluster in the near term, slightly below-market rents embedded in existing leases, a minor deferred-maintenance program, or a small vacancy pocket in an otherwise high-occupancy building. The business plan involves limited active management — re-leasing a floor or two, executing a modest lobby renovation, refreshing common areas — rather than a wholesale repositioning. Return objectives blend current income (a meaningful going-in yield) with a moderate appreciation component driven by either lease-up or market rent growth.

Sponsors pursuing core-plus strategies include closed-end value-creation funds, open-end funds with modest return targets above core, and separate-account advisors who accept slightly higher execution risk in exchange for higher potential distribution rates. The strategy is most attractive when the gap between the going-in cap rate (reflecting current imperfect performance) and the stabilized yield after modest leasing activity is wide enough to generate compelling total returns without requiring the heavy capital spending or entitlement risk of a full value-add play.

Core-plus assets are typically Class A− or Class B+ in quality, located in markets with strong fundamentals but perhaps slightly less liquid than gateway-city core. The distinction from core is a matter of degree: a 10% vacancy versus 5%, or rents 5–10% below comparable stabilized comps, rather than 30% vacancy and a gut rehabilitation.

## Typical Capital Stack

Core-plus deals are financed more aggressively than pure core but more conservatively than value-add. LTV ratios typically range from 50–65% (varies by market and vintage, current as of 2026-05-24), often using senior permanent debt from life companies or CMBS conduits if the going-in occupancy is sufficient for the lender's underwriting threshold. Where the asset has near-term lease rollover or an occupancy gap that disqualifies it for permanent financing, a bridge loan may be deployed initially, with the plan to refinance to a permanent structure once stabilization is achieved.

Equity structures are more likely to involve a promote to the operating partner than in core deals, reflecting the active management component. Joint ventures with institutional LPs are common, typically structured with a preferred return to the LP and a modest [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] in favor of the GP upon hitting return thresholds. [[Preferred Equity]] is occasionally used to fill a gap in the capital stack when senior debt limits restrict proceeds. The overall structure remains relatively simple compared to heavy value-add or opportunistic deals — typically a two-tier waterfall rather than a multi-tier promote structure.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Core-plus underwriting must bridge two states: the as-is income picture (from [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] and [[Rent Roll Analysis]]) and the stabilized pro forma. The delta between going-in and stabilized [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] is the core underwriting question — how large is it, how long will it take to close, and what capital is required to get there? [[Stabilized NOI]] is underwritten conservatively: market rents from [[Comparable Rents (Rent Comps)]] are applied to the vacancy pocket with realistic lease-up timelines (6–18 months for most asset classes), appropriate free-rent concessions, and leasing commissions and tenant improvement allowances (TIs/LCs) deducted as capital costs.

Return metrics emphasize the blended yield across the full hold — the [[Cash-on-Cash Return]] in years one through stabilization will be below the eventual stabilized yield, and the IRR (see [[Internal Rate of Return (IRR)]]) aggregates the total value creation including the mark-to-market at exit. Sensitivity around lease renewal probability is the most important stress case: if the key rollover tenant vacates, the deal effectively becomes a value-add situation requiring heavier re-leasing capex. The [[Sensitivity Analysis]] should flex both lease-up timing and market rental rates to bound the downside.

## Risk Factors

The primary execution risk in core-plus is leasing: if near-term rollover tenants do not renew, or if market rents soften during the re-leasing period, the stabilization timeline extends and the projected NOI growth fails to materialize on schedule. This is especially acute for office assets, where the post-2020 structural demand shift has elevated the probability of large-block departures even in high-quality buildings. Retail core-plus faces similar tenant credit risk, particularly in formats dependent on traffic-anchor tenants.

Financing risk is present if a bridge loan is used to fund the hold during lease-up: if stabilization takes longer than expected, the bridge loan's term may expire before the asset qualifies for permanent takeout financing, forcing a costly extension or a value-dilutive partial payoff. Interest rate exposure during the bridge-to-perm transition is managed by rate caps or hedges, but rising base rates still compress levered returns. Cost overruns on the light capital program are a lower-order risk given the modest scope, but scope creep — expanding a lobby refresh into a full mechanical upgrade — can erode the budget buffer. Market liquidity risk is modest; core-plus assets attract a wide buyer pool at exit.

## Exit Strategies

The most natural exit for a core-plus deal is sale to a core or core-plus institutional buyer once the lease-up is complete and the asset has seasoned for 12–24 months in its stabilized state. At that point the buyer's universe widens to include core open-end funds that could not underwrite the risk at acquisition, typically supporting a tighter exit cap rate. The [[Exit Cap Rate]] assumption in the underwriting should be derived from [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] of fully stabilized comps in the submarket rather than mechanically compressing the going-in cap.

A refinancing-and-hold path makes sense when the stabilized NOI supports attractively priced permanent debt and the hold-period outlook is favorable — particularly in a flat or tightening cap rate environment. A core-plus asset can also be positioned for a portfolio sale (part of a larger institutional portfolio transaction) or REIT acquisition, which may price at a premium to individual asset value. [[Hold vs. Sell Analysis]] should be revisited at stabilization using updated market evidence.

## Example Markets

Core-plus acquisitions are concentrated in primary and high-growth secondary markets where transaction volume is sufficient to provide liquidity but where individual assets can still trade at discounts to peak core pricing due to idiosyncratic issues. Sun Belt multifamily buildings with recent lease rollover at below-market rents are a prototypical core-plus candidate in markets like Phoenix, Atlanta, and Charlotte. Suburban Class A office with a single large tenant rolling in 24 months, in a submarket with low competing supply, represents a core-plus bet on renewal probability. Industrial parks with a partially vacant building alongside fully leased neighbors — in Chicago, Dallas, or the I-78/287 corridor — fit the profile well. Grocery-anchored retail centers with a dark inline suite in dense suburban locations also qualify when the anchor lease is long-term.
