# Entitlement Play

## Profile

An entitlement play is a deal structured around the active pursuit of governmental land use approvals — rezonings, general plan amendments, conditional use permits, subdivision approvals, planned unit development designations — that transform a parcel from its current entitled condition to a higher-value use. Unlike land banking, which waits passively for the market to bring value to the land, an entitlement play requires the sponsor to directly engage with planning departments, community stakeholders, elected officials, and quasi-judicial bodies to advance a specific development program through the approval process. The sponsor's primary value-add is not capital or construction expertise but regulatory navigation, community relations, and political acumen.

The business plan has a clear binary structure: acquire or optionally control the land under its current (lower) entitlement status, invest predevelopment capital in applications, studies, legal representation, and outreach, and either sell the entitled land to a builder/developer at a significant markup or transition directly into [[Ground-Up Development]]. Because entitlement creates a step-change in land value — entitled land in a growth market can trade at a 2×–5× premium to unentitled land of the same acreage — the potential returns are very high. However, entitlement is neither certain nor controllable: approvals can be denied, conditioned beyond economic viability, or delayed indefinitely by appeals or environmental litigation, with predevelopment capital at full loss risk.

The sponsor profile is typically a specialized land developer with deep local relationships and entitlement track record — someone who knows the planning department, has worked with the local city council, and understands the community concerns that must be addressed. Institutional capital partners often enter the structure after a preliminary approval milestone reduces the binary risk, preferring not to fund the pure predevelopment uncertainty.

## Typical Capital Stack

Entitlement plays are typically equity-financed with minimal leverage during the predevelopment phase. Predevelopment capital funds the option or acquisition price (often a low cash-downpayment option rather than an outright purchase), application fees, consultants (civil engineers, land use attorneys, environmental consultants, traffic engineers, architects for concept plans), community outreach programs, and holding costs. Total predevelopment spend on a complex entitlement — a major rezoning with [[General Plan Amendment (GPA)]] and environmental review — can range from $500,000 to several million dollars depending on project scale and jurisdiction.

Where the land is acquired outright (not just optioned), land loans from community banks or private lenders at 40–50% LTV may be used to reduce equity at risk, though lenders are conservative on unentitled land for the reasons described in [[Land Banking]]. The equity structure during predevelopment is typically a simple LLC with a single GP/developer and limited investors who accept pure upside/downside risk on entitlement success. Once entitlements are secured and the sponsor transitions to construction (or sells the entitled land), the capital stack shifts to a construction-phase structure as described in [[Ground-Up Development]]. Institutional equity partners who want to participate in the post-entitlement development program may enter the structure at that inflection point at the entitled land value.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

Entitlement underwriting is fundamentally a probability-weighted scenario analysis. The upside scenario is successful entitlement at the target intensity and use type, with the land then valued at its [[Residual Land Value Method]] supported by the approved development program. The downside scenario is denial or material modification that reduces the approved intensity — which translates to a lower residual land value. The total expected return is the probability-weighted average of these scenarios, with the predevelopment capital treated as fully at-risk.

The [[Development Feasibility Test]] and [[Residual Land Value Feasibility]] tools are used to anchor both ends of the probability tree: what is the land worth if fully entitled at the proposed program, and what is it worth if the entitlement fails (reverting to current use or a lesser entitlement)? The difference is the value that the entitlement creates, and the predevelopment capital is the cost of purchasing that optionality. [[Highest and Best Use Analysis]] informs the target entitlement program — the sponsor must design a program that serves the community's needs (making approval more likely) while maximizing development intensity (maximizing the residual land value uplift). [[Demand Driver Analysis]] and [[Demographic Analysis]] must support the proposed use to withstand scrutiny at public hearings.

## Risk Factors

Entitlement risk is binary in nature: the approval is either granted, conditioned, or denied. Even sophisticated operators with strong track records face denial on a minority of applications, particularly in politically contentious jurisdictions or where community opposition organizes effectively. The [[Public Hearing Process]] and [[Neighborhood Outreach]] process is not merely procedural — organized opposition can derail technically sound applications by raising concerns about traffic, school capacity, neighborhood character, or environmental impact that either the planning commission or city council responds to politically. [[Appeals Process]] allows denied or conditioned approvals to be appealed (extending the timeline by 6–18 months), and third parties can appeal approved projects in court (entitlement litigation) at additional cost and delay.

Environmental risk is present for any project subject to CEQA (California), NEPA (federal nexus), or state environmental equivalents: environmental review can impose significant conditions, require mitigation measures that reduce project economics, or trigger an environmental impact report that takes 18–36 months to complete and litigate. Infrastructure conditions — requirements to fund road improvements, utility extensions, school fees, or park dedications — can be negotiated at the entitlement stage but often escalate in cost as the project grows in scale. The underlying market risk is that by the time entitlement is secured, the development program that was penciling when the predevelopment was started may no longer work due to changes in market rents, construction costs, or interest rates.

## Exit Strategies

The most common exit for a pure entitlement play is sale of the entitled land to a merchant developer or homebuilder at the post-entitlement price. This is a clean, risk-defined exit: the entitlement sponsor captures the full value of the approval without assuming construction, lease-up, or long-term asset management risk. Buyers are typically well-capitalized operators who specifically seek entitled land to feed their development pipelines without investing in the entitlement risk themselves. The transaction is typically priced on a per-unit (residential), per-acre, or per-square-foot-of-approved-GLA basis against [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] of recently entitled land in the submarket.

An alternative exit is a joint venture with a builder or developer where the entitlement sponsor contributes the entitled land at an agreed value as equity and the development partner contributes capital and construction expertise, with profits split according to a negotiated promote structure. This path allows the entitlement sponsor to participate in the full development upside rather than cashing out at the entitlement inflection. A forward purchase contract — negotiated with a builder during or before the entitlement process — is the lowest-risk variant: the builder is obligated to purchase the land at a predetermined price upon receipt of specified entitlements, giving the sponsor a price floor on the upside scenario.

## Example Markets

Entitlement plays are most active in high-growth markets where the gap between entitled and unentitled land values is large, political will for growth exists (at least in some jurisdictions), and a robust builder/developer market exists to purchase the entitled product. Arizona's fast-growing east and west Valley cities (Mesa, Chandler, Gilbert, Goodyear, Buckeye) have historically been active for single-family residential entitlement plays given the region's aggressive growth planning. Texas cities (Georgetown, New Braunfels, Frisco, and other high-growth suburbs) similarly offer large entitlement value-creation opportunities. In contrast, California's entitlement environment is complex enough that the risk premium demanded by sponsors is among the highest in the nation, and entitled land premiums reflect that difficulty.
