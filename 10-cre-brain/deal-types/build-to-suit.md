# Build-to-Suit

## Profile

A build-to-suit (BTS) development is the construction of a commercial building to the specifications of a single tenant (or a small group of anchor tenants) under a lease that is negotiated and signed before or during construction — sometimes even before the land is acquired. The tenant participates in design decisions, approves the program, and commits to occupy the completed building for a defined term, typically 10–20 years. This pre-commitment eliminates the lease-up risk that is otherwise the most significant uncertainty in speculative ground-up development, transforming what would be an opportunistic development risk profile into something closer to core-plus once the tenant commitment is in place.

Build-to-suit projects arise when a tenant's space requirements are specific enough — in size, functional configuration, location, or operational design — that no existing building meets them, and the tenant is willing to commit to a long-term lease in exchange for getting a facility built precisely to their needs. Common examples include distribution centers (purpose-built to the tenant's automation, ceiling height, and dock door requirements), corporate headquarters (designed to the tenant's culture, headcount, and workplace standards), manufacturing facilities, data centers, life science laboratories, and large healthcare campuses. The developer benefits from the contracted rent commitment, which de-risks the development economics and facilitates financing.

The developer's return is a [[Development Spread]] — the difference between the [[Yield on Cost]] on the total development cost and the market cap rate at which the committed-lease asset would trade upon completion. Because the lease is pre-signed and the tenant is typically creditworthy, the completed asset commands a tight cap rate close to core. The developer captures the development profit embedded in that spread. BTS developers are often specialized — a developer with strong relationships in the tenant's sector (industrial developers for logistics, life science developers for biopharma, healthcare real estate developers for health systems) who understands how to design, finance, and deliver the specific facility type.

## Typical Capital Stack

Build-to-suit construction financing is more readily available and more favorably priced than speculative development financing because the pre-leased income stream provides lenders with significantly higher confidence in repayment. Construction LTC typically ranges from 60–70% of total project cost (varies by tenant credit, lease term, and market conditions, current as of 2026-05-24), and construction lenders may offer a lower rate or better proceeds on BTS versus spec because the executed lease effectively pre-underwrites the permanent takeout. In some cases, a forward commitment from a permanent lender (life company or institutional investor) is arranged concurrently with construction financing, providing a guaranteed refinancing path upon certificate of occupancy.

Equity structures follow the standard development JV format: a developer/GP contributes origination, design management, and construction oversight; an institutional LP provides the bulk of equity capital; [[Waterfall Structures and Promote]] govern profit-sharing above the preferred return. For single-tenant assets where the developer intends to sell at completion rather than hold, the JV structure may be a simple development agreement with a fixed developer fee plus promote rather than a long-term equity partnership. Some BTS projects are effectively pre-sold: a net-lease REIT, pension fund, or insurance company agrees to purchase the completed, tenanted asset at a predetermined cap rate, and the developer executes purely for the development margin.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

BTS underwriting is construction-cost-centric rather than market-rent-centric: because the rent is already committed in the lease, the key underwriting variable is whether the total development cost — [[Hard Costs]], [[Soft Costs]], land, contingency, and financing costs (tracked via [[Pro Forma Construction Method]]) — can be held below the level that produces the required [[Yield on Cost]] spread over the anticipated market cap rate. Cost overruns are the enemy of BTS returns; the revenue side is largely fixed by the executed lease.

The [[Development Feasibility Test]] must confirm that the committed rent supports a market cap rate (from [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] of comparable BTS assets) that is tighter than the yield on cost — that is the basic development profit test. The [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] model for a BTS investment (if holding rather than forward-selling) projects income per the executed lease, with renewal and residual assumptions at expiration reflecting the building's functional utility for replacement tenants. [[Sensitivity Analysis]] focuses on cost (hard cost overruns, change orders, GC claims) and timing (construction delay and its effect on developer carry costs and the lender's construction loan term). Tenant credit analysis is performed identically to a sale-leaseback: the quality of the signed commitment drives the project's financing terms and exit cap rate.

## Risk Factors

Although BTS development eliminates lease-up risk, it retains full construction cost and schedule risk. A BTS project commits to delivering a specific facility by a specific date to a tenant whose operations depend on that timeline — a logistics tenant building out a distribution network, a semiconductor manufacturer ramping production, a health system expanding patient capacity. Construction delays not only increase soft costs and financing carry but may trigger lease commencement obligations or force costly temporary arrangements for the tenant. GC contract structure is critical: [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] directly determines how much cost risk is retained by the developer, and [[Payment and Performance Bonds]] and [[Builder's Risk Insurance]] are non-negotiable in BTS due to the binding delivery obligation.

Tenant credit risk is present despite the pre-signed lease: if the tenant's financial condition deteriorates during the construction period (which may span 18–36 months), the lease commitment may be challenged in bankruptcy proceedings or negotiated for modification. Change order risk — the tenant modifying the program during construction, requesting upgrades or scope expansions that add cost — can erode the development margin if the lease rent is fixed but cost increases are not offset by rent adjustment provisions. Functional obsolescence risk over the long lease term is real for highly specialized facilities: a warehouse built for one generation of automation technology may not meet a tenant's needs 15 years later without significant capital investment.

## Exit Strategies

The most common BTS exit is a forward sale or immediate post-completion sale to a net-lease investor. This is the "merchant development" model: the developer builds for a profit margin on the development spread, sells the completed and leased asset at a tight cap rate, and redeploys capital into the next development. Net-lease REITs, open-end core funds, pension accounts, and 1031 exchange buyers are the primary acquirors. The best pricing is achieved when the asset is sold with the maximum remaining lease term — immediately at completion rather than after years of hold that reduce WALT.

Holding through the full lease term is appropriate for developers with a long-duration mandate or for JV structures where the LP has a core investment objective and the development yield on cost already exceeds their required return. In a declining cap rate environment, holding allows the investor to capture mark-to-market appreciation as well as the development yield premium. A [[Sale-Leaseback]] structure can be employed simultaneously with BTS delivery — the tenant who pre-committed to the lease under a BTS arrangement effectively consummates a sale-leaseback at completion when the developer sells the asset. The [[Hold vs. Sell Analysis]] at stabilization should compare the forward-sale proceeds against the present value of holding the long-term lease income.

## Example Markets

Build-to-suit development activity follows major tenants and their growth agendas rather than pure geographic logic. Industrial BTS is concentrated wherever logistics and distribution users are expanding networks: the Sun Belt (Phoenix, Dallas, Atlanta, Las Vegas), Midwest hub cities (Columbus, Indianapolis, Chicago O'Hare), and East Coast supply chain corridors (Bethlehem PA, Savannah, Jacksonville). Life science BTS clusters in established life science submarkets (Cambridge MA, San Diego, San Francisco Bay Area, Raleigh-Durham) where tenant demand for purpose-built laboratory/office space runs ahead of existing supply. Data center BTS is concentrated in Northern Virginia (the world's largest data center market), Phoenix, Dallas, and Chicago, driven by hyperscaler demand for custom facilities. Healthcare BTS follows health system expansion, typically in suburban growth markets adjacent to major metro areas.
