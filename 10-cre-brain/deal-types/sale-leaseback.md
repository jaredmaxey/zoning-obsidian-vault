# Sale-Leaseback

## Profile

A sale-leaseback (SLB) is a transaction structure in which a corporate or institutional property owner sells its real estate to a real estate investor and simultaneously enters into a long-term lease to continue occupying and operating the property. The seller monetizes the real estate — converting an illiquid balance sheet asset into cash — while retaining full operational use of the facility. The buyer acquires a property with a committed, typically creditworthy tenant on a long lease, providing stable, predictable income with minimal re-leasing risk.

The transaction is motivated by different objectives on each side. Sellers pursue SLBs to unlock capital trapped in real estate for deployment into their core business operations, reduce leverage on corporate balance sheets, take advantage of favorable real estate pricing (a seller in a low-cap-rate environment captures peak real estate value), or achieve off-balance-sheet treatment under certain accounting structures. Buyers acquire long-duration, credit-backed income streams in high-quality locations at cap rates that compensate appropriately for the lease term and tenant credit risk — a risk profile that is core to core-plus in nature.

Lease structures in SLBs are most commonly absolute net or triple net (NNN): the tenant is responsible for all property operating expenses (property taxes, insurance, maintenance, and in many cases capital expenditures), meaning the investor receives a pure income stream with minimal management responsibility. Lease terms are typically long — 10–20 years — with built-in annual rent escalators (either fixed percentage bumps or CPI-linked) that provide inflation protection. The combination of long WALT, credit tenancy, and NNN structure makes SLBs among the most bond-like of CRE investments.

## Typical Capital Stack

Sale-leaseback acquisitions are financed similarly to net-lease core acquisitions: conservatively leveraged with long-term fixed-rate senior debt. LTV ranges from 50–65% of appraised or transaction value (varies by tenant credit, lease term remaining, and market conditions, current as of 2026-05-24). Lenders include life companies (for long-term fixed-rate debt), CMBS conduits (for single-tenant NNN assets), and occasionally banks for shorter-duration or smaller-balance deals. The lease itself — particularly if it represents an investment-grade tenant commitment — serves as critical collateral support for lenders.

Equity structures are typically simple for SLBs: a single institutional buyer (net-lease REIT, insurance company account, private fund focused on NNN assets) or a straightforward joint venture. Promote structures are minimal because the return is primarily income-oriented; upside is driven by rent escalators and eventual sale at a favorable cap rate rather than a dramatic value-creation event. Large-scale corporate SLBs — where a company sells a portfolio of dozens of locations — may be structured as programmatic partnerships between the corporate seller and a single institutional buyer, with the buyer having a right of first offer on future locations the corporation is willing to sell.

## Underwriting Hallmarks

SLB underwriting is tenant-credit-forward: the quality and durability of the lessee's commitment drives most of the investment decision. The underwriter's primary work is evaluating the tenant — public company financials (EBITDA, leverage, interest coverage, sector fundamentals), the criticality of the specific property to the tenant's operations (is it a headquarters, a regional distribution center, or a peripheral location?), and the likelihood of renewal at the end of the initial lease term. A tenant that has occupied a location for 20 years and whose business is specifically designed around that facility is a very different risk than a tenant that recently signed a sale-leaseback to unlock capital and for whom the location is operationally fungible.

Cap rate underwriting follows [[Direct Capitalization]] of the in-place [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] (rent escalated to the midpoint of the projected hold in the base case) applied against [[Comparable Sales (Sales Comps)]] of similar NNN comps. The [[Going-In Cap Rate]] relative to the [[Spread to Treasuries]] is the primary relative-value check — NNN cap rates are heavily correlated to 10-year Treasury yields, and the spread at time of acquisition versus historical averages calibrates cycle position. [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] models extend to lease expiration and assume a renewal (at market rent, which may be below or above the in-place rent) or a re-leasing scenario that discounts for re-tenanting costs. The [[Exit Cap Rate]] assumption at the end of the projected hold is the biggest variable: if the lease has 8 years remaining when the investor sells, the exit pricing reflects both the remaining lease security and the impending rollover risk.

## Risk Factors

Tenant credit risk is the dominant risk in a sale-leaseback: if the tenant defaults, goes bankrupt, or vacates the property (even if still paying rent as a credit tenant technically obligated to pay), the asset transitions from a net-lease investment to a real estate re-leasing challenge. The quality of the tenant's credit and the operational criticality of the specific location are the two most important underwriting inputs. A retail chain with deteriorating fundamentals entering a 15-year SLB can look like a core investment at origination and a workout situation within 5 years.

Residual real estate risk is present at lease expiration: the underlying land and building must have fundamental value — location quality, functional utility, and re-usability — sufficient to attract a replacement tenant or buyer if the original tenant does not renew. SLBs involving highly specialized facilities (custom manufacturing, data centers built to the tenant's specifications, bespoke distribution configurations) carry higher residual risk because the replacement tenant universe is narrow. Interest rate risk affects SLB valuation directly: since SLBs are priced as a spread to Treasuries, a rising rate environment compresses market values even if the tenant credit and NOI are unchanged.

## Exit Strategies

The primary exit for a sale-leaseback investment is a sale of the property — either during the lease term with the tenant in place (selling to another net-lease investor) or upon lease expiration with the property repositioned or re-tenanted. Selling with the tenant in place is the path of least resistance: the buyer pool is broad (net-lease REITs, 1031 exchange buyers, private investors) and pricing reflects the remaining lease term and tenant credit. Optimal timing is mid-lease when there is still sufficient WALT to attract institutional buyers who require long-duration income, typically 7–10+ years remaining.

Upon lease expiration, exit options bifurcate by real estate quality: a well-located property that has been maintained to current functional standards may re-lease to a new tenant and be sold to a value-add or core-plus investor at a yield reflecting the shorter remaining lease; a specialized or functionally obsolete facility may require capital investment, use conversion, or in extreme cases demolition and redevelopment. Large portfolio SLBs may be sold as a portfolio to a REIT or institutional buyer, which often commands a modest pricing premium over individual asset sales. [[1031 Exchange Overview]] is a common tax-efficient exit structure for individual real estate investors.

## Example Markets

Sale-leasebacks are not geographically constrained — they follow the corporate footprint of the lessees. Industrial/logistics SLBs are concentrated wherever large distribution and manufacturing users operate: the Sun Belt (Phoenix, Dallas, Atlanta, Inland Empire), Midwest logistics corridors (Chicago, Indianapolis, Columbus), and Mid-Atlantic hubs (New Jersey, Pennsylvania). Corporate office SLBs cluster in major employment centers. Retail SLBs involve drug stores (Walgreens, CVS), fast food and restaurant chains, auto dealerships, and home improvement retailers, and are geographically diversified across their respective chains' footprints. Healthcare SLBs (medical office, urgent care, dialysis clinics) have been an active category driven by healthcare systems monetizing real estate to fund operations and technology investment.
