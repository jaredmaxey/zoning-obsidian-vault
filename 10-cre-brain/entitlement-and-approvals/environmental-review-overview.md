# Environmental Review Overview

## Summary

Environmental review is a legally required process that evaluates the potential impacts of a proposed development project on the physical environment before a government agency approves the project or a discretionary permit. In the United States, environmental review operates under two overlapping frameworks: the federal National Environmental Policy Act (NEPA), which applies when a federal agency takes an action (approves permits, provides financing, transfers land), and state environmental review laws — most notably California's Environmental Quality Act (CEQA), which applies to all discretionary approvals by California public agencies, but analogous laws or programs exist in varying forms in other states. Arizona does not have a standalone state environmental review law equivalent to CEQA; federal NEPA and specific regulatory programs (wetlands, air, hazardous materials) govern the landscape there.

For developers, environmental review is one of the most consequential variables in the entitlement timeline and budget. A project that qualifies for a categorical exemption or a simple negative declaration may add only weeks to the schedule; a project that requires a full Environmental Impact Report (EIR under CEQA) or Environmental Impact Statement (EIS under NEPA) can add years and hundreds of thousands to millions of dollars in direct and indirect costs. Identifying the likely level of environmental review early — ideally during site due diligence — is essential for realistic underwriting and scheduling. See [[Development Feasibility Test]] and [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] for how environmental costs feed into feasibility analysis.

## Triggering Conditions

NEPA is triggered when a federal agency is the applicant, takes a discretionary action (e.g., issues a Section 404 wetlands permit, approves a HUD loan, conveys federal land), or provides federal funding. The level of review ranges from a Categorical Exclusion (CE — no significant impacts likely, no documentation required beyond a checklist) to an Environmental Assessment (EA — analysis to determine if significant impacts will occur) to a full EIS (for actions with significant impacts or public controversy). CEQA applies to all discretionary approvals by California state and local agencies — essentially any project requiring a permit or approval other than purely ministerial building permits. Outside California, most states lack a comprehensive state environmental review law; instead, project-level environmental review may be triggered by specific regulatory programs (wetlands under Section 404 of the Clean Water Act, endangered species under the ESA, RCRA hazardous materials sites, air quality permits). Varies by state and by the presence of federal nexus.

## Decision-Maker

The "lead agency" concept is central to environmental review. Under NEPA, the federal agency with principal responsibility for the action is the lead agency for the EIS/EA. Under CEQA, the public agency with greatest responsibility for approving or carrying out the project serves as the lead agency; local planning departments are often the lead agency for private development projects. The lead agency prepares (or supervises preparation of) the environmental document, responds to public comments, and makes findings on the adequacy of the document and the significance of identified impacts. A responsible agency (a second agency with a discretionary approval role) must rely on the lead agency's document rather than prepare its own. For contested documents, the adequacy of the environmental review is tested in court rather than through an administrative decision-maker.

## Required Submittals

The scope of required submittals depends on the level of review required. For a categorical exemption or negative declaration, the applicant typically provides an Initial Study (a structured questionnaire evaluating the project against a checklist of environmental topics) and project description. For an EIR or EIS, the applicant typically funds preparation of the full document (often through a third-party contract managed by the agency) and provides: project description; technical studies (traffic, air quality, noise, biology/habitat surveys, cultural resources/archaeological survey, hydrology, hazardous materials Phase I/II); visual impact assessments; and socioeconomic data where relevant. In some jurisdictions, the applicant submits the draft document to the agency for review and comment, while in others the agency staff prepares the document using applicant-provided technical data. Varies by jurisdiction and document type.

## Typical Timeline

Categorical exemptions can be processed in days to weeks, adding minimal schedule impact. A Mitigated Negative Declaration (MND) typically takes three to six months. A full EIR in California — including scoping, draft EIR, public comment period (45 days minimum), response to comments, final EIR, and findings — commonly takes twelve to twenty-four months for complex urban projects and can take longer for projects with significant biological, cultural, or air quality issues. NEPA EAs generally take six to eighteen months; full EIS processes often take two to four years for complex federal actions. For Arizona projects without a federal nexus, environmental review time is primarily driven by agency-specific review times for wetland delineations, ESA consultation, and cultural resource studies. ## Fees

Environmental review costs vary enormously. Categorical exemptions may carry only a nominal administrative fee. Full EIRs and EISs are typically funded by the applicant, with costs ranging from tens of thousands to several million dollars depending on the number of required technical studies, the complexity of the project, and the degree of public controversy (which drives the volume of comments requiring response). Agency peer review fees and CEQA litigation costs add further potential expense. Budgeting environmental review as a contingency-heavy line item is advisable for any project likely to require more than a categorical exemption. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

Under CEQA, the lead agency must find either that (1) the project will have no significant effect on the environment (negative declaration); (2) identified significant effects will be mitigated to below a level of significance (mitigated negative declaration); or (3) identified significant effects cannot be mitigated, but overriding considerations justify approval (statement of overriding considerations in an EIR). Under NEPA, the agency must find that it has taken a "hard look" at environmental impacts and that its analysis is reasonable and supported by the administrative record. Courts reviewing environmental documents focus on procedural compliance and adequacy of analysis — not on whether the agency made the "right" substantive decision. Adequate mitigation measures and thorough responses to public comments are critical to document defensibility.

## Appeal Path

NEPA documents are challenged in federal court under the Administrative Procedure Act, with the agency's decision reviewed on an arbitrary-and-capricious standard based on the administrative record. CEQA documents in California are challenged in state superior court via writ of mandate, with a 30–180 day statute of limitations depending on whether the project was noticed. CEQA litigation is a well-established tool for project opponents, and even a technically adequate EIR can face costly challenges that add years to a project timeline. Arizona projects without CEQA exposure are instead subject to challenge under NEPA (where federal nexus exists) or through substantive permit challenges under other regulatory programs. Varies by triggering statute. See [[Appeals Process]] for general challenge mechanics.

## Common Pitfalls

The most consequential pitfall is misclassifying the required level of review — categorizing a project as exempt or as qualifying for a negative declaration when the facts support the need for an EIR. This results in a document that will not survive legal challenge, wasting time and money. A related error is failing to identify all "potentially significant" impacts at the Initial Study stage, which can lead to a challenge that an EIR was required. Inadequate biological surveys (failing to conduct surveys during the proper season for target species) are a frequent source of significant delays when wildlife agencies require supplemental surveys. Cultural resource surveys that miss significant sites are similarly costly to remediate mid-process. Applicants also sometimes underestimate the time required for interagency coordination (Section 7 ESA consultation, Section 404 permitting, Section 106 historic review) when multiple federal programs are triggered simultaneously. Failing to scope the environmental document broadly enough at the outset creates vulnerabilities in the record that opponents exploit in litigation.
