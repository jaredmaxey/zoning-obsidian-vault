# Subdivision Approval

## Summary

Subdivision approval is the process by which land is legally divided into two or more parcels, lots, units, or air-space interests (in the case of condominiums) for the purpose of sale, financing, development, or transfer. Subdivision law in the United States operates primarily at the state level, with enabling statutes that delegate detailed procedures to local governments. The process serves two distinct regulatory functions: ensuring that new lots are properly configured for development (adequate size, shape, access, and utility availability), and securing dedication and construction of public infrastructure — roads, water, sewer, drainage, and parks — necessary to serve the new lots.

For commercial real estate developers, subdivision approval arises in several contexts: master-planned community development (dividing large land holdings into pads and parcels for phased development or sale); condominium mapping (converting a building or project into individual for-sale units or air-space commercial suites); parcel splits to create separately financeable or sellable land pieces; and lot consolidations (merging adjacent parcels under common ownership to create a single larger developable site). Understanding the two-phase map process — tentative map and final map — and the conditions of approval attached to the tentative map is essential for project scheduling and cost planning.

## Triggering Conditions

A subdivision map is triggered any time a parcel is divided into two or more units for transfer, conveyance, or development purposes as defined under applicable state subdivision law. Thresholds vary: some states require map approval for any division into two or more parcels; others allow minor land divisions (typically four or fewer parcels) on a simplified lot split or parcel map process with fewer requirements than a full subdivision map. Condominium conversions of rental buildings into for-sale units trigger a subdivision process in most jurisdictions, often with additional tenant-protection provisions. Lot mergers (combining parcels) may require a certificate of lot merger or similar administrative action. Agricultural exceptions and lot-line adjustments between adjacent parcels of equal or fewer resulting lots are typically exempted from full subdivision requirements. Varies by state and jurisdiction.

## Decision-Maker

In the most common two-phase process, the Planning Commission approves the tentative map (or tentative parcel map) after a public hearing, evaluating the project against general plan consistency, development standards, and conditions of approval for infrastructure and design. The governing body (council or board) may have appeal or call-up rights. Final map approval — confirming that all tentative map conditions have been satisfied and that the map conforms to the approved tentative — is typically a ministerial action by the city engineer or public works director, sometimes ratified by the governing body as a consent agenda item. Because final map approval is ministerial when conditions are met, it is generally not subject to discretionary denial or appeal on the merits. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Required Submittals

Tentative map submittals typically include: a dimensioned tentative map or tentative parcel map showing proposed lot configurations, street dedications, easements, and existing features; a title report; a hydrology/drainage study; a traffic impact analysis (if above thresholds); preliminary grading plan; utility availability letters; environmental documentation per [[Environmental Review Overview]]; HOA or CC&R framework (for residential projects); and applicable fees. Final map submittals include: a stamped survey map (prepared by a registered land surveyor or civil engineer) conforming to the approved tentative map; certificates of completion or bonding for required public improvements; title company guarantee; and documentation that all conditions of approval have been satisfied or bonded. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Typical Timeline

Tentative map processing — from application submission to Planning Commission approval — typically takes three to six months for straightforward projects; larger or more complex maps requiring substantial environmental review can take twelve to eighteen months or longer. After tentative map approval, the approval is valid for a period specified by state law (often two to three years, with possible extensions), during which the applicant must complete conditions, construct or bond required improvements, and submit the final map. Final map processing after submission of a complete package typically takes two to six weeks in most jurisdictions. The total elapsed time from tentative application to recordation of the final map commonly ranges from one to three or more years for larger projects. General, current as of 2026-05-24.

## Fees

Subdivision fees include tentative map application fees (typically based on number of lots), environmental review fees, engineering review fees, park dedication in-lieu fees (calculated per unit or per acre), and traffic mitigation fees. Infrastructure improvement costs — roads, utilities, drainage facilities — can be the most significant cost component and are required as conditions of approval. Fee levels vary substantially by jurisdiction and project size. Always verify against the jurisdiction's current fee schedule. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

For tentative map approval, typical required findings include: (1) the proposed map is consistent with the general plan, specific plan, and applicable zoning; (2) the design of the subdivision is consistent with applicable general and specific plans; (3) the site is physically suitable for the type and density of development; (4) the design will not cause substantial environmental damage or substantially injure fish, wildlife, or their habitats; (5) the design will not cause serious public health problems; and (6) the subdivision will not conflict with easements for public access or for access through or use of property within the proposed subdivision. These findings track common state subdivision map act frameworks — the specific statutory basis varies by state. Jurisdictions may add local findings related to design standards, school capacity, or urban services availability.

## Appeal Path

Tentative map decisions by the Planning Commission are typically appealable to the governing body (city council or board of supervisors) within a prescribed window after the decision — often 10–30 days. Governing body decisions may be subject to further legal challenge. Final map approval, being ministerial, is generally not appealable on the merits, but can be challenged if conditions precedent were not properly satisfied. In states with strong subdivision map act frameworks (such as California's Subdivision Map Act), state law provides specific grounds for challenge and appeal timelines. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Common Pitfalls

The most common pitfall in subdivision processing is underestimating the cost and schedule implications of infrastructure conditions — particularly when tentative map conditions require off-site improvements (street widening, utility main extensions, regional drainage facilities) that involve coordination with other property owners or public agencies. Applicants also sometimes fail to account for the vesting rights window: a tentative map grants project-specific protections against changing code requirements for a limited time, and failing to extend the approval before expiration can reset the entitlement clock. Condominium maps trigger additional requirements (HVAC, utility metering by unit, ingress/egress standards) that are overlooked when the mapping process is treated as purely administrative. Finally, lot configurations that create substandard access or that cannot physically accommodate required development standards (setbacks, parking) on individual pads should be identified and corrected at the tentative map stage, not after final map recordation.
