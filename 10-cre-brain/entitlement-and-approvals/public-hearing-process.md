# Public Hearing Process

## Summary

A public hearing is a formal, noticed proceeding at which a government body receives evidence, testimony, and argument before making a discretionary land use decision. Public hearings are a constitutional and statutory requirement for most significant entitlement actions — rezonings, CUPs, variances, tentative subdivision maps, and general plan amendments — because the law requires affected parties to have an opportunity to be heard before a decision adversely affecting their interests is made. The public hearing is also the primary mechanism by which the administrative record is built: the facts, evidence, and arguments in the hearing record are the raw material for both the decision-maker's action and any subsequent judicial review.

For developers, the public hearing is simultaneously an opportunity and a risk. It provides a structured forum to present a project, respond to concerns, and build a record supporting approval. But an unprepared or poorly conducted hearing can crystallize neighborhood opposition, create record evidence that supports a denial, or produce conditions of approval that the applicant did not anticipate. Understanding hearing procedure — notice requirements, how the record is built, ex parte contact rules for quasi-judicial proceedings, and how to present effectively — is a core entitlement skill.

## Triggering Conditions

A public hearing is required whenever state law or local ordinance mandates it for the type of discretionary approval being sought. Legislative actions (rezonings, GPA, PUD approvals) virtually always require at least one noticed public hearing — often two (Planning Commission and council). Quasi-judicial actions (CUPs, variances, subdivision tentative maps) require hearings by the applicable decision-making body. Administrative approvals (many site plan reviews, ministerial permits) do not require a public hearing. Some jurisdictions have created an intermediate category — noticed staff decisions — where a project is administratively approved but neighbors are notified with an opportunity to request a hearing if they object; this is common for minor CUPs or streamlined development permits. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Decision-Maker

The decision-maker at a public hearing depends on the type of entitlement. Planning Commissions hear CUPs, tentative maps, some rezonings, and design review. Boards of Adjustment (or Boards of Zoning Appeals) hear variances and certain appeals. City councils and county boards of supervisors hear rezonings, GPAs, PUD approvals, and appeals from Planning Commission decisions. Hearing officers — appointed neutrals — are used in some jurisdictions for certain CUPs or variance matters to reduce the burden on elected bodies. Each body operates under its own procedural rules (bylaws, code of procedures, or Roberts Rules), adopted by the jurisdiction and typically set out in the municipal code. The applicable body determines the formality of the proceeding, the time allotted for testimony, and the standard of review it applies.

## Required Submittals

Before the hearing, the project planner prepares a staff report that is the key document in the record. The staff report includes: project description; background on the site and surrounding area; analysis of compliance with applicable standards; staff's recommendation (approval, denial, or approval with conditions); and draft conditions of approval or findings. Applicants typically have an opportunity to review a draft staff report and submit written materials for inclusion in the record before the hearing. At the hearing, applicants present their project (usually with visual materials), respond to staff and commissioners' questions, and rebut testimony from opponents. All written materials submitted before and at the hearing, all oral testimony, and the staff report are part of the official record.

## Typical Timeline

Notice of a public hearing must be provided to property owners within a specified radius (commonly 300–600 feet, varying by jurisdiction), published in a newspaper of general circulation, and sometimes posted on the subject property, all within a minimum period before the hearing (typically 10–30 days depending on jurisdiction and entitlement type). The hearing itself may be a single session or may be continued to a subsequent date if additional time is needed for testimony, evidence, or deliberation. After the hearing, the decision-maker votes on the staff recommendation or an alternative motion; findings and conditions are typically adopted by resolution at the hearing or at a subsequent meeting. ## Fees

Public hearing fees are generally included in the overall application fee for the underlying entitlement action (CUP fee, rezoning fee, etc.) and are not separately itemized. Some jurisdictions charge a separate noticing fee to cover the cost of mailing and newspaper publication. Continued hearings may trigger additional fees in some jurisdictions. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

The public hearing is the procedural vehicle for making the substantive findings required by the applicable entitlement type — not a separate set of findings. For quasi-judicial proceedings (CUP, variance), the decision-maker must make required findings based on substantial evidence in the record. For legislative proceedings (rezoning, GPA), the governing body acts within its broad legislative discretion, but the hearing still builds the factual basis that supports the legislative judgment and defends against legal challenge. The adequacy of the public hearing — proper notice, opportunity to be heard, findings tied to record evidence — is the primary basis on which courts evaluate whether a land use decision was procedurally valid.

## Appeal Path

Hearing decisions are appealed per the rules of the applicable entitlement type — generally to the next decision-making body up the hierarchy (staff decision to Planning Commission; Planning Commission to city council; council to superior court). The hearing record is the critical input on appeal: an appellate body (or court) reviewing a quasi-judicial decision is limited to the record created at the hearing and does not hold a new hearing on the merits. Procedural defects — inadequate notice, denial of opportunity to present evidence, bias by a decision-maker with a prohibited ex parte contact — can require a new hearing or void the decision. See [[Appeals Process]] for general mechanics. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Common Pitfalls

The most consequential pitfall is building a thin or one-sided record — submitting only the applicant's materials without anticipating and addressing opposition arguments — leaving the decision-maker without substantial evidence to support the required findings if challenged. Applicants often underinvest in written evidence (technical studies, letters of support, expert declarations) relative to the time they spend on presentation graphics; courts review the written record, not the slide deck. Ex parte contact violations are a serious procedural risk for quasi-judicial proceedings: decision-makers (Planning Commissioners, Board of Adjustment members) generally cannot communicate privately with applicants or opponents about the merits of a pending quasi-judicial matter without disclosing the contact at the hearing. Applicants who lobby commissioners privately may inadvertently create grounds for recusal or record challenges. Finally, failing to object at the hearing to an improper procedure, a defective notice, or a biased commissioner typically waives that objection on appeal — "preserve or lose" applies to public hearing procedures as much as to trial objections.
