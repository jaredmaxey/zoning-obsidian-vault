# Conditional Use Permit (CUP)

## Summary

A Conditional Use Permit (CUP) — also called a conditional use authorization, special use permit (SUP), or special exception in various jurisdictions — is a quasi-judicial land use approval that allows a use which the zoning code identifies as conditionally permitted in a given district to proceed, subject to project-specific conditions designed to ensure compatibility with the surrounding area. Unlike a rezoning, which changes the base zoning designation itself, a CUP grants approval for a specific use on a specific site while leaving the underlying zoning in place. The permit attaches to the land and runs with the property unless revoked.

CUPs are designed for uses that are generally appropriate in a zone but require case-by-case review because their impacts depend heavily on site design, hours of operation, intensity, or proximity to sensitive uses. Examples include drive-through restaurants in commercial zones, churches in residential areas, daycare centers, self-storage facilities in industrial zones, automobile dealerships, and higher-density residential uses in transitional zones. For developers, understanding whether the desired use requires a CUP — versus being permitted by right or requiring a full rezoning — is one of the first due diligence steps in evaluating a site.

## Triggering Conditions

A CUP is required when the proposed use is listed in the zoning code as "conditionally permitted," "permitted subject to a conditional use permit," or similar language for the applicable zone district. Triggering conditions can also arise from intensity thresholds (e.g., a use that is by-right up to a certain size but requires a CUP above that threshold) or from proximity rules (e.g., a use requiring a CUP when located within a certain distance of a school or residential zone). Applicants must review the use table in the applicable zoning code to determine the correct permit pathway. If the use is not listed as permitted or conditionally permitted, a rezoning (or text amendment to add the use) is required rather than a CUP. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Decision-Maker

The CUP is a quasi-judicial decision — the decision-maker applies predetermined findings and code standards to the specific facts of the application, rather than exercising the broad legislative discretion of a rezoning. In most jurisdictions, the Planning Commission is the primary decision-maker for CUPs, with appeal rights to the city council or board of supervisors. Some jurisdictions delegate lower-impact or ministerial CUPs to a hearing officer, zoning administrator, or planning director, reserving the Planning Commission for larger or more controversial applications. Because the decision is quasi-judicial, it is subject to more rigorous judicial review than a legislative rezoning — courts will examine whether required findings were made based on the evidence in the record.

## Required Submittals

A CUP application typically includes: completed application form and owner/agent authorization; site plan showing the proposed use layout, parking, access, landscaping, and any special features relevant to the use; use description and operating characteristics narrative (hours, staffing, traffic, noise, deliveries, outdoor activities); vicinity map and photos; applicable development standards compliance checklist; environmental documentation per [[Environmental Review Overview]] (often a categorical exemption or limited initial study for minor CUPs); and fees. Specialized uses may require additional studies — parking demand studies, noise studies, odor or hazardous materials assessments, lighting plans, or trip generation analyses. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Typical Timeline

CUP processing is generally faster than a rezoning because there is no general plan consistency issue and the process is quasi-judicial rather than legislative. A straightforward CUP in a cooperative jurisdiction can be resolved in six to twelve weeks from application acceptance to Planning Commission decision. More complex applications, or those requiring environmental documentation beyond a categorical exemption, may take three to six months. Where the Planning Commission's decision is appealed to the council, add another four to eight weeks. General, current as of 2026-05-24.

## Fees

CUP application fees vary by jurisdiction and use complexity, typically ranging from several hundred to several thousand dollars. Some jurisdictions use tiered fee schedules based on project size, use type, or required notice radius. Deposit accounts for staff time are common for complex applications. Verify against the jurisdiction's current fee schedule. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

The quasi-judicial character of the CUP means the decision-maker must make affirmative findings on the record to approve the permit. While the exact findings vary by jurisdiction, the most common framework requires that the proposed use: (1) is consistent with the general plan and applicable specific plans; (2) complies with the zoning ordinance, or any departures are appropriately conditioned; (3) will not be detrimental to the public health, safety, convenience, or welfare, or to adjacent properties; (4) is compatible with surrounding land uses and the character of the area; and (5) has adequate infrastructure (access, parking, utilities) to support it. Jurisdictions sometimes add use-specific findings (e.g., for alcoholic beverage uses, proximity to schools; for drive-throughs, traffic circulation). Failure to make all required findings on the record is the most common basis for a successful legal challenge. Applicants should ensure their application materials and the hearing record contain substantial evidence supporting each finding.

## Appeal Path

A CUP decision by the Planning Commission (or hearing officer) is typically appealable to the city council or board of supervisors within a short window — commonly 10–30 days from the date of the decision, depending on jurisdiction. Both applicants (if denied) and opponents (if approved) may have appeal rights. The appeal is generally a de novo hearing at the council level, meaning the council considers the matter fresh and is not limited to reversing error by the Planning Commission. If the council upholds a denial or reverses an approval, the applicant may seek judicial review. Because the CUP is quasi-judicial, courts apply a substantial evidence standard — reviewing whether the findings are supported by evidence in the record, not substituting judicial judgment for the decision-maker's. See [[Appeals Process]] for general appeal mechanics. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Common Pitfalls

The most common pitfall is submitting an application that fails to specifically address every required finding — particularly the "compatibility with surrounding uses" and "no detriment to public health" findings — leaving gaps that staff and opponents can exploit. Underestimating neighbor sensitivity to specific uses (particularly uses with late hours, traffic, noise, or odor impacts) and failing to engage the neighborhood early (see [[Neighborhood Outreach]]) frequently turns manageable concerns into organized opposition. Applicants sometimes propose conditions of their own as a pre-emptive measure, which can be effective if done strategically. Permit conditions imposed at approval run with the land, so an overly broad or ambiguous condition (e.g., "no outdoor storage") can create compliance problems for future tenants. Finally, a CUP is use-specific and site-specific: if the use or operator changes materially, a new or amended CUP may be required — a fact that affects property value and lease flexibility.
