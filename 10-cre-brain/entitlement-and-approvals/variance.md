# Variance

## Summary

A variance is a quasi-judicial grant of relief from a specific, numeric development standard — such as a setback, height limit, lot coverage maximum, or parking requirement — when strict application of that standard to a particular property would cause undue hardship because of unique physical characteristics of the lot that do not apply generally to the zone. A variance is not a change in the zoning law itself; it is a property-specific exception to one or more dimensional requirements, applied to a specific parcel and typically recorded against the title. It does not authorize uses that are not permitted in the zone — that requires a rezoning or CUP.

The variance is one of the oldest and most tightly regulated tools in land use law. Courts have historically required strict adherence to the hardship findings because the variance power — if applied loosely — effectively allows spot zoning or undermines the predictability of the zoning code. As a result, variances are harder to obtain than commonly assumed, and a request that essentially amounts to "we want more building than the code allows" without a genuine site-specific justification will be denied. Developers should approach a variance application as a legal argument, not a design preference.

## Triggering Conditions

A variance is triggered when a proposed development cannot comply with one or more dimensional development standards applicable to the base zone, and the non-compliance is caused by a physical characteristic unique to the lot — not simply the developer's desire for a larger or different project. Typical scenarios include: an irregularly shaped lot where required setbacks would leave insufficient buildable area; topographic constraints (slopes, floodplain encroachment, rock outcroppings) that prevent standard building placement; pre-existing nonconforming conditions that make full compliance with current standards geometrically impossible; or an unusually small lot that predates current minimum size requirements. A variance is not appropriate where the hardship is self-created (e.g., the owner subdivided the lot), where the hardship applies to all properties in the zone (suggesting the code itself needs amendment), or where the relief sought would alter the character of the neighborhood.

## Decision-Maker

The variance is heard by a Board of Adjustment (also called the Board of Zoning Appeals or Board of Zoning Adjustment, depending on jurisdiction). The Board of Adjustment is typically a quasi-judicial body composed of citizen appointees, separate from the Planning Commission, whose sole function is to hear variance and appeal matters. In some smaller or consolidated jurisdictions, the Planning Commission or a hearing officer may exercise Board of Adjustment functions. Because the decision is quasi-judicial, the Board must make required findings based on the evidence in the record, and its decisions are subject to more searching judicial review than legislative zoning actions.

## Required Submittals

A variance application typically includes: completed application form and property owner authorization; a written hardship statement addressing each required finding in detail (this is the heart of the application); site plan showing existing conditions, proposed development, and the specific standard for which relief is sought; surveys or engineering exhibits demonstrating the physical constraint; photos illustrating the site conditions; and applicable fees. Applicants sometimes include letters of support from adjacent property owners, though this is not a substitute for meeting the findings. Some jurisdictions require a pre-application meeting or routing to the relevant department (e.g., engineering for drainage variances, fire for access variances) before the hearing. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Typical Timeline

Variance hearings before the Board of Adjustment are generally scheduled within thirty to sixty days of application acceptance in most jurisdictions. The total elapsed time from application to decision is commonly six to twelve weeks — significantly faster than a rezoning or CUP. However, if the variance is appealed to the council or the courts, the timeline extends substantially. Note that a variance granted in connection with a development project does not substitute for other required approvals (e.g., [[Site Plan Review]], building permits); those proceed separately. ## Fees

Variance application fees are typically lower than rezoning or CUP fees, reflecting the more limited scope of the process. Fees range from a few hundred to a few thousand dollars depending on jurisdiction and complexity. Verify against the jurisdiction's current fee schedule. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

The variance findings framework is the critical element of the application — every required finding must be met on the record or the variance must be denied. The classic "four variance findings" derived from the Standard State Zoning Enabling Act require that: (1) the property has unique physical characteristics (shape, topography, size, or other conditions) that distinguish it from other properties in the zone; (2) these characteristics create an undue hardship — meaning the strict application of the standard would deny the owner any reasonable use of the property or effectively take its value; (3) the hardship was not self-created by the owner; and (4) granting the variance will not be materially detrimental to public welfare or to other properties in the vicinity, and will not alter the character of the neighborhood. Many jurisdictions include a fifth finding requiring that the variance be the minimum relief necessary. Some jurisdictions distinguish between "area" variances (on dimensional standards, generally easier to obtain) and "use" variances (to permit a non-allowed use, disfavored or prohibited in many jurisdictions). Applicants must address every finding explicitly and build a record of supporting evidence.

## Appeal Path

A Board of Adjustment decision on a variance may be appealed by any aggrieved party — the applicant if denied, or opponents if approved. In many jurisdictions, the appeal goes to the city council or board of supervisors within a short window (commonly 15–30 days). In some states (including Arizona), Board of Adjustment decisions are directly appealable to superior court via special action, bypassing the council; the court applies a substantial evidence standard to the Board's findings. Because the Board of Adjustment's decisions are quasi-judicial and the court's review is limited to whether findings are supported by evidence in the record, the quality of the hearing record is critical. See [[Appeals Process]] for general mechanics. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Common Pitfalls

The most frequent mistake is treating the variance application as a design discussion rather than a legal argument. A hardship statement that says "the setback requirement makes the project uneconomical" will fail — financial hardship is generally not a cognizable hardship under the standard findings framework. Applicants must identify a physical site characteristic, explain how it differs from other properties in the zone, and demonstrate that the hardship is not self-created. Over-requesting relief (asking for more than the minimum necessary variance) invites denial on the fifth finding. Applicants also sometimes fail to distinguish between area and use variances — requesting what is effectively a use variance (e.g., to add a use not permitted in the zone) under the guise of an area variance, which will be denied or overturned on appeal. Finally, neighboring owners who oppose the variance at the hearing can create a record of potential detriment that makes finding 4 difficult to sustain; early outreach (see [[Neighborhood Outreach]]) to address concerns before the hearing is valuable.
