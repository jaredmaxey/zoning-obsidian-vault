# Site Plan Review

## Summary

Site plan review is the process by which a jurisdiction evaluates a project's detailed site layout — including building footprints, parking layout, access, landscaping, grading, utilities, and building design — to confirm compliance with applicable development standards, design guidelines, and any conditions imposed by prior entitlement approvals (rezoning, CUP, PUD, subdivision). It is typically the final entitlement step before building permit submission and represents the translation of a project's approved land use and density into a buildable physical plan.

The scope and complexity of site plan review varies dramatically by jurisdiction and project type. Some jurisdictions conduct site plan review administratively by planning staff with no public hearing, treating it as a ministerial code-compliance check. Others require Design Review Board or Planning Commission approval, with public comment periods, for projects meeting certain size or location thresholds. In jurisdictions with design review overlays (downtown, transit corridors, historic districts), architectural design compatibility becomes a central criterion alongside technical compliance. For developers, understanding whether site plan review is administrative or discretionary is critical for schedule and budget planning.

## Triggering Conditions

Site plan review is triggered by the construction of new buildings, significant additions or exterior alterations, changes in use that require additional parking or access modifications, or development in jurisdictions that apply site plan review to all non-single-family construction. Some jurisdictions tier the requirement by project size — small tenant improvements may be exempt, while projects above a certain square footage, unit count, or value threshold require formal site plan review. Projects within specific overlay districts (design review, historic, transit) may face a lower threshold or different criteria. A project that received a CUP, rezoning, or PUD approval will almost always need subsequent site plan review to confirm that the detailed plans comply with the conditions and standards approved at the entitlement stage. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Decision-Maker

For administrative site plan review, planning staff (a planner or zoning administrator) reviews the submitted plans against an objective checklist of code standards and issues approval, approval with conditions, or a request for revisions. No public hearing is required; neighbors are typically not notified. For discretionary site plan review — including Design Review Board or Planning Commission proceedings — public notice is required, and neighbors may comment. The distinction between administrative and discretionary review determines the level of subjective judgment applied to design and compatibility questions, and drives the timeline. In many Arizona Phoenix-metro jurisdictions, site plan review is primarily administrative for by-right commercial projects, but projects in design overlay zones or those requiring Commission approval follow a quasi-judicial path with required findings.

## Required Submittals

Typical site plan review submittals include: dimensioned site plan showing building footprints, parking counts and layout, drive aisles, loading zones, trash enclosures, and required setbacks; landscape plan with plant schedule and percentages; grading and drainage plan (or preliminary hydraulic report); utility plan (dry and wet utilities, fire hydrant locations); exterior elevations and materials board (for design review); photometric/lighting plan; signage concept (if applicable); and compliance matrix or cover sheet referencing each applicable standard. Projects with prior entitlements include copies of recorded conditions and a compliance checklist. Varies by jurisdiction and project type.

## Typical Timeline

Administrative site plan review typically takes two to six weeks from application acceptance to approval for straightforward projects. Complex projects, or those requiring multiple rounds of comment-and-revision, can take two to four months. Discretionary site plan review (Design Review Board, Planning Commission) adds public notice time and hearing scheduling, typically extending the process to six to twelve weeks. In jurisdictions with concurrent review (where building permit review begins in parallel with site plan review), total elapsed time from site plan submission to permit issuance can be significantly compressed. Many jurisdictions have adopted electronic plan review, which has reduced some processing times. General, current as of 2026-05-24.

## Fees

Administrative site plan review fees are typically lower than legislative entitlement fees, often based on project value or square footage. Design review fees may be higher, particularly for projects requiring architectural consultant peer review. Some jurisdictions charge only for the review itself; others charge per-sheet or per-review-cycle. Verify against the jurisdiction's current fee schedule. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Findings Required

For administrative site plan review, the standard is objective compliance with adopted standards — there are no discretionary findings; the plans either meet the code or they do not. For discretionary site plan review (Design Review Board, Planning Commission), the findings typically include: (1) the project is consistent with adopted design guidelines; (2) the building and site design is compatible with the surrounding context (scale, massing, materials, architectural character); (3) the project complies with applicable development standards; and (4) the site layout adequately addresses circulation, parking, and service access. In some jurisdictions, findings related to "visual impact" or "pedestrian experience" are added for projects in high-visibility locations. The specificity of design guidelines directly determines how much discretion the decision-maker has at site plan review — vague guidelines give more room for subjective judgment; prescriptive standards constrain it.

## Appeal Path

Administrative site plan review decisions are typically appealable to a hearing officer, the Planning Commission, or the planning director within a specified window (commonly 10–30 days). Discretionary Design Review Board or Planning Commission decisions are typically appealable to the city council. Because site plan review involves both objective code compliance and (for discretionary review) subjective design findings, appeals can raise both types of issues. Legal challenge to a site plan decision is possible but less common than for major entitlements, since site plan review rarely involves the same scale of public interest. See [[Appeals Process]] for general mechanics. Varies by jurisdiction.

## Common Pitfalls

The most common pitfall is submitting plans that do not comply with the conditions of a prior entitlement — a failure to read and implement CUP or PUD conditions carefully before drawing the site plan package. This triggers revisions and delays that are entirely avoidable. Applicants also frequently underestimate the design review criteria in overlay districts, submitting conventional suburban designs in urban contexts that require active ground-floor uses, zero setbacks, or specific material standards. Submitting incomplete packages — missing a required plan sheet, an outdated survey, or an unsigned compliance matrix — causes intake rejection and restart delays. In jurisdictions with concurrent review, failing to coordinate the site plan approval timeline with the building permit team can create gaps where permits cannot be issued even after site plan approval. Finally, changes to a project after site plan approval (value-engineering modifications) that affect code-regulated dimensions or conditions of approval may require a plan amendment or new approval cycle.
