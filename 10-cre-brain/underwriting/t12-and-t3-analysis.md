# T-12 and T-3 Analysis

## Definition

T-12 and T-3 analysis refers to the review of a property's trailing twelve-month (T-12) and trailing three-month (T-3) operating financial statements as the primary empirical basis for underwriting in-place income and expenses. T-12 represents a full annual operating cycle, smoothing seasonal effects and capturing the full range of maintenance and expense events over a year. T-3 is a more recent, higher-frequency read that reveals current momentum — whether occupancy, rents, or expenses have moved since the beginning of the trailing year, and whether the T-12 average might be misleading because conditions have changed.

Together, these two data sets form the factual anchor of every acquisition and refinance underwrite. They answer the fundamental due-diligence question: what has this property actually done, as opposed to what its rent roll or offering memorandum claims it should do? The discipline of reading T-12 and T-3 statements critically — with attention to line-item trends, internal consistency, and deviation from prior-year actuals — is one of the core competencies separating sophisticated institutional buyers from less rigorous ones.

Both statements feed directly into the [[Expense Normalization]] process: the analyst starts from T-12 actuals and adjusts each line to reflect a sustainable, recurring cost basis. They also validate the [[Rent Roll Analysis]] by confirming whether scheduled rent collections match the rent roll billings. Any sustained gap between billed rent and collected rent signals credit or occupancy issues that the rent roll alone may obscure. The result, after normalization, is an evidence-based estimate of [[Stabilized NOI]] that can be defended to lenders, equity partners, and appraisers.

## When It's Used

T-12 and T-3 data is requested as part of the initial due-diligence checklist on any income-producing property acquisition. Lenders require it as part of their loan underwriting package — they will not issue a term sheet for a permanent or bridge loan without reviewing at least 12 months of operating statements. Equity sponsors use it to calibrate the gap between the seller's pro forma projections and demonstrated performance. Appraisers use trailing operating statements as one of three income approaches to value (alongside a direct capitalization and DCF based on market data).

The T-3, while shorter, is particularly useful when a property has undergone operational changes during the trailing year — a new management company, a lease-up following a renovation, or a macroeconomic shock. In these cases, the T-12 average may blend pre- and post-change periods and understate or overstate the current run rate. The analyst annualizes the T-3 (multiply monthly average by 12) as a current-run-rate check and compares it to the T-12 average. When they diverge materially (more than 5–10%), the analyst must determine which period is more representative of forward performance and justify the choice in the [[Assumption Hierarchy: Actual vs. Pro Forma]].

## Variations & Edge Cases

Several conditions complicate straightforward use of T-12/T-3 data:

- **Seasonal properties:** Hospitality assets, ski resorts, or beach properties have revenue and expense patterns that are inherently seasonal. A T-3 drawn from the off-season will dramatically understate the annual run rate. In these cases, a full trailing calendar year plus month-by-month seasonality analysis is essential.
- **Recent lease-up or renovation:** A property that was 60% occupied at the start of the trailing year but is now 95% occupied will have a T-12 that understates current income. Here the T-3 run rate and stabilized pro forma projections should be given more weight than the T-12 average.
- **COVID and structural disruptions:** Trailing periods that include major structural disruptions (pandemic shutdowns, natural disasters, major tenant bankruptcies) may contain non-recurring income gaps or expense spikes. These should be called out, normalized, and replaced with forward assumptions supported by market data.
- **Seller manipulation:** In rare but material cases, sellers have inflated reported income in the trailing period by temporarily incentivizing occupancy (e.g., paying several months of free rent upfront in exchange for a higher lease commencement rent) or deferring legitimate expenses into the post-closing period. Cross-checking T-12 operating statements against bank statements, rent receipts, and accounts payable records is the primary defense against this.
- **Cash vs. accrual basis:** Some small operators report on a cash basis while institutional buyers underwrite on an accrual basis. Security deposit accounting, prepaid rents, and deferred expenses must all be reconciled between the two standards.

## Common Mistakes

The most dangerous mistake is treating the T-12 as a mechanical input — entering it into the model without reading the individual line items for reasonableness. Analysts must look for: (a) management fees that are zero or below 2% of EGI (suggesting self-management that won't persist); (b) real estate taxes that are far below what a post-sale reassessment would produce; (c) maintenance expenses in a year with no major repairs despite an aging property; and (d) utility reimbursements that don't reconcile with the utility billing. Each of these is a normalization trigger per [[Expense Normalization]].

A second common error is using only one of the two trailing periods. Relying solely on T-12 misses current-period trends; relying solely on T-3 introduces volatility and may miss seasonal patterns. Best practice is to request both, plus prior-year operating statements for comparison, to build a multi-year perspective. Finally, analysts sometimes fail to reconcile T-12 income with the rent roll — if the rent roll shows $2.0M in scheduled annual income but T-12 actual collections were $1.75M, that $250,000 gap warrants explanation.
