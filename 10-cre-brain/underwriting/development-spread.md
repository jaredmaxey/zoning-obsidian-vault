# Development Spread

## Definition

Development spread is the numerical expression, in basis points, of the value creation premium generated by a new development project. It is computed as the yield on cost (YoC) minus the prevailing market capitalization rate for comparable stabilized assets. A positive spread means the developer is creating equity — the stabilized income stream, when capitalized at the market rate, produces a property worth more than it cost to build. A narrow or negative spread signals that development is not economically rational at current cost and rent levels.

The development spread is the most concise single metric for communicating development viability to capital partners, investment committees, and lenders. Rather than requiring a full pro forma review to assess whether a project creates value, a single spread figure carries the core message: "we build at a 6.5% yield and the stabilized market cap rate is 5.0%, so we create 150 bps of spread and are building roughly $1 of value for every $0.87 of cost." For development-focused funds, tracking the spread available in a market over time provides a leading indicator of development activity — when the spread compresses below the hurdle, rational developers stop starting new projects, which eventually restores market equilibrium.

In formula terms, the spread is:
- Plain text: `Development Spread = Yield on Cost (YoC) − Exit Cap Rate`
- LaTeX: $$ Spread_{dev} = YoC - R_{exit} = \frac{NOI_{stab}}{TDC} - R_{exit} $$

Where: YoC = stabilized NOI divided by total development cost (including land); R_exit = market capitalization rate for stabilized comparable assets; NOI_stab = stabilized net operating income; TDC = total development cost including land, hard costs, soft costs, financing costs.

The implied value creation multiple can be derived from the spread:
- Plain text: `Value / Cost = YoC / Exit Cap Rate`
- LaTeX: $$ \frac{V_{completed}}{TDC} = \frac{YoC}{R_{exit}} $$
At a 6.5% YoC and 5.0% exit cap, value/cost = 6.5 / 5.0 = 1.30 — the developer builds $1.30 of stabilized value for every $1.00 of cost (illustrative).

## When It's Used

Development spread is used at three stages of a development cycle: (1) market entry evaluation — before underwriting a specific site, a developer surveys the spread available in a target market by estimating land costs, construction costs, and achievable rents vs. prevailing cap rates, to decide whether the market is worth pursuing at all; (2) project-level feasibility — at the deal level, the spread is the go/no-go criterion: does the specific project generate a spread above the firm's minimum hurdle, after accounting for all costs?; and (3) land pricing — via the [[Residual Land Value Method]], the required spread determines the maximum land price the developer can pay.

Institutional development funds typically set minimum required spreads as a firm-level underwriting standard (e.g., "we require a minimum 200 bps spread on all speculative office or retail; 150 bps on pre-leased industrial; 175 bps on multifamily"). These hurdles are calibrated to the risk profile of the asset class — speculative development with full leasing risk commands more spread than a pre-leased build-to-suit with a creditworthy tenant. In periods of high construction cost inflation (as experienced in 2021–2023), achievable spreads compressed significantly in many markets even as rents were rising, because cost escalation outpaced rent growth.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The spread can be computed on a total cost basis (including all land and development costs) or on a cost-excluding-land basis. When evaluating whether to proceed with entitlement and design work before a land price is locked, the analyst sometimes computes the "spread before land" — what YoC would be if land were free — and then back-solves for the land cost that still delivers the required spread. This is the residual land value computation.

For adaptive reuse projects ([[Adaptive Reuse]]), the spread is computed the same way but the "cost" includes the building acquisition price, renovation hard costs, and soft costs — the distinction from ground-up is that the land and building are acquired as a bundle rather than land alone. Spreads on adaptive reuse projects are sometimes wider than ground-up development because the repositioning risk is larger and lenders demand a higher return premium.

## Common Mistakes

Conflating development spread with cap rate spread or credit spread is a common error in cross-sector conversations. Development spread is specific to the YoC vs. exit cap comparison for a new project; it should not be confused with the [[Spread to Treasuries]] (the cap rate premium over the risk-free rate used in pricing stabilized acquisitions). A second error is computing the spread at today's cap rates without stress-testing for cap rate expansion over the development timeline — a project with 200 bps of spread at today's cap rate may have only 75 bps of spread if cap rates expand 125 bps by the time the project stabilizes.

Analysts sometimes omit financing costs from TDC, inflating the YoC. Construction loan interest carry, origination fees, and lender reserves are real costs that reduce the effective return — they must be included in TDC to compute an accurate spread. On a 24-month construction and lease-up project with a 7% construction loan rate on 65% LTC, interest carry alone can add 200–300 bps to the effective yield needed before spread even starts to build.
