# Reserves for Replacement

## Definition

Reserves for replacement (also called capital reserves or replacement reserves) are a periodic set-aside — typically expressed as an annual dollar amount per unit (for multifamily) or per square foot (for commercial assets) — intended to fund the eventual replacement of capital items with finite useful lives: roofs, HVAC systems, parking lots, appliances, elevators, plumbing infrastructure, and similar long-lived but depletable building components. Reserves recognize the economic reality that a property's physical plant deteriorates over time and requires periodic capital replacement to maintain its competitive position and physical integrity.

The accounting treatment of reserves sits at the heart of a significant definitional debate in CRE underwriting. Institutional practice and most appraisal conventions treat reserves as a **below-the-line capital expenditure** (deducted after NOI), not as an operating expense (deducted before NOI). Under this convention, NOI does not reflect reserves — NOI is calculated before reserves, and the investor accounts for reserves separately when modeling distributable cash flow. Under an alternative convention (used by some lenders, the HUD underwriting system, and some analysts), reserves are treated as an operating expense deducted before NOI, which produces a lower NOI and a lower cap rate value for the same asset.

The distinction matters because cap rates are applied to NOI: if NOI is defined before reserves, the resulting value is higher. A buyer who acquires based on a NOI-before-reserves cap rate and then fails to fund adequate reserves is effectively deferring the capital cost into the future — a form of financial engineering that will eventually manifest as a deferred maintenance liability or value erosion.

## When It's Used

Reserves are underwritten in every CRE pro forma that models multi-year cash flows. In multifamily underwriting, agency lenders (Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac) require minimum reserve escrows — currently in the range of $250–$400+ per unit annually depending on property age and condition (varies by market and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24). Lenders hold these reserves in escrow and release them only for approved capital expenditures.

For commercial properties (office, retail, industrial), reserves are often expressed as a dollar amount per square foot per year and calibrated to the expected capital expenditure cycle for major building systems. A 30-year-old office building with aging HVAC and a roof approaching end-of-life requires higher reserves than a newly constructed building still under warranty.

When modeling cash-on-cash return and distributable equity cash flow, reserves are deducted from NOI to arrive at "adjusted NOI" or "cash flow after reserves." A property showing a 7% cap rate on a pre-reserves NOI may show a 5.5% "adjusted yield" after deducting $500,000 of annual reserves — a meaningful difference that affects the investor's actual income experience. See [[Cash-on-Cash Return]] and [[CapEx Planning]].

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Per-unit vs. per-square-foot benchmarks:** Multifamily reserves are most commonly benchmarked per unit per year; commercial reserves are typically per square foot per year. The specific amount depends on the property age, physical condition, and the capital replacement cycle.

**Funded vs. unfunded reserves:** Some deal structures require reserves to be funded into a lender-controlled escrow account (held as cash). Others project reserves as a pro forma line item but do not require actual cash funding, effectively reducing the projected cash flow without changing the actual bank account. Lender requirements determine the actual funding obligation.

**Reserves vs. capex:** There is a conceptual distinction between routine reserves (modest annual set-asides for expected component replacement) and value-add or repositioning capital expenditures (one-time large investments to reposition an asset). In underwriting, reserves are modeled as a recurring annual item; discretionary capex is modeled as a specific one-time event.

**Above vs. below NOI in appraisal:** The Appraisal Institute generally treats reserves as a below-NOI item in appraisals of stabilized investment properties, consistent with institutional buyer practice. HUD/FHA multifamily appraisals use a different convention that includes reserves above the NOI line. Always confirm which convention is in use.

## Common Mistakes

**Underreserving to inflate NOI and cap rate value.** A seller who projects $50/unit/year in reserves for a 1985 building is likely underestimating. Institutional buyers and lenders will apply their own reserve standards; artificially low reserves in the offering memorandum will be rejected.

**Using the same reserve assumption for all property ages.** A new building under warranty requires different reserves than a 40-year-old building with aging systems. Reserve requirements should be calibrated to a physical needs assessment or property condition report.

**Forgetting reserves when comparing cap rates.** If two sellers both quote a 6.0% cap rate, but one uses NOI before reserves and the other uses NOI after reserves, they are quoting different things. The pre-reserves cap rate will produce a higher apparent value for the same actual earnings power.

## Sources

Reserve requirements are published by Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac multifamily guidelines, HUD MAP (Multifamily Accelerated Processing) guidelines, and various lender programs. IREM and BOMA publish benchmarks by property type. Physical condition reports (PCRs) from engineering firms provide property-specific reserve estimates. All figures are property-condition and market specific (varies by market and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).
