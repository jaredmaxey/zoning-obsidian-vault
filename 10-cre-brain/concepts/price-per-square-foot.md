# Price per Square Foot

## Definition

Price per square foot (PPSF) is the acquisition price (or total project cost) divided by the rentable or gross square footage of a property, yielding a size-normalized pricing benchmark. It is the most universally applicable pricing metric in commercial real estate — used across every asset class from industrial warehouses to Class A office towers to multifamily apartments — because it normalizes for building size and allows comparison across properties of different scales. Where [[Price per Unit]] applies only to residential/multifamily, price per square foot applies everywhere.

PPSF is closely related to but distinct from the cap rate: cap rate tells you what yield you are purchasing, while PPSF tells you what you are paying in absolute terms per unit of space. Both metrics are necessary for a complete picture. A property can have the same PPSF as a comparable but a different cap rate if the income levels (rents) differ — perhaps because one is below market and being valued on in-place income. Conversely, two properties at the same cap rate can have vastly different PPSF if one has higher rents per square foot.

In development and construction, per-square-foot cost tracking is standard practice. Developers track hard cost per square foot, total cost per square foot, and projected value per square foot continuously throughout a project to monitor budget performance and value creation. When total development cost per square foot exceeds projected value per square foot at the market cap rate, the project is upside down and should be reassessed. See [[Replacement Cost]] and [[Development Feasibility Test]].

## Formula

- Plain text: `Price per Square Foot = Total Price / Rentable Square Footage`
- LaTeX: $$ \text{PPSF} = \frac{\text{Total Price}}{\text{Rentable Square Footage (RSF)}} $$

**Where:**
- **Total Price** — acquisition price or total development/project cost
- **Rentable Square Footage (RSF)** — the area used depends on asset class convention: rentable square feet (per BOMA standards) for office, warehouse square feet for industrial, net rentable area for multifamily, gross leasable area (GLA) for retail

## When It's Used

PPSF is used at every stage of a CRE transaction. Brokers present it on offering memoranda; buyers use it to rank and screen opportunities; appraisers deploy it in the sales comparison approach; developers track it throughout design and construction. For large institutional portfolios, portfolio managers track average PPSF across holdings to assess portfolio-level pricing and compare it to market benchmarks.

In rent analysis, rent per square foot (not price per square foot) is the companion metric that drives the income side of the equation. Understanding both price per square foot and rent per square foot for a market allows a quick back-of-envelope assessment: if rent is $X/sf/year and the cap rate is Y%, the implied value is X/Y per square foot. If the market is trading above or below that implied value, the market is pricing in rent growth, value-add potential, or a correction, respectively.

For renovation and repositioning, developers use cost-per-square-foot for renovation budget benchmarking. Heavy value-add renovation of an older office building might cost $80–150 per square foot in hard costs; a light refresh might cost $20–40. These benchmarks vary by market and vintage (varies by market and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Rentable vs. gross vs. usable square footage:** Different asset classes use different area standards. Office uses rentable square feet (includes a load factor/common area factor); industrial uses the total warehouse footprint; multifamily often uses net livable or gross building area. Comparing PPSF across asset classes or even across properties using different measurement conventions produces invalid comparisons. Always confirm the area basis.

**Land vs. building PPSF:** In land transactions, PPSF refers to the site area (per square foot of land), which is a different metric from the building's PPSF. In dense urban markets, land can be priced per buildable square foot (total FAR × site area), which is more useful for development feasibility.

**Value-add vs. stabilized PPSF:** Buying a property at $150/sf that needs $50/sf of renovation yields an all-in cost of $200/sf. If stabilized value is $220/sf, there is a $20/sf margin. This spread analysis is the PPSF version of yield-on-cost analysis.

## Common Mistakes

**Comparing PPSF across different quality classes without adjustment.** A 1980s Class C warehouse trades at a much lower PPSF than a 2024 Class A logistics facility in the same market, for legitimate reasons (clear height, dock count, truck court depth, sprinklers, office finish). Raw PPSF comparison without quality context is misleading.

**Using gross building area when market convention is rentable area (or vice versa).** A building with heavy common areas (lobbies, corridors) will have a higher GBA but lower RSF. Using GBA in a market where PPSF comps are based on RSF understates the effective PPSF.

**Ignoring land value in PPSF for development.** A development project's all-in PPSF includes land. In high-land-cost urban markets, the land component can represent 30–50% of total PPSF — a developer who tracks only hard cost PPSF and forgets land is dramatically underestimating total cost per square foot.
