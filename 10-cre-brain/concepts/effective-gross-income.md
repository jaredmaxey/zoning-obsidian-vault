# Effective Gross Income (EGI)

## Definition

Effective gross income (EGI) is the total income a property is realistically expected to generate over a period, after deducting vacancy and collection loss from gross potential rent (GPR) and adding all ancillary income streams. It represents the income actually expected to be collected — as opposed to GPR, which is the theoretical income at 100% occupancy at market or contract rents. EGI is the revenue base from which operating expenses are deducted to arrive at [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]]; every dollar of EGI that can be grown or every dollar of vacancy that can be reduced flows directly through to NOI and property value.

The structure of EGI reflects the operating reality of the property. Gross potential rent is the ceiling; vacancy and collection loss is the standard deduction that reflects market conditions and credit quality; and other income includes revenue streams beyond base rent — parking fees, laundry income (for apartments), storage fees, signage income, common area maintenance reimbursements (for commercial properties), and other ancillary sources. For properties with significant other income (parking garages, for example), other income can be a material percentage of EGI and must be underwritten carefully.

EGI is the appropriate denominator for the [[Operating Expense Ratio]] (OER = Operating Expenses / EGI), because it represents the income that the property actually collects and against which expense efficiency should be measured. Using GPR as the denominator for OER would understate the expense burden, since expenses are incurred regardless of whether the property is fully occupied.

## Formula

- Plain text: `EGI = GPR + Other Income − Vacancy & Collection Loss`
- LaTeX:
$$
\text{EGI} = \text{GPR} + \text{Other Income} - \text{Vacancy} - \text{Collection Loss}
$$

**Where:**
- **GPR (Gross Potential Rent)** — total rent if all space is leased at current contract or market rents at 100% occupancy
- **Other Income** — all non-rent revenue: parking, laundry, storage, late fees, pet fees, application fees, utility income (if billed back), tenant reimbursements, signage, antenna/telecom leases, etc.
- **Vacancy** — physical vacancy loss: GPR × vacancy rate (see [[Vacancy and Collection Loss]])
- **Collection Loss** — credit/bad debt loss: GPR × collection loss rate

## When It's Used

EGI appears as a line-item intermediate total in every institutional CRE pro forma. In acquisition underwriting, the buyer builds a year-by-year EGI projection by estimating GPR growth (rent escalations, rollover to market), vacancy, and other income, then deducts operating expenses to reach projected NOI. The EGI line is scrutinized carefully by lenders and equity investors: is the other income sustainable? Is the vacancy assumption realistic for the submarket?

In appraisal, the income approach explicitly models EGI as the revenue starting point for NOI derivation in both the direct capitalization and DCF methods. Appraisers compare the subject property's projected EGI against the EGI of comparable operating properties to assess reasonableness.

For NNN leased properties, other income may be minimal and EGI closely tracks base rent minus vacancy. For hotels, gross operating revenue (the hospitality analog to EGI) includes room revenue, food and beverage, and other departmental revenues. For retail with CAM (common area maintenance) reimbursement structures, EGI includes tenant reimbursements as other income.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Other income volatility:** Certain other income streams are highly stable (cell tower leases, structured parking leases with long-term tenants) and others are highly variable (month-to-month parking revenue, laundry income dependent on occupancy). Stable other income can be capitalized at a cap rate similar to base rent; variable other income may warrant a discount or separate treatment.

**Tenant reimbursements in modified gross leases:** In properties with expense stop or modified gross lease structures, tenants reimburse the landlord for operating expenses above a base year. These reimbursements appear as other income in EGI (or sometimes as a separate "reimbursement income" line). Projecting reimbursements requires modeling both the tenant's rent obligation and the expected expense escalation above the stop.

**Gross potential rent for rent-stabilized properties:** For rent-controlled or rent-stabilized apartments, GPR must reflect the legally allowable rents, not unrestricted market rents. This can create a significant delta between GPR-at-market-rent and GPR-at-legal-rent for long-tenured stabilized units.

## Common Mistakes

**Using GPR as a proxy for EGI.** GPR and EGI are different concepts. On a fully stabilized property with 5% vacancy, using GPR rather than EGI as the revenue base overestimates income by approximately 5%.

**Underestimating other income on the downside.** Just as some buyers over-project other income, some sellers on value-add assets understate the future other income from items like valet parking or premium storage units — a source of additional upside for the buyer.

**Not separating other income that is contractual vs. discretionary.** A cell tower lease with a 20-year term is highly reliable other income; month-to-month parking revenue in a competitive market is not. Blending these in underwriting without differentiation misrepresents the quality of the EGI.
