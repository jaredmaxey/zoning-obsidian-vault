# Price per Unit

## Definition

Price per unit is the acquisition price (or total project cost for development) divided by the number of residential units, yielding a per-unit pricing benchmark. It is the most commonly used comparative metric in multifamily real estate: when evaluating two apartment communities in the same submarket, a broker, buyer, or appraiser will immediately compare price per unit to assess relative value, even before running full cap rate or NOI analysis. The intuition is that units are the fundamental income-producing element of a multifamily asset — more units means more rent, so normalizing by unit count removes the size variable and enables apples-to-apples comparison.

Price per unit is a blunt instrument that captures size-normalized pricing but ignores unit mix, unit size, quality, amenities, and location within a submarket. A garden-style complex with 300 studio and one-bedroom units will trade at a lower price per unit than a mid-rise building with 200 two- and three-bedroom units even if total income per property is similar — because the unit size and the income per unit differ. Sophisticated buyers use price per unit as an initial screen, then immediately drill into price per square foot, NOI per unit, and cap rate for proper analysis.

Price per unit is also used to benchmark development costs: a developer tracking cost-per-unit across comparable construction projects can quickly identify whether a proposed project's $250,000/unit total cost (for example) is in line with recent comparable developments in that submarket, or whether cost overruns or an unusual program are inflating the budget. When per-unit development cost significantly exceeds what stabilized per-unit values support, the project may not pencil — see [[Development Feasibility Test]].

## Formula

- Plain text: `Price per Unit = Total Price / Number of Units`
- LaTeX: $$ \text{Price per Unit} = \frac{\text{Total Price}}{\text{Number of Units}} $$

**Where:**
- **Total Price** — the agreed acquisition price (for sales) or total all-in project cost (for development, including land, hard costs, soft costs, and carry)
- **Number of Units** — total number of residential dwelling units (apartments, condos, townhomes); exclude commercial ground-floor units unless the intent is a mixed-use per-unit benchmark

## When It's Used

Price per unit is the first metric brokers put on a marketing flyer for an apartment community and the first number institutional buyers use to locate a deal on the market spectrum. Large multifamily investors and REITs track price-per-unit comps to build a submarket pricing map — understanding where deals are trading per unit allows quick underwriting of new opportunities before full diligence.

Lenders also use price per unit informally to sanity-check appraisals and loan requests: an unusual price per unit (much higher or lower than recent comparables) triggers additional scrutiny. Appraisers use price per unit as one output of the sales comparison approach in the multifamily income property appraisal.

For development, tracking per-unit replacement cost (total construction cost per unit) against per-unit market value (price per unit at market) is one of the simplest feasibility tests: if you can build for $200,000 per unit and the market trades at $250,000 per unit, there appears to be development margin. If the market trades at $180,000 per unit, development doesn't pencil. See [[Replacement Cost]] and [[Yield on Cost]].

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Unit mix adjustment:** Comparing price per unit across properties with different unit mixes is imprecise. A complex of all studios will have many more units per rentable square foot than a complex of three-bedroom units, so per-unit pricing will look different even at the same per-square-foot price. Use [[Price per Square Foot]] alongside price per unit for mixed-unit-type comparisons.

**Affordable vs. market rate:** Affordable housing communities may have lower rents and NOI per unit, and therefore lower market prices per unit — but their development cost per unit may equal or exceed market-rate development due to compliance and regulatory costs. Comparing price per unit across affordable and market-rate is not meaningful without context.

**Geographic variation:** Price per unit varies enormously by market. As of 2026-05-24, multifamily assets in gateway urban markets can trade at multiples of per-unit prices seen in secondary Sunbelt markets, reflecting rent differentials, growth expectations, and capital availability (varies by market and vintage).

## Common Mistakes

**Using price per unit without considering unit size.** A low price per unit may simply reflect small units. Always cross-reference with price per square foot and rent per unit/square foot.

**Ignoring physical quality and age.** A 1985-vintage Class B complex will naturally trade at a lower price per unit than a 2022-vintage Class A complex in the same submarket — the comparison is meaningful only within comparable vintage and quality tiers.

**Applying national averages to local decisions.** Price per unit figures from national surveys are not actionable for underwriting a specific submarket deal. Always use local comparable sales.
