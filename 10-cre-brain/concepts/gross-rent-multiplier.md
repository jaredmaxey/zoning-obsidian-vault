# Gross Rent Multiplier (GRM)

## Definition

The gross rent multiplier (GRM) is the ratio of a property's sale price to its gross scheduled rental income for a period — typically expressed as a multiple of annual gross rent, though some markets use monthly GRM. It is a rapid-fire screening metric that requires no expense analysis: if you know the price and the rent, you can compute a GRM in seconds. This simplicity makes it useful for quick portfolio screening and back-of-envelope comparisons, but it omits expenses entirely, making it a crude approximation at best.

GRM is most commonly used in residential and small multifamily markets (1–4 units, and sometimes up to 20–30 units in smaller markets) where buyers often have limited operating expense data, transactions are relatively homogeneous, and speed of analysis matters. Institutional CRE practitioners nearly always use [[Cap Rate]] and [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] instead — but GRM remains relevant for understanding how less-sophisticated market participants price assets and for identifying obvious mispricings in thin, data-poor markets.

Because GRM ignores expenses, two properties with identical gross rents and GRMs may have dramatically different NOI and value if one has higher property taxes, vacancy rates, or utility costs. Properties in high-tax jurisdictions, older buildings with high maintenance costs, or assets with large landlord-paid utilities will produce lower NOI from the same gross rent — making a simple GRM comparison unreliable without context.

## Formula

- Plain text: `GRM = Purchase Price / Gross Scheduled Annual Rent`
- LaTeX: $$ \text{GRM} = \frac{\text{Purchase Price}}{\text{Gross Scheduled Annual Rent}} $$

**Where:**
- **Purchase Price** — the agreed sale price (or estimated market value)
- **Gross Scheduled Annual Rent** — total annual rental income if the property were 100% occupied at current contract rents (not effective gross income — vacancy is not deducted)

Monthly GRM variant: `Monthly GRM = Purchase Price / Gross Monthly Rent`

For example: a duplex purchased for $600,000 with gross annual rent of $48,000 has a GRM of 12.5x. In some markets, practitioners use monthly GRM: $600,000 / $4,000/mo = 150x monthly GRM.

## When It's Used

GRM is used by residential investors, 1031 exchange buyers evaluating replacement properties, and small private investors who are screening large numbers of listings quickly. In thin or private markets where operating expense data is hard to obtain, GRM provides a pricing framework based on publicly available rent data. It is also used in retrospective market analysis — tracking how GRMs have moved over time in a market can reveal trends in pricing relative to income.

Some analysts use GRM as a "sanity check" on a cap rate: if GRM seems unreasonably high relative to the market (property is expensive per dollar of gross rent), it's a signal to look carefully at whether expenses are being understated in the cap rate presentation. GRM is occasionally used in narrative appraisals of small residential income properties as a supporting valuation check alongside the income and sales comparison approaches.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Annual vs. monthly GRM:** Annual GRM is more common in institutional analysis; monthly GRM is widely used among small residential investors. Always confirm the period.

**Gross scheduled rent vs. effective gross income:** GRM typically uses gross scheduled rent (100% occupancy, no vacancy deduction). Some practitioners use effective gross income (net of vacancy) in the denominator, which produces a slightly different (higher) GRM. Consistency within a comparison set is more important than which convention is used.

**GRM compression as a market indicator:** Rising GRMs (falling cap rates, rising prices relative to rents) indicate a market where buyers are paying more per dollar of gross income — usually a sign of demand exceeding supply, expected rent growth, or capital chasing returns in a low-interest-rate environment.

GRM values vary considerably by market and vintage. In major gateway multifamily markets, GRMs for apartment buildings have historically ranged from roughly 10–20x annual gross rent; in secondary and tertiary markets they tend to be lower. These figures vary significantly with interest rates and local market conditions (varies by market and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

## Common Mistakes

**Using GRM alone to make acquisition decisions.** A low GRM may reflect high expenses that make the property's cap rate unattractive once properly analyzed. Always follow up with full NOI analysis.

**Comparing GRMs across different property types.** A 12x GRM for a small multifamily is not comparable to a 12x GRM for a NNN retail strip because expense structures and growth profiles differ entirely. GRM is only useful within a narrow comparable set.

**Forgetting vacancy.** GRM is computed on gross scheduled rent — 100% occupancy. A property with chronic 15% vacancy actually generates much less income than the GRM implies, making the price effectively higher per dollar of collected rent.

## Related Concepts

- [[Net Operating Income (NOI)]] — the metric that cap rate uses; GRM's absence of expense analysis makes it inferior to NOI-based valuation
