# Net Operating Income (NOI)

## Definition

Net operating income (NOI) is the annual income generated by a commercial real estate property after subtracting all operating expenses from effective gross income, but before deducting debt service, income taxes, depreciation, and capital expenditures. It is the single most important metric in income-producing real estate: it is the numerator of the cap rate formula, the input to the [[Direct Capitalization]] valuation method, and the foundation of every [[Discounted Cash Flow (DCF)]] model. The quality, durability, and growth rate of a property's NOI ultimately determines its value.

NOI is a property-level, financing-agnostic metric. Because it excludes debt service, it is the same regardless of how the deal is capitalized — making it useful for comparing properties across different ownership and leverage structures. It also excludes capital expenditures, which distinguishes it from free cash flow: a property may report strong NOI while simultaneously requiring large capital outlays for roof replacement, HVAC, or tenant improvements that the NOI figure does not capture. Practitioners must always analyze NOI in conjunction with capital expenditure requirements to assess true economic performance.

The calculation path is: Gross Potential Rent + other income − vacancy and collection loss = [[Effective Gross Income (EGI)]] − operating expenses = NOI. Each step requires careful underwriting: gross potential rent must reflect market rents (not above- or below-market leases in isolation), vacancy must reflect realistic stabilized rates for the submarket, and operating expenses must be normalized for extraordinary items. See [[T-12 and T-3 Analysis]] and [[Expense Normalization]].

## Formula

- Plain text: `NOI = EGI − Operating Expenses`
- Or expanded: `NOI = (Gross Potential Rent + Other Income − Vacancy & Collection Loss) − Operating Expenses`
- LaTeX:
$$
\text{NOI} = \text{EGI} - \text{Operating Expenses}
$$
$$
\text{EGI} = \text{GPR} + \text{Other Income} - \text{Vacancy} - \text{Collection Loss}
$$

**Where:**
- **GPR (Gross Potential Rent)** — total rent if 100% of units/space leased at market or contract rates
- **Other Income** — parking, laundry, storage, fee income, reimbursements (in gross/modified gross leases)
- **Vacancy & Collection Loss** — market-rate vacancy allowance plus projected bad debt; see [[Vacancy and Collection Loss]]
- **EGI (Effective Gross Income)** — gross income actually expected to be collected; see [[Effective Gross Income (EGI)]]
- **Operating Expenses** — property taxes, insurance, management fees, maintenance/repairs, utilities (where landlord-paid), landscaping, administrative costs; excludes debt service, depreciation, capex, and income tax

## When It's Used

NOI is used in every phase of a CRE transaction. In acquisition underwriting, buyers build pro formas projecting NOI over the hold period, then capitalize terminal-year NOI by the exit cap rate to determine the projected sale price. In appraisal, the income approach divides stabilized NOI by the market cap rate to derive a value indication. In lending, lenders compute DSCR (NOI divided by debt service) to size loans and test coverage. See [[Debt Service Coverage Ratio (DSCR)]].

In asset management, actual NOI is tracked against budget monthly and quarterly. Deviations — rising vacancies, expense overruns, below-market rollover leases — are early warning signals. NOI growth is the primary mechanism for value creation in core and core-plus strategies where leverage is modest; in value-add strategies, the execution thesis is explicitly about growing NOI from a depressed baseline to a stabilized level. See [[Stabilized NOI]].

## Variations & Edge Cases

**In-place vs. stabilized NOI:** In-place NOI reflects actual current occupancy and leases. Stabilized NOI assumes market-rate occupancy and normalized expenses. For a value-add asset at 70% occupancy, stabilized NOI may be 30–40% higher than in-place NOI, producing dramatically different valuations.

**Trailing vs. forward NOI:** T-12 (trailing twelve months) actual NOI is the historical reality; year-one pro forma NOI is the forward projection. Buyers and sellers often negotiate whether to underwrite to trailing or forward NOI, with sellers preferring forward (higher) and buyers preferring trailing (more conservative).

**NOI in triple-net vs. gross leases:** In a triple-net (NNN) lease, tenants pay operating expenses directly; landlord NOI equals base rent minus the few landlord-borne costs. In a gross lease, the landlord collects gross rent and pays expenses; the NOI is gross rent minus all operating costs. The structures can produce similar NOI but very different expense risk profiles.

**Treatment of reserves:** Most institutional practice excludes reserves for replacement from NOI (treating them as below-the-line capex). Some analysts include them as an operating expense, producing a lower "adjusted NOI." Clarity on treatment is essential when comparing properties or trading cap rates. See [[Reserves for Replacement]].

## Common Mistakes

**Using gross rent instead of EGI.** The NOI calculation requires effective gross income (post-vacancy, post-collection loss), not gross potential rent. Using gross rent inflates NOI and understates the cap rate.

**Under-projecting operating expenses.** Sellers and aggressive brokers often present NOI based on low historical expense ratios or exclude management fees (for owner-managed properties). Institutional buyers normalize expenses — add market-rate management fees, verify tax assessments post-sale, and stress insurance costs. See [[Operating Expense Ratio]].

**Ignoring above/below-market leases.** A property's in-place NOI may be artificially high (leases above market, rolling in 12 months) or artificially low (leases below market, rolling to market rents). Pro forma NOI must reflect the lease-roll reality.

**Confusing NOI with cash flow.** NOI is pre-debt-service and pre-capex. Actual investor cash flow is lower: after paying the mortgage and funding capital improvements, distributable cash may be significantly less than NOI.
