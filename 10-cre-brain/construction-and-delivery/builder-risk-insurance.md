# Builder's Risk Insurance

## Definition

Builder's risk insurance (also called "course of construction" insurance) is a specialized form of property insurance that covers a building and its materials while under construction. It protects against physical loss or damage to the structure, building materials, and equipment on site from covered perils—typically fire, lightning, windstorm, hail, explosion, vandalism, theft, and water damage. Builder's risk is not liability insurance (that is covered by the GC's general liability and the owner's policy); it is property insurance for the work-in-place and materials during the construction window.

Builder's risk policies are temporary: they are in force from the start of construction (or when materials are delivered to site) until substantial completion, when the property is turned over to the owner and a permanent property insurance policy takes effect. The policy period typically aligns with the construction schedule, with provisions for extension if the project runs over. Coverage applies to the completed value of the work—the insured value increases as the building is constructed, and the policy must be structured to keep pace with the growing replacement value of the in-progress structure.

Builder's risk is structured as either an "all-risk" (open-perils) or a "named-perils" policy. All-risk policies cover any physical loss unless specifically excluded; named-perils policies cover only the listed perils. Most institutional projects use all-risk policies, which provide broader coverage and reduce disputes about whether a particular cause of loss is covered. Common exclusions include: faulty workmanship, design error, mechanical breakdown, earthquake (unless endorsed), flood (unless endorsed), and consequential losses such as lost rents or delay damages.

## When It's Used

Builder's risk is typically required by the [[Construction Loan]] lender as a condition of loan funding. Lenders are named as additional insureds and loss payees on the policy, ensuring that any insurance proceeds resulting from a covered loss are applied to the restoration of the property (rather than paid out freely to the borrower). The lender's construction loan agreement will specify minimum coverage requirements including the insured amount, deductible limits, and required endorsements.

The question of who procures builder's risk varies by project and contract structure. In most institutional development, the **owner** purchases builder's risk (an "owner-controlled builder's risk" policy), because the owner can often negotiate better terms and pricing through their portfolio relationships, and because a single policy eliminates the risk of coverage gaps between the owner's and GC's separate policies. In some GMP and lump-sum contracts, the GC may be required to provide builder's risk as part of their scope, with the cost included in [[General Conditions]] or the contract price—this "contractor-controlled" approach is also common.

Premium for builder's risk is a [[Soft Costs]] line item in the development budget. Pricing depends on the type of construction, project value, location, deductibles, and coverage terms, and varies by market and market conditions (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24). Projects in catastrophe-exposed areas (hurricane zones, flood-prone locations, wildfire interface) face materially higher builder's risk premiums and may require separate flood and wind endorsements or standalone policies.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Soft costs endorsement** (also called "loss of rents" or "delay in opening" coverage) is an optional endorsement that extends builder's risk to cover certain soft costs—interest carry, lost rental income, additional financing costs—that the developer incurs because a covered physical loss delayed the project. This is valuable on projects with tight schedules where even a short delay has significant financial consequences. Not all builder's risk policies offer this endorsement, and coverage terms vary widely.

**Installation floaters** cover equipment and materials that are off-site (in transit, at a fabricator's shop, or in a staging yard) before they are incorporated into the building. Standard builder's risk policies may exclude or limit coverage for materials that are not yet on the project site. For high-value items with long lead times—structural steel, elevators, curtain wall—an installation floater may be needed to ensure continuous coverage from the point of order to installation.

**Completed value vs. reporting form:** A "completed value" policy is the most common approach—it is written at the projected completed value of the structure from the beginning, and the premium is calculated on that full value. A "reporting form" policy requires the owner to report insured values monthly as construction progresses. Completed value policies are simpler to administer and avoid the risk of under-insurance due to missed reports.

## Common Mistakes

The most significant error is allowing a coverage gap between the builder's risk policy expiration and the permanent property insurance commencement. If the project runs over schedule and the builder's risk policy lapses without extension, there may be a window when the building—now substantially complete but not yet fully turned over—is uninsured or inadequately insured. Policy extensions should be secured proactively as soon as schedule slippage is anticipated.

Failing to properly insure off-site stored materials is a recurring problem. Materials stored at a staging area, fabricator, or supplier location are vulnerable to fire, theft, and damage, and may not be covered under the standard builder's risk policy. A specific endorsement or installation floater should be secured for high-value off-site materials.

Confusing builder's risk with the GC's general liability policy is a conceptual error. Builder's risk covers property (physical damage to the building and materials); general liability covers bodily injury and property damage to third parties caused by the GC's operations. Both are required—they cover different risks and neither substitutes for the other.
