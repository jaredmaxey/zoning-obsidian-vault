# Value Engineering

## Definition

Value engineering (VE), also called value analysis, is a structured process of reviewing the design and specifications of a building project with the goal of identifying cost savings that do not materially reduce functional performance, safety, or marketability. The term originates in manufacturing (developed at General Electric in the 1940s) and was adapted for construction. In CRE development, VE is conducted when a preliminary cost estimate or GC bid exceeds the development budget, forcing the team to find cost reductions before the GMP can be established within the pro forma.

The core logic of VE is to evaluate each design element in terms of cost relative to value delivered—asking "what does this do, and is there a less expensive way to accomplish the same function?" Common VE substitutions include: switching structural systems (steel to wood frame, cast-in-place concrete to precast), simplifying facade materials (curtain wall to storefront, masonry to EIFS), reducing mechanical system complexity (four-pipe to two-pipe HVAC), value-substituting interior finishes (engineered stone to LVT, custom millwork to stock cabinetry), eliminating non-essential program elements, or simplifying site work and landscaping.

VE is distinct from scope reduction: a true VE action maintains the function or performance outcome while lowering cost through a different means of achieving it. Eliminating a fitness center is scope reduction; substituting commercial-grade equipment for luxury-tier equipment while maintaining the fitness function is VE. In practice, the line blurs and the terms are used interchangeably on many projects.

## When It's Used

VE is most commonly triggered when the GC's GMP estimate or the cost consultant's design-phase estimate exceeds the developer's hard cost budget. This typically occurs at the transition from Schematic Design to Design Development, or when DD-level documents are priced before the GMP is negotiated. The timing is critical: VE is far less costly and disruptive when done during design than during construction, when redesign requires redrawing permit documents, re-bidding subcontractor scopes, and potentially re-permitting—all of which consume time and [[Soft Costs]].

On [[Design-Build vs. Design-Bid-Build]] design-build projects, VE is ongoing throughout design development, since the design-builder has direct incentive to reduce cost (savings flow to their margin). On design-bid-build projects, VE may occur as a formal workshop post-bid—the owner, GC, and design team meet to review the estimate and generate a list of VE options, each priced by the GC with an estimated cost delta. The owner then decides which VE items to accept.

In feasibility analysis and [[Development Feasibility Test]] modeling, developers sometimes model a "VE scenario"—a hard cost reduction representing the potential savings achievable through an aggressive VE process—to test whether a project can be made feasible if initial estimates come in high. This is a legitimate sensitivity case but should not be the base case; VE savings are uncertain until the GMP is executed.

## Variations & Edge Cases

"Reverse VE"—adding cost to improve quality or performance—is sometimes called "value add" in construction contexts (distinct from the deal type). This occurs when a market analysis suggests that higher-quality finishes or systems will command rents that justify the incremental cost. A developer who upgrades kitchen finishes from standard to premium in a luxury multifamily project is making an investment in rent premium, not cutting cost. [[Yield on Cost]] analysis should support this decision.

Long-lead procurement is a related concept. Some VE actions involve switching to systems or materials with shorter lead times, which benefits schedule rather than (or in addition to) direct cost. In periods of supply chain stress (as seen in 2020–2022), choosing a structural system with available domestic steel over one reliant on imported materials could be a VE decision that protects schedule even if it costs marginally more per ton.

In [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] GMP contracts with savings sharing provisions, both owner and GC have incentive to pursue VE—savings below the GMP are split according to the pre-negotiated formula. This can motivate genuine cost innovation. In lump-sum contracts, VE savings accrue entirely to the GC (who priced the higher-cost approach in the bid); owners may negotiate VE provisions explicitly to share in post-award savings.

## Common Mistakes

The most dangerous VE mistake is accepting cost cuts that reduce long-term durability or building performance in ways that increase future operating costs or accelerate capital replacement needs. Substituting a lower-quality roofing system may save $200,000 at construction but require full replacement in year 12 rather than year 25, with a net-present-value impact far greater than the initial savings. [[Reserves for Replacement]] and lifecycle cost analysis should inform VE decisions, not just first-cost comparison.

Cutting items that are critical to leasing velocity or rent premium is a common but poorly-understood error. Eliminating or downgrading amenities (fitness center, co-working, rooftop deck) in a multifamily project to save $500,000 in hard costs may cost more in reduced lease-up pace and rent per unit than the savings—but this trade-off is difficult to quantify and often underweighted relative to the visible budget savings.

Conducting VE too late in the design process is expensive. Every redesign cycle after schematic design is locked carries architectural fees, consultant coordination costs, and schedule risk. A project that discovers it needs $3 million in VE after 100% Construction Documents have been completed will spend $300,000–$600,000 in redesign fees to implement it—consuming a large share of the budget savings. VE should begin at the earliest design stage.
