# Hard Costs

## Definition

Hard costs are the direct physical expenditures required to construct a building and prepare a site. They represent everything that becomes embedded in—or permanently attached to—the real property: earthwork, foundations, structural framing, exterior envelope, mechanical/electrical/plumbing (MEP) systems, vertical transportation, interior finishes, and landscaping. In institutional development underwriting, hard costs are the single largest cost category on a pro forma, typically representing 50–65% of total project cost depending on asset class, location, and scope (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

The term "hard" is meant to convey that these costs are tangible, physical, and relatively measurable by quantity takeoffs and contractor bids—as opposed to [[Soft Costs]], which are fees, carrying costs, and other non-physical expenditures. However, the line between hard and soft can blur in practice: some practitioners include [[General Conditions]] within hard costs (since GC costs are part of the construction contract), while others break them out as a separate line. The convention varies by firm, lender, and project delivery method—always confirm which definition a given budget or pro forma is using.

Hard costs are further divided into on-site and off-site work. On-site hard costs cover everything within the project's property boundary. Off-site hard costs—utilities extensions, road improvements, public infrastructure dedications—may be required by entitlement conditions and can be material, particularly on large or greenfield developments.

## When It's Used

Hard cost budgets are assembled at multiple stages of a project's lifecycle. Early in feasibility (Schematic Design or earlier), developers use cost-per-unit, cost-per-square-foot, or cost-per-key benchmarks to model total hard costs; these benchmarks carry wide ranges and large contingencies because design is incomplete. As design advances through Design Development and Construction Documents, costs are refined via subcontractor buyout and ultimately fixed (or risk-allocated) through the delivery contract—see [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]].

Lenders and equity investors scrutinize hard costs heavily during due diligence. Construction lenders build the hard cost budget into the loan-to-cost calculation and require third-party construction cost reviews before funding. Appraisers use hard cost replacement benchmarks when appraising under the cost approach—see [[Replacement Cost]]. Hard costs also flow directly into the [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] and the [[Development Feasibility Test]].

## Variations & Edge Cases

The most common definitional debate is whether [[General Conditions]] are "hard" or "soft." General conditions (the GC's direct project overhead: superintendent, field trailer, temporary utilities, insurance, equipment) are part of the construction contract and are sometimes lumped into hard costs. Many institutional developers and lenders break GC costs out as a distinct line so they can benchmark them separately. Neither convention is wrong—but mixing conventions between a developer's budget and a lender's cost review can create apparent discrepancies.

Tenant improvement (TI) allowances in commercial leases are hard costs to the developer even though they flow through a lease transaction; they must be included in the hard cost budget on ground-up retail, office, or industrial projects. Likewise, shell-and-core vs. warm-shell vs. full build-out distinctions in commercial projects significantly affect the hard cost scope per square foot. Always clarify what "scope" a quoted hard cost PSF figure includes.

## Common Mistakes

The most frequent error is using benchmark costs from a different market, vintage, or product type without adjustment. A multifamily wood-frame cost PSF in Phoenix does not translate to a podium project in San Francisco—cost variation across markets, labor environments, and supply chain conditions is large and persistent.

A second common error is treating hard costs as fixed once a GMP is executed. Change orders—driven by owner-directed scope changes, unforeseen conditions, or design errors/omissions—routinely expand the hard cost budget. [[Contingency: Hard vs. Soft]] exists precisely to absorb this risk; underwriters who assume zero change orders will routinely be wrong.

A third mistake is omitting off-site improvement costs from the pro forma. Off-site utility extensions, signal improvements, and public infrastructure required by conditions of approval can add 5–15% or more to on-site hard costs on difficult sites—these must be modeled, not assumed away.
