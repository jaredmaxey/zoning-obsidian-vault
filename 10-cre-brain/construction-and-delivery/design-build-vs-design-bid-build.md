# Design-Build vs. Design-Bid-Build

## Definition

Project delivery method describes how design and construction services are contracted and sequenced. The two foundational methods are **design-bid-build (DBB)**—the traditional linear sequence—and **design-build (DB)**—an integrated approach in which one entity is responsible for both. The choice of delivery method has profound implications for schedule, cost certainty, risk allocation, owner control, and the design quality achievable within budget.

**Design-Bid-Build (DBB):** The owner contracts separately with a design team (architect, engineers) and a contractor. Design is completed first (or substantially completed), then the project is competitively bid, and a GC is selected. The contractor constructs what the design team has specified. The owner sits between design and construction and bears the coordination risk between them—if the architect makes an error that costs money to fix, the GC may claim a change order, the architect may deny responsibility, and the owner is caught in the middle. DBB is the traditional "architect-of-record" model, produces the most competitive pricing on well-defined scopes, and gives the owner maximum design control, but it is the slowest delivery method.

**Design-Build (DB):** A single entity—a design-build firm, a GC who hires a design team, or a developer-led integrated team—contracts with the owner for both design and construction. The designer and builder are on the same team, which eliminates the interface dispute problem and allows design and construction to overlap (fast-tracking). The design-builder typically prices on a lump-sum or GMP basis from a set of preliminary criteria or performance specifications that the owner develops. The trade-off is reduced owner control over design decisions: the design-builder has latitude to make cost-saving design choices within the agreed performance specifications, and the owner may receive a building that meets specs but not their preferred aesthetic or layout.

## When It's Used

DBB is most appropriate when the owner has a clear program and design vision, wants maximum control over architectural decisions, and has sufficient time to complete design before construction begins. It is standard practice for public works and government projects (where competitive bidding laws often require it) and for highly custom or architecturally significant projects where design quality cannot be compromised for cost savings.

DB is preferred when schedule compression is a priority, when the owner is comfortable defining requirements through performance criteria rather than detailed drawings, and when the project type is sufficiently routine that the design-builder can price the risk. Warehouse and distribution center development, [[Build-to-Suit]] corporate facilities, and commodity housing types are well-suited to design-build delivery. DB can deliver 10–25% schedule savings compared to DBB by allowing structural engineering and early trade packages to proceed while architecture is still completing (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

**CM at Risk** is a third delivery method that combines elements of both: the owner hires a construction manager (CM) during design to provide constructability and cost input, and the CM then enters a [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] GMP contract to build the project. This is common on complex institutional projects (hospitals, universities, civic buildings) where the owner wants the design control of DBB but the cost certainty and early engagement of design-build. The CM at Risk model is widely used by institutional real estate developers for large multifamily and mixed-use projects.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Integrated Project Delivery (IPD)** is an emerging variant in which the owner, architect, and GC enter a single tripartite contract with shared risk and reward pools. IPD is designed to eliminate adversarial relationships and align all parties toward cost and schedule performance. It is still a minority delivery method in commercial real estate but has found traction in complex healthcare and institutional projects.

**Progressive Design-Build** allows the design-builder to develop the design and cost estimate collaboratively with the owner before locking in a GMP. This addresses the owner's concern about surrendering design control: the owner participates in design development, then the design-builder proposes a GMP once the design is sufficiently defined. This is a compromise between traditional DB (owner hands over criteria and waits) and CM at Risk.

The [[Construction Loan]] underwriting process is affected by delivery method. Lenders are generally most comfortable with a completed design and lump-sum or GMP contract, because they can verify the cost and scope with certainty. Design-build with an early GMP can satisfy lenders; early-stage design-build (performance spec only, no GMP) is harder to finance because cost certainty is limited.

## Common Mistakes

In design-build projects, the most common owner error is writing weak performance specifications. If the criteria document doesn't specify quality levels, material standards, and system performance requirements with precision, the design-builder can meet the letter of the spec with lower-quality solutions. Owners who value design quality must invest heavily in developing detailed performance specifications and hiring an independent design advisor (owner's architect) to review design-builder submittals.

In design-bid-build projects, the most common error is starting construction before design documents are truly complete. "We'll figure it out in the field" is the origin story of most change order disputes. Compressed schedules that push bidding before DD is complete almost always result in more change orders, not less total time—the schedule savings from early start are eroded by RFI delays and change order negotiations during construction.

Owners sometimes assume that design-build costs less than design-bid-build. This is not always true. On commodity projects with experienced design-builders, DB pricing can be very competitive. On complex or novel project types, the design-builder prices risk into their GMP that a fully-competed lump-sum might not include. The right delivery method is context-dependent, not universally cheaper or more expensive.
