# GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum

## Definition

Construction contracts can be priced in three principal ways, each representing a different allocation of cost risk between the project owner and the general contractor (GC). Understanding this allocation is fundamental to development underwriting because the pricing structure affects the developer's budget certainty, the lender's collateral protection, the GC's incentive to control costs, and the project's overall risk profile.

**Lump-Sum (Stipulated Sum):** The GC agrees to complete the defined scope for a fixed price. All cost risk above the lump-sum price (for that scope) falls on the GC; the owner knows their hard cost exposure in advance. This is the highest-certainty structure for the owner—but it requires nearly complete design documents before bidding, and the GC prices in a risk premium to compensate for unknown exposures. Lump-sum contracts are most appropriate when the design is fully complete, the scope is well-defined, and the owner does not anticipate significant changes. AIA Document A101 is the standard form.

**Cost-Plus (Time and Materials):** The owner pays the GC's actual costs (labor, materials, subcontractor invoices, general conditions, equipment) plus a fixed fee or percentage fee for profit and overhead. All cost risk (up or down) flows to the owner. This structure is used when the scope is uncertain, when the project must start before design is complete, or when the owner wants transparency into actual costs. The owner bears the risk of an inefficient or poorly managed GC, and the GC has limited incentive to minimize costs. Cost-plus with a fee percentage actually creates a perverse incentive: a higher cost base means a higher absolute fee.

**GMP (Guaranteed Maximum Price):** A hybrid structure in which the owner pays actual cost-plus (reimbursable costs plus fee), but the GC guarantees the total will not exceed a negotiated maximum price—the GMP. Savings below the GMP are shared between owner and GC according to a pre-negotiated split (e.g., 75% to owner, 25% to GC). Cost overruns above the GMP are the GC's liability. This structure is common in institutional development and allows work to begin with incomplete documents (unlike lump-sum) while still giving the owner a cost ceiling. GMP contracts are usually built on AIA Document A102 or A133 (CM at Risk).

## When It's Used

The choice of contract structure is driven by project characteristics, schedule constraints, and market conditions at the time of procurement. In [[Ground-Up Development]] with complex programs, GMP is the dominant structure used by institutional developers because it allows design and construction to overlap (reducing schedule) while providing cost certainty once the GMP is set. The GMP is typically established once design is at least 60–75% complete, with a defined scope of allowances and clarifications covering remaining design uncertainty.

Lump-sum bidding is common in simpler, repetitive project types (garden-style multifamily, basic industrial) where design can be completed before bidding, and on public projects where competitive bidding law may require lump-sum. It is also prevalent in strong bidding markets where multiple GCs compete, driving down prices and the risk premium.

Cost-plus arrangements are used in emergency or fast-track situations where work must begin with minimal documentation, in highly custom or complex projects (high-end custom residential, complex retrofits), or in early stages of work where the owner is still defining scope. [[Construction Loan]] lenders typically prefer GMP or lump-sum because they provide cost certainty; lenders are generally uncomfortable with pure cost-plus without a negotiated cap.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The "open-book GMP" is increasingly common among institutional owners. In this structure, the owner has full visibility into the GC's cost breakdown—all subcontractor bids, general conditions line items, and fee—allowing the owner to audit and understand every dollar. The transparency builds trust and often results in lower total cost because the GC cannot obscure inefficiencies in an opaque lump-sum estimate. This pairs with the savings-sharing provision to create aligned incentives.

CM at Risk (Construction Manager at Risk) is a delivery variant where the project owner contracts directly with a construction manager who serves as GC-equivalent under a GMP. The CM provides preconstruction services (cost estimating, constructability review, scheduling) during design, then converts to a GMP at an agreed design milestone. This is common on larger, more complex projects and is effectively a GMP delivered through a CM structure—see also [[Design-Build vs. Design-Bid-Build]].

Early Work Packages (EWPs) or Early Release Packages allow work on portions of the project to begin under cost-plus or letter of intent before the full GMP is established. Foundation work, site work, and steel fabrication are commonly started on EWPs to capture schedule—while the balance of scope is compiled into the full GMP. Each EWP must be carefully scoped to avoid creating overrun liability before the GMP ceiling is in place.

## Common Mistakes

The most common owner mistake with GMP contracts is allowing the GMP to be set with too many open allowances and unresolved scope items. An allowance is a placeholder cost for an undefined scope; if the actual cost of an allowance exceeds the budgeted amount, it becomes a GMP overage only if the overall GMP is exceeded—but allowance blowouts frequently consume contingency and push budgets over. Owners should minimize allowances and push for maximum scope definition before GMP execution.

With lump-sum contracts, the risk of change orders is the primary hazard. GCs bid aggressively on base scope and then recover margin through change orders on anything that can be characterized as owner-directed change, design error/omission, or differing site condition. A thorough review of the plans and specifications for gaps and ambiguities before bidding—and a well-crafted contract with clear change order provisions—is the mitigation.

Cost-plus contracts require rigorous owner oversight of general conditions billings. Without active monitoring, GCs may bill home office costs, unrelated equipment, or inflated labor rates as reimbursable project costs. An owner's representative or construction manager should audit all billings monthly.
