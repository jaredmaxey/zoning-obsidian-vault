# Certificate of Occupancy (CO)

## Definition

A Certificate of Occupancy (CO) is an official document issued by the local building or planning department certifying that a building or a defined portion of it has been constructed in accordance with the approved building permit, applicable building codes, and zoning requirements, and is safe and legal for human occupancy. In most jurisdictions, a building cannot be legally occupied—by residents, tenants, employees, or the public—until the CO is issued. The CO represents the culmination of the building inspection process: all required inspections (foundation, framing, rough MEP, insulation, life safety, final) must be completed and approved before the CO is issued.

The CO is one of the most consequential milestones in any development project because it is the triggering event for: (1) building occupancy and the start of lease-up, (2) conversion of the construction loan to permanent financing (or repayment), (3) commencement of the lender's "completion guarantee" cure period, and (4) often, the beginning of the developer's promote clock under equity partnership agreements. Delays in CO issuance—due to failed inspections, outstanding punchlist items, or municipal backlog—directly delay revenue generation and increase carry costs.

Different jurisdictions use different terminology. "Certificate of Occupancy" is the most common term; others include "Temporary Certificate of Occupancy" (TCO), "Occupancy Permit," "Certificate of Completion," and "Certificate of Use." The specific local usage and issuance process must be confirmed in every market.

## When It's Used

CO timing is modeled from the first pro forma: the construction schedule drives the expected CO date, which then determines when rent revenue begins, how long the interest carry runs, and when the construction loan must be repaid or converted. Delays in CO issuance—which are common and frequently underestimated—add carrying costs that accrue under [[Soft Costs]] and may trigger loan extension fees.

Construction lenders track CO issuance as a critical loan milestone. Many [[Construction Loan]] agreements include a "completion date" or "CO deadline" by which the borrower must achieve CO—failure to do so can constitute an event of default or require a formal extension request, often with an extension fee and additional equity contribution. Lenders also require the CO as a condition for final draw funding and the release of retainage.

In multiphase developments, multiple COs may be issued sequentially—one per building, per floor, or per defined "phase" or "tranche." Early-phase COs allow partial occupancy and lease-up to begin before the entire project is complete. This sequencing must be coordinated with the construction loan draw schedule and lender approval.

## Variations & Edge Cases

**Temporary Certificate of Occupancy (TCO):** Many jurisdictions will issue a TCO—sometimes called a Temporary Certificate of Completion—allowing partial or conditional occupancy while minor outstanding items (typically low-safety-impact items on the [[Punchlist and Final Completion]]) are completed. A TCO is extremely common on multifamily projects: the CO may be issued for all common areas and a portion of units, with the remaining units receiving CO through follow-on inspections as they are completed. Lenders and equity investors are generally comfortable with TCOs for lease-up purposes, but the final CO (or the last TCO tranche) must be achieved within the agreed timeline.

**Phased COs** on large projects allow portions of the building to be occupied in sequence. A mixed-use project might receive a residential CO for the upper floors while the ground-floor retail shell remains under construction. This requires careful coordination of fire-life-safety systems, egress, and code compliance for each occupied portion.

**Certificate of Completion vs. CO:** In some jurisdictions, a "Certificate of Completion" is issued for buildings that are structurally complete but do not require a CO (e.g., some tenant improvements, certain non-habitable structures). The legal effect is different—a Certificate of Completion does not authorize occupancy.

## Common Mistakes

The most frequent underwriting error is building a development pro forma with an optimistic CO date that does not account for inspection scheduling lead times or municipal processing backlogs. In high-volume construction markets (Phoenix, Las Vegas, Austin during growth periods), building department inspection schedules can be backlogged weeks, and final inspections may require multiple rounds before approval. This does not appear on the CPM schedule as a trade activity but is a real-time risk.

Treating the TCO as equivalent to final CO is a second error. Some lease obligations, loan covenants, and partnership agreements require final CO (not TCO) for certain milestones—the distinction matters and must be reviewed in every financing document.

Ignoring CO requirements at the time of design is a less-noticed error. Projects that add uses or program elements (a rooftop amenity, a basement commercial component, a pool, a daycare) without confirming the CO path for each use can discover late in construction that additional inspections, fire suppression upgrades, or accessibility modifications are required that were not in the original scope or budget.
