# Punchlist and Final Completion

## Definition

The **punchlist** (also called a punch list or deficiency list) is the formal list of incomplete or defective work items identified during a walkthrough of the completed (or substantially completed) building by the owner, architect, and contractor. Each item on the punchlist represents something that must be corrected or completed before the project achieves **final completion**—the contractual state at which all work is fully done, all deficiencies are resolved, all close-out documentation is received, and the contractor is entitled to final payment including retained funds.

**Substantial completion** is the interim milestone, recognized before the punchlist is resolved, at which the work is sufficiently complete that the owner can occupy or use it for its intended purpose. AIA Document G704 (Certificate of Substantial Completion) memorializes this date, which starts the contractual clock for several critical timelines: the owner's lien waiver rights, warranty periods, and the period within which the contractor must complete the punchlist. Substantial completion is not the same as [[Certificate of Occupancy (CO)]]—though they often occur close together, they are legally distinct (one is a contractual milestone, the other a municipal permit).

Final completion occurs when all punchlist items are complete, all contractor-required close-out documents are delivered (warranties, as-built drawings, O&M manuals, lien waivers), and the architect issues a final certificate for payment. This triggers release of the **retainage**—typically 5–10% of the contract value that has been withheld from each progress payment as security for the contractor's completion obligation (varies by market, project type, and contract terms; current as of 2026-05-24).

## When It's Used

Punchlist and final completion management is a critical project close-out discipline. From the developer's perspective, retainage represents a real cash liability on the balance sheet that is released only at final completion—for a $20 million hard cost project with 10% retainage, that is $2 million held back. Delays in punchlist completion delay retainage release, which extends the [[Construction Loan]] balance (if the loan is still open) or reduces net equity returned to investors.

From the lender's perspective, final completion is often a condition for releasing the holdback portion of the construction loan and closing out the loan. Lenders may also require evidence of final completion and retainage release before transitioning to permanent financing or releasing the completion guarantee. A lingering punchlist with significant open items is a red flag for lender inspectors.

Practically, the punchlist walkthrough is conducted by the owner's representative or architect of record (in DBB) or by the owner's team (in design-build) once substantial completion is claimed. The list is compiled in a documented format—item number, location, description, responsible subcontractor, and required completion date—and tracked through a formal tracking system or construction management software until all items are signed off.

## Variations & Edge Cases

In practice, punchlists are rarely completed quickly. On large multifamily or commercial projects, punchlists may contain hundreds or thousands of line items at substantial completion, and final completion may lag substantial completion by 3–6 months or longer if trade subcontractors demobilize and are slow to return for punch work. This is a chronic problem in the industry: trades prioritize new active projects over punch work on completed ones, and the GC's leverage over subcontractors diminishes once the project is substantially complete. Retainage mechanics exist precisely to address this—the subcontractor cannot receive their final retainage until punch items are complete.

**Retainage reduction** is increasingly common in institutional construction contracts. Some states have enacted laws capping or reducing retainage once the project reaches 50% completion (e.g., California, Texas). Many sophisticated owners and GCs negotiate contracts that allow retainage to be reduced from 10% to 5% at a defined completion milestone, or to be substituted with a letter of credit. This reduces the contractor's cost of capital but still provides security for the owner.

In [[GMP vs. Cost-Plus vs. Lump-Sum]] GMP contracts, the close-out process also includes reconciliation of the GMP—comparing actual costs to the GMP ceiling and calculating savings sharing amounts. This accounting process runs in parallel with punchlist completion and must be finalized before final payment.

## Common Mistakes

The most widespread mistake is treating substantial completion as final completion for financial modeling purposes. A developer who assumes retainage flows back at substantial completion rather than final completion will have an inaccurate cash flow model. Retainage can be withheld for 3–9 months beyond substantial completion on complex projects, and this timing affects investor return calculations and loan payoff sequencing.

Failing to establish a complete and accurate punchlist at the substantial completion walkthrough creates disputes later. If the list is incomplete at the walkthrough, the contractor may claim that subsequently discovered items are not punchlist items but rather warranty claims or new work—this distinction has legal and cost implications. The substantial completion walkthrough should be comprehensive, thorough, and well-documented with photographs.

Owners who reduce retainage too aggressively, or waive it as a good-faith gesture to the GC, lose their primary leverage for punchlist completion. A GC who has been paid in full has limited incentive to return and complete minor punch items—the remaining liability is the [[Payment and Performance Bonds]] performance bond, which is a slow and contentious remedy. Retainage should be released only when the punchlist is genuinely complete.
