# Payment and Performance Bonds

## Definition

Payment and performance bonds are surety instruments—three-party contracts among the project owner (the "obligee"), the general contractor (the "principal"), and a surety company (the "surety")—that provide financial guarantees protecting against contractor default. Though always discussed together, they address distinct risks:

A **performance bond** guarantees that the GC will complete the construction contract in accordance with its terms. If the GC defaults—becomes insolvent, abandons the project, or is terminated for cause—the surety is obligated to either (1) complete the project by financing the defaulting contractor's completion, (2) hire a replacement contractor at the surety's cost, (3) provide a cash payment to the owner up to the bond penalty amount, or (4) deny the claim and litigate. The performance bond amount is typically equal to the full contract value.

A **payment bond** guarantees that the GC will pay all subcontractors, material suppliers, and laborers on the project. If the GC fails to pay (due to insolvency, cash flow issues, or bad faith), subcontractors and suppliers can make a claim against the payment bond rather than filing a mechanic's lien against the property. The payment bond protects the owner's title by ensuring downstream parties have a remedy that doesn't require clouding the property with liens.

Both bonds are underwritten by the surety based on the GC's financial strength, experience, backlog, and credit—bonding capacity is a meaningful indicator of a contractor's financial health and industry standing. Bond premiums are paid by the GC (and typically included in the contract price or [[General Conditions]]) and range from roughly 0.5–3% of the contract value, varying by contract size, GC creditworthiness, and market conditions (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

## When It's Used

Payment and performance bonds are standard requirements on public projects (federal law under the Miller Act requires both bonds on federal contracts over $150,000; most states have similar "Little Miller Act" requirements for state and local projects). On private development, bonds are optional—they are a developer/owner decision based on risk tolerance, lender requirements, and project characteristics.

Private institutional developers increasingly require bonds on large projects, particularly when using a GC with whom they have limited prior experience or on projects where contractor default would be catastrophic. [[Construction Loan]] lenders may require bonds as a condition of the loan, especially in markets where GC defaults have occurred or where the developer has a thin equity cushion. Bonds add cost (the premium) and administrative burden but provide meaningful protection—particularly the payment bond, which protects the owner's title from mechanic's liens, a critical consideration when the construction loan is collateralized by the property.

In competitive bid situations (particularly [[Design-Build vs. Design-Bid-Build]] design-bid-build public work), requiring bonds as a bid condition filters out financially weak contractors who cannot qualify for bonding. Bidders who cannot obtain bonds from a creditworthy surety are arguably not qualified to perform the work—the bond requirement is thus a prequalification screen as well as a risk management tool.

## Variations & Edge Cases

The surety's response to a performance bond claim is a legally complex and often protracted process. Sureties do not simply write a check—they investigate the claim, often dispute the owner's characterization of the default, and may assert that the owner's own actions (failure to pay, scope changes, interference) caused or contributed to the GC's default. Performance bond claims frequently result in litigation or arbitration and take years to resolve. This means that a performance bond is a backstop against catastrophic loss, not a quick remedy—the owner must still manage the project through default and recovery.

**Subcontractor bonds** are a related instrument: some owners or GCs require major subcontractors to post their own performance and payment bonds, protecting against default by a critical subcontractor (structural, mechanical, electrical) rather than the prime GC. This adds cost but is appropriate for large, complex scopes where subcontractor default would materially disrupt the project.

**Letters of credit (LOCs)** are sometimes used as an alternative to bonds for smaller projects or private work. An LOC from a creditworthy bank provides a liquid cash security—the owner can draw on the LOC upon contractor default without the protracted surety claims process. However, LOCs are limited in amount (typically 5–15% of contract value vs. 100% for a bond) and consume the contractor's banking credit capacity, making them less practical for large projects.

**Completion guarantees** (often required by [[Construction Loan]] lenders from the developer, not the GC) are not bonds—they are personal or entity guarantees by the developer that the project will be completed. They address project-level completion risk, not contractor default risk. A project can have both a completion guarantee from the developer and a performance bond from the GC.

## Common Mistakes

A common error is assuming that holding a performance bond eliminates the need for contractor prequalification. Bonds are not a substitute for vetting the GC's financial health, project management capacity, and relevant experience. An underqualified GC who happens to be bondable will still perform poorly; the bond merely backstops the financial consequences of their eventual default.

Owners who do not understand the bond claim process are sometimes surprised by the surety's aggressiveness in defending against claims. The surety is in the business of underwriting, not completing projects—they will assert every available defense to avoid paying. Owners should involve legal counsel experienced in surety law before making a performance bond claim.

Requiring bonds on all projects without considering cost-benefit is a third error. On a small project (under $2–3 million) with an established GC, the bond premium (which is ultimately passed through in the contract price) may exceed the realistic risk mitigation benefit. Bond requirements should be calibrated to project size, GC risk profile, and lender requirements.
