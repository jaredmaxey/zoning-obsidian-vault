# Soft Costs

## Definition

Soft costs are the non-physical expenditures required to develop a project—everything that does not become embedded in the structure itself but is necessary to design, entitle, finance, and deliver it. Canonical soft costs include: architecture and engineering (A&E) fees, civil engineering and surveying, geotechnical studies, environmental reports, entitlement/permitting fees and consultants, legal fees, title and escrow, developer overhead and fee, construction loan interest, lender fees, insurance during construction (other than builder's risk, which some allocate to hard costs), property taxes during construction, leasing commissions, and marketing. Together they typically represent 15–30% of total project cost, though the range is wide and varies by project type, complexity, and jurisdiction (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24).

The distinction between hard and soft costs matters because they behave differently in pro forma analysis, carry different contingency reserves, and are treated differently by construction lenders—who typically advance against hard costs via draw requests tied to physical progress, while soft costs may be funded upfront or on a different schedule. See [[Hard Costs]] for the physical complement.

One important definitional note: what counts as "soft" is not universally agreed. Some practitioners include [[General Conditions]] (GC project overhead) in soft costs; others treat them as hard. Loan interest and carry—sometimes called "financing costs" or "carry costs"—are universally soft but are often presented as a separate budget line. Developer fee may or may not be included in the developer's internal budget depending on whether the developer is modeling project-level or investor-level returns. Always inspect a pro forma's soft cost definition before benchmarking or comparing figures.

## When It's Used

Soft costs are assembled in the project budget from the earliest feasibility stage. At concept, broad estimates are applied as a percentage of hard costs or total cost—typically developers assume 15–25% of hard costs as a soft cost proxy early in underwriting, then refine as fees are contracted and carry is modeled. The [[Pro Forma Construction Method]] requires soft costs as a line item to arrive at total development cost, which feeds into [[Yield on Cost]] analysis.

Construction lenders review soft cost budgets as part of loan underwriting, often requiring evidence that key contracts (A&E, civil, geotech) are executed before funding. Interest carry on the [[Construction Loan]] is a significant and volatile soft cost line—it depends on loan amount, draw timing, interest rate, and construction duration. As interest rates rise, carry cost can increase materially and compress development feasibility, a dynamic that became acute in 2022–2025 as rates spiked.

Soft costs also matter at disposition and refinance: a buyer or appraiser evaluating a newly-completed project under the cost approach will assess whether the developer's incurred soft costs are market-consistent or bloated—abnormally high developer fees or excessive carrying periods can raise questions about project economics.

## Variations & Edge Cases

Developer fee is a contested line item. Some institutional developers charge a percentage of total costs (often 3–6% of total project cost, varying by market, product type, and vintage) as compensation for their services; others regard the developer's profit as captured in the promote/carry structure rather than a separate fee. Lenders may scrutinize or limit developer fee, particularly if the developer is also the general contractor or if the fee structure appears to extract value before project completion.

Financing costs—interest carry, loan origination fees, extension fees, and rate cap premiums—are technically soft costs but often modeled separately because they are highly sensitive to deal timing and market rates. Projects that slip their schedule see carry explode as a percentage of total cost, which is why schedule management is a financial discipline as well as a construction one (see [[Construction Schedule and Critical Path]]).

Lease-up costs (leasing commissions, free rent, tenant improvement allowances on commercial projects) straddle the boundary: commissions are soft costs; TI allowances are often categorized as hard costs or treated as a separate line. Be explicit about where these live in any budget comparison.

## Common Mistakes

Underestimating permit and entitlement costs is common, especially in high-fee jurisdictions (California, certain Nevada and Arizona municipalities) where impact fees, school fees, and development agreements can add $20,000–$80,000+ per unit or more—these are soft costs that are often missed in early back-of-envelope models (varies by market and jurisdiction; current as of 2026-05-24).

Failing to carry adequate interest reserve is another recurring error. Developers who underwrite construction draws front-loaded and carry only 18 months of interest on a project that takes 24 months to lease up will face a shortfall. Sensitivity analysis on the interest carry line should be standard practice.

Treating developer fee as "free" by excluding it from project-level returns distorts the true cost of capital deployed. Even a developer using their own overhead, when properly modeled, has a cost—omitting the fee inflates apparent project-level yields.
