# FF&E

## Definition

FF&E stands for Furniture, Fixtures, and Equipment—the movable personal property items that furnish and equip a completed building but are not permanently affixed to the structure itself. Classic examples include hotel room furniture and bedding, multifamily unit appliances, restaurant equipment, lobby seating, signage, audio/visual systems, fitness equipment, and kitchen appliances. FF&E is distinguished from the building's fixed components (walls, MEP systems, built-in cabinetry) in that it can theoretically be removed without damaging the structure. In accounting and tax, this distinction is meaningful: FF&E is classified as personal property and depreciated on a shorter schedule than the real property shell—typically 5–7 years under U.S. GAAP and MACRS, versus 27.5 or 39 years for residential and non-residential real property respectively.

The magnitude of FF&E varies enormously by asset class. In full-service and select-service hotels, FF&E is one of the most significant cost categories—commonly $20,000–$60,000+ per key depending on brand standards, flag, and market tier (varies by market, product type, and vintage; current as of 2026-05-24). Multifamily FF&E is comparatively modest (appliances, window coverings, fitness equipment in amenity areas). In office, industrial, and retail development, FF&E may be near zero in the base development budget, though tenant improvement packages can include significant FF&E.

FF&E also encompasses the OS&E category in hospitality—Operating Supplies and Equipment, which includes linens, tableware, kitchen utensils, and other consumables needed for opening. OS&E is sometimes budgeted separately from FF&E because it has an even shorter useful life and is replaced more frequently.

## When It's Used

FF&E budgeting is relevant at two distinct points in a project's life: (1) at initial development, where FF&E is a capital cost line in the development budget flowing through the [[Pro Forma Construction Method]]; and (2) in ongoing operations, where FF&E replacement is funded through a recurring reserve—the [[Reserves for Replacement]] line that appears in lender underwriting and hotel management agreements.

In hotel development, brand flags dictate specific FF&E standards through their Property Improvement Plan (PIP) requirements. These standards define the quality tier of furniture, fixtures, and finishes and can significantly affect initial cost and replacement cycles. Acquirers of flagged hotels must review the PIP at acquisition to understand deferred FF&E replacement liability, which affects the [[Value-Add Acquisition]] underwriting.

For ground-up [[Ground-Up Development]], FF&E is modeled as a separate budget line from [[Hard Costs]] and [[Soft Costs]], and it is funded differently—often not eligible for construction loan draws (construction lenders typically fund building costs, not personal property) and instead funded from equity or a separate FF&E credit facility. This creates a cash flow timing consideration: FF&E must be procured and installed in the weeks before opening, requiring a separate funding mechanism.

## Variations & Edge Cases

In multifamily development, the treatment of in-unit appliances (refrigerators, dishwashers, washers/dryers) as FF&E vs. hard cost varies by firm. Some developers include appliances in the hard cost budget since they are typically installed during construction; others break them out as FF&E because they depreciate as personal property. The accounting treatment has real tax consequences—personal property depreciation can be accelerated, which is a meaningful benefit in the early years of ownership. Cost segregation studies are performed after project completion to formally allocate costs between categories and maximize accelerated depreciation.

In [[Adaptive Reuse]] projects, existing FF&E from a prior use may need to be disposed of—a cost that must be budgeted. Conversely, some adaptive reuse projects (converting a historic hotel to residential or back to hotel use) may be able to retain and refurbish existing FF&E, a potential cost savings.

[[Build-to-Suit]] development for corporate users (e.g., a custom office campus) may include extensive FF&E as part of the turnkey scope, in which case the developer must decide whether to carry FF&E on balance sheet or pass it through to the tenant. The lease structure and accounting treatment should be coordinated with tax counsel.

## Common Mistakes

A common error in hospitality development is underestimating FF&E cost or treating it as fungible. Brand flag PIP requirements are non-negotiable and have escalated in quality standards over time; using outdated per-key benchmarks without updating for current brand standards leads to budget shortfalls.

In non-hospitality projects, the most frequent mistake is omitting FF&E from the development budget entirely—particularly for multifamily amenity spaces (fitness centers, co-working areas, rooftop decks) where FF&E costs are real and material even if modest on a per-unit basis.

Failing to establish adequate [[Reserves for Replacement]] for FF&E in operating pro formas is a chronic underwriting error that flatters year-one returns at the expense of long-term hold performance. Lenders on hotel financing in particular will impose reserve requirements; failure to fund them results in a deteriorating physical plant and flag compliance risk.
